The World of Matthew

Life Under Rome — The World Jesus Walked Into

Read Matthew in the Interlinear
The people who sat in darkness have seen a great light, and upon those who sat in the region and shadow of death, light has dawned. — Matthew 4:16

To understand what Jesus said, you have to understand what He walked into. Matthew’s Gospel was written to a Jewish audience living under Roman occupation. Every parable, every confrontation, every healing happens against a backdrop of political tension, religious corruption, crushing poverty, and desperate hope for a deliverer. If you do not understand that world, you will misread the text.

The Political Landscape

❖ Rome Rules Everything

In 63 BC, the Roman general Pompey marched into Jerusalem and walked straight into the Holy of Holies—a room only the High Priest could enter, and only once a year. That single act told Israel everything: your God does not protect you from us. By the time Jesus was born, Israel had been under Roman control for over sixty years.

Rome governed Judea through client kings (like Herod) and later through direct prefects (like Pontius Pilate). The system was designed to extract wealth while maintaining order. As long as you paid your taxes and did not rebel, Rome mostly left you alone. But the taxes were crushing, and the threat of violence was constant.

Josephus records that when Herod the Great died in 4 BC, Rome crucified 2,000 Jews in a single suppression campaign (Wars of the Jews, Book II). Crucifixion was not rare. It was a public deterrent. Jesus’s audience knew exactly what a cross meant.

❖ Herod’s Dynasty

Herod the Great (37–4 BC) was the king who rebuilt the Temple into one of the ancient world’s most magnificent structures—and also the king who ordered the slaughter of infants in Bethlehem (Matthew 2:16). He was not Jewish by lineage (he was Idumean—from Edom) but had converted. The Jewish establishment tolerated him because he built their Temple. The people hated him because he was a murderous, paranoid tyrant who killed three of his own sons.

When Herod died, his kingdom was split among his surviving sons:

  • Archelaus — Judea and Samaria (so brutal Rome removed him in 6 AD and installed direct Roman governors)
  • Herod Antipas — Galilee and Perea (the “Herod” who beheaded John the Baptist, Matthew 14)
  • Philip — territories northeast of the Sea of Galilee

This is why Joseph, returning from Egypt, avoided Judea and settled in Galilee instead (Matthew 2:22–23)—Archelaus was as dangerous as his father.

❖ Tax Collectors — Why They Were Hated

Tax collectors (telōnēs) were not government employees. They were Jews who had bought franchise rights from Rome to collect taxes from their own people. They could charge whatever they wanted above the Roman requirement and pocket the difference. They were collaborators, thieves, and traitors all at once.

When Jesus called Matthew the tax collector (Matthew 9:9) and ate dinner at his house, every person in that room understood the scandal. A rabbi choosing a tax collector as a disciple was like a pastor today recruiting from the people his congregation despises most. It was deliberate provocation—and a statement about who the Kingdom is actually for.

The Religious Sects

❖ Pharisees

The Pharisees were the populist religious party. They believed in the resurrection of the dead, the authority of oral tradition (which they considered equal to written Torah), angels, and demons. They controlled the synagogues. The word Pharisee likely comes from perushim—“separated ones.”

They added hundreds of regulations on top of the Torah. The Sabbath alone had 39 categories of prohibited work, each subdivided into dozens of specific rules. Jesus’s conflicts with the Pharisees in Matthew were not about whether to obey God’s law—they were about whether human traditions had replaced God’s actual commands (Matthew 15:1–9).

❖ Sadducees

The Sadducees were the aristocratic priestly class. They controlled the Temple and its enormous revenue. They did not believe in the resurrection, angels, or an afterlife (Matthew 22:23). They only accepted the written Torah (first five books) as authoritative.

They were wealthy collaborators with Rome. The High Priest was effectively a political appointment—Rome chose who served. This is why Jesus’s trial before the Sanhedrin (Matthew 26:57–68) was a political event as much as a religious one. The Sadducees needed Jesus gone because He threatened their arrangement with Rome.

❖ Essenes

Not mentioned in Matthew but crucial for context. The Essenes had withdrawn from mainstream Judaism entirely, considering both the Temple priesthood and the Pharisees corrupt. They lived in isolated communities (like Qumran, where the Dead Sea Scrolls were found). Some scholars believe John the Baptist had Essene connections based on his wilderness lifestyle and baptismal practices.

❖ Zealots

The Zealots wanted armed revolution against Rome. They believed God would intervene militarily if they started the fight. One of Jesus’s disciples, Simon, is called “the Zealot” (Matthew 10:4 / Luke 6:15). The fact that Jesus had both a tax collector (Roman collaborator) and a Zealot (anti-Roman revolutionary) in the same group of twelve tells you everything about His approach to politics.

Daily Life in First-Century Israel

❖ What People Ate

The staple diet was bread, olive oil, and wine. Bread (artos) was not a side dish—it was the meal. “Give us this day our daily bread” (Matthew 6:11) was not a metaphor for the average person. It was a literal request: let us eat today.

Fish from the Sea of Galilee was the primary protein for those who could afford it. Meat was rare and reserved for festivals. Lentils, beans, figs, dates, and pomegranates rounded out the diet. Most families ate two meals a day—a light morning meal and a larger evening meal.

❖ Housing

Most families lived in single-room stone houses with flat roofs. The roof was used as living space in warm weather—for sleeping, drying food, and socializing. This is the context for “what you hear whispered, proclaim on the housetops” (Matthew 10:27)—housetops were where you shouted news to your neighbors.

❖ Clothing

A person’s “coat” (chitōn) was the inner garment worn next to the skin. The “cloak” (himation) was the outer garment that also served as a blanket at night. Exodus 22:26–27 actually forbade keeping someone’s cloak overnight as collateral because they needed it to sleep.

When Jesus said “if anyone wants to sue you and take your tunic, let him have your cloak also” (Matthew 5:40), He was saying: give them everything—stand there naked. In that culture, the shame fell on the person who caused the nakedness, not the naked person. It was an act of nonviolent resistance that exposed the oppressor.

❖ Work and Labor

Most people were agricultural workers, fishermen, or artisans. A day laborer earned about one denarius per day (Matthew 20:2)—barely enough to feed a family. Unemployment meant starvation. This is why the parable of the workers in the vineyard (Matthew 20:1–16) was so provocative—the men standing idle at 5 PM were not lazy. They were desperate. Nobody had hired them.

Money and Economics

❖ Currency

  • Denarius (dēnarion) — A Roman silver coin. One day’s wage for a laborer. The coin Jesus asked for in the “render unto Caesar” exchange (Matthew 22:19–21).
  • Talent (talanton) — Not a coin but a unit of weight (~75 lbs of silver). One talent equaled roughly 6,000 denarii—about 20 years’ wages. The servant who owed 10,000 talents (Matthew 18:24) owed an impossible, absurd amount—200,000 years of labor. That was the point. The debt was unpayable.
  • Mite / Lepton — The smallest coin in circulation. Two mites equaled one quadrans (1/64 of a denarius). This is what the widow gave (Mark 12:42).
  • Stater (statēr) — A coin worth four drachmas (four days’ wages). This is the coin Peter found in the fish’s mouth to pay the temple tax (Matthew 17:27).

❖ Debt and Poverty

The economy was extractive. Rome taxed the land. The Temple taxed the harvest (tithes, first fruits, temple tax). Local rulers taxed trade. Tax collectors added their cut. A typical family might lose 35–40% of their production to various taxes. Drought, illness, or a bad harvest could push a family into debt slavery.

Jesus’s teachings on forgiveness of debts were not abstract theology to His audience. They were talking about real money owed to real creditors who could sell your family into servitude.

The Temple

❖ Herod’s Temple

The Temple in Jesus’s time was a massive construction project that had been underway for over 40 years and was still not complete. It was one of the largest religious structures in the ancient world. The outer walls enclosed an area the size of several football fields. The stones were enormous—some over 40 feet long and weighing hundreds of tons.

When the disciples marveled at the Temple buildings (Matthew 24:1), this is what they were looking at. When Jesus said “not one stone shall be left upon another” (Matthew 24:2), He was predicting the destruction of the most impressive building they had ever seen. It was destroyed by Rome in 70 AD—within the lifetime of the people He was speaking to.

❖ The Court of the Gentiles

The Temple had concentric zones of increasing holiness. Gentiles could only enter the outermost court. Signs in Greek and Latin warned that any non-Jew who passed beyond would be responsible for his own death.

The “money changers” Jesus overturned (Matthew 21:12) operated in this court. They were necessary because Roman coins (which bore Caesar’s image) were considered idolatrous and could not be used for Temple offerings. But the exchange rates were exploitative, and the animal sellers charged inflated prices for sacrificial animals. The Temple establishment was running a religious marketplace in the only space where Gentiles could pray.

Jesus quoted Isaiah 56:7—“My house shall be called a house of prayer for all nations”—and Jeremiah 7:11—“you have made it a den of robbers.” He was not angry about commerce in general. He was angry that they had turned the Gentiles’ prayer space into a shopping mall.

Customs and Culture

❖ Synagogues

The synagogue (synagogē—literally “gathering together”) was the center of Jewish community life outside Jerusalem. Every town with at least ten Jewish men had one. Services included Scripture reading, prayer, and teaching. Any qualified Jewish male could be invited to teach—which is how Jesus regularly taught in synagogues (Matthew 4:23).

❖ Rabbi-Disciple Relationships

A rabbi (rhabbi—“my great one”) was a Torah teacher. Students did not choose their rabbi—the rabbi chose them. Being chosen by a rabbi meant he believed you could become like him. When Jesus called the Twelve, He was making a radical claim: these fishermen, tax collectors, and zealots could carry His teaching.

Disciples literally followed their rabbi everywhere—eating with him, sleeping near him, imitating his prayers, his habits, his interpretations. The saying was that a disciple should “be covered in the dust of your rabbi”—walking so close behind him on the road that his dust covered you. “Follow me” (Matthew 4:19) was not a casual invitation. It was a total life commitment.

❖ Ritual Purity

Purity laws governed every aspect of daily life. Touching a dead body, a menstruating woman, a leper, or certain animals made you ritually “unclean” (akathartos). Being unclean did not mean being sinful—it meant being temporarily unable to participate in Temple worship. But the Pharisees had expanded purity regulations far beyond the Torah’s requirements.

When Jesus touched lepers (Matthew 8:3), ate with sinners (Matthew 9:10–11), and allowed a bleeding woman to touch Him (Matthew 9:20), He was not breaking God’s law. He was demonstrating that purity flows outward from Him rather than impurity flowing inward. He made the unclean clean by contact—not the other way around.

❖ Burial Customs

Bodies were washed, wrapped in linen with spices, and placed in a tomb (usually a cave or carved-out rock). After about a year, when the flesh had decomposed, the bones were collected and placed in an ossuary (bone box). This is the background for “let the dead bury their own dead” (Matthew 8:22)—the man may have been asking to wait for his father’s secondary burial, which could be months away.

Geography That Matters

❖ Galilee vs. Judea

Galilee (in the north) was considered backward and provincial by the Judean establishment. Galileans had a distinctive accent (Matthew 26:73). The region was more ethnically mixed (“Galilee of the Gentiles,” Matthew 4:15), more agricultural, and further from the Temple’s control. Jesus chose to base His ministry here, not in Jerusalem.

❖ The Wilderness

The Judean wilderness between Jerusalem and the Dead Sea was not a forest—it was barren, rocky desert. When Jesus was “led into the wilderness” (Matthew 4:1), He was in a place with no food, no water, extreme heat by day, and freezing cold at night. Forty days there was genuinely life-threatening.

❖ Key Locations in Matthew

  • Bethlehem — David’s city, 5 miles south of Jerusalem. Small village, not a major city.
  • Nazareth — Tiny village of perhaps 400 people. “Can anything good come from Nazareth?” (John 1:46) was a real question.
  • Capernaum — Jesus’s base of operations on the Sea of Galilee. A fishing village and trade hub.
  • Sea of Galilee — Actually a freshwater lake, about 13 miles long and 8 miles wide. Known for sudden violent storms caused by wind tunneling through surrounding valleys.
  • Jerusalem — The holy city, population ~40,000 normally, swelling to 250,000+ during festivals. The Temple dominated the skyline.
  • Golgotha — “Place of the Skull” — outside the city walls, along a major road. Crucifixions were placed where maximum people would see them.

Why This All Matters

When you read “Blessed are the poor in spirit” (Matthew 5:3), you are reading words spoken to people who were literally poor, literally oppressed, literally powerless. These were not comfortable suburbanites being told to be humble. These were crushed people being told the Kingdom belongs to them.

When you read “love your enemies” (Matthew 5:44), Jesus was speaking to people whose enemies had crucified their neighbors, taxed them into poverty, and desecrated their holy places. He was not giving advice about difficult coworkers. He was commanding something that no one in that room thought was possible or sane.

When you read “the kingdom of heaven is at hand” (Matthew 4:17), every person who heard it understood it as a political statement. The “kingdom” was not an abstract spiritual concept—it was a direct challenge to Caesar’s kingdom. Jesus was announcing a regime change. His audience just had the wrong idea about what kind of regime it would be.

Read Matthew with these eyes and the entire Gospel transforms.
