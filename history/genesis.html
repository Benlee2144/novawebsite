The World of Genesis

Creation, Covenant, and the Ancient Near East

Read Genesis in the Interlinear • Genesis 1:1 Word Study
In the beginning God created the heavens and the earth. — Genesis 1:1

Genesis was not written in a vacuum. It was written to people who already knew the creation stories of Babylon, Egypt, and Canaan. Every choice Moses made in telling the story—what to include, what to leave out, how to frame it—was a deliberate response to the narratives his audience already carried. If you do not understand the stories Genesis was answering, you will misread the answers.

The Ancient Near Eastern World

❖ When and Where

Genesis covers an enormous span of time—from creation through the patriarchs (Abraham, Isaac, Jacob) to Joseph in Egypt. The patriarchal narratives (chapters 12–50) are set roughly in the period 2000–1800 BC, during the Middle Bronze Age. The world of the patriarchs was the Fertile Crescent—the arc of arable land stretching from Mesopotamia (modern Iraq) through Syria, down through Canaan (modern Israel/Palestine), and into Egypt.

This was not a primitive world. Mesopotamia had writing systems (cuneiform), legal codes (Hammurabi’s Code, c. 1754 BC), advanced irrigation, astronomy, mathematics, and complex religious systems. Egypt had the pyramids, hieroglyphics, sophisticated medicine, and a massive bureaucracy. The patriarchs lived among highly developed civilizations.

❖ Polytheism Was the Default

Every culture surrounding Israel worshiped multiple gods. The Mesopotamians had Marduk, Ishtar, Enlil, and hundreds more. The Canaanites had El, Baal, Asherah, and Mot. The Egyptians had Ra, Osiris, Isis, and an entire pantheon. These gods were territorial—each city had a patron deity, and the gods themselves were understood to be part of the created order, subject to birth, death, conflict, and desire.

Genesis 1 is a direct challenge to every one of these systems. The sun is not a god—it is a lamp God made on Day 4. The sea monsters (tannin, Genesis 1:21) are not primordial chaos gods—they are creatures God made and called good. The moon is not a deity—it is a “lesser light” to govern the night. Genesis 1 is a systematic dethronement of every god the surrounding nations worshiped.

Creation Narratives in Context

❖ The Enuma Elish (Babylonian Creation Epic)

The primary Mesopotamian creation story begins with the gods Apsu (freshwater) and Tiamat (saltwater/chaos) mingling to produce younger gods. When the younger gods become noisy and annoying, Apsu wants to destroy them. The god Marduk eventually defeats Tiamat in battle, splits her body in half, and makes heaven from one half and earth from the other. Humans are created from the blood of a defeated god as slaves to serve the gods so the gods can rest.

Compare Genesis: Creation comes from a single God who speaks things into existence—no battle, no chaos monster defeated. The earth is not a dismembered corpse. Humans are not slave-blood; they are made in God’s tselem (image) and given dominion. God rests not because He is tired but because the work is complete and good. Every detail of Genesis 1 is a polemic against this kind of narrative.

❖ The Atrahasis Epic (Babylonian Flood Story)

Humans are created to do the labor the gods refuse to do. When humans become too noisy (population growth), the gods try to reduce them through plague, famine, and finally a great flood. One man (Atrahasis/Utnapishtim) is warned and builds a boat. Sound familiar?

The Genesis flood narrative (Genesis 6–9) parallels many structural details—a divine decision to destroy, one righteous man warned, a boat, animals saved, birds sent out, a sacrifice after. But the reason is different. In Atrahasis, the gods flood humanity because humans are too noisy. In Genesis, the flood comes because of moral corruption (Genesis 6:5—“every intent of the thoughts of his heart was only evil continually”). The Hebrew God cares about righteousness, not quiet.

Covenant Culture

❖ What a Covenant Was

A covenant (berith) was the most binding agreement in the ancient world. It was sealed in blood. The standard form: animals were cut in half, the pieces laid out in two rows, and both parties walked between them. The symbolism was explicit: “May what happened to these animals happen to me if I break this covenant.”

This is the background for Genesis 15—God’s covenant with Abraham. Abraham prepared the animals, but when the covenant-walk happened, only God (as a smoking furnace and burning torch) passed between the pieces. Abraham did not walk. God took both sides of the oath. God was saying: “If this covenant is broken—by either party—I will pay the price.” For the full significance of this, see our study: Blood Covenants — Why Jesus HAD To Die.

❖ Types of Covenants

  • Suzerain-vassal treaties — A powerful king imposes terms on a weaker party. The vassal gets protection; the suzerain gets loyalty and tribute. This pattern shapes the Sinai covenant (Exodus 19–24).
  • Royal grant covenants — A king rewards a faithful servant with an unconditional gift. This is the pattern of the Abrahamic covenant—God gives land and descendants as a unilateral promise.
  • Parity treaties — Between equals. Jacob and Laban’s agreement (Genesis 31:44–54) follows this form.

Patriarchal Society

❖ Family Structure

The family unit was the bet av (“house of the father”)—an extended family of three or four generations living together under the authority of the eldest male. This included sons, their wives, grandchildren, servants, and livestock. Abraham’s household included 318 trained fighting men (Genesis 14:14)—his “family” was essentially a small tribe.

❖ Inheritance and Birthright

The firstborn son received a double portion of the inheritance and became the family leader when the father died. This is why Esau selling his birthright (Genesis 25:29–34) was so shocking—he traded permanent family authority for a single meal. And why Jacob stealing the blessing (Genesis 27) was so devastating—the spoken blessing of a patriarch was considered legally and spiritually binding.

❖ Marriage Customs

Marriage was an economic alliance between families. The groom or his family paid a bride price (mohar) to the bride’s father. Jacob worked seven years for Rachel (Genesis 29:18–20)—his labor was the bride price. Laban’s deception in substituting Leah was both a family betrayal and a breach of contract.

❖ Hospitality

Hospitality to strangers was a sacred obligation across the ancient Near East. Refusing a traveler food and shelter was a serious social offense. This is the context for Abraham’s elaborate reception of the three visitors (Genesis 18:1–8)—he was not being unusually generous. He was fulfilling the most fundamental duty of a patriarch. The sin of Sodom (Genesis 19) included the grotesque violation of this hospitality code.

Egypt and Mesopotamia

❖ Ur of the Chaldeans

Abraham came from Ur (Genesis 11:31), one of the most advanced cities in the ancient world. Located in southern Mesopotamia (modern Iraq), Ur had a massive ziggurat (step-pyramid temple), extensive trade networks, writing schools, and sophisticated legal systems. Abraham was not a primitive nomad who wandered out of nowhere. He left one of the most cultured cities on earth because God told him to.

❖ Egypt in the Joseph Narrative

The Joseph story (Genesis 37–50) is set in Egypt’s Middle Kingdom or Second Intermediate Period. Key details align with Egyptian culture:

  • Dreams — Egyptians placed enormous importance on dream interpretation. Professional dream interpreters served in the royal court.
  • Pharaoh’s signet ring (Genesis 41:42) — The ring was the seal of royal authority. Giving it to Joseph made his decrees legally equal to Pharaoh’s.
  • Grain storage — Egypt’s entire civilization depended on the Nile’s annual flood and careful grain management. Joseph’s seven-year storage plan was not unusual in concept—Egypt had a long tradition of centralized grain management.
  • Foreign officials — Egyptian records confirm that Semitic (Asiatic) foreigners did occasionally rise to positions of authority in the Egyptian court, especially during the Hyksos period.
  • Embalming (Genesis 50:2–3) — The 40-day embalming and 70-day mourning period for Jacob matches Egyptian mummification practices.

Names and Their Meanings

In the ancient world, names were not labels—they were declarations. A name expressed identity, destiny, or the circumstances of birth.

  • Adam (’adam) — From ’adamah (ground/earth). The man made from the earth.
  • Eve (Chavvah) — “Life-giver” or “living one.” Named after the promise, not the fall.
  • Abram → Abraham — From “exalted father” to “father of many” (Genesis 17:5). God renamed him while he was still childless. The name was a prophecy.
  • Israel (Yisra’el) — “He struggles with God” or “God prevails” (Genesis 32:28). Jacob’s new name defined his descendants: a people who wrestle with God and do not let go.
  • Isaac (Yitschaq) — “He laughs.” Both Abraham and Sarah laughed when told they would have a son in old age.
  • Babel — In Hebrew, linked to balal (“to confuse”). The Babylonians called it Bab-ilu (“gate of god”). Genesis is making a word play: what they called the gate of god, God called confusion.
