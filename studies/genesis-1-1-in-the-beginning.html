In the Beginning

A Word-by-Word Journey Through Genesis 1:1 in the Original Hebrew

~45 min read • 7 Hebrew words • 23 cross-references
בְּרֵאשִׁית בָּרָא אֱלֹהִים אֵת הַשָּׁמַיִם וְאֵת הָאָרֶץ In the beginning God created the heaven and the earth. — Genesis 1:1 (KJV)

Seven Hebrew words. That is all it takes to establish the foundation of everything—the existence of God, the origin of matter, the beginning of time, and the purpose of creation. Genesis 1:1 is not merely the opening line of the Bible; it is the most concentrated theological statement in all of Scripture. Every doctrine, every promise, every prophecy that follows rests upon these seven words. And yet, in most English translations, the depth of what is being communicated is compressed into a single, familiar sentence that we read past without a second thought.

This study will take those seven words apart, one by one, in their original Hebrew. We will examine what each word meant to the ancient Hebrew reader, how the meaning has shifted (or been flattened) through translation, what connections exist to the rest of Scripture, and what these words reveal about the nature of God, His creative power, and His relationship to the universe He spoke into existence.

Before we begin, it is essential to understand something about Biblical Hebrew that English simply cannot replicate: Hebrew is a pictographic, root-based language. Every word is built from a two- or three-letter root, and those root letters carry meaning in themselves. Hebrew words are not arbitrary labels—they are descriptions. When we read the Hebrew, we are not just reading a word; we are reading a picture, a concept, a theology compressed into a handful of consonants.

✝

The Seven Words

Let us first see the complete verse with all seven words identified. Hebrew reads right to left, but we will present each word in the order an English reader encounters them in translation:

בְּרֵאשִׁית Bereshit “In the beginning”
בָּרָא Bara “Created”
אֱלֹהִים Elohim “God”
אֵת Et (untranslatable)
הַשָּׁמַיִם HaShamayim “The heavens”
וְאֵת Ve'et “And” (+ marker)
הָאָרֶץ Ha'Arets “The earth”

Now let us examine each one in the depth it deserves.

Word 1: בְּרֵאשִׁית — Bereshit

בְּרֵאשִׁית
Bereshit
beh-ray-SHEET
H7225
From the root ראש (rosh) — “head, chief, beginning, first, top, summit”

What English Bibles Say

Nearly every English translation renders this as “In the beginning.” The KJV, NIV, ESV, NASB, and NKJV all agree. It seems straightforward. But this translation, while not wrong, only scratches the surface.

What the Hebrew Actually Says

Bereshit is a compound word. It combines the prefix בְּ (be-) meaning “in” with רֵאשִׁית (reshit), which comes from the root ראש (rosh). Understanding rosh is critical.

Rosh (ראש) does not merely mean “beginning” in the chronological sense. Its primary meaning is “head”—as in the head of a body, the chief of a tribe, the top of a mountain, the first of a series. It carries inherent authority and primacy. When you see rosh, you are seeing something that is first not just in sequence but in rank, importance, and authority.

Reshit (the feminine form of rosh) occurs 51 times in the Hebrew Bible. Look at how it is used elsewhere:

Cross-References for Reshit (רֵאשִׁית)

  • Proverbs 8:22 — “The LORD possessed me in the beginning [reshit] of his way, before his works of old.” — Wisdom speaking, identifying herself as present at creation.
  • Jeremiah 2:3 — “Israel was holiness unto the LORD, and the firstfruits [reshit] of his increase.” — Israel as God’s “first portion.”
  • Deuteronomy 18:4 — “The firstfruit [reshit] of thy corn, of thy wine, and of thine oil... shalt thou give him.” — The best, first portion given to God.
  • Proverbs 1:7 — “The fear of the LORD is the beginning [reshit] of knowledge.” — Not just the start, but the foundation and chief principle.

Do you see the pattern? Reshit never means merely “the chronological starting point.” It means the first, the best, the most important, the chief, the foundation upon which everything else rests. It is the word used for the firstfruits offering—the best of the harvest given to God before anything else is touched.

So when Genesis opens with Bereshit, it is not simply saying “once upon a time, at the start.” It is making a theological statement: what follows is the chief thing, the first thing, the most important thing, the foundation of everything.

The Rabbinic Insight

Ancient Jewish commentators noticed something remarkable about Bereshit. The very first letter of the Torah is ב (Bet), the second letter of the Hebrew alphabet—not א (Aleph), the first. Why would God begin His Word with the second letter instead of the first?

The rabbis taught that this was intentional. Bet is closed on three sides and open on one—the side facing the direction of reading (left, in Hebrew). This means: you can inquire about what comes after creation, but what came before it is closed to you. It is a visual representation of a theological boundary: God is inviting you forward into His revelation, but He has placed a wall behind the beginning. The mystery before creation belongs to God alone.

Furthermore, Bet has the numerical value of 2. The rabbis saw this as a hint: creation is about duality—heaven and earth, light and darkness, day and night, male and female, the seen and the unseen. The very first letter tells you that this universe is built on pairs, on relationship, on the tension between complementary opposites that together reflect the fullness of God.

The New Testament Connection

The apostle John deliberately echoes Bereshit when he opens his Gospel:

In the beginning was the Word, and the Word was with God, and the Word was God. He was in the beginning with God. All things were made through him, and without him was not any thing made that was made. — John 1:1-3

The Greek phrase En arche (Ἐν ἀρχῇ) is the Septuagint’s translation of Bereshit. John is not just alluding to Genesis—he is reinterpreting it. He is saying: the reshit, the first, the chief, the foundation of all creation—is a Person. The Word (Logos) was there in the reshit. In fact, the Word is the reshit.

Further Cross-References

  • Colossians 1:15-18 — “He is the image of the invisible God, the firstborn [prototokos] of all creation... He is the beginning [arche], the firstborn from the dead.”
  • Revelation 3:14 — “The words of the Amen, the faithful and true witness, the beginning [arche] of God’s creation.”
  • Revelation 22:13 — “I am the Alpha and the Omega, the first and the last, the beginning and the end.”

How Translation Changed the Meaning

~250 BC — Septuagint (Greek)
The Jewish scholars in Alexandria translated Bereshit as En arche (Ἐν ἀρχῇ) — “In the beginning.” Arche in Greek carries meanings of “origin, first cause, ruling principle, authority”—preserving much of the Hebrew depth. This is the same arche from which we get “architect,” “archetype,” and “monarchy.”
~400 AD — Latin Vulgate
Jerome translated it as In principio—“In the principle/origin.” The Latin principio (root of “principle”) still carries the sense of “first cause, foundation.” The theological weight is mostly preserved.
1611 AD — King James Version
“In the beginning.” The English word “beginning” shifts the meaning toward pure chronology—a starting point on a timeline. The senses of “firstfruits,” “chief,” “head,” and “foundation” are lost. Most English readers now understand this as “at the start of time” and nothing more.
Modern translations
Nearly all follow KJV precedent. Some study Bibles add footnotes, but the main text reads “In the beginning” universally. The theological richness of reshit—the firstfruits, the chief thing, the head—remains buried in the Hebrew.

Word 2: בָּרָא — Bara

בָּרָא
Bara
bah-RAH
H1254
Root: ברא — To create, to bring into existence from nothing; used exclusively with God as the subject

Why This Word Matters

Hebrew has several words for “making” things. Understanding the distinctions is critical:

Hebrew Transliteration Meaning Who Can Do It
בָּרָא Bara To create from nothing (ex nihilo) Only God
עָשָׂה Asah To make, fashion, accomplish God or humans
יָצַר Yatsar To form, mold, shape (like a potter) God or humans

This distinction is profound. In the entire Hebrew Bible, bara is never used with a human subject. Not once. Humans can asah (make) and yatsar (form), but only God can bara (create from nothing). This word is reserved exclusively for divine creative action that has no precedent, no raw material, no prior model.

When Genesis 1:1 uses bara, it is making an ontological claim: the universe did not emerge from pre-existing material. It did not evolve from chaos. It did not emanate from God’s being. God bara—He brought into existence something that had absolutely no prior existence. This is the doctrine of creatio ex nihilo (creation from nothing), and it is established in the very second word of the Bible.

Where Bara Appears in Genesis 1

Fascinatingly, bara appears only three times in the entire creation account of Genesis 1:

The Three Uses of Bara in Genesis 1

  • Genesis 1:1 — “God created [bara] the heavens and the earth.” — The creation of matter (the physical universe).
  • Genesis 1:21 — “God created [bara] great sea creatures and every living creature that moves.” — The creation of animal life (nephesh, the soul/consciousness).
  • Genesis 1:27 — “God created [bara] man in his own image.” — The creation of the image of God in humanity (the spirit, the imago Dei).

Everything else in Genesis 1 uses asah (made) or yatsar (formed). But these three moments—the creation of matter, of animal life, and of the divine image in humanity—required something genuinely new, something that had never existed before. The ancient Hebrew text is telling us: there are three categories of existence that only God could bring into being. Three unbridgeable gaps that no natural process can cross. Three bara moments.

The Grammatical Detail

Bara in Genesis 1:1 is in the Qal perfect form—third person masculine singular. The perfect tense in Hebrew indicates a completed action. God’s creation of the heavens and earth is presented as a finished act, not an ongoing process. It was done. It was complete. This same grammatical form echoes in Jesus’s words on the cross: “It is finished” (tetelestai, John 19:30)—another completed act with cosmic implications.

Word 3: אֱלֹהִים — Elohim

אֱלֹהִים
Elohim
eh-lo-HEEM
H430
Plural of אֱלוֹהַ (Eloah) — God, mighty one, judge, divine being. Occurs 2,606 times in the Hebrew Bible.

The Plural Mystery

Here is one of the most discussed features of Genesis 1:1: the word for God is plural. Elohim is the masculine plural of Eloah (a singular form of God). The -im ending in Hebrew is like adding “-s” in English—it makes the word plural. Yet the verb bara that precedes it is singular.

A plural noun with a singular verb. In Hebrew grammar, this is called the plural of majesty or plural intensive—a way of expressing the fullness, magnitude, and supreme nature of God. It does not mean “gods” (polytheism); it means God in the fullness of His being, in all His attributes, in the totality of His power.

Jewish scholars have historically explained this as the “majestic plural”—similar to how a king might say “we decree” when speaking alone. Christian theologians have seen in this plural form an early hint of the Trinity—one God existing as three Persons: Father, Son, and Holy Spirit, acting in unified creative power.

What is indisputable is this: the very first name God reveals about Himself in Scripture is plural in form but singular in action. God is one—but His oneness contains a fullness that a simple singular word cannot express.

The Root Meaning

The root of Elohim is debated, but the most commonly accepted derivation is from אלה (alah)—meaning “to be strong, to be mighty.” Some scholars connect it to אל (El)—the basic Semitic word for “God” or “mighty one,” found across ancient Near Eastern languages (Akkadian ilu, Ugaritic il).

Elohim is used in Scripture for:

  • The one true God (Genesis 1:1, overwhelmingly)
  • Angelic beings (Psalm 8:5, “a little lower than elohim”)
  • Human judges acting in God’s authority (Exodus 21:6, 22:8-9)
  • False gods of the nations (Exodus 12:12, “against all the elohim of Egypt”)

The word carries the sense of supreme authority, power, and judgment. When Genesis 1:1 introduces God as Elohim rather than by His covenant name YHWH (יהוה), it is presenting Him in His universal role as Creator and Sovereign of all that exists—not yet in His intimate, relational role as the covenant God of Israel. That revelation comes later, in Genesis 2:4, when the name YHWH Elohim first appears.

Elohim vs. YHWH — The Two Primary Names

  • Elohim (אֱלֹהִים) — God as Creator, Judge, Sovereign. His universal nature. Power and majesty. Used in Genesis 1:1–2:3.
  • YHWH (יהוה) — God as covenant-keeper, the relational “I AM.” His personal, intimate nature. Used from Genesis 2:4 onward when He relates to humanity directly.

This is not accidental. The text is deliberately showing you two dimensions of God: He is the cosmic Sovereign who spoke galaxies into existence (Elohim), and He is the intimate Father who walks with Adam in the cool of the day (YHWH). Both are true. Both are the same God. And the order matters: first He creates, then He relates.

Word 4: אֵת — Et

אֵת
Et
eht
H853
Untranslatable particle. The direct object marker of Hebrew grammar. Composed of the first letter (א Aleph) and the last letter (ת Tav) of the Hebrew alphabet.

The Word That English Cannot Translate

This is perhaps the most overlooked and most theologically stunning word in Genesis 1:1. Et (אֵת) has no English equivalent. It is a grammatical particle that marks the direct object of a verb—it tells you what was created. In English, word order does this job (we know “the heavens” is the object because it comes after the verb). Hebrew needs et to perform this function.

Most translators simply skip it. It appears nowhere in your English Bible for this verse. But look at what it is made of:

א (Aleph) + ת (Tav) = אֵת

Aleph is the first letter of the Hebrew alphabet. Tav is the last. Together they encompass the entire alphabet—every letter, every word, every concept that can be expressed in Hebrew. Et is, symbolically, everything from A to Z (or more precisely, from Aleph to Tav).

The Christological Connection

In the book of Revelation, Jesus declares:

I am the Alpha and the Omega, the first and the last, the beginning and the end. — Revelation 22:13

Alpha and Omega are the first and last letters of the Greek alphabet. But Jesus was a Hebrew speaker. The Hebrew equivalent of “Alpha and Omega” is Aleph and Tav—which is et (אֵת).

Read Genesis 1:1 again with this in mind:

“In the beginning, God created אֵת [the Aleph-Tav] the heavens and וְאֵת [and the Aleph-Tav] the earth.”

Many Hebrew scholars and early Church fathers saw in this untranslatable particle a hidden signature—the mark of the Messiah embedded in the very first verse of the Bible, standing silently between God and His creation. He is the Aleph and the Tav, the Beginning and the End, present at the foundation of the world, holding all things together.

The Aleph-Tav Across Scripture

  • Isaiah 44:6 — “I am the first, and I am the last; and beside me there is no God.”
  • Revelation 1:8 — “I am Alpha and Omega, the beginning and the ending, saith the Lord, which is, and which was, and which is to come, the Almighty.”
  • Colossians 1:17 — “He is before all things, and in him all things hold together.”
  • Hebrews 1:3 — “He upholds the universe by the word of his power.”

Word 5: הַשָּׁמַיִם — HaShamayim

הַשָּׁמַיִם
HaShamayim
hah-shah-MAH-yeem
H8064
From הַ (ha, “the”) + שָׁמַיִם (shamayim, “heavens, sky”). The word shamayim is itself a dual/plural form, always appearing in the plural in Hebrew.

Why “Heavens” is Plural

English translations sometimes say “heaven” (KJV) and sometimes “heavens” (ESV, NASB). The Hebrew is always plural: shamayim. This is not an accident. The ancient Hebrew understanding recognized multiple heavens—multiple dimensions or layers of the created order above the earth.

Paul references this understanding explicitly:

I know a man in Christ who fourteen years ago was caught up to the third heaven. — 2 Corinthians 12:2

The Jewish tradition identified three heavens:

Heaven Hebrew Understanding Description
First Heaven The atmosphere The sky we see—clouds, weather, birds. The “heavens” that declare God’s glory (Psalm 19:1).
Second Heaven Outer space The sun, moon, stars, and cosmic expanse. The “firmament” of Genesis 1:6-8.
Third Heaven God’s dwelling The spiritual dimension where God’s throne exists. Where Paul was caught up to (2 Cor 12:2). Called shamei hashamayim—“the heaven of heavens” (Deut 10:14, 1 Kings 8:27).

When Genesis 1:1 says God created hashamayim, it is saying He created all of these—the atmosphere, outer space, and the spiritual realm. The entire multi-dimensional reality of existence beyond the earth, both physical and spiritual, was brought into being by a single word.

The Root Word

Some scholars derive shamayim from שָׁם (sham), meaning “there”—the heavens are “the there-place,” the beyond, the realm that is above and other. Others connect it to מַיִם (mayim), meaning “water”—suggesting the heavens are the “waters above,” which corresponds to the description in Genesis 1:6-7 of God separating the “waters above” from the “waters below.” Both etymologies reveal something about how the ancient Hebrews perceived the heavens: as the transcendent place where the divine waters of life are stored.

Word 6: וְאֵת — Ve’et

וְאֵת
Ve’et
veh-EHT
וְ (ve, “and”) + אֵת (et, the direct object marker). The conjunction links heaven and earth in a single creative act.

The Significance of “And”

The Hebrew conjunction וְ (vav, pronounced “ve” or “veh” before a consonant) is the most common word in the Hebrew Bible. It is a single letter—ו—attached to the front of the next word. It means “and,” but it does far more than simply connect two nouns.

In Hebrew thought, the vav is called the “vav of connection” (vav hachibur). It doesn’t just add one thing to another; it binds them together into a unity. When God created the heavens and the earth, the “and” is not incidental. It declares that heaven and earth, the spiritual and the physical, the above and the below, are part of one creation, one reality, one interconnected whole.

The vav (ו) is also the sixth letter of the Hebrew alphabet, and its pictographic form in ancient Hebrew was a nail or a hook—something that connects, fastens, and holds together. The heavens and the earth are “nailed together” by divine intent. They are not separate realities; they are two dimensions of one creation, linked by the purpose and presence of God.

And again we see et (אֵת)—the Aleph-Tav, the Alpha and Omega—appearing a second time. If the first et stands between God and the heavens, this second et stands between the conjunction and the earth. The signature of the Creator is on both halves of His creation.

Word 7: הָאָרֶץ — Ha’Arets

הָאָרֶץ
Ha’Arets
hah-AH-rets
H776
From הָ (ha, “the”) + אֶרֶץ (erets) — earth, land, ground, territory, country. Occurs 2,504 times in the Hebrew Bible.

More Than “Earth”

In modern English, “the earth” makes us think of a blue-and-green sphere floating in space. But the Hebrew erets carries a much wider range of meaning:

  • The physical ground — the soil, the dirt, the material world underfoot
  • A specific land — “the land of Israel” (erets Yisrael)
  • The entire earth — the whole world, all the nations
  • The realm of humanity — the sphere of human habitation and activity

In Genesis 1:1, ha’arets represents everything that is not heaven—the entire physical, material dimension of reality. While hashamayim encompasses the spiritual and cosmic realms, ha’arets is the tangible, touchable, inhabitable creation.

The Pair: Heavens and Earth

The phrase “the heavens and the earth” (hashamayim ve’et ha’arets) is a Hebrew literary device called a merism—where two extremes are named to encompass everything between them. When we say “day and night,” we mean all 24 hours. When we say “young and old,” we mean everyone.

“The heavens and the earth” means absolutely everything that exists. Every galaxy, every atom, every angel, every dimension, every law of physics, every moment of time, every possibility and actuality. Nothing is excluded. God created all of it.

The Merism “Heavens and Earth” Elsewhere

  • Psalm 121:2 — “My help comes from the LORD, who made heaven and earth.”
  • Isaiah 65:17 — “For behold, I create new heavens and a new earth.”
  • Matthew 5:18 — “Till heaven and earth pass away, not one jot or one tittle shall pass from the law.”
  • Revelation 21:1 — “Then I saw a new heaven and a new earth, for the first heaven and the first earth had passed away.”

The story of the Bible, from Genesis to Revelation, is the story of the heavens and the earth. It begins with their creation (Genesis 1:1), it traces their separation by sin, and it ends with their reunification in a new creation (Revelation 21:1). The first verse and the last chapters of the Bible form a perfect ring—a literary structure that spans the entire canon.

Putting It All Together

Seven words. In Hebrew, seven is the number of completeness, of divine perfection, of covenant. It is no coincidence that the foundational verse of all Scripture contains exactly seven words. The very structure of the sentence reflects the theology of its content: God’s creation is complete, perfect, and covenantal.

Let us read Genesis 1:1 one final time, now carrying the full weight of what each word means:

Bereshit — In the chief-beginning, the first and highest foundation of all things,
bara — there was an act of absolute creation from nothing, something only God can do,
Elohim — the one God in the fullness of His majestic being and power,
et — [the Aleph-Tav, the First and the Last, the hidden signature of the Creator],
hashamayim — the heavens in all their dimensions—atmospheric, cosmic, and spiritual,
ve’et — and, bound together as one creation, [again the Aleph-Tav],
ha’arets — the entire physical, material realm of existence. — Genesis 1:1, expanded from the Hebrew

What This Verse Establishes

In seven words, Genesis 1:1 establishes the following doctrines without argument, without proof, without apology:

  • Monotheism — There is one God (singular verb with Elohim)
  • God’s eternality — He exists before the beginning; He is not created
  • Creation ex nihilo — The universe was created from nothing (bara)
  • God’s sovereignty — He alone creates; it is His choice, His act, His initiative
  • The reality of the material world — Creation is real, not illusion
  • The distinction between Creator and creation — God is not the universe; He made it
  • The unity of all creation — Heaven and earth are one creation, bound together
  • The goodness of creation — God chose to create; existence is intentional
  • The existence of both physical and spiritual realms — Both are real, both are created
  • Time has a beginning — Bereshit marks the start of time itself

Every false theology is refuted in this single verse. Atheism is refuted (“God”). Polytheism is refuted (singular verb). Pantheism is refuted (God is separate from creation). Materialism is refuted (the spiritual heavens exist). Dualism is refuted (one God created both). Nihilism is refuted (creation has a purpose). Fatalism is refuted (God acts by choice). Every philosophical dead-end that humanity has wandered down is anticipated and answered in seven Hebrew words.

The Hidden Numerical Structure

Hebrew is an alphanumeric language—every letter has a numerical value. When you add up the values of the letters in Genesis 1:1, patterns emerge that have fascinated scholars for millennia:

Feature Value Significance
Number of Hebrew words 7 The number of divine completeness and covenant
Number of Hebrew letters 28 7 × 4 — completeness (7) multiplied by the material world (4 corners, 4 seasons)
Total gematria value 2,701 37 × 73 — both prime numbers, both mirrors of each other
First three words (“In the beginning God created”) 1,202 The subject and action
Last four words (“the heavens and the earth”) 1,499 The objects of creation

The number 37 appears repeatedly in Genesis 1:1’s mathematical structure. In gematria, 37 is the value of the word יחיד (yechid)—“the only one, the unique one.” This is the word used for Isaac in Genesis 22:2 (“your only son”) and is a title applied to the Messiah. The very mathematics of the first verse encode the singularity and uniqueness of God.

Conclusion: Why This Verse Matters

We have spent considerable time in just one verse. Some may ask: is this depth really necessary? Can we not simply read “In the beginning God created the heavens and the earth” and move on?

We can. But when we do, we miss the forest for the trees. We miss the plural majesty of God’s name, the exclusivity of His creative power, the hidden Aleph-Tav standing between God and His cosmos, the seven-word covenant structure, the merism that encompasses all of reality, the deliberate use of reshit instead of a simpler word for “start,” and the countless threads that connect this verse to every major doctrine in Scripture.

If seven words contain this much depth, imagine what awaits in the rest of the sixty-six books. The Bible was not written in English. It was written in Hebrew, Aramaic, and Greek by people who thought in images, in roots, in numbers, in poetry, in chiastic structures, in layers of meaning that our translations can only gesture toward. To study the Word in its original languages is not academic vanity. It is the act of pressing your ear closer to the mouth of God.

It is the glory of God to conceal a matter; to search out a matter is the glory of kings. — Proverbs 25:2

God hid depths in His Word. Searching them out is our royal privilege.
