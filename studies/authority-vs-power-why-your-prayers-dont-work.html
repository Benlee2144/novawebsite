Authority vs Power

Why Your Prayers Don’t Work — And the Two Greek Words That Explain Everything

~70 min read • 22 Hebrew & Greek words explored • 65+ cross-references
Behold, I give you the authority to trample on serpents and scorpions, and over all the power of the enemy, and nothing shall by any means hurt you. — Luke 10:19

A police officer stands in the middle of an intersection. A forty-ton semi truck barrels toward him at sixty miles per hour. The truck has infinitely more raw force than the officer. It could flatten him without slowing down. But the officer raises one hand—and the truck stops. Why? The officer is not stronger than the truck. He is not faster. He cannot physically stop it. What he has is something the truck driver recognizes as greater than force: authority. Behind that badge is the entire legal system, the courts, the police force, the government of a nation. The truck stops not because of what the officer can do, but because of who the officer represents.

Now flip that to the spiritual realm—and you have the answer to why most Christians pray and nothing happens.

The New Testament uses two completely different Greek words that English compresses into one vague concept. The first is dynamis (δύναμις)—raw power, explosive force, inherent ability. This is the word behind “dynamite.” The second is exousia (ἐξουσία)—authority, delegated jurisdiction, the legal right to act on behalf of someone greater. These are not synonyms. They are not interchangeable. And the difference between them is the difference between a prayer that shakes the heavens and a prayer that bounces off the ceiling.

Most believers have been taught to ask God for power. What Jesus actually gave them was authority. And until you understand the difference—in the original Greek, in the legal framework of the spirit realm, in the finished work of the cross—you will keep praying like a beggar when you were authorized to speak like a king.

This study will take you through every occurrence that matters. Every word in its original language. Every verse that the church either skips or softens. By the end, you will not pray the same way again.

The Two Words: Dynamis and Exousia

Let us start where all real Bible study must start—with the actual words. English translations routinely render both dynamis and exousia as “power,” which flattens a distinction that the original authors were making deliberately, in every single instance, with surgical precision. When Luke wrote Luke 10:19, he did not accidentally use two different words. He used them because they mean two fundamentally different things.

δύναμις
dynamis
DOO-nah-mis
G1411
Inherent power, ability, might, force. The capacity to do something. Raw energy, explosive capability. From dynamai (“to be able”). Used 120 times in the NT. Root of English “dynamic,” “dynamite,” and “dynamo.”

Dynamis is what the truck has. It is brute strength, inherent capability, the ability to do something. When the New Testament describes the miracles of Jesus, it often calls them dynameis (the plural)—acts of power. When Paul says “the gospel is the dynamis of God unto salvation” (Romans 1:16), he means it has explosive, transformative force. When Acts 1:8 says “you shall receive dynamis when the Holy Spirit comes upon you,” it means the Spirit brings inherent supernatural ability—the energy to do what flesh cannot.

Dynamis in Key Passages

  • Romans 1:16 — “The gospel…is the dynamis of God unto salvation.”
  • Acts 1:8 — “You shall receive dynamis when the Holy Spirit has come upon you.”
  • 1 Corinthians 1:18 — “The message of the cross is…the dynamis of God.”
  • 1 Corinthians 2:4 — “My speech…was in demonstration of the Spirit and of dynamis.”
  • 2 Corinthians 12:9 — “My dynamis is made perfect in weakness.”
  • Ephesians 1:19 — “The exceeding greatness of His dynamis toward us who believe.”
  • 2 Timothy 1:7 — “God has not given us a spirit of fear, but of dynamis, love, and a sound mind.”

Now the second word:

ἐξουσία
exousia
ex-oo-SEE-ah
G1849
Authority, jurisdiction, delegated right, permission, liberty, privilege. The legal right to act. Not the ability to do something, but the authorization to do it. From ek (“out of”) + eimi (“to be”)—literally “out of one’s being,” i.e., the right that proceeds from who you are (or who you represent). Used 103 times in the NT.

Exousia is what the officer has. It is not muscle. It is jurisdiction. It is delegated right. When the centurion in Matthew 8:9 told Jesus “I am a man under exousia, having soldiers under me,” he was explaining chain of command. He did not say “I am a powerful man.” He said “I am a man under authority, and therefore I have authority.” His authority came from being under someone else’s authority. He represented Rome. When he spoke, Rome spoke.

And Jesus marveled. Not at a display of power. At an understanding of authority. “I have not found such great faith, not even in Israel” (Matthew 8:10). The centurion understood something that the Pharisees, the Sadducees, and apparently most of the modern church still does not: authority operates by representation, not by personal strength.

Exousia in Key Passages

  • Matthew 7:29 — “He taught them as one having exousia, and not as the scribes.”
  • Matthew 8:9 — “I am a man under exousia, having soldiers under me.”
  • Matthew 28:18 — “All exousia has been given to Me in heaven and on earth.”
  • Mark 1:27 — “With exousia He commands even the unclean spirits, and they obey Him.”
  • Luke 10:19 — “I give you exousia over all the dynamis of the enemy.”
  • John 1:12 — “He gave them exousia to become children of God.”
  • John 10:18 — “I have exousia to lay down my life, and exousia to take it again.”

Look at Luke 10:19 one more time, slowly, in the Greek:

ἰδοὺ δέδωκα ὑμῖν τὴν ἐξουσίαν τοῦ πατεῖν ἐπάνω ὄφεων καὶ σκορπίων καὶ ἐπὶ πᾶσαν τὴν δύναμιν τοῦ ἐχθροῦ — Luke 10:19 (Greek text)

Jesus gave you exousia (authority, legal right, delegated jurisdiction) over all the dynamis (raw power, force, ability) of the enemy. Your authorization outranks the enemy’s capability. You do not need to be stronger than the enemy. You just need to know who you represent.

How Jesus Operated — Command, Not Petition

Watch how Jesus handled everything He encountered. He never begged His Father to do anything on His behalf in the context of exercising authority over creation, sickness, or the demonic. He never said “Father, if it be thy will, please maybe possibly heal this person if you feel like it.” He commanded. He spoke. And creation obeyed—because He had the legal right to command it.

Every case is worth examining in the Greek, because the verbs reveal everything:

ἐπιτιμάω
epitimaō
eh-pee-tee-MAH-oh
G2008
To rebuke, to charge sternly, to censure, to admonish sharply. A word of authority—like a judge issuing a ruling. Used when Jesus rebuked the wind (Mark 4:39), rebuked demons (Mark 1:25), and rebuked fevers (Luke 4:39). You rebuke what is under your jurisdiction.
φιμόω
phimoō
fee-MOH-oh
G5392
To muzzle, to silence, to make speechless. From phimos (a muzzle). When Jesus told the storm “Peace, be still” (Mark 4:39), the Greek is siopa, pephimoso—“Be silent! Be muzzled!” He muzzled the storm the way you would muzzle a wild animal. This is not prayer language. This is command language.

Consider the specific encounters:

Mark 4:39 — He “rebuked (epitimaō) the wind and said to the sea, ‘Peace (siōpa), be muzzled (pephimōso)!’” This is a direct command to a natural phenomenon. He did not pray about the storm. He spoke to the storm. The perfect passive imperative pephimōso means “be muzzled and remain muzzled”—a permanent command.

Mark 1:25 — “Be quiet (phimōtheti), and come out (exelthe) of him!” Same verb—phimoō—used on a demon. Muzzled. And exelthe is the aorist imperative of exerchomai—“come out, right now, once and for all.” Not a request. A legal eviction.

John 11:43 — “Lazarus, come forth (deuro exo)!” He shouted into a tomb and commanded a dead man to walk out. He did not ask His Father to raise Lazarus. He exercised His own exousia over death itself.

Mark 1:41 — “I am willing. Be clean (katharisthēti).” Aorist passive imperative—“be cleansed, right now.” He spoke one word and leprosy obeyed.

John 5:8 — “Rise (egeire), take up (aron) your bed and walk (peripatei).” Three imperatives in sequence. Not a prayer. A series of commands to a paralyzed body.

ἐξέρχομαι
exerchomai
ex-ER-khoh-my
G1831
To go out, to come out, to depart, to exit. Compound of ek (“out of”) + erchomai (“to come”). The standard command Jesus used for casting out demons. The aorist imperative exelthe means “come out—now, completely, finally.”

In every case, Jesus operated by declaration, not petition. He spoke with the authority of the One who sent Him. “The Son can do nothing of Himself, but what He sees the Father do” (John 5:19). He was not freelancing. He was executing delegated authority. And then He said the most staggering sentence in the entire New Testament:

Most assuredly, I say to you, he who believes in Me, the works that I do he will do also; and greater works than these will he do, because I go to My Father. — John 14:12
μείζων
meizōn
MAY-zohn
G3187
Greater, larger, more important. Comparative form of megas (“great”). Jesus did not say “similar works” or “lesser works.” He said meizona—greater works. The comparative is explicit and unmistakable.

Greater works. Not lesser. Not “similar but smaller.” Greater. Because when He went to the Father, He sent the Holy Spirit, and now the same authority that was concentrated in one man walking in Galilee is distributed across every believer on the planet simultaneously. The scope is wider. The scale is larger. The authority is identical.

Why Your Prayers Don’t Work

Most Christians pray like beggars. “God, please heal my mother. God, please fix my finances. God, if it’s your will…” This is sincere. It is well-intentioned. And in many cases, it is fundamentally backwards.

Imagine the police officer standing in the intersection, saying: “Truck, please stop. Truck, if it’s your will, could you maybe consider slowing down?” He would be dead. Authority is exercised by declaration, not petition. You do not ask the enemy to leave. You tell him. You do not ask sickness to depart. You command it. You do not beg God to do what He already authorized you to do. You exercise the authority He already gave you.

Now—there is a place for petition in Scripture. Paul writes in Philippians 4:6, “In everything by prayer and supplication (deēsis), with thanksgiving, let your requests be made known to God.” The Greek word deēsis does mean “request, petition, supplication.” But notice: it is directed at God—your Father—regarding the things that are His to do. There is a difference between communing with your Father about your needs and begging Him to do something He already delegated to you.

δέησις
deēsis
DEH-ay-sis
G1162
Petition, supplication, request, entreaty. From deomai (“to need, to ask”). Used 18 times in the NT. This is the proper word for bringing needs to the Father in relationship. It is NOT the word used for spiritual warfare, healing, or exercising authority over the enemy.
προσευχή
proseuchē
pros-yoo-KHAY
G4335
Prayer (general term), communion with God. From pros (“toward”) + euchē (“a wish, a vow”). The broadest Greek word for prayer. It encompasses adoration, thanksgiving, confession, and petition. It is face-toward-God communication.

The problem is not prayer itself. Prayer is communion with God—proseuchē—and it is essential. The problem is using petition language when command language is required. You do not petition the devil. You do not petition sickness. You do not ask a storm to calm down. You rebuke it (epitimaō). You muzzle it (phimoō). You cast it out (ekballō).

ἐκβάλλω
ekballō
ek-BAL-loh
G1544
To cast out, to drive out, to expel, to throw out by force. Compound of ek (“out of”) + ballō (“to throw”). Used 81 times in the NT—overwhelmingly for casting out demons. This is eviction language. Legal removal. Not asking. Throwing.

Think of it this way. A CEO delegates a project to a manager. The CEO gives the manager full authority to execute it—budget, staff, decisions, everything. Then the manager keeps calling the CEO every day: “Should I do this? Can I do that? Please handle this for me.” The CEO would eventually say: “I gave you the authority. Use it. Stop calling me to do your job.”

God already said: “I give you authority over all the power of the enemy.” And we are on our knees saying: “God, please deal with the enemy for me.” He is saying: “I already dealt with him. I gave you the badge. Go exercise it.”

The Legal Framework of the Spirit Realm

Here is what almost nobody teaches. The spirit realm operates on legality. Like a courtroom. Not raw force. Satan is not fighting God with power—he lost that fight at the cross (Colossians 2:15). What he does now is operate as an attorney. His primary title in Scripture is not “the destroyer” or “the tempter,” though he is both. His primary title is far more revealing:

κατήγορος
katēgoros
kah-TAY-go-ros
G2725
Accuser, prosecutor. From kata (“against”) + agoreuo (“to speak in the assembly/court”). Literally “one who speaks against someone in a legal assembly.” This is a courtroom word. Satan is not described as a warrior fighting God. He is described as a prosecutor bringing charges.
Then I heard a loud voice saying in heaven, “Now salvation, and strength, and the kingdom of our God, and the authority of His Christ have come, for the accuser of our brethren, who accused them before our God day and night, has been cast down.” — Revelation 12:10

The Hebrew equivalent is equally revealing. The word satan itself:

שָׂטָן
satan
sah-TAHN
H7854
Adversary, accuser, one who opposes in a legal proceeding. From the root satan (שׂטן, H7853)—“to be an adversary, to accuse.” In Job 1–2 and Zechariah 3:1, he appears in God’s court as a legal prosecutor, bringing formal accusations. Not a battlefield warrior—a courtroom attorney.

His whole strategy is legal accusation:

  • “This person sinned—I have a right to afflict them.”
  • “This person is in fear—fear gives me legal access.”
  • “This person doesn’t know their authority—they can’t enforce the verdict against me.”
  • “This person agrees with my lies—their agreement is my permission.”

And here is the crucial thing: he is technically right about the legal framework. There are legal principles in the spirit realm. Sin can give the enemy access. Fear can open doors. Ignorance can prevent you from enforcing what is yours. The word for this access point in Hebrew is significant:

מָקוֹם
maqom
mah-KOHM
H4725
Place, standing, position. A recognized location. In legal contexts, “a standing ground,” “a place of legal positioning.” When Scripture speaks of “giving place” to the devil (cf. Ephesians 4:27), the concept is granting him a recognized standing—a legal foothold.
τόπος
topos
TOH-pos
G5117
Place, room, position, opportunity. In Ephesians 4:27—“Do not give a topos to the devil”—Paul uses a word that means a recognized position, a foothold, a legal opportunity. Do not give the accuser ground to stand on.

But—and this is everything—the blood of Jesus settled the legal case permanently.

Tetelestai — The Case Is Closed

When Jesus cried out from the cross “It is finished” (John 19:30), the English barely hints at what the Greek actually says. The word is tetelestai—and it is not merely a statement of completion. It is a legal and financial term that would have been immediately recognized by every person in the first-century Roman world.

τετέλεσται
tetelestai
teh-TEL-es-tie
G5055
It is finished, it has been completed, it is accomplished, it has been paid in full. Perfect passive indicative of teleō (“to complete, to accomplish, to bring to an end”). The perfect tense means a completed action with ongoing results. The passive voice means the action was done to the debt, not by it. The root teleō was used in commercial and legal contexts to indicate the completion or fulfillment of an obligation. The perfect tense declares that the work is finished and the results stand permanently. When Jesus cried this word, He was declaring a completed legal transaction: the debt is settled, the obligation fulfilled, the case closed.

Jesus was not merely saying “I’m done suffering.” He was filing the final legal document. Every accusation Satan could ever bring against any human being—past, present, and future—was answered by the blood. The debt was stamped tetelestai. Paid. In. Full.

The related word teleō appears throughout the New Testament in the context of completing, fulfilling, and accomplishing divine purpose:

τελέω
teleō
teh-LEH-oh
G5055
To complete, to accomplish, to bring to an end, to fulfill, to finish. Root of tetelestai. Also the root of teleios (“perfect, complete, mature”—G5046) and telos (“end, goal, purpose”—G5056). The entire semantic field points to completed purpose.

Paul describes what happened at the cross in explicitly legal language:

Having disarmed principalities and powers, He made a public spectacle of them, triumphing over them in it. — Colossians 2:15
ἀπεκδύομαι
apekdyomai
ah-pek-DOO-oh-my
G554
To strip off, to disarm, to divest completely. Compound of apo (“away from”) + ekdyo (“to strip”). The imagery is of a conquered general being stripped of his armor, his insignia, and his weapons in a Roman triumph. Jesus did not merely defeat the enemy. He stripped him—publicly, completely, permanently.
θριαμβεύω
thriambeuo
three-am-BYOO-oh
G2358
To triumph over, to lead in a triumphal procession. A Roman triumphus was a public victory parade where the conquered enemy was paraded in chains through the streets. Paul is saying: Jesus did not defeat Satan in secret. He held a public triumph. The verdict is on the record.

So when Satan accuses you now, he is bringing charges against a case that has already been adjudicated. He is a prosecutor trying to retry a case that has a permanent verdict of NOT GUILTY. He has no legal standing. But he does it anyway—because if you do not know the verdict, you will accept the accusation as if it is valid.

Your authority is the ability to enforce the verdict that has already been rendered.

You are not fighting for victory. You are fighting from victory. The war is won. You are enforcing the outcome.

The Blood Speaks — Better Than You Think

Hebrews 12:24 contains a phrase that most readers pass over without stopping. It says Jesus is “the Mediator of the new covenant, and to the blood of sprinkling that speaks better things than that of Abel.” Abel’s blood cried out from the ground for justice (Genesis 4:10). Jesus’s blood speaks for mercy. But the Greek word “speaks” is critical:

λαλέω
laleō
lah-LEH-oh
G2980
To speak, to talk, to utter, to make a sound. Used 296 times in the NT. The present tense here indicates continuous action: the blood of Jesus is speaking—right now, continuously, without ceasing. It does not need you to remind it. It is making its case in the courtroom of heaven at this very moment.

The blood speaks. Present tense. Continuously. It is not a one-time statement. The blood of Jesus is making its legal argument in the heavenly courtroom right now, every moment of every day. When the accuser brings a charge, the blood answers. You do not even need to be aware of it. The blood has its own voice, its own testimony, and its own legal standing.

But your authority is to agree with what the blood is already saying. To align your words with the verdict that has already been rendered. To enforce on earth what has already been decided in heaven.

Assuredly, I say to you, whatever you bind on earth will be bound in heaven, and whatever you loose on earth will be loosed in heaven. — Matthew 18:18
δέω
deō
DEH-oh
G1210
To bind, to tie, to fasten, to put in chains. Legal term: to forbid, to declare unlawful. When Jesus says “whatever you bind,” the Greek construction is a periphrastic future perfect: “whatever you bind on earth will have already been bound in heaven.” You are not initiating the binding. You are enforcing what heaven has already decided.
λύω
lyō
LOO-oh
G3089
To loose, to untie, to release, to dissolve, to break. Legal term: to permit, to declare lawful. The complement of deō. “Whatever you loose on earth will have already been loosed in heaven.” You are ratifying heaven’s decision on earth. Enforcing what is already true.

The verb tenses here are routinely mistranslated or misunderstood. Many English translations read as if you bind something on earth and then heaven agrees. The Greek says the opposite: whatever you bind on earth shall have been already bound in heaven. You are not commanding heaven. You are executing heaven’s commands on earth. You are the enforcement arm of a verdict that has already been rendered.

Your Position — Seated, Not Groveling

The final piece is position. Where you are determines what you can do. A soldier in a trench fights differently than a general on a hill. Scripture is explicit about where you are right now:

…and what is the exceeding greatness of His power toward us who believe, according to the working of His mighty power which He worked in Christ when He raised Him from the dead and seated Him at His right hand in the heavenly places, far above all principality and power and might and dominion, and every name that is named, not only in this age but also in that which is to come. — Ephesians 1:19–21
…and raised us up together, and made us sit together in the heavenly places in Christ Jesus. — Ephesians 2:6
συνεκάθισεν
synekathisen
soon-eh-KAH-thee-sen
G4776
To seat together with, to cause to sit down with. Compound of syn (“together with”) + kathizō (“to seat, to cause to sit”). The aorist tense indicates a completed action—this is not future. It is not potential. It has already happened. You have already been seated with Christ.

Where is Christ? Far above all principality (archē, G746), power (exousia, G1849), might (dynamis, G1411), and dominion (kyriotēs, G2963). Every name that is named. Not some. All. Where are you? Seated with Him. Synekathisen—seated together. Same position. Same altitude. Same authority.

κυριότης
kyriotēs
koo-ree-OH-tays
G2963
Lordship, dominion, ruling power, sovereign authority. From kyrios (“lord, master”). Refers to an order of spiritual authority or governance. Christ is seated above every level of dominion that exists—and you are seated with Him.

You are not under the enemy. You are not beside the enemy. You are above the enemy. Seated with Christ, far above all of it. The question is not whether you have authority. The question is whether you know it.

What This Changes — Practically

This is not abstract theology. It changes how you face every circumstance of your life. Here is what authority looks like in practice, contrasted with what religion has trained most believers to do:

Sickness shows up. The religious response: “God, please heal me if it’s your will.” The authority response: “By His stripes I was healed (1 Peter 2:24—past tense, completed action, iathēte, aorist passive indicative). Sickness, you have no legal right to this body. The blood speaks a better word (Hebrews 12:24). I enforce the finished work. Leave.”

ἰάθητε
iathēte
ee-AH-thay-tay
G2390
You were healed. Aorist passive indicative of iaomai (“to heal, to cure, to make whole”). The aorist tense means a completed, one-time action. The passive voice means it was done to you. The indicative mood means it is a statement of fact, not a wish. Peter does not say “you might be healed” or “you will be healed.” He says you were healed. Past tense. Done.

Fear attacks. The religious response: “God, please take this fear away.” The authority response: “God has not given me a spirit of fear, but of dynamis, love, and a sound mind (2 Timothy 1:7). Fear, you are not from God and you have no authority (exousia) here. I refuse your presence.”

Financial lack. The religious response: “God, please provide.” The authority response: “My God supplies all my needs according to His riches in glory (Philippians 4:19—present tense, ongoing). Lack, you do not belong here. I am an heir of God and co-heir with Christ (Romans 8:17). Everything the Father has is available to me.”

The difference is not arrogance. It is position. You are not commanding these things in your own name. You are commanding them in the name of Jesus—which means you are commanding them based on His authority, His finished work, His legal victory. The officer does not stop the truck because he is personally powerful. He stops it because of the government behind the badge.

Why The Church Doesn’t Teach This

The same reason as everything else. An empowered believer does not need a mediator. If you know you have authority, you handle things directly. You pray with boldness. You speak to mountains (Mark 11:23). You resist the devil and he flees (James 4:7). You do not need a “man of God” to pray over you. You do not need a special anointing service. You do not need to wait for a revival.

The authority is in you. Right now. Already given. Already authorized. Already legal. Already purchased by blood that is still speaking in the courtroom of heaven at this very moment.

The church has often kept believers begging because beggars come back every week. People who know they are kings do not.

Submit yourselves therefore to God. Resist the devil, and he will flee from you. — James 4:7
ἀντίστητε
antistēte
an-TEE-stay-teh
G436
To resist, to stand against, to oppose, to withstand. Compound of anti (“against”) + histēmi (“to stand, to make to stand”). Aorist imperative—a definitive, decisive command. Not “keep trying to resist.” Stand. Once. Decisively. And the devil will flee (pheuzetai—future middle indicative—he will run, it is certain, it is his inevitable response to legitimate resistance).

The promise is unconditional. Resist the devil—he will flee. Not “might.” Not “sometimes.” Will. The Greek is unambiguous. The only variable is whether you resist.

The Bottom Line

You were never meant to beg. You were meant to reign. The Greek words are not ambiguous. Exousia—delegated authority, legal jurisdiction, the right to act on behalf of the King of kings—was given to you the moment you believed (John 1:12). The enemy’s dynamis—all of it, every bit—is under your exousia. The case was settled at the cross. The receipt says tetelestai. The blood is still speaking. You are seated with Christ above every principality and power. The only thing missing is your awareness of it.

Start praying like someone who knows the verdict. Stop petitioning the enemy and start evicting him. Stop asking God to do what He already authorized you to do. The badge is on your chest. The government of heaven is behind it.

Use it.
