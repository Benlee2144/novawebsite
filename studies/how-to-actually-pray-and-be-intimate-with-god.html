How To Actually Pray

And Be Intimate With God — Not the Religious Version

~75 min read • 20 Hebrew & Greek words explored • 60+ cross-references
So the Lord spoke to Moses face to face, as a man speaks to his friend. — Exodus 33:11

You can know all the theology. You can understand the Greek behind every word in this study series—metanoia, exousia, tetelestai, pisteuō eis. You can see through every mistranslation the institutional church has layered on top of Scripture for sixteen centuries. And you can still feel like God is a million miles away when you try to talk to Him. Because knowing about God is not the same as knowing God. And most Christians have been taught a version of prayer that almost guarantees they will never experience the difference.

What passes for prayer in most churches is a formula. “Dear God… thank you for… please help with… in Jesus’s name, amen.” It is a template. A script. And reading a script to your wife every night instead of actually talking to her will, over time, replace the relationship with a ritual. That is what has happened to prayer. The script replaced the conversation. The formula replaced the encounter. The performance replaced the Person.

The New Testament uses at least six different Greek words for prayer. English uses one. And the Hebrew behind the great prayers of the Old Testament adds layers that most believers never see. When you actually trace these words—when you see what Jesus told us to do and what He told us to stop doing—what emerges is not a technique. It is a relationship. Spirit to Spirit. Face to face. The way God spoke to Moses as a man speaks to his friend.

The Six Greek Words for Prayer

English has one word: “prayer.” It covers everything from desperate begging to quiet contemplation. The New Testament writers had surgical precision. They chose different words for different kinds of prayer because they are different things. Understanding which word is used where changes everything.

προσευχή
proseuchē
pros-yoo-KHAY
G4335
Prayer (the broadest term), communion with God. From pros (“toward, face-to-face”) + euchē (“a wish, a vow, a prayer”). Used 36 times in the NT. This is the general word for face-toward-God communication. The preposition pros is the same one in John 1:1—“the Word was with (pros) God”—which means “face-to-face with.” Prayer, at its root, is turning your face toward God’s face.

Notice: the root of proseuchē is not about asking. Not about requesting. Not about getting things. It is about orientation—being face-to-face with God. The broadest, most general word for prayer in the New Testament is fundamentally about direction, not transaction.

δέησις
deēsis
DEH-ay-sis
G1162
Petition, supplication, specific request born from need. From deomai (“to need, to lack, to beg”—G1189). Used 18 times in the NT. This is prayer that arises from a specific, felt need. It is the child going to the Father and saying “I need this.” It is personal, specific, and urgent. This is the asking word. When Paul says “in everything by prayer (proseuchē) and supplication (deēsis)” (Philippians 4:6), he names both the general communion AND the specific request.
ἔντευξις
enteuxis
EN-tyook-sis
G1783
Intercession, a coming together, a meeting, an audience with a king. From entynchanō (“to meet with, to have an audience, to make intercession”—G1793). Used only twice in the NT (1 Timothy 2:1, 4:5). This is boldly approaching the throne. In secular Greek, enteuxis was the technical term for a petition presented to a king—a formal audience. But here it is used for prayer: you have an audience with the King of the universe. Not cowering. Not groveling. Meeting with Him.
εὐχαριστία
eucharistia
yoo-khar-is-TEE-ah
G2169
Thanksgiving, gratitude, giving of thanks. From eu (“good, well”) + charis (“grace, gift”). Used 15 times in the NT. The root of “Eucharist.” Thanksgiving is not a category tacked onto prayer—it is a form of prayer. Paul never separates petition from thanksgiving. They are always together: “with thanksgiving let your requests be made known” (Philippians 4:6). You ask from a posture of gratitude, not desperation.
αἴνεσις
ainesis
AH-ee-neh-sis
G133
Praise, the act of praising. From aineō (“to praise, to speak well of”—G134). Used in Hebrews 13:15—“let us continually offer the sacrifice of ainesis”—the “fruit of our lips, giving thanks to His name.” Praise is a sacrifice—it costs something, especially when circumstances are terrible and you praise anyway.
κοινωνία
koinōnia
koy-noh-NEE-ah
G2842
Fellowship, communion, partnership, sharing, participation. From koinos (“common, shared”). Used 19 times in the NT. While not technically a “prayer word,” koinōnia describes the goal of prayer: shared life between you and God. “Our koinōnia is with the Father and with His Son Jesus Christ” (1 John 1:3). This is what prayer exists for—not to get things, but to have shared life with God.

So prayer in the New Testament is: turning your face toward God (proseuchē), bringing specific needs to Him (deēsis), having a bold audience with the King (enteuxis), responding with gratitude (eucharistia), offering the sacrifice of praise (ainesis), and living in shared communion (koinōnia). That is six dimensions of one relationship. Not a formula. A life.

What Jesus Said To Stop Doing

Before Jesus taught His disciples how to pray, He told them what to stop doing. And everything He told them to stop is exactly what most churches still do every Sunday.

And when you pray, you shall not be like the hypocrites. For they love to pray standing in the synagogues and on the corners of the streets, that they may be seen by men. Assuredly, I say to you, they have their reward. — Matthew 6:5
ὑποκριτής
hypokritēs
hoo-pok-ree-TAYS
G5273
Actor, stage player, pretender, hypocrite. From hypo (“under”) + krinō (“to judge, to decide”). Literally “one who answers from under a mask”—a reference to Greek theater, where actors wore masks and spoke from behind them. A hypokritēs is someone performing for an audience. Jesus is saying: if your prayer sounds like it is meant to be overheard—fancy words, religious tone, theatrical emotion—you are acting. You are wearing a mask. And you have already received your full payment: the approval of the audience. God owes you nothing.
And when you pray, do not use vain repetitions as the heathen do. For they think that they will be heard for their many words. Therefore do not be like them. For your Father knows the things you have need of before you ask Him. — Matthew 6:7–8
βαττολογέω
battologeō
bat-toh-loh-GEH-oh
G945
To babble, to use vain repetitions, to stammer meaninglessly. Possibly from the name Battos (a stammerer) or onomatopoeia imitating senseless repetition. Used only here in the entire NT. Jesus is not condemning passion or persistence in prayer (He praised the persistent widow in Luke 18). He is condemning the pagan belief that more words = more power—that God is impressed by volume, or that repetition is a magical incantation. God is not a vending machine. You do not need to insert the same coin five times.

And then the most liberating sentence in the passage: “Your Father knows the things you have need of before you ask Him” (Matthew 6:8). He already knows. Before you open your mouth. Before you form the thought. So prayer is not informing God of your situation. He is already fully aware. Prayer is communing with a Father who already knows you completely and loves you anyway.

The Lord’s Prayer — A Framework, Not a Script

Immediately after telling them to stop performing and stop babbling, Jesus said “In this manner, therefore, pray” (Matthew 6:9). The Greek is houtōs oun proseuchesthe—“pray in this manner.” Not “pray these exact words.” In this manner. It is a framework, not a liturgy. A pattern, not a script.

Our Father in heaven, hallowed be Your name. — Matthew 6:9
Πάτερ ἡμῶν
Pater hēmōn
pah-TEHR hay-MOHN
G3962
“Our Father.” Patēr = father, the one who generates, the source. Jesus begins prayer with relationship. Not “O Almighty, Omnipotent, Transcendent Sovereign of the Universe.” Father. The Aramaic behind this is almost certainly Abba (אַבָּא)—the intimate address of a child to a parent. Not “Daddy” (as is sometimes claimed—that trivializes it). But not “Sir” either. It is the warm, confident, intimate address of a child who knows they belong. Prayer begins with knowing who you are talking to and who you are to Him.
אַבָּא
abba
AH-bah
G5
Father, papa. An Aramaic term of intimate, familial address. Used three times in the NT: Mark 14:36 (Jesus in Gethsemane), Romans 8:15, and Galatians 4:6. In both Romans and Galatians, Paul says the Spirit cries out Abba from within us—it is the Spirit testifying that you are God’s child. This is the most intimate address available in ancient Semitic culture for addressing a father.

The entire Lord’s Prayer follows a pattern: (1) Relationship—“Our Father.” (2) Worship—“Hallowed be Your name.” (3) Alignment—“Your kingdom come, Your will be done.” (4) Dependence—“Give us this day our daily bread.” (5) Reconciliation—“Forgive us…as we forgive.” (6) Protection—“Lead us not into temptation, deliver us from evil.” Not a script to recite. A posture to inhabit. Start with relationship. Move into worship. Align with His purposes. Bring your needs. Deal with relational debris. Ask for covering. That is the architecture of communion.

The Three Layers of Prayer — Mapped to the Temple

Your body is the temple of the Holy Spirit (1 Corinthians 6:19). The temple had three sections. Prayer operates the same way—and most Christians live permanently in the first room.

The Outer Court — Soul Prayer

This is where most prayer happens. Praying from the soul—your mind, emotions, and will. Talking at God with your thoughts. Reading a prayer list. Asking for things. Expressing emotions—joy, grief, anger, fear. This is valid. This is not wrong. David prayed like this constantly in the Psalms. Raw, emotional, honest soul-prayer.

How long, O Lord? Will You forget me forever? How long will You hide Your face from me? — Psalm 13:1

David did not sanitize his prayers. He screamed. He wept. He raged. He accused God of forgetting him. And God called him “a man after My own heart” (Acts 13:22). Not because David was well-behaved. Because he was honest. God is not fragile. You cannot offend Him with honesty. What offends Him is pretending—performing—saying words you do not mean in a voice you do not normally use.

The key to soul-prayer: be real. Stop using church language. If you are angry, say “I am angry.” If you are doubting, say “I am doubting.” If you do not feel Him, say “I do not feel you and it is driving me crazy.” Talk to God like He is actually there. Because He is.

The Holy Place — Spirit Prayer

Deeper than words. Paul describes it:

Likewise the Spirit also helps in our weaknesses. For we do not know what we should pray for as we ought, but the Spirit Himself makes intercession for us with groanings which cannot be uttered. — Romans 8:26
στεναγμός
stenagmos
steh-nag-MOS
G4726
A groaning, a sigh, an inarticulate expression of deep feeling. From stenazō (“to groan, to sigh deeply”—G4727). These are alaletos (ἀλάλητος, G215)—“unutterable, inexpressible, too deep for words.” The Spirit intercedes with groans that cannot be expressed in human language. Your spirit is communicating things your mind does not have vocabulary for.
ἀλάλητος
alalētos
ah-LAH-lay-tos
G215
Unspeakable, inexpressible, unable to be articulated in words. From alpha-privative + laleō (“to speak”). These groanings are literally beyond speech. They bypass the mind entirely. The Spirit is not limited by your vocabulary, your theology, or your understanding. He prays what needs to be prayed, whether you comprehend it or not.

There are things your spirit needs to communicate to God that your mind cannot formulate. The Holy Spirit—who lives in your spirit (Romans 8:9, 1 Corinthians 6:19)—intercedes from within you. He knows the will of God (Romans 8:27) and prays it through you. Your mind may not understand what is happening. That is the point. This is spirit-to-Spirit communication that does not need the soul’s permission or comprehension.

Practically, this looks like moments where you are simply present with God. No words. No requests. No agenda. Just… there. Together. Your spirit touching His Spirit without your mind’s involvement. You have probably felt it—a shift in the room, a settling in your chest, a quietness in your mind that does not feel like your own doing. That is your spirit taking over from your soul. Stay there. Do not analyze it. Do not try to understand it. Your mind will try to hijack it: “What is this? Am I doing this right? Is this real?” Ignore it. Stay in the stillness.

The Holy of Holies — Union

The deepest level. Barely talked about because it cannot be taught—only experienced.

Be still, and know that I am God. — Psalm 46:10
רָפָה
raphah
rah-FAH
H7503
To let go, to release, to relax, to let drop, to become slack, to stop striving, to sink down. This is not “be quiet.” It is “Stop. Let go of everything. Release your grip. Cease your striving. Become weak.” The same root appears in contexts of letting things fall from your hands—releasing control. “Be still” is a command to stop trying and simply be in the presence of God.
יָדַע
yada
yah-DAH
H3045
To know, to perceive, to experience, to be intimate with. The deepest knowing word in Hebrew. “Be still (raphah) and know (yada) that I am God.” Release your grip—and then you will experience God. Not understand Him. Not learn facts about Him. Know Him. Intimate, experiential, face-to-face encounter. The kind of knowing that only happens when you stop striving.

When you truly let go—when your body is quiet, your soul is quiet, your mind stops churning—what is left is your spirit. And your spirit is already one with God:

But he who is joined to the Lord is one spirit with Him. — 1 Corinthians 6:17
ἑν πνεῦμά
hen pneuma
hen PNYOO-mah
G1520 + G4151
One spirit. Not “close to God’s spirit” or “connected to God’s spirit.” One. Your spirit and God’s Spirit are joined into a single unity. You do not need to create the connection. It is already there. You just need to stop drowning it out with noise. The deepest prayer is not reaching for God. It is realizing He is already holding you.

This is the place mystics and contemplatives throughout church history have described—what the anonymous author of The Cloud of Unknowing (14th century) called the cloud between the mind and God that you must push through by abandoning thought. What Brother Lawrence (17th century) called “practicing the presence of God”—a Carmelite lay brother who found deeper communion washing dishes in a monastery kitchen than most monks found in choir. What the Desert Fathers (3rd–5th century) sought in the silence of the Egyptian wilderness: a knowing of God that does not need words.

And it is available to you because the veil was torn:

Then, behold, the veil of the temple was torn in two from top to bottom. — Matthew 27:51

From top to bottom. God tore it, not man. The barrier between you and the deepest presence of God was ripped open by the hand of God Himself. The Holy of Holies is not closed. It has been open since the cross. You have access. Right now. Always. The only thing between you and the deepest intimacy with God is the noise of your own soul.

Pray Without Ceasing — The Ongoing Conversation

Paul wrote three words in Greek that demolish the idea that prayer is an appointment:

Pray without ceasing. — 1 Thessalonians 5:17
ἀδιαλείπτως
adialeiptos
ah-dee-ah-LIPE-toce
G89
Without ceasing, unceasingly, without interruption, constantly. From alpha-privative + dialeipō (“to leave an interval, to intermit”). Without gaps. No intermission. An unbroken continuity. You cannot maintain “unceasing” prayer if prayer only happens when you close your eyes and fold your hands. Adialeiptos demands that prayer become your default state—an ongoing awareness of God that runs beneath everything else.

This is not about muttering prayers under your breath all day. It is about developing a continuous awareness of God. An ongoing inner conversation. Brother Lawrence described it perfectly: while cooking, while cleaning, while doing the most mundane tasks, he maintained an inner orientation toward God. Not formal prayers. Just ongoing presence. “Lord, I sense you here. What do you think about this? I am struggling right now. Thank you for that.”

It is like texting someone you love throughout the day. Not formal letters. Just staying connected. “Hey.” “Thinking about you.” “That was cool.” “I need help.” Your spirit is always connected to God’s Spirit. The connection never drops. You are just not always aware of it. The practice is learning to be aware of what is already there.

This is the life Jesus lived. “The Son can do nothing of Himself, but what He sees the Father do” (John 5:19). He was in constant communion. Every decision, every conversation, every miracle flowed from that ongoing connection. And He said you would do greater works (John 14:12). Not because you are greater than Him. Because the same Spirit that connected Him to the Father now lives inside you—and He has gone to the Father, so the connection flows both ways simultaneously through every believer on earth.

Hearing God’s Voice

Jesus said something that most Christians quote but few actually believe:

My sheep hear My voice, and I know them, and they follow Me. — John 10:27
ἀκούω
akouō
ah-KOO-oh
G191
To hear, to listen, to understand, to obey. Used 430 times in the NT. In Hebrew thought, hearing and obeying are the same thing—the Hebrew shama (H8085) means both. Akouō carries this same weight. “My sheep hear My voice” means not merely “they perceive sound” but “they recognize, understand, and respond to My voice.” Present tense—they are continuously hearing.
שָׁמַע
shama
shah-MAH
H8085
To hear, to listen, to obey, to understand, to pay attention. Used over 1,100 times in the OT. The Shema (“Hear, O Israel”—Deuteronomy 6:4) uses this word. In Hebrew, hearing IS obeying. You have not truly “heard” God if you have not responded. Hearing without response is not hearing at all—it is noise.

God’s voice in your spirit does not usually sound like an audible voice (though it can—1 Samuel 3). More often it comes as:

  • A quiet knowing — Suddenly you know something you did not know before. It drops in, fully formed, from outside your normal thought patterns.
  • A phrase or sentence that appears in your mind but does not feel like your own thought. It often surprises you.
  • A deep peace that settles for no external reason—especially in the middle of chaos.
  • A pull toward something—a person, an action, a scripture—that you did not plan or initiate.
  • Conviction that is specific and gentle, not vague and condemning. God’s conviction points to a specific thing and offers a path forward. The enemy’s condemnation is vague, heavy, and hopeless.

How to tell the difference between God, yourself, and the enemy:

  • God — Specific. Gentle. Builds up. Aligns with Scripture. Produces peace even when it challenges you. Usually quieter than you expect.
  • Yourself — Scattered. Self-centered. Familiar inner monologue pattern. Sounds like “you.”
  • The enemy — Condemning. Vague guilt. Accusation. Fear. Urgency without peace. “You are not good enough. God is disappointed. It is too late.”

The Secret Nobody Tells You

Intimacy with God is more about receiving than doing. We are so programmed to perform—say the right words, pray the right way, generate the right feelings—that we miss the most fundamental truth about prayer:

O Lord, You have searched me and known me. You know my sitting down and my rising up; You understand my thought afar off. You comprehend my path and my lying down, and are acquainted with all my ways. For there is not a word on my tongue, but behold, O Lord, You know it altogether. — Psalm 139:1–4

He already knows everything about you. Every thought. Every fear. Every hidden thing you would never tell another human being. And He is still here. He did not leave. Knowing everything about you did not make Him step back. He moved closer. The deepest prayer might simply be: “You know me. You see all of it. And you are still here. Thank you.”

We love Him because He first loved us. — 1 John 4:19

You are not initiating this relationship. He already did. Every time you feel a pull toward prayer, toward Scripture, toward seeking Him—that is not you being spiritual. That is Him drawing you (helkō—John 6:44). He started it. He is always starting it. Your job is not to create the connection. It is to stop resisting the one that is already there.

The Word as Living Encounter

One of the most powerful forms of prayer is not talking at all—it is reading Scripture slowly and letting God speak through it. Not as a study exercise. As an encounter.

For the word of God is living and powerful, and sharper than any two-edged sword, piercing even to the division of soul and spirit, and of joints and marrow, and is a discerner of the thoughts and intents of the heart. — Hebrews 4:12
ζῶν
zōn
DZOHN
G2198
Living, alive, having life. Present participle of zaō (“to live”). The Word of God is living—present tense, right now, actively alive. It is not a historical document. It is not a textbook. It is a living entity that acts upon you. It pierces. It divides. It discerns. When you read it in the posture of prayer, it reads you.
τομώτερος
tomōteros
tom-OH-teh-ros
G5114
Sharper, more cutting. Comparative of tomos (“cutting, sharp”). The Word is sharper than a machaira distomos (two-edged sword)—it cuts in both directions. It divides between soul and spirit—the two things most Christians cannot tell apart. It exposes what is merely soulish (emotional, intellectual, willful) and what is truly spiritual. This is why reading Scripture prayerfully is so disruptive: it will not let you stay confused about your own motives.

The early church had a practice called lectio divina—“divine reading.” Take a short passage. Read it slowly. Read it again. Let a word or phrase catch you. Sit with it. Ask: “God, what are you saying to me in this?” Then listen. Not with your ears. With your spirit. Something might jump out. A phrase might hit differently. A word might feel alive in a way it has never felt before. That is the living Word dividing soul and spirit. Follow that thread.

The Bottom Line

Prayer is not a technique. It is not a formula. It is not a performance. It is the most natural thing in the universe: two persons who love each other being together. Your spirit and God’s Spirit—already united (1 Corinthians 6:17), already connected, already one. The veil is torn. The access is open. The Father is waiting.

Stop performing. Stop babbling. Stop reading scripts. Turn your face toward His face (proseuchē). Bring your needs honestly (deēsis). Approach the throne boldly (enteuxis). Respond with gratitude (eucharistia). Offer praise even when it costs you (ainesis). And above all—live in shared communion (koinōnia) with the God who already knows you completely, loves you perfectly, and is closer than your next breath.

Be still. Let go. And know.
