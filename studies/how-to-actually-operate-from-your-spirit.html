How To Actually Operate From Your Spirit

The Daily War Between Pneuma and Psychē — And How To Let the Right One Lead

~75 min read • 21 Hebrew & Greek words explored • 65+ cross-references
I say then: Walk in the Spirit, and you shall not fulfill the lust of the flesh. — Galatians 5:16

You have two signals running at all times. Your spirit broadcasts one frequency. Your soul broadcasts another. Your body and your life follow whichever signal is stronger. For most Christians, the soul signal is blasting—it has been running unchecked since the day you were born. Every worry, every reactive emotion, every anxious thought, every defensive reflex—that is your soul running the show. Your spirit is still connected to God. It is still carrying the Kingdom. But it is a whisper in a room full of screaming.

The entire New Testament assumes you will learn to tell the difference between these two signals and choose which one leads. Paul, John, and Jesus all use language that distinguishes the “spiritual” person from the “soulish” person—and the Greek words they use are so precise that the distinction is impossible to miss, unless you only read English translations that blur both into “natural.”

This study is the practical application of everything we have covered: the three-part human, being born from above, intimacy with God, and exercising authority. You know the theology. Now—how do you actually live it? How do you turn down the soul volume and turn up the spirit volume in the middle of an ordinary Tuesday?

Pneumatikos vs. Psychikos — The Two Kinds of Person

Paul makes a distinction in 1 Corinthians 2 that most English translations obscure completely. He describes two kinds of people—not “saved vs. unsaved,” but two kinds of operating system within believers. The words are surgical:

ψυχικός
psychikos
psoo-khee-KOS
G5591
Soulish, of the soul, governed by the psychē (soul). From psychē (G5590—soul, the seat of mind, emotions, and will). English translations render this as “natural” or “unspiritual,” which erases the precision. Paul is not saying “non-Christian.” He is saying “soul-operated.” A psychikos person processes everything through the mind, emotions, and will—even spiritual things. They may be genuinely saved (their spirit is reborn) but their soul is still running the show.
πνευματικός
pneumatikos
pnyoo-mah-tee-KOS
G4152
Spiritual, of the spirit, governed by the pneuma (spirit). From pneuma (G4151—spirit, breath, wind). A pneumatikos person is one whose spirit is in the lead. Not more intelligent. Not more emotional. More spirit-governed. They have learned to receive from the Spirit and let the Spirit direct their soul, rather than the reverse.
But the soulish man (psychikos anthrōpos) does not receive the things of the Spirit of God, for they are foolishness to him; nor can he know them, because they are spiritually discerned. But he who is spiritual (pneumatikos) judges all things, yet he himself is rightly judged by no one. — 1 Corinthians 2:14–15

The psychikos person—the soul-operated person—cannot receive the things of the Spirit. Not because they are stupid. Because the wrong instrument is trying to process the signal. Your mind cannot perceive what your spirit perceives. Your emotions cannot discern what your spirit discerns. Spiritual things require spiritual reception equipment. If your soul is doing the listening, God’s voice sounds like foolishness, or silence, or nothing at all.

The pneumatikos person—the spirit-operated person—“judges (anakrinō) all things.” The word anakrinō means to examine, to investigate, to discern:

ἀνακρίνω
anakrinō
ah-nah-KREE-noh
G350
To examine, to investigate, to judge, to discern, to sift through. Compound of ana (“up, through”) + krinō (“to judge, to separate, to distinguish”). The spiritual person has an ability to see through situations, to discern motives, to perceive what is really happening beneath the surface. Not through intelligence. Through spirit-perception.

Jude uses the same distinction even more bluntly:

These are sensual persons (psychikoi), who cause divisions, not having the Spirit. — Jude 19

Psychikoi—soul-driven people. Jude does not call them evil. He calls them soulish. They process everything through their mind, their emotions, their preferences, their offenses—and they cause divisions because the soul is inherently self-centered. The soul asks “How does this affect me?” The spirit asks “What is God doing?”

The Flesh — The Third Competitor

Paul adds a third category that complicates the picture further: the sarx—the flesh. This is not merely the physical body. It is the entire system of operating apart from God—the fallen human nature that defaults to self-preservation, self-promotion, and self-gratification.

σάρξ
sarx
sarks
G4561
Flesh, the physical body, but more often in Paul: the fallen human nature, the unregenerate operating system, the default mode of humanity apart from God. When Paul says “walk not according to the sarx” (Romans 8:4), he does not mean “ignore your body.” He means “stop operating from the old system that runs on fear, self-interest, and independence from God.” The sarx is the soul untethered from the spirit—running on its own fuel.

The practical hierarchy looks like this:

  • Spirit-led (pneumatikos): Spirit receives from God → informs the soul → the soul directs the body. God’s design. Peace, authority, clarity.
  • Soul-led (psychikos): Soul (mind/emotions/will) runs the show. Spirit is present but overridden. Anxiety, overthinking, emotional reactivity. The spirit is whispering but the soul is shouting.
  • Flesh-led (sarkikos): The old nature dominates completely. Self-interest, appetites, survival instincts, independence from God. This is the default for unregenerate humanity—and a constant temptation for believers.
σαρκικός
sarkikos
sar-kee-KOS
G4559
Fleshly, carnal, governed by the sarx. Paul uses this for the Corinthian believers: “You are still sarkikoi” (1 Corinthians 3:3). They were saved. They had the Spirit. But they were operating from the flesh—jealousy, strife, divisions. Reborn spirits, carnal lifestyles. This is the condition of most of the Western church.
For you are still carnal (sarkikoi). For where there are envy, strife, and divisions among you, are you not carnal and behaving like mere men? — 1 Corinthians 3:3

“Behaving like mere men.” In Greek: kata anthrōpon peripateit—“walking according to man.” You are not mere men anymore. You are kainē ktisis—new creations (2 Corinthians 5:17). But if you walk according to the flesh, you behave like the old thing you no longer are. Identity and behavior are misaligned. The spirit is a king; the soul is still acting like a slave.

Walk by the Spirit — The Greek Behind the Command

Paul’s most practical instruction for daily spirit-led living is in Galatians 5:16, and the Greek verb he chooses reveals everything about how this works:

I say then: Walk in the Spirit, and you shall not fulfill the lust of the flesh. — Galatians 5:16
περιπατέω
peripateō
peh-ree-pah-TEH-oh
G4043
To walk, to walk around, to conduct one’s life, to live. Compound of peri (“around, about”) + pateō (“to walk, to tread”). Used 96 times in the NT. In Pauline usage, it is the standard word for daily conduct—how you actually live your life, step by step. Not a one-time decision. A walk. One step at a time. Continuous. Present tense imperative: keep walking.

Peripateite pneumati—“keep walking by the Spirit.” Present imperative: this is not a one-time decision. It is a continuous, ongoing, step-by-step lifestyle. You do not “arrive” at spirit-led living. You walk in it. Every step is a choice. Every moment is an opportunity to let the spirit lead or let the soul take over.

And look at the promise attached: “and you shall not fulfill the lust of the flesh.” The Greek uses the strongest negation available: ou mē (οὐ μή)—an emphatic double negative. “You will absolutely not, by no means, never fulfill the flesh’s desires.” The solution to the flesh is not fighting the flesh. It is walking by the Spirit. When you are walking by the Spirit, the flesh has no fuel. It starves.

Paul says the same thing with a different word in Romans 8:

φρονέω
phroneō
froh-NEH-oh
G5426
To think, to set one’s mind on, to have a mindset, to be inclined toward. Not mere intellectual thought—the orientation of the whole inner person. The direction your mind defaults to. What occupies your mental bandwidth. What your mind gravitates toward when it has no instruction.
For those who live according to the flesh set their minds (phronousin) on the things of the flesh, but those who live according to the Spirit, the things of the Spirit. For to be carnally minded (phronēma tēs sarkos) is death, but to be spiritually minded (phronēma tou pneumatos) is life and peace. — Romans 8:5–6
φρόνημα
phronēma
FROH-nay-mah
G5427
Mindset, way of thinking, mental inclination, thought pattern. The noun form of phroneō. Your phronēma is your default operating system—what your mind automatically gravitates toward. Phronēma tou pneumatos (mindset of the Spirit) = life and peace. Phronēma tēs sarkos (mindset of the flesh) = death. Your default mindset determines your default experience.

This is not about trying harder. It is about where your mind defaults. When you have a free moment, where does your mind go? Anxiety? Planning? Fear? Replaying offenses? Or does it go toward God? Toward gratitude? Toward awareness of His presence? Your phronēma—your default mindset—determines whether you experience life and peace or death and turmoil. And it is trainable. That is what Romans 12:2 is about:

And do not be conformed to this world, but be transformed by the renewing of your mind, that you may prove what is that good and acceptable and perfect will of God. — Romans 12:2
ἀνακαίνωσις τοῦ νοός
anakainōsis tou noos
ah-nah-KAI-noh-sis too NOOS
G342 + G3563
Renewal of the mind. Anakainōsis = renovation, complete making-new (from kainos, new in kind). Nous (G3563) = the mind, the intellect, the faculty of understanding. Not merely “thinking new thoughts”—a fundamental renovation of the entire mental framework. Your mind’s operating system being replaced. Not updated. Replaced.

The Umpire — How Peace Tells You Which Signal You Are On

If the spirit and soul both speak, how do you tell them apart in real time? Paul gives a simple, practical diagnostic tool:

And let the peace of God rule in your hearts, to which also you were called in one body; and be thankful. — Colossians 3:15
βραβεύω
brabeuō
brah-BYOO-oh
G1018
To act as an umpire, to arbitrate, to decide, to rule, to preside. From brabeion (“a prize in the games”—G1017). This is an athletic term. The brabeus was the official at the Greek games who made final calls—safe or out, valid or foul. Paul says: let peace be the umpire. When you have peace, the spirit is leading. When peace disappears, the soul or flesh has taken over. Peace is the indicator light on the dashboard of your life.

This is extraordinarily practical:

  • Making a decision and you have peace → your spirit is leading. Move forward.
  • Something “makes sense” logically but you have no peace → your soul says yes, your spirit says no. Trust the peace.
  • Anxious, agitated, reactive, defensive → your soul or flesh has the microphone. Pause. Be still. Wait for peace to return before acting.
  • Calm in the middle of chaos → your spirit is in the lead. That supernatural peace (Philippians 4:7—“the peace that surpasses understanding”) is the Spirit’s signature.
εἰρήνη
eirēnē
eye-RAY-nay
G1515
Peace, harmony, tranquility, rest, wholeness. The Greek equivalent of the Hebrew shalom. Not the absence of conflict—the presence of wholeness. Supernatural peace does not require favorable circumstances. It exists despite circumstances because it comes from a source that circumstances cannot touch: the Spirit of God within you.
שָׁלוֹם
shalom
shah-LOHM
H7965
Peace, wholeness, completeness, well-being, harmony, prosperity, welfare. From shalam (“to be complete, to be sound, to be whole”—H7999). Shalom is not merely the absence of war. It is the presence of everything as it should be. Total wholeness. When your spirit leads, shalom is the result—because your spirit is connected to the source of all wholeness.
You will keep him in perfect peace (shalom shalom), whose mind is stayed on You, because he trusts in You. — Isaiah 26:3

The Hebrew is shalom shalom—peace peace, doubled for emphasis. Perfect, complete, total peace. The condition: a mind “stayed” (samak, H5564—“supported, leaning, resting”) on God. Not a mind that never wanders. A mind that rests its weight on God the way you lean on a wall. Default orientation. Default trust. The spirit’s influence on the mind producing sustained peace.

סָמַךְ
samak
sah-MAHK
H5564
To lean, to lay upon, to rest upon, to support oneself on, to sustain. The same word used for laying hands on a sacrifice (Leviticus 1:4)—pressing your full weight onto it. A mind “stayed” on God is a mind that rests its full weight on Him. Not hovering near Him. Leaning on Him. Dependent. Resting.

The Real-Time Switch — Catching the Shift

The most practical skill in the entire Christian life is learning to recognize the moment your soul takes over from your spirit—and shift back. In real time. In the middle of life. Not in a prayer closet. At the office. In traffic. In an argument. During a crisis.

Here is what the shift feels like:

Soul Response Spirit Response
SpeedInstant, reactive, automaticRequires a pause, a breath, a moment
Sound“Fix this! React NOW! Defend yourself!”“The King is on the throne. Be still.”
FuelFear, anger, offense, anxietyPeace, authority, trust, clarity
FocusThe circumstance, the problem, the personThe One in charge, the Kingdom reality
ResultEscalation, regret, exhaustionResolution, wisdom, supernatural calm

The practice: When you feel that rush—anger, anxiety, fear, the urge to react immediately—pause. One second. One breath. Ask: “Is this my soul or my spirit?” At first you will catch it after you react. Over time, you catch it during. Eventually, you catch it before. That is anakainōsis tou noos—the renewal of the mind happening in real time. Your mind learning a new default.

Speaking From the Kingdom, Not From the Circumstance

Most people pray from their problems. “God, I am broke. God, I am sick. God, I am scared.” That is your soul talking to God about how bad things are. You are actually agreeing with the circumstance and asking God to change what you have just verbally established as reality.

Kingdom prayer speaks from the reality of who God is and what He has already done:

(As it is written, “I have made you a father of many nations”) in the presence of Him whom he believed—God, who gives life to the dead and calls those things which do not exist as though they did. — Romans 4:17
καλέω
kaleō
kah-LEH-oh
G2564
To call, to summon, to name, to invite. God “calls (kalountos) the things that do not exist as though they did.” He speaks to what is not yet as if it already is. He spoke light into darkness before light existed. He called Abraham “father of many nations” when he was childless. This is how the Kingdom operates: reality is spoken into existence by authority, not discovered by observation.

This is not positive thinking. This is pistis—faith. Calling things that are not as though they are. Abraham did it. God does it. He told you to do it. You are not denying reality. You are choosing which reality to operate from. Your circumstances say one thing. The Kingdom says another. Your spirit knows which one is more real.

Feed Your Spirit More Than Your Soul

Whatever you feed grows. Whatever you starve dies. This is not guilt. It is spiritual physics.

For he who sows to his flesh will of the flesh reap corruption, but he who sows to the Spirit will of the Spirit reap everlasting life. — Galatians 6:8
σπείρω
speirō
SPAY-roh
G4687
To sow, to scatter seed. The agricultural metaphor is deliberate: what you plant is what grows. What you invest your time, attention, and energy into is what produces the harvest. Speirōn eis tēn sarka (sowing into the flesh) = feeding the old nature. Speirōn eis to pneuma (sowing into the Spirit) = feeding the spirit. Input determines output.

If your soul is getting a feast—three hours of scrolling, news cycles running on fear, social media fueling comparison, entertainment providing constant distraction—and your spirit is getting crumbs, do not be surprised when your soul runs the show. The signal that gets the most input gets the most output.

Feeding your spirit looks like: time in silence, the Word read as conversation (not study assignment), worship as frequency adjustment, awareness of God’s presence during ordinary tasks, journaling what He shows you. Not as duty. As sowing. You are investing in the signal you want to be stronger.

What the Early Church Understood

The earliest Christians did not separate “spiritual life” from “daily life.” There was no category for “secular.” Everything was lived coram Deo—before the face of God.

The Desert Fathers (3rd–5th century) retreated to the Egyptian wilderness specifically to practice what Paul described—turning down the soul volume and learning to live from the spirit. Abba Anthony said: “I no longer fear God, but I love Him. For love casts out fear” (cf. 1 John 4:18). He had moved from soul-religion (fear-based) to spirit-communion (love-based).

Macarius of Egypt (c. 300–391 AD) taught that the Christian life was a progressive “exchange”—the old nature being replaced by the Spirit’s nature, not through effort but through yielding. He called it “the prayer of the heart”—when prayer moved from the mind to the spirit and became continuous awareness rather than periodic activity. This is adialeiptos (1 Thessalonians 5:17) in practice.

Brother Lawrence (1614–1691), a Carmelite lay brother, worked in a monastery kitchen. He reported feeling as close to God washing dishes as receiving communion. His secret: he practiced the presence of God in every moment, not just designated prayer times. He wrote: “The time of business does not with me differ from the time of prayer; and in the noise and clatter of my kitchen, while several persons are at the same time calling for different things, I possess God in as great tranquility as if I were upon my knees at the Blessed Sacrament.” That is pneumatikos living in the real world.

The Bottom Line

You are not becoming something new. You are becoming what you already are. Your spirit has been reborn (gennaō anōthen). You are a new creation (kainē ktisis). You are one spirit with the Lord (hen pneuma). The Kingdom of God is within you (Luke 17:21). All of this is already true. The work is not achieving it. The work is living from it.

Walk by the Spirit (peripateite pneumati)—one step at a time. Set your mind on the Spirit (phronēma tou pneumatos)—life and peace. Let peace be the umpire (brabeuō)—it will never lie to you. Feed the spirit more than the soul—what you sow (speirō) is what you reap. And when your soul takes the microphone—because it will, every day, multiple times a day—catch it, pause, and hand the microphone back to your spirit.

That is the practice. Not perfection. Awareness. Every time you catch yourself in your soul and shift back to your spirit, that is a rep. That is spiritual muscle. That is the renewal of the mind (anakainōsis tou noos) happening in real time.

Tomorrow morning. Before you touch your phone. Five minutes of stillness. Just you and Him. That is the first step.

  • Romans 8:4 — “Who do not walk according to the flesh but according to the Spirit.”
  • Romans 8:5–6 — Mindset (phronēma) of the flesh = death. Mindset of the Spirit = life and peace.
  • Romans 8:14 — “As many as are led by the Spirit of God, these are sons of God.”
  • Romans 12:2 — Transformed by the renewal (anakainōsis) of the mind.

Peace as the Indicator

  • Colossians 3:15 — Let peace rule (brabeuō—act as umpire) in your hearts.
  • Philippians 4:7 — The peace of God surpasses understanding—guards heart and mind.
  • Isaiah 26:3 — Perfect peace (shalom shalom) for the mind stayed (samak) on God.
  • John 14:27 — “My peace I give to you—not as the world gives.”
  • John 16:33 — “In Me you have peace. In the world you have tribulation.”

Sowing, Feeding, and Transformation

  • Galatians 6:8 — Sow (speirō) to the Spirit = reap life. Sow to the flesh = reap corruption.
  • Romans 4:17 — God calls (kaleō) things that do not exist as though they did.
  • 2 Corinthians 3:18 — Transformed from glory to glory by the Spirit.
  • 2 Corinthians 5:17 — New creation (kainē ktisis)—the old has passed.
  • Ephesians 4:22–24 — Put off the old man, be renewed in the spirit of your mind, put on the new man.
  • Colossians 3:1–2 — “Set your mind (phroneit) on things above, not on things on the earth.”

The Spirit Within You

  • 1 Corinthians 6:17 — One spirit (hen pneuma) with the Lord.
  • 1 Corinthians 6:19 — Your body is the temple of the Holy Spirit.
  • Luke 17:21 — “The kingdom of God is within you.”
  • Romans 8:9 — “The Spirit of God dwells in you.”
  • Psalm 46:10 — Be still (raphah) and know (yada) that I am God.
  • 1 Thessalonians 5:17 — Pray without ceasing (adialeiptos).
  • 1 John 4:18 — Perfect love casts out fear.
