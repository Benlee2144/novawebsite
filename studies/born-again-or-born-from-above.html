Born Again — or Born From Above?

How One Mistranslated Word Created the Entire “Born Again” Conversion Industry — and What Jesus Actually Told Nicodemus in the Original Greek

~85 min read • 20 Hebrew & Greek words explored • 55+ cross-references
The wind blows where it wishes, and you hear the sound of it, but cannot tell where it comes from and where it goes. So is everyone who is born of the Spirit. — John 3:8

Introduction: The Most Famous Misunderstanding in the Bible

“You must be born again.” These five words have launched a thousand altar calls, a billion tracts, an entire evangelical subculture. “Born again Christian” is a phrase so embedded in Western culture that pollsters use it as a demographic category. Billy Graham built a global ministry on it. Entire denominations define themselves by it. Ask any evangelical what you need to do to be saved and they will tell you: “You must be born again.”

Here is the problem: Jesus never said “born again.” Not in the language He was speaking to Nicodemus, and not in the Greek that John used to record the conversation. The Greek word that gets translated “again” is anōthen—and it does not mean “again.” It means “from above.”

This is not a minor quibble. The difference between “born again” and “born from above” is the difference between two entirely different theologies. “Born again” puts the emphasis on a human decision—a moment, a prayer, a choice to start over. “Born from above” puts the emphasis on God—a divine action, a heavenly origin, something that comes down to you, not something you manufacture within yourself.

And the irony is devastating: Nicodemus made the exact same mistake that modern translators make. He heard anōthen and understood it as “again”—which is why he asked the absurd question about crawling back into his mother’s womb. Jesus corrected him. But most English Bibles preserve Nicodemus’s misunderstanding as the translation, and two billion Christians have been reading the wrong word ever since.

Anōthen: The Word That Changes Everything

Let’s go straight to the Greek. Here is what Jesus said in John 3:3:

ἀμὴν ἀμὴν λέγω σοι, ἐὰν μή τις γεννηθῇ ἄνωθεν, οὐ δύναται ἰδεῖν τὴν βασιλείαν τοῦ θεοῦ. — John 3:3

Amēn amēn legō soi, ean mē tis gennēthē anōthen, ou dynatai idein tēn basileian tou theou.

“Truly, truly I say to you, unless someone is born anōthen, he is not able to see the kingdom of God.”

ἄνωθεν
anōthen
AH-no-then
G509
From above, from a higher place, from the top, from the beginning. From anō (“above, upward”) + the suffix -then (“from”). Used 13 times in the NT. Primary meaning in every other Johannine usage: “from above.” CAN mean “again” in the sense of “from the beginning / anew,” but this is the SECONDARY meaning, and John never uses it that way.

Here is every single use of anōthen in the New Testament:

  • Matthew 27:51 — The temple veil was torn “from the top (anōthen) to the bottom.” Not “torn again.”
  • Mark 15:38 — Same event: torn “from the top (anōthen) to the bottom.”
  • Luke 1:3 — Luke says he traced everything “from the beginning (anōthen).” (Some argue “from above”—i.e., by divine guidance.)
  • John 3:3 — The verse in question.
  • John 3:7 — “You must be born anōthen.” Same conversation.
  • John 3:31 — “He who comes from above (anōthen) is above all.” Clearly “from above,” not “again.”
  • John 19:11 — “You could have no power against Me unless it had been given you from above (anōthen).”
  • John 19:23 — Jesus’s tunic was woven “from the top (anōthen) throughout.”
  • Acts 26:5 — “They knew me from the beginning (anōthen).”
  • Galatians 4:9 — “Do you turn back again (anōthen) to the weak elements?” (This is the one verse where “again” fits best—but note it means “back to the beginning,” not a simple repeat.)
  • James 1:17 — “Every good gift is from above (anōthen), coming down from the Father of lights.”
  • James 3:15 — Worldly wisdom “does not descend from above (anōthen).”
  • James 3:17 — “The wisdom that is from above (anōthen) is first pure, then peaceable.”

Count them. Out of 13 uses:

  • 9 clearly mean “from above” or “from the top” (Matt 27:51, Mark 15:38, John 3:31, 19:11, 19:23, James 1:17, 3:15, 3:17, and arguably Luke 1:3)
  • 1 means “from the beginning” (Acts 26:5)
  • 1 means “again” in the sense of “back to the start” (Galatians 4:9)
  • 2 are the verses in question (John 3:3, 3:7)

In John’s own Gospel, anōthen appears 5 times. In the three uses outside John 3:3 and 3:7, it means “from above” every single time. John never uses anōthen to mean “again.” Not once. If John had wanted to write “again,” Greek has a perfectly clear word for it:

πάλιν
palin
PAH-lin
G3825
Again, once more, back. Used 141 times in the NT. The standard, unambiguous Greek word for “again.” John uses it 45 times in his Gospel. If Jesus meant “born again,” John would have written gennaō palin—not gennaō anōthen.

John used palin 45 times in his Gospel. He knew the word. If he meant “again,” he would have used it. He chose anōthen because Jesus was talking about origin—where the birth comes from—not repetition.

Nicodemus’s Mistake — And Ours

Now watch what happens in the conversation. Jesus says: “Unless someone is born anōthen (from above), he cannot see the kingdom of God.” Nicodemus responds:

How can a man be born when he is old? Can he enter a second time into his mother’s womb and be born? — John 3:4

Nicodemus heard anōthen and took it as “again”—a second physical birth. His question is absurd on purpose. John is showing us that Nicodemus misunderstood. He took a heavenly word and dragged it down to earth. He heard “from above” and thought “a second time.”

This is a classic Johannine literary device. Throughout his Gospel, John shows people misunderstanding Jesus by taking spiritual statements literally or confusing a double-meaning word:

  • John 2:19-20 — Jesus says “Destroy this temple and in three days I will raise it up.” The Jews think He means Herod’s temple. He means His body.
  • John 4:10-11 — Jesus tells the Samaritan woman about “living water.” She thinks He means well water.
  • John 6:51-52 — Jesus says “Eat my flesh.” The crowd thinks He means cannibalism.
  • John 8:33 — Jesus says “The truth will set you free.” They say “We’ve never been slaves.”
  • John 11:11-12 — Jesus says “Lazarus sleeps.” The disciples think He means literal sleep.

In every case, the pattern is the same: Jesus speaks on a heavenly level, and the listener drags it to an earthly level. The reader is supposed to see the misunderstanding and grasp the deeper meaning. In John 3, Nicodemus is the one who gets it wrong. He hears “born from above” and thinks “born again.” And then most English translations side with Nicodemus’s misunderstanding instead of Jesus’s intended meaning.

Jesus immediately corrects him:

Truly, truly I say to you, unless someone is born of water and the Spirit, he is not able to enter the kingdom of God. That which is born of the flesh is flesh, and that which is born of the Spirit is spirit. Do not marvel that I said to you, “You must be born anōthen.” — John 3:5-7

Notice: Jesus doesn’t say “born a second time.” He says born of water and Spirit. Born of flesh vs. born of Spirit. The contrast is not between a first birth and a second birth. It is between an earthly origin and a heavenly origin. Where does your life come from? Flesh produces flesh. But Spirit—the Spirit that comes from above (anōthen)—produces spirit.

Gennaō: Born, Begotten, or Both?

The word translated “born” is also worth examining:

γεννάω
gennaō
gen-NAH-oh
G1080
To beget (of a father), to give birth (of a mother), to generate, to produce. Used 97 times in the NT. When the subject is the father, it means “to beget / to father.” When the subject is the mother, it means “to give birth.” In John 3, the subject is God (the Spirit)—making this “begotten by God from above,” not simply “born again.”

This distinction matters. Gennaō when applied to a father means to beget—to generate, to cause to come into being. When God is the one doing the gennaō, you are being begotten by God. The emphasis is on the source of the new life—God Himself—not on a human decision or a repeated experience.

This is why anōthen makes perfect sense as “from above.” To be gennēthē anōthen is to be begotten from above—to receive life whose origin is God, whose source is heaven, whose nature is Spirit rather than flesh. It is not a human experience you generate by praying a prayer. It is a divine act that comes down to you.

John makes this explicit later in his Gospel and in his first epistle:

But as many as received Him, to them He gave the right to become children of God, to those who believe in His name: who were born, not of blood, nor of the will of the flesh, nor of the will of man, but of God. — John 1:12-13

Three things this birth is not: not of blood (genetics), not of the will of the flesh (human effort), not of the will of man (human decision). It is of God. The origin is divine, not human. This is not something you do. It is something God does to you, in you, from above.

Whoever has been born of God does not sin, for His seed remains in him; and he cannot sin, because he has been born of God. — 1 John 3:9
σπέρμα
sperma
SPER-mah
G4690
Seed, offspring, posterity. From speirō (“to sow”). God’s “seed” (sperma) remains in the one begotten by God. The imagery is explicitly paternal: God is the Father who begets; the believer carries the Father’s seed/nature. This is an ontological change, not a decision.

God’s sperma—His seed, His generative nature—remains in the person born from above. This is fathering language. God begets. You receive His nature. This is not a prayer you pray on a Tuesday night at a revival meeting. It is a transformation of nature—receiving divine DNA, as it were.

“Water and Spirit” — What Does It Mean?

Jesus’s clarification in verse 5 has generated centuries of debate: “Unless someone is born of water and the Spirit, he cannot enter the kingdom of God.” What is the “water”?

ὕδωρ
hydōr
HOO-dor
G5204
Water. Used 76 times in the NT. Literal water, but often with symbolic significance: cleansing, purification, the Word of God (Eph 5:26), the Spirit (John 7:38-39), or physical birth (amniotic fluid).

Four major interpretations exist. Each has textual support:

View 1: Water = Physical Birth

“Water” refers to the amniotic fluid of natural birth. Jesus is contrasting natural birth (water/flesh) with spiritual birth (Spirit). “You were born once by water (physically). You need to be born from above by the Spirit.” This fits the immediate context: Nicodemus is talking about physical birth, and Jesus is distinguishing two kinds of birth.

View 2: Water = Baptism

“Water” refers to baptism (baptisma—immersion; see our Baptism study). Born of water (immersion) and Spirit. This reading connects to John’s own baptizing ministry (John was still baptizing at this point—John 3:23) and to the early church practice of baptism as entry into the community.

View 3: Water = The Word of God

Paul writes that Christ cleanses the church “with the washing of water by the word” (Ephesians 5:26). James says God “brought us forth by the word of truth” (James 1:18). Peter says we are “born again… through the word of God which lives and abides forever” (1 Peter 1:23). On this reading, “water” symbolizes the cleansing, life-giving Word.

View 4: Water and Spirit = One Thing

In the Old Testament, God promised: “I will sprinkle clean water on you, and you shall be clean… I will put My Spirit within you” (Ezekiel 36:25-27). The “water” and “Spirit” are parallel images for the same divine renewal. Nicodemus, as a teacher of Israel (John 3:10), should have recognized this reference immediately. Jesus is pointing him to Ezekiel’s prophecy of inner transformation.

The Greek construction ex hydatos kai pneumatos (“of water and Spirit”) uses a single preposition (ex) governing both nouns, linked by kai (“and”). This can indicate a hendiadys—two words expressing one concept: “Spirit-water” or “the water that is the Spirit.”

πνεῦμα
pneuma
PNYOO-mah
G4151
Wind, breath, spirit. Neuter gender in Greek. Used 385 times in the NT. Jesus exploits the double meaning in John 3:8: “The wind (pneuma) blows where it wishes… So is everyone born of the Spirit (pneuma).” The Spirit, like wind, is invisible, sovereign, and not under human control.

Whichever view you hold on “water,” the point Jesus is making is the same: this birth is not something you control. It comes from above. It is Spirit-generated. And as Jesus says in the very next verse, using the double meaning of pneuma (both “wind” and “spirit”):

The wind blows where it wishes, and you hear the sound of it, but cannot tell where it comes from and where it goes. So is everyone who is born of the Spirit. — John 3:8

You do not control the wind. You cannot manufacture it. You cannot schedule it for a Sunday morning altar call at 11:45 AM. It blows where it wishes. And “so is everyone born of the Spirit.” The Spirit moves sovereignly. The birth from above comes when and how God determines, not when a preacher gives the invitation.

The Sinner’s Prayer — A Modern Invention

If being “born from above” is a divine act—something God does, not something you do—then what is the Sinner’s Prayer? You know the one: “Dear Lord Jesus, I know I am a sinner. I believe You died for my sins. I ask You to come into my heart. Amen.”

This prayer does not appear anywhere in the Bible. Not in the Gospels. Not in Acts. Not in Paul’s letters. Not anywhere. No apostle ever led anyone in a “Sinner’s Prayer.” No one in the book of Acts was told to “ask Jesus into your heart.” The phrase “ask Jesus into your heart” is not in any biblical manuscript in any language.

Here is what the apostles actually told people to do:

  • Acts 2:38 — Peter: “Change your mind (metanoeō) and be immersed (baptisthētō) every one of you in the name of Jesus Christ.”
  • Acts 3:19 — Peter: “Change your mind (metanoēsate) and turn back (epistrepsate).”
  • Acts 16:31 — Paul to the jailer: “Trust in (pisteuson epi) the Lord Jesus.”
  • Acts 17:30 — Paul: “God commands all people everywhere to change their minds (metanoein).”
  • Acts 22:16 — Ananias to Paul: “Arise and be immersed (baptisai), and wash away your sins.”
μετανοέω
metanoeō
meh-tah-noh-EH-oh
G3340
To change one’s mind, to think differently afterward, to shift perception. From meta (“change”) + noeō (“to think, perceive”). NOT “feel sorry for your sins.” See our Repentance study.
πιστεύω
pisteuō
pis-TYOO-oh
G4100
To trust, to rely on, to have faith in, to commit to. From pistis (“trust, faithfulness”). NOT mere mental agreement (“I believe God exists”). Active, ongoing trust—like trusting a bridge enough to walk across it. In John 3:16, the present participle (ho pisteuōn = “the one continuously trusting”) implies ongoing reliance, not a one-time decision.

The apostolic pattern was: metanoeō (shift your entire way of thinking) + pisteuō (actively trust/rely on Jesus) + baptizō (be immersed). Not a prayer. Not a hand raised. Not walking an aisle. Not “repeat after me.” A fundamental reorientation of mind, an active ongoing trust, and a public identification through immersion.

So where did the Sinner’s Prayer come from?

30–300 AD
The apostolic and early church era. No Sinner’s Prayer. Conversion involved instruction (catechism), repentance, profession of faith, and baptism (immersion). The process took weeks or months. There was no instant conversion formula.
1730s–1740s
The First Great Awakening. Jonathan Edwards, George Whitefield. Mass revival preaching creates emotional conversion experiences. Still no Sinner’s Prayer, but the pattern of “crisis conversion”—a specific moment of decision—begins to take shape.
1820s–1830s
Charles Finney introduces the “anxious bench”—a front-row seat where convicted sinners come forward during the service. This is the origin of the altar call. Finney explicitly designed it as a psychological technique to produce decisions.
1880s–1890s
D.L. Moody popularizes the “inquiry room” where counselors lead people through a decision process after the sermon. Getting closer to the Sinner’s Prayer but not there yet.
1940s–1950s
Billy Graham combines the altar call with a specific prayer formula. “Just as I am, I come to you. I accept Jesus Christ as my personal Lord and Savior.” The Sinner’s Prayer as we know it takes its modern form. Graham’s crusades made it the standard evangelical conversion method worldwide.
1960s–present
The Four Spiritual Laws (Campus Crusade, 1965) codifies the Sinner’s Prayer into a portable tract format. “God loves you. Man is sinful. Christ is the bridge. Pray this prayer.” Hundreds of millions of copies distributed. The prayer becomes the assumed method of conversion for most of evangelical Christianity.

The Sinner’s Prayer is less than 100 years old in its current form. For 1,900 years of church history, nobody was told to “pray this prayer to accept Jesus.” It was invented by American revivalists as a technique for producing measurable conversion numbers. It has no basis in the Greek New Testament.

“Believe In” — What John 3:16 Actually Says

Since we’re in John 3, let’s look at the most famous verse in the Bible:

For God so loved the world that He gave His only begotten Son, that whoever believes in Him should not perish but have everlasting life. — John 3:16

The phrase “believes in Him” is ho pisteuōn eis auton. Each word matters:

πιστεύων εἰς
pisteuōn eis
pis-TYOO-own ice
G4100 + G1519
Trusting INTO. Present active participle of pisteuō + the preposition eis (“into, toward, unto”). Not pisteuo + dative (“believe that something is true”). Pisteuo eis implies movement, commitment, entering into union with. “The one who is continuously trusting into Him.”

First: ho pisteuōn is a present active participle. It means “the one who is continually trusting.” Not past tense (“believed once”). Not aorist (“trusted at a point in time”). Present, continuous, ongoing. The one who keeps trusting.

Second: the preposition eis means “into,” not just “in.” Greek distinguishes between believing that something is true (pisteuō + hoti clause), believing in someone’s reliability (pisteuō + dative), and believing into someone (pisteuō + eis). John overwhelmingly uses the last construction. Pisteuō eis implies movement into—casting yourself into, committing your whole being toward, entering into union with.

εἰς
eis
ice
G1519
Into, toward, unto, for the purpose of. Indicates motion toward and into. Distinguished from en (“in, within”—static location) and epi (“upon”—surface contact). Pisteuo eis = “to trust INTO”—directional, dynamic, implying entering into relationship/union.

So John 3:16 literally says: “God so loved the world that He gave His uniquely-begotten Son, so that everyone continuously trusting into Him should not be destroyed but have life of the age.”

That is wildly different from “anyone who mentally agrees that Jesus existed and prayed a prayer one time gets eternal fire insurance.” The Greek describes an ongoing, active, directional commitment—a life of trusting into Christ, not a moment of intellectual assent.

αἰώνιος
aiōnios
eye-OH-nee-oss
G166
Of the age, age-lasting, pertaining to an age (aiōn). Translated “everlasting” or “eternal” in most English Bibles but means “belonging to the age / of the age to come.” “Aiōnios life” = “the life of the coming age”—a quality of life, not just duration. See our Hell study for the full breakdown of this word.

And the “everlasting life”? The Greek is zōēn aiōnion—“life of the age.” Not just infinite duration, but a quality of life. The life that belongs to the coming age. Jesus defines it Himself in John 17:3: “And this is aiōnios life: that they may know You, the only true God, and Jesus Christ whom You have sent.” Knowing God. Intimate, relational knowledge. Not a ticket punched at an altar call.

γινώσκω
ginōskō
gih-NOHS-ko
G1097
To know (by experience, relationship, intimacy). Distinguished from oida (G1492 — to know intellectually, to perceive). Ginōskō implies experiential, relational, progressive knowledge—the kind of “knowing” used for the most intimate human relationships. Eternal life IS this knowing.

⚠ The Big Controversy: What Does “Born From Above” Actually Require?

If being born from above is a divine act rather than a human decision, what is the human’s role? Three major positions exist:

Position 1: Calvinism / Reformed Theology (Monergism)

The claim: Regeneration (being born from above) is entirely God’s work. It precedes and enables faith. You cannot believe until God first regenerates you. Election is unconditional—God chooses who will be born from above. Human will plays no role.

Key verses cited:

  • John 3:8 — The wind/Spirit blows where IT wishes (sovereign)
  • John 1:13 — Born “not of the will of man, but of God”
  • Ephesians 2:1 — You were “dead in trespasses” (dead people can’t choose)
  • John 6:44 — “No one CAN come to Me unless the Father draws him”

Strengths: Takes the “from above” meaning of anōthen seriously. Acknowledges the divine origin of the new birth. Consistent with the wind metaphor (you don’t control it).

Weaknesses: Makes God responsible for who is NOT chosen (double predestination). Struggles with “God desires all to be saved” (1 Timothy 2:4) and “whoever” language. Can lead to fatalism.

Position 2: Arminianism / Free Will (Synergism)

The claim: God’s grace is available to all (prevenient grace). Humans must respond with faith and repentance. Being born from above is God’s response to human faith—God regenerates those who believe. The “whoever” in John 3:16 is genuinely open.

Key verses cited:

  • John 3:16 — “Whoever believes” (open invitation)
  • Acts 2:38 — “Repent and be baptized” (human response required)
  • Revelation 3:20 — “If anyone opens the door” (human choice)
  • 2 Peter 3:9 — God is “not willing that any should perish”

Strengths: Preserves human responsibility and genuine choice. Makes sense of the universal “whoever” language. God’s love extends to all.

Weaknesses: Can reduce “born from above” to “born again by my decision.” The wind metaphor (John 3:8) and John 1:13 (“not of the will of man”) are hard to fit.

Position 3: Participatory / Relational (Both/And)

The claim: Being born from above is neither purely God’s unilateral act nor purely a human decision. It is a relational reality. God initiates (from above), the Spirit moves (like wind), and humans respond by trusting (pisteuō eis) and reorienting (metanoeō). The birth is from above—but it happens in the context of a person turning toward God. Like physical conception, it takes two parties. Like wind, you don’t control it—but you can set your sails.

Key verses cited:

  • John 3:3-8 — The birth is from above AND requires response (v. 5, v. 16)
  • Philippians 2:12-13 — “Work out your salvation… for it is GOD who works in you”
  • Acts 16:14 — “The Lord opened her heart to respond” (God opens; she responds)
  • John 6:44-45 — The Father draws AND “everyone who has heard and learned comes”

Strengths: Holds the tension the text itself holds. Doesn’t flatten either side. Consistent with John’s both/and pattern (divine sovereignty + human responsibility running in parallel).

Weaknesses: Less tidy than either pure Calvinism or pure Arminianism. Can feel like a dodge.

What the Original Languages Support

Based on the Greek evidence in this study:

  • Anōthen means “from above” in every other Johannine usage. The birth is from above—its origin is God, not human decision.
  • Gennaō with God as subject means “begotten by God”—divine fathering, not human manufacturing.
  • Pneuma (wind/Spirit) blows where IT wishes—not where the preacher wishes or where the sinner decides.
  • John 1:13 explicitly says this birth is “not of the will of man, but of God.”
  • But ho pisteuōn eis (the one continuously trusting into) implies an active, ongoing human response.
  • And metanoeō (change your mind) is commanded—you can’t command what is impossible.

The Greek text holds both realities simultaneously: the birth comes from above (God’s initiative) AND requires ongoing trust (human response). What it does not support is the reduction of this mystery to a one-time prayer, a raised hand, or an altar call. The birth from above is a work of the Spirit, and like wind, it cannot be manufactured on demand.

What This Changes

If anōthen means “from above” and not “again,” several things shift:

Conversion is not a moment. It is a life. The present participle ho pisteuōn (“the one continuously trusting”) describes a posture, not a punctual event. You don’t “get born from above” on March 14th, 2003, at a youth camp and then coast. The trusting is ongoing. The relationship is living. The Spirit keeps blowing.

The Sinner’s Prayer gives false assurance. Millions of people have been told “if you prayed that prayer, you’re saved.” But nothing in the Greek text connects salvation to a single recited formula. The assurance comes from an ongoing relationship—ginōskō (experiential knowing) of the Father. If there is no ongoing trust, no transformed mind, no life in the Spirit, the fact that you once repeated some words in a stadium means nothing.

You cannot mass-produce the new birth. Revival meetings, crusade events, altar calls—these are techniques designed to produce decisions at scale. But the wind blows where it wishes. The Spirit is not a machine you crank by playing the right worship song at the right emotional pitch. Being born from above is a mysterious, sovereign, deeply personal encounter between the Spirit of God and a human soul. It cannot be factory-farmed.

“Are you born again?” is the wrong question. The right question is: “Are you being born from above? Is the Spirit producing something in you that flesh cannot produce? Are you continuously trusting into Christ? Is your mind being transformed?” It’s not a checkbox. It’s a life.

This doesn’t diminish the new birth. It deepens it. “Born from above” is actually more radical than “born again.” “Again” implies repetition—a second copy of something. “From above” implies a completely new origin—a life whose source is heaven, whose nature is Spirit, whose Father is God. You don’t get a second version of your old life. You receive a new kind of life entirely—one that comes from above.

The Rest of the New Testament Confirms “From Above”

John is not alone in describing the new birth as originating from God, not from human effort:

Of His own will He brought us forth by the word of truth, that we should be a kind of firstfruits of His creatures. — James 1:18
ἀπεκύησεν
apekyeōsen
ah-peh-KOO-eh-sen
G616
He brought forth, He gave birth to. From apo (“from”) + kyeō (“to be pregnant, to give birth”). God is the one who gave birth. “Of HIS OWN WILL He brought us forth.” The initiative is entirely divine.
Having been born again, not of corruptible seed but incorruptible, through the word of God which lives and abides forever. — 1 Peter 1:23
ἀναγεγεννημένοι
anagegennēmenoi
ah-nah-geh-gen-nay-MEH-noy
G313
Having been born again / begotten again. From ana (“again, anew”) + gennaō (“to beget”). Note: Peter uses a DIFFERENT word than John. Peter uses anagennao (with the prefix ana = “again”). If John had wanted to say “born again,” he could have used this word. He didn’t. He chose anōthen (“from above”).

This is critical. Peter has a word that specifically means “born again”—anagennaō (G313). John had access to this word. He chose not to use it. He used anōthen instead—because the point is not repetition but origin.

Therefore, if anyone is in Christ, he is a new creation; old things have passed away; behold, all things have become new. — 2 Corinthians 5:17
καινὴ κτίσις
kainē ktisis
kie-NAY KTEE-sis
G2537 + G2937
New creation. Kainē = new in kind/quality (not neos = new in time). Ktisis = creation, act of creating. Not a renovation of the old. Not a second attempt. An entirely new kind of creation—something that didn’t exist before.

Paul uses kainē—“new in kind”—not neos (“new in time / recent”). This is not a refurbished old person. This is a new species of being. A creation. Created by whom? By the Creator. From where? From above. The entire NT witness points in the same direction: the new life originates with God, comes from above, and produces something the old flesh cannot produce on its own.

The Bottom Line

Jesus told Nicodemus he needed to be born from above—to receive life whose origin is God, whose nature is Spirit, whose source is heaven. Nicodemus misunderstood and heard “born again”—a second physical birth. Jesus corrected him. But English translators sided with Nicodemus’s misunderstanding, and 2,000 years of Christianity built an entire conversion industry on the wrong word.

The Sinner’s Prayer is not in the Bible. The altar call was invented in the 1830s. “Asking Jesus into your heart” is not a biblical phrase. The Greek text describes something far more profound than any of these techniques: a divine birth—begotten by God, produced by the Spirit, originating from above, resulting in a new kind of creation that flesh cannot manufacture.

This is not something you do by raising your hand. It is something that happens to you when the Spirit—like wind—moves. Your part is to metanoeō (let your mind be completely reoriented) and pisteuō eis (continuously trust into Christ). Not a moment. A life. Not a prayer. A relationship. Not a decision you made. A birth you received.

From above. Not again. From above.

Key Verses

The New Birth Is From God (Not Human Will)

  • John 3:3-8 — Born anōthen (from above), born of water and Spirit, the wind blows where IT wishes
  • John 1:12-13 — Born “not of blood, nor of the will of the flesh, nor of the will of man, but of God”
  • James 1:18 — “Of HIS OWN WILL He brought us forth by the word of truth”
  • 1 Peter 1:23 — “Born again (anagegennēmenoi), not of corruptible seed but incorruptible”
  • 1 John 3:9 — “Whoever has been born of God… His seed (sperma) remains in him”
  • Titus 3:5 — “Not by works of righteousness which we have done, but according to His mercy He saved us, through the washing of regeneration”

Anōthen Means “From Above” (John’s Usage)

  • John 3:31 — “He who comes FROM ABOVE (anōthen) is above all”
  • John 19:11 — “Unless it had been given you FROM ABOVE (anōthen)”
  • John 19:23 — Jesus’s tunic woven “from the top (anōthen)”
  • James 1:17 — “Every good gift is FROM ABOVE (anōthen)”
  • James 3:15,17 — “Wisdom that is FROM ABOVE (anōthen)”

Faith Is Ongoing Active Trust (Not a One-Time Decision)

  • John 3:16 — Ho pisteuōn (present participle: “the one continuously trusting”)
  • John 17:3 — “Aiōnios life IS knowing (ginōskō) You”—relational, experiential
  • Philippians 2:12-13 — “Work out your salvation… for it is GOD who works in you”
  • Hebrews 11:1 — “Faith (pistis) is the substance of things hoped for”—not mental agreement but concrete trust

New Creation — New Kind, Not Renovation

  • 2 Corinthians 5:17 — “If anyone is in Christ: new (kainē) creation”—new in kind
  • Galatians 6:15 — “Neither circumcision nor uncircumcision, but a new (kainē) creation”
  • Ephesians 2:10 — “We are His workmanship, CREATED in Christ Jesus”
  • Ephesians 4:24 — “Put on the new (kainos) man which was CREATED according to God”

The Apostolic Response (Not a Sinner’s Prayer)

  • Acts 2:38 — “Metanoeō and be baptized”
  • Acts 3:19 — “Metanoeō and turn back”
  • Acts 16:31 — “Trust in (pisteuson epi) the Lord Jesus”
  • Acts 22:16 — “Be immersed and wash away your sins”

ἄνωθεν (anōthen)GreekFrom above, from the top. NOT “again” in John’s usage.G509
πάλιν (palin)GreekAgain, once more. The actual Greek word for “again.” John uses it 45 times.G3825
γεννάω (gennaō)GreekTo beget (father) / give birth (mother). With God as subject: begotten by God.G1080
ἀναγεννάω (anagennaō)GreekTo beget again. Peter’s word (1 Pet 1:23). John chose NOT to use this.G313
πνεῦμα (pneuma)GreekWind / breath / spirit. Jesus exploits the double meaning in John 3:8.G4151
ὕδωρ (hydōr)GreekWater. Debated in John 3:5: physical birth, baptism, the Word, or Spirit.G5204
σπέρμα (sperma)GreekSeed, offspring. God’s seed remains in the one born of God (1 John 3:9).G4690
πιστεύω (pisteuō)GreekTo trust, rely on, commit to. Active, ongoing. Not mental agreement.G4100
εἰς (eis)GreekInto, toward. Pisteuo eis = trust INTO (directional commitment).G1519
μετανοέω (metanoeō)GreekTo change one’s mind. NOT “feel sorry for sins.”G3340
αἰώνιος (aiōnios)GreekOf the age, age-lasting. “Aiōnios life” = life of the age, quality not just duration.G166
γινώσκω (ginōskō)GreekTo know by experience/relationship. Aiōnios life IS this knowing (John 17:3).G1097
καινός (kainos)GreekNew in kind/quality (not neos = new in time). A new species of creation.G2537
κτίσις (ktisis)GreekCreation, act of creating. “New creation” = created, not renovated.G2937
ἀπεκύησεν (apekyeōsen)GreekHe brought forth / gave birth (James 1:18). God’s initiative.G616
βασιλεία (basileia)GreekKingdom, reign, royal rule. The kingdom of God = God’s active reign.G932
