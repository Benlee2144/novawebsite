The Trinity

Is It Biblical? — What the Original Languages Actually Say About the Nature of God, and How a Latin Lawyer’s Word Became the Litmus Test for Orthodoxy

~90 min read • 22 Hebrew & Greek words explored • 65+ cross-references
Hear, O Israel: The LORD our God, the LORD is one. — Deuteronomy 6:4

Introduction: The Word That Isn’t in the Bible

The word “Trinity” does not appear anywhere in Scripture. Not in the Hebrew Old Testament. Not in the Greek New Testament. Not in any manuscript, any fragment, any scroll. The concept that God is “one God in three co-equal, co-eternal Persons—Father, Son, and Holy Spirit” was never articulated by Moses, David, Isaiah, Jesus, Paul, Peter, or John. It was articulated by a North African Latin-speaking lawyer named Tertullian around 200 AD, using the Latin word trinitas.

That alone should make every Bible student stop and ask a simple question: if the Trinity is the central doctrine of Christianity, why didn’t any biblical author ever state it?

This is not an attack on anyone’s faith. Millions of sincere believers hold to Trinitarian theology because they were taught it, and they assume it comes directly from Scripture. What this study will do is go to the original Hebrew and Greek texts—the actual words God inspired—and examine every major passage used to support the doctrine. We will look at what those words meant to the people who wrote them, in the languages they wrote them in, in the historical context they lived in. And we will let the text speak for itself.

Along the way, we will trace exactly how this doctrine developed historically: who introduced the terminology, what political pressures shaped it, which councils codified it, and what happened to people who disagreed. Because the history of the Trinity is not just a theological story. It is a political one.

The Shema: The Foundation of Everything

Every conversation about God’s nature must start where the Bible starts: with the Shema. This is the most important statement in all of Judaism, recited twice daily by observant Jews for over three thousand years. Jesus Himself called it the greatest commandment (Mark 12:29). Here it is in Hebrew:

שְׁמַע יִשְׂרָאֵל יְהוָה אֱלֹהֵינוּ יְהוָה אֶחָד — Deuteronomy 6:4

Shema Yisrael YHWH Eloheinu YHWH echad. — “Hear, O Israel: YHWH our God, YHWH is one.”

The key word here is echad. Trinitarian apologists have built an entire argument on this single word, claiming it means a “compound unity” rather than an absolute “one.” This claim is the foundation of their Old Testament case. Let’s examine it.

אֶחָד
echad
ekh-AHD
H259
The cardinal number “one.” Used 952 times in the OT. Means one, single, first, alone. Used for: one man (Genesis 42:13), one day (Genesis 1:5), one place (Genesis 1:9), one people (Genesis 11:6), one heart (1 Chronicles 12:38). It is the standard Hebrew number “one.”

The Trinitarian argument: because echad is used in phrases like “the two shall become one (echad) flesh” (Genesis 2:24), it implies a composite unity. Therefore, they say, “YHWH is echad” could mean God is a composite being of multiple persons.

The problem: this argument proves nothing. Echad means “one.” When two people become “one flesh,” the word echad doesn’t carry the plurality—the context does. Two people are involved, so “one” describes what they become together. But when echad modifies a single noun, it simply means one. “One man.” “One day.” “One God.”

If echad inherently meant “compound unity,” then Abraham had a “compound unity” of sons when offering Isaac (Genesis 22:2—“your echad son”). The Israelites would have camped in a “compound unity” of places. Every instance of the number “one” in the Old Testament would be suspicious. The argument collapses under its own weight.

Trinitarians sometimes claim that if Moses had wanted to say God is absolutely one, he would have used yachid instead:

יָחִיד
yachid
yah-KHEED
H3173
Only, only one, solitary, unique. Used 12 times in the OT. Carries emotional weight: “only son” (Genesis 22:2), “only child” (Judges 11:34), “my darling” / “my only one” (Psalm 22:20).

But yachid is not a counting word. It means “unique, precious, only.” It would be strange Hebrew to say “YHWH is yachid”—that would mean “YHWH is an only child,” not “YHWH is one.” Moses used the correct word: the number one. Echad. YHWH is one. Not two. Not three. One.

Jesus confirmed this. When asked which commandment was the greatest, He did not say “Believe in the Trinity.” He quoted the Shema:

Jesus answered, “The first of all the commandments is: ‘Hear, O Israel, the Lord our God, the Lord is one.’” — Mark 12:29

The Greek word Mark used for “one” here is heis:

εἷς
heis
hice
G1520
One (masculine). The cardinal number. One person, one thing. The most basic, unambiguous word for numerical oneness in the Greek language.

Jesus quoted the Shema in Greek using the simplest possible word for “one.” And the Jewish scribe who heard Him responded: “Well said, Teacher. You have spoken the truth, for there is one God, and there is no other but He” (Mark 12:32). Jesus told this man he was “not far from the kingdom of God.” Not “You’re missing the Trinity.” Not “Actually, God is three persons.” He affirmed the man’s strict monotheism.

Elohim: Does the Plural Prove Trinity?

The next common Trinitarian argument from the Old Testament: the Hebrew word for God is Elohim, which is grammatically plural. “See?” they say. “God is plural!” Let’s look at this carefully.

אֱלֹהִים
Elohim
el-oh-HEEM
H430
God, gods, judges, rulers. Plural of Eloah (H433). Used 2,606 times. When referring to YHWH, takes SINGULAR verbs. The plural form is the “majestic plural” (pluralis majestatis), a common Semitic grammatical convention for expressing greatness, majesty, or fullness—not numerical plurality.

This is critical: Hebrew grammar tells us whether Elohim is meant as singular or plural by the verb attached to it. In Genesis 1:1: “In the beginning Elohim bara the heavens and the earth.” The verb bara (“created”) is third person masculine singular. If multiple beings were creating, the verb would be bar’u (plural). It isn’t. God created. Singular.

בָרָא
bara
bah-RAH
H1254
To create (out of nothing). Qal perfect, 3rd person masculine singular. Used exclusively of God’s creative activity. Always singular when YHWH is the subject.

The plural of majesty is well-attested in Hebrew. The word for “master” or “lord” is adon, but when referring to a human master of great authority, Hebrew uses adonim (plural)—with singular verbs. Pharaoh is called adonim in Genesis 42:30. Nobody argues Pharaoh is three persons.

Similarly, the word for “face” in Hebrew is always plural (panim), even when referring to one face. The word for “water” (mayim) is always plural. The word for “heaven” (shamayim) is always plural. Hebrew uses plural forms for things that are singular but immense, complex, or majestic. Elohim fits this pattern perfectly.

“Let Us Make Man” — Genesis 1:26

“Then God said, ‘Let Us make man in Our image, according to Our likeness.’” Trinitarians point to the plural pronouns “Us” and “Our” as proof of multiple persons within God.

But notice what happens in the very next verse: “So God created man in His own image; in the image of God He created him” (Genesis 1:27). The pronouns immediately switch to singular. If verse 26 proves multiple persons, verse 27 disproves it. The most consistent reading is the divine council—God speaking to His heavenly court, a concept found throughout the OT (1 Kings 22:19-22; Job 1:6; Isaiah 6:8—“Who will go for Us?”). Or it is the royal “we,” consistent with the plural of majesty.

No Jewish reader in the history of ancient Israel read Genesis 1:26 and concluded God was multiple persons. That interpretation was read backward into the text by Christians centuries later.

John 1:1 — “The Word Was God”

This is the flagship Trinitarian proof text from the New Testament. Let’s look at it in Greek, word by word, because the nuance here is everything.

Ἐν ἀρχῇ ἦν ὁ λόγος, καὶ ὁ λόγος ἦν πρὸς τὸν θεόν, καὶ θεὸς ἦν ὁ λόγος. — John 1:1

Three clauses. Let’s break them apart:

Clause 1: En archē ēn ho logos — “In the beginning was the Word.”

Clause 2: kai ho logos ēn pros ton theon — “And the Word was with [toward] God.”

Clause 3: kai theos ēn ho logos — “And the Word was God” / “And God was the Word” / “And the Word was divine.”

The critical issue is clause 3. Notice the Greek carefully:

λόγος
logos
LOH-goss
G3056
Word, reason, plan, expression, account. From legō (to speak). In Greek philosophy (Heraclitus, Stoics), the rational principle governing the universe. In Jewish wisdom literature, the personified wisdom/word of God through which He acts. John is drawing on both traditions.
θεός
theos
theh-OSS
G2316
God, a god, divine being. Used 1,343 times in the NT. Can refer to the Father (most commonly), to judges (John 10:34, quoting Psalm 82:6), or qualitatively to describe divine nature.

In clause 2, theon has the definite article (ton theon)—“the God,” clearly referring to the Father. In clause 3, theos has no article. This is significant in Greek grammar. When a predicate noun (here, theos) precedes the verb and lacks the article, it is typically qualitative—describing the nature or character of the subject, not identifying it as the same person.

Greek scholar Philip Harner published a landmark study in 1973 on exactly this construction. His conclusion: anarthrous (no article) predicate nouns before the verb in John are qualitative. They describe what something is like, not who something is. The Logos has the nature of God. The Logos is divine. The Logos is not identified as the Father.

This is actually what Trinitarians want to hear—except it cuts both ways. The text says the Word has divine nature. It does not say the Word is a co-equal “Person” in a triune Godhead. That requires reading the Nicene Creed back into John’s gospel. John is saying something profound about the Logos’s divine nature and intimate relationship with the Father. He is not articulating a three-person ontological Trinity.

Consider what John says next: “All things were made through him” (John 1:3). The Greek word is dia (διά)—“through,” the preposition of agency or instrumentality. Creation came through the Word, not from the Word independently. The Father is the source; the Logos is the agent. This language of mediated creation recurs in Paul: “one Lord Jesus Christ, through whom are all things” (1 Corinthians 8:6).

διά
dia
dee-AH
G1223
Through, by means of, on account of. When used with the genitive case (as here), indicates the agent or instrument through which something is accomplished. Distinguished from ek/ex (“out of,” origin/source) and hypo (“by,” direct cause).

John 10:30 — “I and the Father Are One”

Another cornerstone verse. Jesus says: Egō kai ho patēr hen esmen. “I and the Father—one we are.” This is used to prove that Jesus and the Father are the same God, co-equal, co-eternal. But what does the Greek actually say?

Ἅν
hen
hen
G1520
One (neuter form of heis). Because it is NEUTER (hen), not MASCULINE (heis), it indicates oneness of purpose, will, or nature—not oneness of person. If Jesus meant “I and the Father are one person,” Greek grammar required the masculine heis.

This is the kind of detail that changes everything. The word “one” here is hen—neuter. Not heis (masculine). In Greek, when you say two people are heis (masculine “one”), you’re saying they are the same person. When you say they are hen (neuter “one”), you’re saying they are the same thing—unified in purpose, nature, or will. It’s the difference between “we are the same person” and “we are on the same page.”

How do we know this? Because Jesus uses the exact same word to describe His followers in John 17:11: “Holy Father, keep them in Your name, that they may be one (hen) just as We are.” And again in John 17:21-22: “That they all may be one (hen), as You, Father, are in Me, and I in You; that they also may be one (hen) in Us.”

If “I and the Father are hen” means Jesus is God in a Trinitarian sense, then “that they may be hen just as We are” means the disciples are also God. Obviously, nobody believes that. The word describes unity of purpose, not ontological identity.

Matthew 28:19 — The Baptismal Formula

“Go therefore and make disciples of all the nations, baptizing them in the name of the Father and of the Son and of the Holy Spirit.” This is the strongest Trinitarian proof text in the entire Bible. Three names, one “name” (singular). Case closed? Let’s look deeper.

First, the textual issue. This verse has a complicated manuscript history. Eusebius of Caesarea (263–339 AD), the most important early church historian, quotes Matthew 28:19 eighteen times in his pre-Nicene writings. His version? “Go and make disciples of all nations in my name.” No Trinitarian formula. Just “in my name.” After the Council of Nicaea in 325—where Trinitarian theology became the official imperial position—Eusebius started quoting the longer version.

This doesn’t prove the longer version is a forgery. But it raises a serious question that scholars have debated for centuries. The fact that the Book of Acts—which records actual baptisms performed by the apostles—never uses the Trinitarian formula is striking:

  • Acts 2:38 — “Be baptized in the name of Jesus Christ”
  • Acts 8:16 — “Baptized in the name of the Lord Jesus”
  • Acts 10:48 — “Baptized in the name of the Lord”
  • Acts 19:5 — “Baptized in the name of the Lord Jesus”
  • Acts 22:16 — “Be baptized and wash away your sins, calling on the name of the Lord”

Every single recorded baptism in Acts is in the name of Jesus alone. If Jesus commanded a Trinitarian formula in Matthew 28:19, why did none of His apostles obey it? Either they all disobeyed a direct command from the risen Christ, or the command as we have it was expanded later to reflect developing Trinitarian theology. Both options are worth considering honestly.

Even taking the verse as authentic, notice: it says “the name” (singular, Greek onoma)—not “the names.” This could indicate that Father, Son, and Spirit share one divine identity. But it could equally indicate that the “name” (authority, character) of God is expressed in these three roles or manifestations. The verse mentions three—it does not define their ontological relationship as “co-equal, co-eternal Persons of one substance.” That definition came from councils, not from this verse.

ὄνομα
onoma
OH-noh-mah
G3686
Name, character, authority, reputation. In Semitic thought, a “name” is not just a label but represents the entire character, authority, and nature of a person. “In the name of” = “by the authority of.”

1 John 5:7 — The Comma Johanneum

“For there are three that bear witness in heaven: the Father, the Word, and the Holy Spirit; and these three are one.” This is the only verse in the entire Bible that explicitly states a Trinitarian formula. There’s just one problem: it’s a forgery.

This passage, known as the Comma Johanneum (Latin comma = “clause”), is absent from:

  • Every Greek manuscript before the 14th century (with two very late, disputed exceptions)
  • All early Latin manuscripts before the 6th century
  • Every ancient translation (Syriac, Coptic, Armenian, Ethiopic, Arabic, Slavonic)
  • Every Greek church father (Athanasius, Basil, Gregory of Nazianzus, Gregory of Nyssa, Chrysostom—none quote it, even when arguing for the Trinity)
  • The earliest Latin fathers (Tertullian, Cyprian, Hilary, Ambrose—none cite it)

The verse first appears as a marginal gloss in a handful of Old Latin manuscripts around the 5th-6th century. It crept into the Latin Vulgate text and from there into Erasmus’s printed Greek New Testament (1522, third edition—under pressure from the Catholic Church after he omitted it in his first two editions). From Erasmus, it entered the Textus Receptus and was carried into the King James Version (1611).

Today, virtually every modern Bible translation removes it or relegates it to a footnote: the ESV, NIV, NASB, CSB, NRSV, and even the New King James Version includes a footnote acknowledging the textual problem. The scholarly consensus is overwhelming: this verse was not written by John. It was added by a later scribe to provide scriptural support for a doctrine that lacked an explicit proof text.

This matters. If the clearest Trinitarian statement in the Bible is a later addition, that tells us something important about what the original authors did and did not write.

The Holy Spirit — Person or Power?

The Trinity requires the Holy Spirit to be a co-equal, co-eternal “Person”—the Third Person of the Godhead. What do the original languages say?

πνεῦμα
pneuma
PNYOO-mah
G4151
Wind, breath, spirit. From pneō (to blow, to breathe). NEUTER gender in Greek. Used 385 times in the NT. Can refer to the Spirit of God, the human spirit, wind, or spiritual beings. The Greek word is grammatically neuter (it/its), not masculine (he/his).
רוּחַ
ruach
ROO-akh
H7307
Wind, breath, spirit. Used 378 times in the OT. FEMININE gender in Hebrew. Refers to wind, the breath of life, the Spirit of God, and the human spirit. Never personified as a separate being from YHWH in the Old Testament.

Here is something most Christians never learn: pneuma is neuter in Greek, and ruach is feminine in Hebrew. In neither language is the word for “spirit” grammatically masculine. English translations that render the Holy Spirit as “He” are making a theological choice, not a grammatical one.

In John 14-16, Jesus uses masculine pronouns for the Spirit—but this is because He is calling the Spirit the Paraclete (paraklētos), which is a masculine noun:

παράκλητος
paraklētos
par-AK-lay-toss
G3875
Called alongside, advocate, helper, comforter, intercessor. Masculine noun. Used 5 times in the NT: 4 times for the Spirit (John 14:16, 14:26, 15:26, 16:7) and once for Jesus Himself (1 John 2:1). The masculine pronouns in John 14-16 grammatically agree with this masculine NOUN, not with pneuma (neuter).

In Greek, pronouns must agree in gender with their antecedent noun. When the antecedent is paraklētos (masculine), the pronoun is “he.” When the antecedent is pneuma (neuter), the pronoun should be “it.” In Romans 8:16 and 8:26, where Paul uses pneuma as the subject, the pronouns are indeed neuter in the Greek—though many English translations still render them as “He” for theological reasons.

In the Old Testament, the “Spirit of God” (Ruach Elohim) is never treated as a separate person from God. It is God’s active presence, His power at work, His breath animating creation. “The Spirit of the LORD came upon him” (Judges 14:6) means God’s power came upon him—not that a third Person showed up. “Where can I go from Your Spirit?” (Psalm 139:7) is parallel with “Where can I flee from Your presence?”—the Spirit is God’s presence.

Does the Spirit have personal attributes? The Spirit speaks, leads, can be grieved, can be lied to (Acts 5:3). But personification does not equal personhood. Wisdom is personified as a woman in Proverbs 8—she calls out, she builds a house, she existed before creation. Nobody argues Wisdom is a fourth Person of the Godhead. Sin is personified in Romans 6-7—it reigns, it deceives, it kills. Personification is a literary device, not an ontological statement.

What Paul Actually Wrote About God

Paul is the most prolific New Testament author. If anyone was going to articulate Trinitarian theology, it would be him. Here is what he actually wrote:

Yet for us there is one God, the Father, from whom are all things and for whom we exist, and one Lord, Jesus Christ, through whom are all things and through whom we exist. — 1 Corinthians 8:6

Read that carefully. Paul identifies “one God” as the Father. Not as the Trinity. Not as “one God in three persons.” The Father is the one God. Jesus is the one Lord. These are distinct roles. And notice the prepositions: all things are from (ek) the Father and through (dia) Christ. The Father is the source; Christ is the agent. This is not co-equal Trinitarian language.

ἐκ
ek
ek
G1537
Out of, from, away from. Indicates origin or source. “From whom are all things”—the Father is the ultimate source/origin of everything.

Paul writes similarly in 1 Timothy 2:5: “For there is one God and one Mediator between God and men, the Man Christ Jesus.” A mediator, by definition, stands between two parties. If Jesus is God in an absolute, co-equal sense, He cannot mediate between God and humanity. He would be mediating between Himself and humanity.

μεσίτης
mesitēs
meh-SEE-tays
G3316
Mediator, go-between, arbitrator. From mesos (“middle”). One who stands between two parties to reconcile them. Used 6 times in the NT. Requires being distinct from both parties being reconciled.

Paul also writes that Christ will ultimately hand the kingdom back to the Father “that God may be all in all” (1 Corinthians 15:24-28). The Son Himself “will be subjected to Him who put all things under Him.” A co-equal, co-eternal Person does not submit to or become subjected to another co-equal Person. This passage describes a Son who received authority from the Father and returns it to the Father. It is subordination, not co-equality.

ὑποταγήσεται
hypotagēsetai
hoo-poh-tah-GAY-seh-tie
G5293
Will be subjected, will be placed under. From hypo (“under”) + tassō (“to arrange, order”). Future passive indicative. The Son will be placed in subordinate rank to the Father.

What Jesus Said About Himself and the Father

If Jesus believed He was co-equal with the Father—the second Person of a co-eternal Trinity—His own words are puzzling. Consider what He actually said:

  • “The Father is greater than I” (John 14:28)
  • “I can of Myself do nothing” (John 5:30)
  • “My teaching is not Mine, but His who sent Me” (John 7:16)
  • “The Son can do nothing of Himself, but what He sees the Father do” (John 5:19)
  • “I do not seek My own will but the will of the Father who sent Me” (John 5:30)
  • “But of that day and hour no one knows, not even the angels in heaven, nor the Son, but only the Father” (Mark 13:32)
  • “My God, my God, why have You forsaken me?” (Matthew 27:46)
  • “I am ascending to My Father and your Father, and to My God and your God” (John 20:17)

Jesus calls the Father “My God.” A co-equal Person in the Trinity does not have a God. The Father never calls Jesus “My God.” Jesus says the Father is “greater” than He is. Jesus says He doesn’t know things the Father knows. Jesus says His teaching comes from the Father, His power comes from the Father, His will is submitted to the Father. In every statement, there is a clear hierarchy: the Father is the source, the authority, the sender. Jesus is the sent one, the obedient one, the one who receives.

Trinitarian theology handles this by introducing a distinction between Jesus’s “divine nature” and “human nature”—the communicatio idiomatum. When Jesus says “the Father is greater,” that’s His human nature speaking. When He says “I and the Father are one,” that’s His divine nature speaking. But this framework was developed in the 4th-5th centuries. Jesus Himself never made this distinction. He spoke as one person—the Son—who consistently placed the Father above Himself.

⚠ The Big Controversy: Is the Trinity Biblical?

This is the question that has split Christianity for 1,700 years. Three major positions exist, and each claims biblical support. Let’s examine them honestly.

Position 1: Trinitarianism

The claim: God is one Being in three co-equal, co-eternal Persons—Father, Son, and Holy Spirit—each fully God, yet not three Gods but one God. Defined at the Council of Nicaea (325 AD) and clarified at Constantinople (381 AD).

Key verses cited:

  • Matthew 28:19 — Trinitarian baptismal formula
  • John 1:1 — “The Word was God”
  • John 10:30 — “I and the Father are one”
  • John 20:28 — Thomas says “My Lord and my God”
  • 2 Corinthians 13:14 — Trinitarian benediction
  • Hebrews 1:8 — “Your throne, O God, is forever”

Strengths: Takes seriously the divinity passages about Christ. Has 1,700 years of theological development and creedal support. Most Christians worldwide hold this view.

Weaknesses: The word “Trinity” is not in Scripture. The formulation relies on 4th-century philosophical categories (Greek ousia, hypostasis) foreign to biblical authors. Must explain away numerous subordination passages. The clearest proof text (1 John 5:7) is a known interpolation.

Position 2: Modalism (Oneness / Sabellianism)

The claim: God is one Person who manifests in three modes or roles—as Father in creation, as Son in redemption, as Spirit in sanctification. Not three Persons, but three manifestations of one Person. Held today by Oneness Pentecostals and the United Pentecostal Church International.

Key verses cited:

  • Deuteronomy 6:4 — “YHWH is one”
  • Isaiah 9:6 — The Son is called “Everlasting Father”
  • John 14:9 — “He who has seen Me has seen the Father”
  • Colossians 2:9 — “In Him dwells all the fullness of the Godhead bodily”
  • Isaiah 44:6 — “Besides Me there is no God”

Strengths: Preserves strict monotheism. Takes the Shema seriously. Avoids the philosophical complexity of Trinitarian formulations.

Weaknesses: Cannot adequately explain Jesus praying to the Father (who is He praying to—Himself?), the baptism of Jesus where Father, Son, and Spirit appear simultaneously (Matthew 3:16-17), or Jesus’s statement “I and the Father are one” (which implies two who are unified, not one pretending to be two).

Position 3: Biblical Unitarianism / Subordinationism

The claim: The Father alone is the one God (YHWH). Jesus is the Son of God—exalted, divine in nature (as God’s unique agent and image), but not co-equal or co-eternal with the Father. The Holy Spirit is God’s active power/presence, not a separate Person. This was the dominant view of many early Jewish Christians and several pre-Nicene church fathers.

Key verses cited:

  • 1 Corinthians 8:6 — “One God, the Father”
  • John 17:3 — “This is eternal life, that they may know You, the only true God, and Jesus Christ whom You have sent”
  • 1 Timothy 2:5 — One God, one Mediator
  • Mark 12:29 — Jesus quotes the Shema
  • John 14:28 — “The Father is greater than I”
  • 1 Corinthians 15:24-28 — The Son subjected to the Father
  • Acts 2:36 — “God has made this Jesus both Lord and Christ”

Strengths: Reads consistently with the OT monotheism Jesus and the apostles affirmed. Doesn’t require philosophical categories foreign to biblical authors. Handles subordination passages naturally. The language of “sending,” “giving authority,” “exalting,” and “making Lord” all imply a hierarchy.

Weaknesses: Must carefully explain divinity passages (John 1:1, Thomas’s confession, Hebrews 1:8). Can be confused with Arianism (which said the Son was created and unlike the Father—a position most biblical unitarians reject).

What the Original Languages Support

Based on the Greek and Hebrew evidence examined in this study:

  • Echad in the Shema means the number one. It does not imply compound unity.
  • Elohim is a plural of majesty with singular verbs. It does not prove multiple persons.
  • John 1:1 describes the Logos as having divine nature (qualitative theos), not as being identical with the Father.
  • John 10:30 uses neuter hen (unity of purpose), not masculine heis (identity of person).
  • Pneuma is neuter in Greek and ruach is feminine in Hebrew. The “He” pronouns for the Spirit are based on the masculine noun paraklētos, not on pneuma itself.
  • 1 John 5:7 (the Comma Johanneum) is a later interpolation, not original Scripture.
  • Paul consistently identifies “one God” as the Father and distinguishes the Son as Lord, mediator, and agent.
  • Jesus consistently places the Father above Himself in authority, knowledge, and will.

The original languages support a high Christology—Jesus is exalted, divine in nature, the unique Son of God through whom all things were made. But the original languages do not support the specific Nicene formulation of three co-equal, co-eternal Persons sharing one substance (homoousios). That language comes from Greek philosophy, not from Hebrew prophets or Greek apostles.

How the Trinity Became “Orthodox”: The Political History

The doctrine of the Trinity was not delivered by Jesus on a mountain. It was hammered out over centuries of bitter debate, political maneuvering, imperial pressure, exile, and violence. Here is the timeline:

~30 AD
The apostolic era. No Trinitarian formulation exists. Jesus is proclaimed as Lord, Messiah, Son of God. The Father is identified as the one God. The Spirit is God’s active presence and power.
~100–150 AD
The Apostolic Fathers (Clement of Rome, Ignatius, Polycarp) speak of Father, Son, and Spirit but do not define their ontological relationship. No one uses the word “Trinity” or articulates three co-equal Persons.
~150–180 AD
Justin Martyr describes the Logos as a “second God” who is subordinate to the Father. Uses Platonic philosophical categories. This is NOT Nicene Trinitarianism—it is subordinationist.
~200 AD
Tertullian (North Africa, Latin-speaking) coins the word trinitas and the formula “three persons, one substance” (tres personae, una substantia). He was a lawyer, not a bishop, and later joined the Montanist heresy. He explicitly taught the Son was subordinate to the Father.
~185–254 AD
Origen of Alexandria—the most influential early theologian—taught that the Son was subordinate to the Father. The Son was divine but derived His divinity from the Father. The Spirit was subordinate to the Son. Origen was later condemned as a heretic by the 5th Ecumenical Council (553 AD), partly for these views.
318–325 AD
The Arian controversy erupts. Arius (a presbyter in Alexandria) teaches that the Son was created by the Father—“there was a time when the Son was not.” Alexander (bishop of Alexandria) and his deacon Athanasius argue the Son is co-eternal. The debate threatens to split the empire.
325 AD
Council of Nicaea. Emperor Constantine convenes the council—not for theological reasons but for political unity. He needed a unified religion for a unified empire. The council produces the Nicene Creed declaring the Son homoousios (“of the same substance”) with the Father. Arius is exiled. But homoousios is a philosophical term, not a biblical one. Many bishops signed under imperial pressure.
325–381 AD
Fifty-six years of chaos. The “settled” Nicene position was anything but settled. Councils overturned councils. Emperors switched sides. Athanasius was exiled five times. At one point, the majority of bishops in the empire were semi-Arian or Arian. Jerome wrote: “The whole world groaned and marveled to find itself Arian.”
380 AD
Emperor Theodosius I issues the Edict of Thessalonica, making Nicene Christianity the official state religion. All other forms of Christianity are declared heretical. This is legislation, not theology.
381 AD
Council of Constantinople. Expands the Nicene Creed to include the full divinity of the Holy Spirit (“who proceeds from the Father, who with the Father and the Son is worshipped and glorified”). The Trinity as we know it is now fully formulated—351 years after Christ.
381 AD onward
Dissent criminalized. Non-Trinitarian Christians are stripped of civil rights, banned from meeting, their books burned. The theological debate is over not because one side proved their case from Scripture, but because the state made disagreement illegal.
ὁμοούσιος
homoousios
hoh-moh-OO-see-oss
Not in NT
“Of the same substance/essence.” A Greek philosophical term from Aristotelian metaphysics. Never used in Scripture. Introduced at Nicaea to define the Son’s relationship to the Father. The key word in the entire Trinitarian debate—and it is not a biblical word.

This history does not prove the Trinity is wrong. But it proves the Trinity was not a settled biblical teaching that everyone always believed. It was a conclusion reached through centuries of debate, shaped by Greek philosophy, and enforced by Roman imperial power. The question every Christian should ask is simple: should my understanding of God come from the original Hebrew and Greek Scriptures, or from 4th-century councils using philosophical categories the biblical authors never employed?

What the Early Greek-Speaking Fathers Actually Taught

Before Nicaea codified one position and criminalized others, early Christian teachers held a range of views. Many of these were Greek speakers reading the New Testament in their native language. What did they say?

Justin Martyr (~100–165 AD) described the Logos as “another God and Lord subject to the Maker of all things” (Dialogue with Trypho, ch. 56). He called the Son a “second God” who was numerically distinct from the Father and subordinate to Him. This is not Nicene orthodoxy. Justin was a subordinationist.

Irenaeus of Lyon (~130–202 AD) identified the Son and Spirit as the “two hands of God” through whom the Father works. He did not use Trinitarian language or articulate three co-equal Persons.

Clement of Alexandria (~150–215 AD) described the Logos as subordinate to the Father, calling the Father “the only true, almighty God” and the Son as “next to the only almighty Father” (Stromata VII.2).

Origen (~185–254 AD), the most influential early theologian, explicitly taught subordinationism: the Son was divine but derived His divinity from the Father. The Father alone was autotheos—God in Himself. The Son was theos (divine) but not ho theos (the God). Origen even argued the Son should not be the ultimate object of prayer—prayer should go through the Son to the Father.

Eusebius of Caesarea (~263–339 AD), the father of church history, was semi-Arian. He signed the Nicene Creed reluctantly and under political pressure. His own theology placed the Son as subordinate to the Father.

The point is not that these fathers were right about everything. The point is that the people closest to the apostolic era, reading the New Testament in its original Greek, did not articulate Nicene Trinitarianism. They saw the Son as divine, exalted, the agent of creation—and subordinate to the Father. The co-equal, co-eternal formulation was a later development.

Addressing the Strongest Trinitarian Passages

Intellectual honesty requires engaging with the best evidence for the opposing view. Here are the strongest Trinitarian proof texts and what the Greek actually says.

John 20:28 — Thomas Says “My Lord and My God”

After the resurrection, Thomas sees Jesus and exclaims: Ho kyrios mou kai ho theos mou—“The Lord of me and the God of me!” This is a powerful text. Thomas calls Jesus theos with the article.

Several observations: (1) This could be an exclamation directed to the Father in response to seeing Jesus—like saying “My God!” in amazement. (2) Even if directed at Jesus, calling someone theos does not necessarily identify them as YHWH. The word theos is applied to Moses (Exodus 7:1), to judges (Psalm 82:6, quoted by Jesus in John 10:34), and to Satan (2 Corinthians 4:4). (3) Most importantly, Jesus’s own response in John 20:17—just 11 verses earlier—was: “I am ascending to My God and your God.” If Jesus has a God, He is not the ultimate God.

Hebrews 1:8 — “Your Throne, O God”

“But to the Son He says: ‘Your throne, O God, is forever and ever.’” This quotes Psalm 45:6, which was originally addressed to a human king (likely at a royal wedding). The Hebrew can also be translated: “Your throne is God’s forever” or “God is your throne forever.” And notice what Hebrews 1:9 says immediately after: “Therefore God, Your God, has anointed You.” The Son has a God who anointed Him. Again: if the Son has a God, He is not the ultimate God.

Isaiah 9:6 — “Mighty God, Everlasting Father”

“His name shall be called Wonderful Counselor, Mighty God, Everlasting Father, Prince of Peace.” The Hebrew El Gibbor (“Mighty God”) is used of the Messiah here. But El Gibbor is also used of human warriors in Ezekiel 32:21. And “Everlasting Father” (Avi-Ad) means “Father of Eternity”—a title, not an identification with YHWH the Father. Hebrew throne names expressed the king’s God-given authority, not his ontological identity with God.

Colossians 2:9 — “The Fullness of the Godhead Bodily”

The Greek word translated “Godhead” here is theotēs:

θεότης
theotēs
theh-OH-tays
G2320
Deity, divine nature, divinity. Used only once in the NT (Colossians 2:9). Describes the quality or essence of being God. The fullness of God’s nature dwells in Christ—meaning God chose to fully express Himself through the Son. This does not require a Trinitarian framework; it is consistent with the Son being the perfect image and agent of the Father.

Paul is saying that in Christ, the full divine nature is present. This is a high Christology. But it is also consistent with the Father choosing to fully express Himself through the Son (Colossians 1:19—“it pleased the Father that in Him all the fullness should dwell”). The fullness dwells in the Son because the Father put it there.

The Bottom Line

The Bible teaches that the Father is the one God. The Bible teaches that Jesus is the Son of God—exalted, divine in nature, the unique agent through whom God created and redeems. The Bible teaches that the Holy Spirit is God’s active presence and power at work in the world and in believers. The Bible does not teach that these three are co-equal, co-eternal Persons sharing one divine substance.

That formulation came from Tertullian’s legal mind, was debated for centuries, was shaped by Greek philosophical categories, and was enforced by Roman imperial power. It may be true. But it is not biblical in the sense that the Bible states it. It is a theological conclusion drawn from biblical data using non-biblical categories—and that distinction matters.

What the original languages show is a relationship between the Father and the Son that is intimate, unique, and hierarchical. The Father sends, the Son obeys. The Father gives authority, the Son exercises it. The Father is the source (ek), the Son is the agent (dia). The Son is divine, but the Father is greater. The Son will ultimately submit all things back to the Father. The Spirit is the way God’s presence and power operate in the world—not a separate Person in a committee, but God Himself at work.

If the Nicene Creed captures something true about God, wonderful. But the Nicene Creed is not Scripture. And when a creed shapes how we read Scripture—rather than Scripture shaping what we believe—we have the cart before the horse.

Read the Hebrew. Read the Greek. Let the text speak. That is what this site exists to help you do.

  • Blue Letter Bible — The best free tool for original language study. Search any verse, click any word to see Greek/Hebrew, Strong’s number, definition, and every verse using the same word.

  • STEP Bible (Tyndale House, Cambridge) — Advanced morphology, interlinear, word studies, and cross-references. Free and scholarly.
  • Strong’s Concordance Online — Look up any number and see every verse that uses it.

Compare Translations

  • Bible Gateway — 60+ English translations. Compare how different translators handle key Trinitarian verses.
  • Bible Hub Parallel — 25+ translations side by side for any verse.
  • Scripture4All Interlinear — Extremely literal word-by-word. No interpretation. Just what the text says.

Read the Early Church Fathers

  • New Advent: Church Fathers — Free, searchable translations of the Ante-Nicene, Nicene, and Post-Nicene Fathers. Read Justin Martyr, Origen, Irenaeus, Eusebius in their own words.
  • Christian Classics Ethereal Library — Thousands of classic Christian texts free online, including the Nicene and Ante-Nicene Fathers series.

Verify History • Learn the Languages

  • World History Encyclopedia — Peer-reviewed articles on the councils of Nicaea, Constantinople, and early Christianity.
  • JSTOR — Academic articles on biblical studies. Many free with a free account. Search for Harner’s qualitative nouns study.
  • Bill Mounce’s Greek Resources — Free Greek dictionary from a top NT scholar.
  • Aleph with Beth (YouTube) — Free Biblical Hebrew video lessons. Learn to read the text yourself.
