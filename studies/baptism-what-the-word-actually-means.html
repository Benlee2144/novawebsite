Baptism — What the Word Actually Means

Why the Greek Word Was Never Translated

~12 min read • Greek: βαπτίζω (baptizo)
Or do you not know that as many of us as were baptized into Christ Jesus were baptized into His death? Therefore we were buried with Him through baptism into death. — Romans 6:3-4

The word "baptism" is not a translation. It is a transliteration—the English letters that make the same sound as the Greek letters. The translators did not translate the word βαπτίζω (baptizo) because they knew what it meant, and what it meant would have challenged the established church practice of their day. So they simply carried the Greek sound into English and let tradition define it instead of Scripture.

This single decision has caused centuries of confusion, denominational splits, and Christians missing the profound symbolism that God intended. It is time to see what the word actually means.

The Greek Word and Its Meaning

❖ Baptizo (G907)

The Greek word βαπτίζω (baptizo) comes from βάπτω (bapto), which means to dip, immerse, or submerge. In classical Greek literature, baptizo was used for:

  • Ships sinking — When a ship was "baptized," it went completely under water
  • Dyeing cloth — Fabric was "baptized" in dye by complete immersion
  • Drawing wine — A cup was "baptized" by dipping it completely into a wine jar
  • Drowning — A person could be "baptized" to death by being held underwater
  • Overwhelming — Being "baptized" with troubles meant being completely engulfed

The word never meant sprinkling or pouring. It always implied complete immersion, submersion, or overwhelming coverage.

❖ Why Translators Didn't Translate It

When the King James Bible was commissioned in 1604, the Church of England practiced infant sprinkling, not immersion. King James's translation instructions specifically stated that the word "baptism" should be kept unchanged. If they had translated baptizo as "immerse" or "dip," it would have challenged the established church practice and caused political problems.

The translators knew exactly what the word meant. They chose not to translate it for institutional reasons, not linguistic ones.

What the New Testament Actually Says

❖ Jesus's Baptism

When He had been baptized, Jesus came up immediately from the water. — Matthew 3:16

The Greek says Jesus "came up from (apo) the water"—not "from beside" the water. If Jesus had been sprinkled, the text would say He moved away from the water's edge. Instead, it says He came up out of the water, indicating He had been down in it.

Mark 1:9-10 is even clearer: Jesus was baptized "into" (eis) the Jordan River and came up "out of" (ek) the water. This language describes immersion, not sprinkling.

❖ Philip and the Ethiopian Eunuch

So he commanded the chariot to stand still. And both Philip and the eunuch went down into the water, and he baptized him. Now when they came up out of the water... — Acts 8:38-39

Both Philip and the eunuch went down into (katabainō eis) the water and came up out of (anabainō ek) the water. If baptism were sprinkling, they would not both need to enter the water. Philip could have sprinkled the eunuch from the shore.

❖ Romans 6 — The Symbolism

Therefore we were buried with Him through baptism into death, that just as Christ was raised from the dead by the glory of the Father, even so we also should walk in newness of life. — Romans 6:4

Paul explicitly compares baptism to burial. When someone is buried, they are completely covered, not sprinkled with dirt. The symbolism breaks down entirely if baptism is sprinkling:

  • Going under the water = dying to the old life, being buried with Christ
  • Coming up from the water = resurrection to new life with Christ

Sprinkling does not picture death and resurrection. Only immersion carries this profound symbolism.

❖ Colossians 2:12

Buried with Him in baptism, in which you also were raised with Him through faith. — Colossians 2:12

Again, Paul uses burial language. You cannot be "buried" and "raised" through sprinkling. The metaphor requires complete submersion.

Historical Evidence

❖ Early Church Practice

For the first several centuries, the Christian church practiced immersion exclusively:

  • The Didache (60-120 AD) — The earliest Christian manual instructs baptism in "living water" (running rivers) by immersion
  • Justin Martyr (100-165 AD) — Describes baptism as being "led to where there is water" and being "washed" entirely
  • Early baptisteries — Archaeological remains show pools deep enough for immersion, not fonts for sprinkling
  • Christian art — Early depictions show candidates waist-deep or completely underwater

❖ When Did Sprinkling Begin?

Sprinkling began as an emergency exception, not the normal practice:

  • 3rd century — "Clinical baptism" for those too sick to be immersed
  • 4th century — Infant baptism became common; immersion was difficult for babies
  • 8th-10th centuries — Sprinkling gradually replaced immersion in Western churches
  • Catholic Council of Ravenna (1311) — First official approval of sprinkling as equal to immersion

For over 1,000 years after Christ, immersion was the standard practice. Sprinkling is a later development that contradicts both the original language and early Christian practice.

Other Greek Words Available

If God had wanted to communicate sprinkling or pouring, He had precise Greek words available:

  • ῥαντίζω (rhantizo) — to sprinkle
  • χέω (cheo) — to pour
  • ἐπιχέω (epicheo) — to pour upon

These words appear elsewhere in the New Testament for their specific actions. The Holy Spirit chose baptizo (immerse) deliberately, not rhantizo (sprinkle). Word choice matters in Scripture.

Jesus's Use of Baptism Language

❖ Baptized with the Holy Spirit

He will baptize you with the Holy Spirit and fire. — Matthew 3:11

When the Holy Spirit came on the Day of Pentecost, the disciples were not "sprinkled" with a few drops of spiritual influence. They were completely overwhelmed, filled, immersed in the Spirit's power. The word baptizo perfectly captures this complete saturation.

❖ Baptized with Sufferings

But I have a baptism to be baptized with, and how distressed I am till it is accomplished! — Luke 12:50

Jesus spoke of His coming death as a "baptism"—He would be completely submerged in suffering, overwhelmed by the weight of sin and God's wrath. This usage shows that baptizo means complete immersion or overwhelming, not light sprinkling.

The Mode and the Message

❖ Why It Matters

This is not merely about getting wet differently. The method carries the meaning:

Method Symbolism
Immersion Death to old life, burial with Christ, resurrection to new life
Sprinkling Light cleansing, getting blessed, religious ritual

God designed baptism to be a powerful visual representation of the gospel itself—we die with Christ, are buried with Him, and rise to new life. When the mode changes, the message is lost.

❖ The Candidate

The New Testament shows a consistent pattern for baptism candidates:

  • They heard the gospel — Acts 8:35-37
  • They believed — Acts 8:37, Acts 16:31-33
  • They chose baptism — Acts 8:36 ("What hinders me from being baptized?")
  • They were baptized immediately — Acts 8:38, Acts 16:33

Every New Testament example involves someone old enough to hear, understand, believe, and choose. There are no examples of infant baptism in Scripture.

Common Objections Answered

❖ "Household Baptisms Included Infants"

Response: The "household" baptisms in Acts (Lydia's household, the jailer's household) do not mention infants. In the ancient world, "household" often included servants, extended family, and business associates—not necessarily babies. Most importantly, the text says these households heard and believed before being baptized.

❖ "What if There's Not Enough Water?"

Response: The early church practiced baptism in rivers, lakes, and constructed baptisteries. If immersion were impossible, they waited until it was possible or found another location. The Desert Fathers traveled great distances to find adequate water because they understood the importance of proper baptism.

❖ "John 3:5 Says We Must Be Baptized to Be Saved"

Response: John 3:5 says we must be "born of water and the Spirit." This likely refers to physical birth (water = amniotic fluid) and spiritual birth (the Spirit), not baptism. Baptism is an act of obedience that follows salvation, not a requirement for it (Ephesians 2:8-9).

Conclusion: Let Scripture Define Scripture

The evidence is overwhelming:

  • The Greek word baptizo means to immerse completely
  • The New Testament examples describe immersion
  • The symbolism requires immersion (burial and resurrection)
  • Early church history practiced immersion exclusively for centuries
  • Alternative Greek words were available if sprinkling were intended

The word "baptism" was not translated because translation would have revealed what the early translators knew: the Bible teaches immersion, not sprinkling.

This does not make your faith invalid if you were sprinkled. God looks at the heart, not the method. But if you want to follow the biblical example as closely as possible—experiencing the full symbolism of dying to your old life and rising with Christ—immersion is what Scripture describes.

The question is not what tradition says or what is most convenient. The question is: what does the Word of God actually teach?
