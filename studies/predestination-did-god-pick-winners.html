Predestination: Did God Pick Winners?

How a 16th-Century System Got Imposed on 1st-Century Letters — and What Paul Actually Wrote About Election in the Original Greek

~90 min read • 17 Hebrew & Greek words explored • 60+ cross-references
For those whom he foreknew he also predestined to be conformed to the image of his Son, in order that he might be the firstborn among many brothers. — Romans 8:29

The Question That Splits Christianity in Half

Here’s the question that has fractured the church for 500 years, started wars, ended friendships, and spawned more angry YouTube comments than any other theological debate:

Did God, before the foundation of the world, handpick specific individuals for salvation — and by extension, pass over everyone else to damnation?

That’s the Calvinist position. And roughly half of Protestant Christianity says yes. The other half says absolutely not. Both sides quote the same Bible. Both sides claim the Greek supports them. Both sides think the other side is either ignoring scripture or twisting it.

So who’s right?

Here’s what we’re going to do: we’re going to forget every sermon you’ve ever heard on this topic. We’re going to forget Calvin. We’re going to forget Arminius. We’re going to open the Greek text, trace every word back to its roots, read every passage in its actual context, and let the text tell us what it says.

Some of you aren’t going to like what we find. That’s fine. The text says what it says.

Let’s go.

Part 1: The Greek Words Everyone Fights Over

Before we touch a single passage, we need to understand the actual Greek words at the center of this debate. Because here’s the thing — English translations have already made interpretive decisions for you before you ever read the verse. The Greek hasn’t.

❦ προορίζω (proorizō) — “Predestine”

προορίζω
proorizō
pro-or-ID-zo
G4309
From πρό (pro, “before”) + ὁρίζω (horizō, “to set a boundary, to determine, to mark out”). Literally: “to set boundaries beforehand” or “to determine in advance.” Used 6 times in the NT. The word itself does NOT specify WHAT is being predetermined — whether individuals for salvation or the destination for believers.

Look at that second word: ὁρίζω. We get our English word “horizon” from it. A horizon is a boundary line — the line where earth meets sky. That’s what this word means. It’s about setting boundaries. Marking out limits. Determining the scope of something.

Now here’s what’s critical: the word itself doesn’t tell you WHAT is being predetermined. Is God predetermining who gets saved? Or is He predetermining what happens to those who are saved? That’s the whole debate — and the word alone doesn’t settle it.

Every single use of προορίζω in the NT:

  1. Acts 4:28 — “to do whatever your hand and your plan had predestined (προώρισεν) to take place.” Context: talking about Christ’s crucifixion. God predetermined the PLAN, the event — not which individuals would carry it out.
  2. Romans 8:29 — “those whom he foreknew he also predestined (προώρισεν) to be conformed to the image of his Son.”
  3. Romans 8:30 — “And those whom he predestined (προώρισεν) he also called.”
  4. 1 Corinthians 2:7 — “a secret and hidden wisdom of God, which God decreed (προώρισεν) before the ages for our glory.” God predetermined HIS PLAN of wisdom — not specific recipients.
  5. Ephesians 1:5 — “he predestined (προορίσας) us for adoption as sons.”
  6. Ephesians 1:11 — “having been predestined (προορισθέντες) according to the purpose of him who works all things according to the counsel of his will.”

Pattern to notice: In Acts 4:28 and 1 Corinthians 2:7, the object of predetermination is clearly a PLAN or EVENT — not individual people. The question for Romans 8 and Ephesians 1 is whether Paul shifts to talking about individual selection or continues talking about God’s predetermined plan.

❦ ἐκλέγομαι (eklegomai) — “To Choose / Elect”

ἐκλέγομαι
eklegomai
ek-LEG-oh-my
G1586
Middle voice. From ἐκ (ek, “out of”) + λέγω (legō, “to gather, to pick out”). The middle voice means the subject acts upon or for himself — “choosing for oneself” or “selecting for one’s own purpose.” In the OT (LXX), used overwhelmingly for corporate, missional election: God chose Israel as a nation for a purpose, not individuals for heaven vs. hell.

The first thing you need to know is that it’s in the middle voice in Greek. English doesn’t have a middle voice. We have active (“I chose”) and passive (“I was chosen”). Greek has a third option — the middle voice means the subject acts upon or for himself. It carries the sense of “choosing for oneself” or “selecting for one’s own purpose.”

So when God ἐκλέγεται (eklegetai, middle voice), He’s choosing for His own purpose. The emphasis is on God’s purpose, not on the worthiness or unworthiness of what’s being chosen.

Critical question: When God “chooses,” is He choosing individuals for salvation or a people for a mission?

In the Old Testament (LXX), ἐκλέγομαι is used overwhelmingly for corporate, missional election:

  • God chose (ἐξελέξατο) Israel as a nation — not because they were better, but for a purpose (Deut 7:6-8)
  • God chose (ἐξελέξατο) Jerusalem as the place for His name (1 Kings 11:13)
  • God chose (ἐξελέξατο) the tribe of Judah for kingship (Psalm 78:68)
  • God chose (ἐξελέξατο) David for a role, a task — to shepherd Israel (Psalm 78:70)

In every single OT case, election is about purpose and role, not about individual eternal destiny. God chose Israel to be a light to the nations — not to go to heaven while everyone else goes to hell. In fact, being “chosen” by God in the OT usually meant more responsibility and harder consequences, not a free pass.

You only have I known of all the families of the earth; therefore I will punish you for all your iniquities. — Amos 3:2

Being “chosen” meant being held to a higher standard. Not getting a golden ticket.

❦ ἐκλεκτός (eklektos) — “Chosen / Elect”

ἐκλεκτός
eklektos
ek-lek-TOS
G1588
Adjective from ἐκλέγομαι. “Picked out, chosen, select.” Used 23 times in the NT. Applied to Christ as the Chosen One (Luke 23:35), to believers corporately (1 Peter 2:9), and in the “many called, few chosen” saying (Matt 22:14). The adjective describes STATUS, not the process of selection.

This is the adjective form of ἐκλέγομαι, and it’s the word usually translated “elect” or “chosen ones.” Crucially, Jesus Himself is called ὁ ἐκλεκτός — “the Chosen One” — in Luke 23:35. Christ is THE Elect. Believers share in His election by being ἐν αὐτῷ (“in Him”). The election is primarily Christological — it’s about Christ’s chosen status, which believers participate in through union with Him.

In 1 Peter 2:9, the entire church community is called “a chosen race” (γένος ἐκλεκτόν) — using the same corporate election language that the OT applied to Israel. Peter is saying: the church has inherited Israel’s corporate election status. Same pattern. Same meaning. Still corporate.

❦ πρόγνωσις (prognōsis) — “Foreknowledge”

πρόγνωσις
prognōsis
PROG-no-sis
G4268
From πρό (pro, “before”) + γνῶσις (gnōsis, “knowledge”). Noun appears only twice in the NT. The verb form προγινώσκω (proginōskō, G4267) appears 5 times. Genuinely ambiguous: can mean cognitive foresight (“knowing in advance”) or relational/covenantal choosing (“fore-loving”). Both meanings have textual support.

Here’s where it gets heated: what kind of “knowing” is this?

The Calvinist reading: Foreknowledge means God’s sovereign decision to “know” someone — it’s an active, choosing kind of knowing. “Foreknew” basically equals “foreloved” or “forechose.” They point to the Hebrew יָדַע (yada), which can mean intimate, covenantal knowing (as in “Adam knew Eve” or “You only have I known of all the families of the earth”).

The non-Calvinist reading: Foreknowledge means God’s awareness of future events, including the free responses of humans. God looks down the corridor of time, sees who will respond in faith, and predestines them. Foreknowledge precedes and informs predestination.

The verb form προγινώσκω appears 5 times:

  • Romans 8:29 — “those whom he foreknew (προέγνω) he also predestined”
  • Romans 11:2 — “God has not rejected his people whom he foreknew (προέγνω)” — talking about Israel as a nation
  • Acts 26:5 — “they have known (προγινώσκοντες) me from the beginning” — simple cognitive knowing
  • 1 Peter 1:20 — Christ was foreknown (προεγνωσμένου) before the foundation of the world
  • 2 Peter 3:17 — “you therefore, beloved, knowing this beforehand (προγινώσκοντες)” — simple cognitive knowing

The text is genuinely ambiguous here. Anyone who tells you the Greek “clearly proves” their position on foreknowledge is overstating their case.

❦ βούλομαι (boulomai) vs. θέλω (thelō) — Two Words for “Will”

βούλομαι
boulomai
BOO-lo-my
G1014
Deliberate, planned intention. A sovereign decree. When 2 Peter 3:9 says God is “not willing (βουλόμενος) that any should perish,” it uses βούλομαι — the strong, sovereign intention word. This isn’t God saying “it would be nice.” This is God’s deliberate plan.
θέλω
thelō
THEL-oh
G2309
Desire, wish, want. What someone would like. When 1 Timothy 2:4 says God “desires (θέλει) all people to be saved,” it uses θέλω — God’s genuine desire and wish for all humanity.

This distinction matters enormously and almost no one talks about it. If God sovereignly decrees (βούλομαι) that none should perish AND genuinely desires (θέλω) all to be saved, the Calvinist system has a serious problem. Because in Calvinism, God’s sovereign will is always accomplished. If God’s βούλομαι is that none perish, and God’s sovereign will is irresistible, then universalism — not limited atonement — would be the logical conclusion.

❦ πρόθεσις (prothesis) — “Purpose / Plan”

πρόθεσις
prothesis
PROTH-eh-sis
G4286
From πρό (pro, “before”) + τίθημι (tithēmi, “to place, set”). Literally “a setting forth, a plan laid out in advance.” Used in Romans 8:28 — “called according to his πρόθεσιν (purpose).” Also Ephesians 1:11, 3:11, 2 Timothy 1:9. Describes God’s overarching PLAN — not a list of pre-selected names.

This word is quietly critical. In Romans 8:28, believers are “called according to His πρόθεσιν” — called according to His plan. The plan is what’s predetermined. The plan is that those who are in Christ will be conformed to Christ’s image. The plan has a purpose. The plan has a destination. But a plan for a category of people is not the same thing as a pre-selected roster of individuals.

Part 2: Romans 8:28-30 — The “Golden Chain”

This is ground zero. The passage Calvinists call the “Golden Chain of Salvation” and the passage that has launched a thousand theological systems. Let’s read it in Greek and break it apart link by link.

οἴδαμεν δὲ ὅτι τοῖς ἀγαπῶσιν τὸν θεὸν πάντα συνεργεῖ εἰς ἀγαθόν, τοῖς κατὰ πρόθεσιν κλητοῖς οὖσιν.

“And we know that for those who love God all things work together for good, for those who are called according to his purpose.” — Romans 8:28
ὅτι οὓς προέγνω, καὶ προώρισεν συμμόρφους τῆς εἰκόνος τοῦ υἱοῦ αὐτοῦ, εἰς τὸ εἶναι αὐτὸν πρωτότοκον ἐν πολλοῖς ἀδελφοῖς·

“For those whom he foreknew he also predestined to be conformed to the image of his Son, in order that he might be the firstborn among many brothers.” — Romans 8:29
οὓς δὲ προώρισεν, τούτους καὶ ἐκάλεσεν· καὶ οὓς ἐκάλεσεν, τούτους καὶ ἐδικαίωσεν· οὓς δὲ ἐδικαίωσεν, τούτους καὶ ἐδόξασεν.

“And those whom he predestined he also called, and those whom he called he also justified, and those whom he justified he also glorified.” — Romans 8:30

Let’s break down the key words from this chain that we haven’t covered yet:

συμμόρφους
symmorphous
soom-MOR-foos
G4832
From σύν (syn, “together with”) + μορφή (morphē, “form, essential nature”). “Conformed to, sharing the same form.” Used only twice in the NT: Romans 8:29 and Philippians 3:21. This is the DESTINATION of predestination — being shaped into Christ’s likeness. The predestination is about the goal, not the guest list.
εἰκών
eikōn
ay-KOHN
G1504
“Image, likeness, representation.” We get “icon” from this. In Romans 8:29, believers are predestined to be conformed to the εἰκών of God’s Son. In Colossians 1:15, Christ IS the εἰκών of the invisible God. Believers are being shaped to reflect what Christ already IS. The image is the destination; Christ is the template.
δικαιόω
dikaiōō
dik-ay-OH-oh
G1344
“To declare righteous, to acquit, to vindicate.” In Romans 8:30, “those whom he called he also ἐδικαίωσεν (justified).” Aorist tense — viewed as a completed action from God’s perspective. The forensic declaration of right standing. In the chain, justification follows calling — and calling, as Matthew 22:14 shows, can be refused.
δοξάζω
doxazō
dox-AD-zo
G1392
“To glorify, to honor, to clothe with splendor.” From δόξα (doxa, “glory”). In Romans 8:30, Paul writes ἐδόξασεν (aorist) — “he glorified” — as if it’s already done. But believers obviously haven’t been glorified yet. Paul is speaking from God’s eternal perspective: the plan is so certain it’s described as completed. This is a statement about the certainty of God’s purpose, not a mechanical timeline.

What the Calvinist Sees

A logical chain: Foreknowledge → Predestination → Calling → Justification → Glorification. Each link causes the next. God foreknew (chose) specific individuals, predestined them, called them irresistibly, justified them, and will glorify them. It’s a done deal from eternity past.

What the Text Actually Says

First, the object of predestination: Look carefully at v.29. What are the foreknown ones predestined TO?

συμμόρφους τῆς εἰκόνος τοῦ υἱοῦ αὐτοῦ — “conformed to the image of his Son.”

Read that again. Paul does NOT say “predestined to be saved.” He does NOT say “predestined to believe.” He says predestined to be conformed to the image of Christ.

This is about DESTINATION, not about SELECTION. God’s predetermined plan is that those who are His will ultimately look like Jesus. The predestination is about the goal — Christlikeness — not about the selection process of who gets in.

Think of it like this: A university might predetermine that all enrolled students will graduate with certain competencies. That’s a statement about the destination — what the university has planned for its students. It’s NOT a statement about the admissions process — who gets enrolled.

Second, the verb tenses: In v.30, Paul uses the aorist tense for every verb: ἐκάλεσεν (called), ἐδικαίωσεν (justified), ἐδόξασεν (glorified). The aorist in Greek views an action as a complete whole — a snapshot, not a process. Paul is describing God’s plan as a completed whole from God’s eternal perspective.

This is important because the aorist ἐδόξασεν (glorified) describes glorification as already completed — but obviously, believers haven’t been glorified yet. Paul is speaking from God’s perspective, where the plan is already done. It’s a statement about the certainty of God’s plan, not a mechanical description of how each individual gets processed through a salvation factory.

Third, the context: What is Paul’s argument in Romans 8? Go back to v.28. The whole point is assurance. Paul is telling suffering believers that nothing can separate them from God’s love (he’ll make this explicit in v.35-39). He’s not writing a treatise on individual election. He’s saying: “God’s got a plan, it’s been in motion since before creation, and nothing — not suffering, not persecution, not death — can derail it.”

The passage is pastoral comfort, not systematic theology.

Part 3: Romans 9 — Jacob, Esau, and the Potter

If Romans 8 is ground zero, Romans 9 is the nuclear blast. This is the passage Calvinists point to as the knockout punch. Let’s see if it actually says what they think it says.

The Context Everyone Ignores

Paul isn’t writing Romans 9 in a vacuum. He’s in the middle of a massive argument that runs from Romans 9 through 11. And the question he’s answering is NOT “how does an individual get saved?” The question is:

Has God’s word failed?

μὴ ὅτι ἐκπέπτωκεν ὁ λόγος τοῦ θεοῦ — Romans 9:6

Paul’s Jewish kinsmen have largely rejected Jesus as Messiah. So has God’s promise to Israel failed? Paul’s answer through chapters 9-11 is a resounding NO. And his argument is about how God’s election of Israel has always worked — it’s always been about God’s sovereign choice of who carries His purposes forward, not about ethnic lineage or human works.

Jacob and Esau: Individuals or Nations?

Look at the quote Paul uses: “Jacob I loved, but Esau I hated.” This comes from Malachi 1:2-3. Go read Malachi 1. It’s not about two individual babies. It’s about two NATIONS:

“I have loved you,” says the LORD. But you say, “How have you loved us?” “Is not Esau Jacob’s brother?” declares the LORD. “Yet I have loved Jacob but Esau I have hated. I have laid waste his hill country and left his heritage to jackals of the desert.” — Malachi 1:2-3

Malachi is talking about Israel and Edom — nations. God chose the nation of Israel for a covenantal, missional purpose. He didn’t choose Edom for that purpose. This is vocational election — chosen for a role — not soteriological election — chosen for heaven vs. hell.

And the word “hated” (ἐμίσησα / שָׂנֵאתִי) — in Semitic idiom, “love and hate” often means “prefer and not prefer” or “choose and not choose.” See Luke 14:26 where Jesus says you must “hate” your father and mother — He doesn’t mean literal hatred. He means prioritize differently. It’s a comparative idiom.

The Genesis reference (“the older will serve the younger”) is also about nations:

Two nations (גּוֹיִם, goyim) are in your womb, and two peoples (לְאֻמִּים, le’ummim) from within you shall be divided; the one shall be stronger than the other, the older shall serve the younger. — Genesis 25:23

And here’s the historical kicker: Esau personally never served Jacob. Read the Genesis narrative. When Jacob and Esau finally meet in Genesis 33, it’s Jacob who bows to Esau seven times and calls him “my lord.” The prophecy was fulfilled nationally — Edom eventually became subject to Israel. It was never about the individual men’s personal salvation.

The Potter and the Clay (v.19-23)

The Calvinist reads this as: God makes some people for salvation (honor) and some for damnation (dishonor), and you have no right to complain about it.

But Paul is quoting Jeremiah 18:1-10 — and the Jeremiah passage says something the Calvinist reading doesn’t expect:

If at any time I declare concerning a nation or a kingdom, that I will pluck up and break down and destroy it, and if that nation, concerning which I have spoken, turns from its evil, I will relent of the disaster that I intended to do to it. And if at any time I declare concerning a nation or a kingdom that I will build and plant it, and if it does evil in my sight, not listening to my voice, then I will relent of the good that I had intended to do to it. — Jeremiah 18:7-10

Wait. In Jeremiah’s potter metaphor — the source Paul is drawing from — God explicitly says the clay’s response matters. If a nation repents, God changes His plan. If a nation does evil, God changes His plan. The potter metaphor in its original context is NOT about irresistible, unchangeable decrees. It’s about God’s sovereign freedom to respond to human choices.

Vessels of Wrath — “Prepared” by Whom?

Look at the word κατηρτισμένα (katērtismena) in Romans 9:22. This is a perfect passive participle. The passive voice means the subject is being acted upon. But in Greek, the perfect passive can also function as a middle voice — meaning the vessels have prepared themselves for destruction.

μακροθυμία
makrothymia
mak-roh-thoo-MEE-ah
G3115
From μακρός (makros, “long, far”) + θυμός (thymos, “passion, anger”). Literally: “long-tempered,” the opposite of short-tempered. Patience, longsuffering, endurance. In Romans 9:22, God “endured with much μακροθυμίᾳ (patience)” the vessels of wrath. Why would God patiently endure vessels He Himself created for destruction? Patience implies waiting for a possible change.

And notice: Paul says God “endured with much patience” (ἤνεγκεν ἐν πολλῇ μακροθυμίᾳ) these vessels. Why would God patiently endure vessels He Himself created for destruction? Patience implies waiting for a possible change. You don’t patiently endure something you deliberately manufactured to be exactly what it is.

σκεῦος
skeuos
SKYOO-os
G4632
“Vessel, container, instrument, implement.” In Romans 9:21-23, Paul uses σκεῦος for both vessels of wrath and vessels of mercy. Also used in 2 Timothy 2:20-21, where Paul says anyone who cleanses themselves will become a vessel for honor — implying the vessel’s condition is NOT fixed by divine decree but can change through response.

Now contrast with v.23:

…in order to make known the riches of his glory for vessels of mercy, which he prepared beforehand (προητοίμασεν) for glory… — Romans 9:23

Notice the different word: προητοίμασεν (proētoimasen) — “prepared beforehand.” This is active voice. God actively prepared the vessels of mercy beforehand.

But for the vessels of wrath, Paul used the ambiguous passive/middle κατηρτισμένα — NOT the active voice. If Paul wanted to say God actively prepared the vessels of wrath for destruction, he had the vocabulary to say it clearly. He didn’t.

This grammatical asymmetry is enormously significant and rarely discussed in sermons.

Part 4: Ephesians 1:3-14 — Chosen Before the Foundation

καθὼς ἐξελέξατο ἡμᾶς ἐν αὐτῷ πρὸ καταβολῆς κόσμου εἶναι ἡμᾶς ἁγίους καὶ ἀμώμους κατενώπιον αὐτοῦ ἐν ἀγάπῃ, προορίσας ἡμᾶς εἰς υἱοθεσίαν διὰ Ἰησοῦ Χριστοῦ εἰς αὐτόν

“even as he chose us in him before the foundation of the world, that we should be holy and blameless before him. In love he predestined us for adoption as sons through Jesus Christ” — Ephesians 1:4-5

The Two-Word Phrase That Changes Everything: ἐν αὐτῷ

“He chose us ἐν αὐτῷ (en autō) — in Him.”

The election is IN CHRIST. Not “in a list.” Not “in a decree.” In Him. The location of the choosing is a Person, not a ledger.

This changes the picture dramatically. Before the foundation of the world, God chose that all who are “in Christ” will be holy, blameless, and adopted as sons. The plan is predetermined. The category is predetermined. But Paul doesn’t say individual names were predetermined — he says the destination for everyone in that category was predetermined.

It’s like a company announcing: “All employees hired before January 1st will receive a bonus.” The decision about the benefit was made in advance. The category was established in advance. But individual people still had to become employees. The “predestination” is about what God planned for the “in Christ” group — not about pre-selecting who would be in the group.

υἱοθεσία
huiothesia
hwee-oth-es-EE-ah
G5206
From υἱός (huios, “son”) + θέσις (thesis, “a placing”). Literally: “placing as a son” — adoption. A Roman legal term: the adopted son received the FULL rights and inheritance of a natural-born son. In Ephesians 1:5, believers are predestined εἰς υἱοθεσίαν (“for adoption as sons”) — the predestination defines the DESTINATION (full sonship), not who individually gets adopted. Also Romans 8:15, 8:23, Galatians 4:5.

“Us” — Who is Paul Talking About?

In Ephesians 1, Paul shifts between “we/us” and “you.” Watch it:

  • v.3-12: “we” and “us” — Paul and Jewish believers who first hoped in Christ
  • v.13: “you also” (καὶ ὑμεῖς) — the Gentile Ephesian believers who heard the gospel and believed
  • v.13-14: “you” — sealed with the Holy Spirit

Paul is telling the story of salvation history. “We” (Jewish believers) were part of God’s plan first — Israel’s election. “You also” (Gentiles) were included when you heard and believed. The passage traces the corporate story of how God’s plan unfolded from Israel to the nations.

What Were They Chosen FOR?

Look at v.4: chosen “εἶναι ἡμᾶς ἁγίους καὶ ἀμώμους” — “to be holy and blameless.”

And v.5: predestined “εἰς υἱοθεσίαν” — “for adoption as sons.”

Just like Romans 8:29 (predestined to be conformed to Christ’s image), the predestination in Ephesians is about the DESTINATION — what God has planned for His people. Holiness. Blamelessness. Adoption. Christlikeness.

God predetermined the destination. The text doesn’t say He predetermined which specific individuals would arrive there by overriding their response to the gospel.

Part 5: The Counter-Evidence Calvinism Has to Explain

If God unconditionally elected specific individuals for salvation before they existed, these passages create serious problems:

“Not wanting any to perish” — 2 Peter 3:9

μὴ βουλόμενός τινας ἀπολέσθαι ἀλλὰ πάντας εἰς μετάνοιαν χωρῆσαι

“not wanting (βουλόμενος — sovereign intention) any to perish but all to come to repentance” — 2 Peter 3:9

Remember — βούλομαι is the strong word. God’s deliberate, sovereign intention. If God’s sovereign intention is that NONE perish and ALL come to repentance, then either: (a) God gets what He sovereignly intends and universalism is true, or (b) God’s sovereign intention can be resisted by human choice and irresistible grace falls apart, or (c) “any” doesn’t mean “any” and “all” doesn’t mean “all” — which requires interpretive gymnastics.

“Desires all people to be saved” — 1 Timothy 2:4

ὃς πάντας ἀνθρώπους θέλει σωθῆναι καὶ εἰς ἐπίγνωσιν ἀληθείας ἐλθεῖν

“who desires (θέλει) all people to be saved and to come to the knowledge of the truth” — 1 Timothy 2:4

πάντας ἀνθρώπους — “all humans.” Not “all kinds of humans.” Not “all the elect.” All humans. If you have to redefine “all” to not mean “all,” your system is doing work the text isn’t doing.

“Many are called, few are chosen” — Matthew 22:14

πολλοὶ γάρ εἰσιν κλητοί, ὀλίγοι δὲ ἐκλεκτοί.

“For many are called, but few are chosen.” — Matthew 22:14

This verse is devastating to the Calvinist system. If God’s call is irresistible and those called are identical to those chosen, then “many called but few chosen” is a contradiction. The only way this verse works is if the call CAN be refused — and many refuse it.

“How often I wanted to gather you” — Matthew 23:37

ποσάκις ἠθέλησα ἐπισυναγαγεῖν τὰ τέκνα σου… καὶ οὐκ ἠθελήσατε

“How often I wanted (ēthelēsa) to gather your children… and you were not willing (ouk ēthelēsate)” — Matthew 23:37

Jesus wanted (θέλω). Jerusalem was not willing. Jesus’ desire was frustrated by human unwillingness. If Jesus’ will is identical to the Father’s sovereign will, and that will is irresistible, then this verse makes no sense.

Pharaoh: Who Hardened First?

Calvinists love the Pharaoh passage (Romans 9:17-18). But go back to Exodus and trace the hardening carefully:

  • Exodus 7:13 — Pharaoh’s heart “was hardened” (וַיֶּחֱזַק, wayyeḥezaq — Qal, intransitive — it hardened itself)
  • Exodus 7:22 — Pharaoh’s heart “was hardened” (same form — self-hardening)
  • Exodus 8:15 — “Pharaoh hardened his own heart” (וְהַכְבֵּד, wehakhbed — Hiphil — he made it heavy)
  • Exodus 8:19 — Pharaoh’s heart “was strong” (self-description)
  • Exodus 8:32 — “Pharaoh hardened his own heart” (active)

It’s not until Exodus 9:12 — the SIXTH plague — that the text says “the LORD hardened Pharaoh’s heart” (וַיְחַזֵּק יְהוָה, wayḥazzeq YHWH — Piel — active causation by God).

Pharaoh hardened himself first, repeatedly, through his own choices. God’s hardening came AFTER Pharaoh’s self-hardening. God confirmed Pharaoh in the direction Pharaoh had already chosen. That’s a vastly different picture than “God made Pharaoh evil from the start so He could punish him.”

Part 6: The Corporate Election Reading

There’s a reading of these texts that most American evangelicals have never heard, because they’ve been trained to read the Bible as a book about individual salvation. But the biblical authors — Paul especially — were thinking in categories the modern West doesn’t think in.

Israel Was “Elected” — And Israel Rejected the Messiah

This is the elephant in the room. If election means God chose specific individuals who can never fall away, what do you do with Israel? God chose Israel. God elected Israel. And Israel largely rejected Jesus.

Paul’s entire argument in Romans 9-11 is grappling with this problem. His answer isn’t “well, God chose individual Jews within Israel and those ones believed.” His answer is: God’s election was always about purpose, not guarantee. It was always about “not all who are descended from Israel belong to Israel” (Rom 9:6) — meaning being part of the chosen people was never just about bloodline. It was about faith-response to God’s call.

Corporate Election: The View the Early Church Held

Before Augustine (4th-5th century), the dominant view in the early church was that God chose a PEOPLE — first Israel, then the Church — for a mission. Individuals enter or leave that chosen people through faith or unbelief. The “election” is of the body, and individuals participate in that election by being “in Christ.”

This is exactly what Paul says in Ephesians 1:4 — chosen ἐν αὐτῷ (in Him). Christ is the Elect One. Those who are in Christ share in His election. Those who are not in Christ do not.

This view:

  • Makes sense of “many are called, few are chosen”
  • Makes sense of warnings against falling away (Hebrews 6, Hebrews 10)
  • Makes sense of God desiring all to be saved
  • Makes sense of Israel’s election AND Israel’s rejection
  • Makes sense of the OT pattern where election is always about purpose and mission
  • Was held by most church fathers before Augustine

What About Augustine and Calvin?

Augustine developed his strong predestination theology partly in response to Pelagius, who argued humans could save themselves by willpower alone. Augustine overcorrected. He was also heavily influenced by his Neoplatonic philosophical background — particularly the idea of divine determinism.

Calvin systematized Augustine’s views 1,100 years later. The TULIP framework (Total Depravity, Unconditional Election, Limited Atonement, Irresistible Grace, Perseverance of the Saints) was formalized at the Synod of Dort in 1618-1619 — not by the apostles, not by the early church, but by Dutch Reformed theologians in a political-religious controversy.

This doesn’t make it wrong. But it should make you cautious about treating it as “what the Bible clearly teaches” when it took 1,600 years to be formally articulated.

Does God Choose Who Gets Saved?

This is the central question that has divided Christianity for centuries. Calvinism says yes — God unconditionally elects specific individuals for salvation. Arminianism says no — God’s election is based on His foreknowledge of who will freely believe. Corporate election says the whole question is framed wrong — God elected Christ and a people-group, not individual names on a roster. Let’s lay out each position and let the original languages judge.

Position 1: Calvinist / Reformed — Unconditional Individual Election

The claim: Before creation, God chose specific individuals for salvation, not based on anything in them (including foreseen faith), but solely by His sovereign good pleasure. Those chosen will irresistibly be brought to faith. Those not chosen are “passed over.”

Key verses cited: Romans 9:11-13 (Jacob and Esau), Ephesians 1:4-5 (chosen before the foundation), Romans 9:16 (“not of him who wills”), John 6:44 (“no one can come unless the Father draws him”), John 15:16 (“you did not choose me, but I chose you”).

Greek problems:

  • Romans 9:11-13 is about nations (Malachi 1) and vocational election, not individual salvation
  • Ephesians 1:4 locates election ἐν αὐτῷ (“in Him”) — in Christ, not in a decree
  • John 6:44 uses ἑλκύσῃ (G1670, “draw, attract”) — and John 12:32 says Jesus will draw ALL people to Himself, using the same word
  • John 15:16 is spoken to the Twelve about apostolic appointment, not about salvation of all believers

Position 2: Arminian / Classical — Conditional Election Based on Foreknowledge

The claim: God, in His omniscience, foresaw who would freely respond to the gospel in faith. He “elected” those He foreknew would believe. Election is real, but conditioned on God’s foreknowledge of human faith.

Key verses cited: Romans 8:29 (foreknew THEN predestined), 1 Peter 1:1-2 (“elect according to the foreknowledge of God”), John 3:16 (“whoever believes”).

Greek strengths and weaknesses:

  • The sequence in Romans 8:29 does place foreknowledge BEFORE predestination, which is the natural reading
  • 1 Peter 1:2 explicitly ties election to foreknowledge (κατὰ πρόγνωσιν)
  • Weakness: πρόγνωσις can mean relational “foreknowing” (covenantal), not just cognitive foresight
  • Weakness: Can make God’s election seem reactive rather than purposeful

Position 3: Corporate Election — Christ Is the Elect One

The claim: God elected Christ as the foundation of salvation. He predetermined a plan: all who are “in Christ” will be saved, sanctified, conformed to His image, and glorified. The election is of a corporate body (the people of God), and individuals participate in that election by faith-union with Christ. This was the dominant pre-Augustinian view.

Key evidence:

  • Every OT instance of ἐκλέγομαι is corporate and vocational (Israel, Judah, Jerusalem, David for a role)
  • Christ Himself is called ὁ ἐκλεκτός (“the Chosen One”) in Luke 23:35 and 1 Peter 2:4-6
  • Ephesians 1:4 says “chose us ἐν αὐτῷ (in Him)” — election is located in a Person
  • Romans 9-11 is explicitly about Israel’s corporate election and how Gentiles are grafted in
  • Harmonizes “many called, few chosen” (Matt 22:14) — the call is wide, but few enter the Chosen One
  • Harmonizes warnings against apostasy (Hebrews 6:4-6, 10:26-31, 2 Peter 2:20-22) with genuine assurance
  • Preserves God’s genuine desire for all to be saved (1 Tim 2:4, 2 Pet 3:9)

Where the Greek Points

The corporate election reading has the strongest cumulative case from the original languages because it:

  1. Reads Paul’s election language the same way the OT uses it (corporate, vocational)
  2. Accounts for the ἐν αὐτῷ (“in Him”) of Ephesians 1:4
  3. Explains the Jeremiah 18 source of the potter metaphor (human response matters)
  4. Preserves the grammatical asymmetry of Romans 9:22-23
  5. Doesn’t require redefining “all” and “any” in universal passages
  6. Matches the earliest church fathers’ readings (Irenaeus, Clement of Alexandria, Origen, the Cappadocians, John Chrysostom)

But intellectual honesty demands this: the Calvinist reading has genuine textual touchpoints. Romans 9:16 (“it depends not on human will or exertion, but on God, who has mercy”) is a powerful statement of divine initiative. The question is whether divine initiative requires divine selection of specific individuals. You can affirm God takes all the initiative without concluding He pre-selected a limited list of winners.

If you’ve been taught Calvinism your whole life, this doesn’t mean your faith is invalid. It means the system through which you understood election may need adjusting. The God who loves you hasn’t changed. The Christ who died for you hasn’t changed. What changes is this: the gospel really is for everyone. Not theoretically. Actually.

Part 7: The Honest Assessment

What the Greek Text Clearly Says

  1. God has a predetermined plan for those who are in Christ — they will be conformed to Christ’s image, adopted as sons, made holy and blameless.
  2. God’s election in the OT was consistently corporate and vocational — Israel was chosen for a mission (to bless the nations), not for individual eternal destiny.
  3. Paul’s argument in Romans 9-11 is about Israel’s corporate election and how God’s purposes haven’t failed even though most of Israel rejected Christ.
  4. The grammar of Romans 9:22 distinguishes between vessels of wrath (passive/middle — possibly self-prepared) and vessels of mercy (active — God prepared).
  5. Ephesians 1:4 locates election “in Christ” — making Christ the Elect One and believers participants in His election.
  6. God genuinely desires all people to be saved (1 Tim 2:4) and sovereignly intends that none perish (2 Pet 3:9).

What the Greek Text Does NOT Clearly Say

  1. That God chose specific individuals for salvation before they existed based on nothing in them
  2. That God’s calling is irresistible (Matthew 22:14 seems to directly contradict this)
  3. That God created some people specifically for damnation (the grammar of Romans 9:22 doesn’t require this)
  4. That foreknowledge equals foreordination (the word can mean either cognitive foresight or relational choosing — it’s genuinely ambiguous)

Where the Stronger Evidence Points

The corporate election reading has stronger textual support. Not because it’s more comfortable — but because:

  • It reads Romans 9 in its actual context (the question of Israel’s corporate election)
  • It takes the OT background seriously (every OT instance of election is corporate and vocational)
  • It accounts for the Jeremiah 18 source of the potter metaphor (which explicitly includes human response)
  • It doesn’t require redefining “all” and “any” in the universal-sounding passages
  • It preserves the grammatical asymmetry of Romans 9:22-23
  • It explains the ἐν αὐτῷ (“in Him”) of Ephesians 1:4
  • It was the dominant reading before Augustine

But the debate isn’t closed. The Calvinist reading has genuine textual touchpoints. Romans 9:16 (“it depends not on human will or exertion, but on God, who has mercy”) is a powerful statement of divine initiative. The question is whether divine initiative in salvation requires divine selection of specific individuals. You can affirm that God takes the initiative in salvation — that no one seeks God on their own, that faith itself is enabled by grace — without concluding that God pre-selected a limited list of winners.

Part 8: So What? The Practical Implications

Because if this is just an academic exercise, we’ve wasted everyone’s time.

If God Didn’t “Pick Winners,” What Changes?

1. The gospel is genuinely for everyone.
Not theoretically for everyone but actually only for the elect. When you share the good news with someone, you can honestly say “God wants you. This is for you.” You don’t have to mentally add the asterisk: “unless God didn’t choose you, in which case this is all theater.”

2. Evangelism makes sense again.
If God has already decided who’s in and who’s out, evangelism is reduced to discovering who’s elect — you’re just finding the names already on the list. But if the call is genuine and the response matters, then sharing the gospel is actually doing something that matters. Your neighbor’s eternal trajectory isn’t sealed in a cosmic ledger. Your witness could be the moment everything changes.

3. God’s character looks different.
A God who genuinely desires all people to be saved, who patiently endures vessels of wrath (hoping for change?), who weeps over Jerusalem’s unwillingness — that’s a God whose love is real and whose grief is genuine. A God who predetermined most of humanity for damnation and then says He loves them is a God with a credibility problem.

4. Suffering makes more sense.
If God picked winners, then every person born in a place where they’ll never hear the gospel was created for destruction. Every child who dies without hearing about Jesus was a vessel of wrath from the womb. The corporate election view says God’s plan is for all to be saved, and the tragedy of those who don’t hear is a failure of the church’s mission — not a feature of God’s design.

5. You’re not a robot.
Your faith isn’t a puppet show where God pulls the strings and you feel like you’re choosing. Your response to God is real. Your “yes” to Jesus actually meant something. It wasn’t a cosmic illusion of choice performed by a God who had already decided the outcome.

But Don’t Lose the Good Stuff

The Calvinist tradition gets some things profoundly right:

  • God takes the initiative in salvation. You didn’t find God. God found you. Grace came first.
  • Salvation isn’t about human merit. You can’t earn it. You can’t boast about it. It’s gift.
  • God’s purposes won’t ultimately fail. The plan WILL be accomplished. The Kingdom WILL come.
  • Assurance is real. If you’re in Christ, nothing can separate you from God’s love (Romans 8:38-39 — Paul’s actual conclusion).

You don’t need unconditional individual election to hold onto these truths. God can take initiative, give grace freely, accomplish His purposes, and provide assurance — while still genuinely inviting all people and honoring their response.

What This Means for Your Daily Life

If you’ve been told God picked you before the foundation of the world — that can feel like an incredible privilege. But it can also create a subtle arrogance. “I’m elect. Those people aren’t.” Or worse: a subtle fatalism about people who haven’t believed. “Well, maybe they’re just not chosen.”

The text invites you into a different posture: urgency. God genuinely wants all people saved. The call is real. People can respond. Your love, your witness, your prayer — these aren’t cosmic theater. They’re the means by which God’s genuine desire for all people reaches actual people.

And if you’ve been tormented by the question “Am I really elect? Did God really choose me?” — stop. That question only exists inside a system the text doesn’t clearly teach. The real question is simpler: Are you in Christ? Have you responded to the call? Then you’re in. Not because your name was on a list before the Big Bang — but because you heard the call and answered, and God’s predetermined plan for everyone “in Christ” is now your inheritance.

That’s better news than predestination ever was.

The Bottom Line

God’s plan is real, His sovereignty is sure, and His love is genuine — for all people. Not some. All.

The question was never whether God is sovereign. The question was whether sovereignty means He picks winners and losers — or whether it means He’s powerful enough to genuinely offer salvation to every human being and sovereign enough to honor their response.

The Greek word προορίζω tells us God set the destination in advance: Christlikeness, adoption, glorification. The word ἐκλέγομαι tells us God chose for His purposes — and every OT use is corporate and vocational. The phrase ἐν αὐτῷ tells us election is located in a Person, not a ledger. And βούλομαι in 2 Peter 3:9 tells us God’s sovereign intention is that none perish.

The Greek points to corporate election. And that’s better news.

God’s Sovereignty in Election

  • Romans 8:28-30 — The “Golden Chain” — foreknown, predestined, called, justified, glorified
  • Romans 9:11-13 — Jacob and Esau — election by purpose, not works (about nations, not individuals)
  • Romans 9:16 — “It depends not on human will or exertion, but on God, who has mercy”
  • Romans 9:21-23 — The potter and the clay — vessels of wrath (passive/middle) vs. vessels of mercy (active)
  • Ephesians 1:4-5 — “He chose us IN HIM before the foundation of the world”
  • Ephesians 1:11 — “Predestined according to the purpose (πρόθεσιν) of him who works all things”
  • Acts 4:28 — God predetermined the PLAN of the crucifixion, not which individuals would carry it out
  • 1 Corinthians 2:7 — God predetermined His wisdom before the ages for our glory

Human Response Is Real

  • Matthew 22:14 — “Many are called, but few are chosen” — the call can be refused
  • Matthew 23:37 — “I wanted to gather you… and you were not willing”
  • John 3:16 — “Whoever believes” (πᾶς ὁ πιστεύων) — open, universal invitation
  • John 7:37 — “If anyone thirsts, let him come to me and drink”
  • Romans 10:9-13 — “Everyone who calls on the name of the Lord will be saved”
  • Revelation 22:17 — “Let the one who is thirsty come; let the one who desires take the water of life”
  • Jeremiah 18:7-10 — The potter metaphor’s original context: God responds to human repentance

God’s Universal Offer of Salvation

  • 2 Peter 3:9 — God is not βουλόμενος (sovereignly willing) that any should perish
  • 1 Timothy 2:4 — God θέλει (desires) ALL people to be saved
  • 1 Timothy 2:6 — Christ gave Himself as a ransom for ALL
  • 2 Corinthians 5:19 — God was in Christ reconciling THE WORLD to Himself
  • 1 John 2:2 — He is the propitiation not only for our sins but for the sins of THE WHOLE WORLD
  • Titus 2:11 — “The grace of God has appeared, bringing salvation for ALL people”
  • John 12:32 — “I, when I am lifted up, will draw ALL people to myself”

Corporate Election Pattern

  • Deuteronomy 7:6-8 — God chose Israel as a NATION — corporate, vocational election
  • Amos 3:2 — “You only have I known” = chosen for higher responsibility, not free pass
  • Romans 9:6 — “Not all who are descended from Israel belong to Israel” — corporate identity, not bloodline
  • Romans 11:17-24 — Branches grafted in (by faith) and broken off (by unbelief) — corporate, conditional
  • 1 Peter 2:9 — “A chosen race, a royal priesthood” — OT election language applied corporately to the church
  • Luke 23:35 — Christ is ὁ ἐκλεκτός — THE Chosen One. Believers share in His election.
  • Genesis 25:23 — “Two NATIONS are in your womb” — the Jacob/Esau prophecy was about peoples, not persons
  • 2 Timothy 2:20-21 — Anyone who cleanses themselves becomes a vessel for honor — vessel status is changeable

Additional Predestination Passages

  • Romans 9:18–21 — “He hath mercy on whom he will have mercy, and whom he will he hardeneth (sklērynei)... Hath not the potter (kerameus) power over the clay?” The strongest Calvinist text. But context matters: Paul is answering the question “Is God unjust?” (v.14) and using Pharaoh as his example — a man who hardened himself first (Exodus 8:15, 32) before God confirmed it.
  • John 6:37 — “All that the Father giveth me shall come to me; and him that cometh to me I will in no wise (ou mē) cast out.” Two truths in tension: the Father gives people to Christ (sovereignty), AND anyone who comes will never be rejected (genuine offer). Both are true simultaneously.
  • Acts 13:48 — “As many as were ordained (tetagmenoi) to eternal life believed.” Tassō can mean “appointed” or “disposed/arranged.” Calvinists read “pre-appointed by God.” Others read “those who had arranged themselves toward eternal life” (middle voice reading). The Greek is genuinely ambiguous here.
  • 2 Thessalonians 2:13 — “God hath from the beginning chosen (heilato) you to salvation through sanctification of the Spirit and belief of the truth.” Note: salvation comes THROUGH two channels — the Spirit’s work AND human belief. Both are present. Neither is optional.
