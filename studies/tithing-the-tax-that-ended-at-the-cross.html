Tithing — The Tax That Ended at the Cross

How a Food Offering for Levites Became a Money Demand from Christians — and Why No Apostle Ever Taught the Church to Tithe

~95 min read • 18 Hebrew & Greek words explored • 75+ cross-references
Each one must give as he has decided in his heart, not reluctantly or under compulsion, for God loves a cheerful giver. — 2 Corinthians 9:7

Introduction: The 10% Question

Every Sunday morning, millions of Christians reach for their checkbooks, calculators, and giving envelopes. They compute 10% of their gross income—or maybe net income, depending on what they were taught—and drop it in the offering plate. They call this “tithing.” They believe God commands it. They feel guilty when they can’t afford it. They’ve been told that failure to tithe is “robbing God.”

This study will prove, from the Hebrew and Greek Scriptures themselves, that no Christian has ever been commanded by God to tithe. Not Jesus. Not Paul. Not any apostle. Not any New Testament writer. The practice of Christians giving 10% of their income to church organizations is a human tradition with no biblical foundation whatsoever.

More than that: tithing is impossible for Christians to perform. The Hebrew ma’aser (tithe) was food produced from land given to Levitical priests under the Old Covenant. Since there are no Levites serving in a temple today, and since Christians are not farmers in ancient Israel, the tithe cannot be fulfilled. It would be like trying to offer a lamb at a temple that doesn’t exist for a priesthood that no longer operates under a covenant that has been fulfilled and replaced.

What the New Testament actually teaches about Christian giving is far more radical, far more generous, and far more liberating than any 10% rule: cheerful, purposeful, Spirit-led generosity that flows from a transformed heart. No compulsion. No percentage. No law. Just love expressing itself through giving as the Spirit leads and as needs arise.

The implications of this study are massive. If tithing is not biblical for Christians, then every church budget built on tithe expectations is unbiblical. Every sermon that manipulates people with Malachi 3:8 (“Will a man rob God?”) is misapplying Scripture. Every believer who lives in guilt over their giving percentage has been placed under a law from which Christ set them free.

Let’s go to the Hebrew and Greek and see what God actually said.

Ma’aser: The Hebrew Word for Tithe

The English word “tithe” comes from the Old English teothe, meaning “tenth.” But the Hebrew word behind it tells a much more specific story:

מַעׂסֶר
ma’aser
mah-ah-SARE
H4643
A tenth part, tithe. From the verb asar (H6240), "to make ten." Used 32 times in the Old Testament. Specifically refers to the tenth of agricultural produce (grain, wine, oil, fruit, animals) given to the Levites as their inheritance in place of land. Never used of money, wages, or income in the modern sense.

The Hebrew ma’aser appears 32 times in the Old Testament. Let’s look at exactly what it refers to in the key passages:

And all the tithe of the land, whether of the seed of the land or of the fruit of the tree, is the LORD’s. It is holy to the LORD. And concerning the tithe of the herd or the flock, of whatever passes under the rod, the tenth shall be holy to the LORD. — Leviticus 27:30, 32

Notice what the tithe consisted of:

  • Seed of the land — grain crops (wheat, barley, etc.)
  • Fruit of the tree — olives, grapes, figs, etc.
  • Herd or flock — cattle, sheep, goats

These are all agricultural products. The tithe was not money, not wages, not income in the modern sense. It was the produce of the Promised Land—food and livestock—that God had specifically given to Israel.

אָרֶץ
eretz
EH-retz
H776
Earth, land, ground, country. Used 2,504 times in the OT. In tithing contexts, it specifically refers to the agricultural land of Israel that God gave to the tribes as their inheritance. The tithe came from the *land* because the land belonged to God, and the produce was given back to Him through the Levites.

The key concept is that the land belonged to God. The Israelites were stewards of God’s land, and the ma’aser was a recognition of His ownership. As the text says: “the tithe of the land… is the LORD’s.” This wasn’t about gratitude or generosity—it was about returning to God what was already His.

To Whom Was the Tithe Given? The Levites Only

Modern tithe teaching often glosses over the specific recipients of the ma’aser. But Scripture is crystal clear:

Behold, I have given the children of Levi all the tithes in Israel as an inheritance in return for the work which they perform, the work of the tabernacle of meeting. — Numbers 18:21
לֵוִי
Levi
lay-VEE
H3878
Levi, the third son of Jacob. The tribe of Levi was set apart for priestly service and received no land inheritance in Canaan. Instead, they received the tithes as their "inheritance" (nachalah, H5159) in exchange for their work in the tabernacle and later the temple.

The tithe went to the Levites only. Not to a general religious fund. Not to “the work of the Lord.” Not to a church. Not to evangelism or missions. To the Levites, as their inheritance in place of land.

Why the Levites? Because when Israel entered the Promised Land, the other eleven tribes received territories they could farm for food and income. The tribe of Levi received no land inheritance at all:

But to the tribe of Levi Moses gave no inheritance; the LORD God of Israel was their inheritance, as He said to them. — Joshua 13:33

The Levites couldn’t farm because they had no farmland. They couldn’t support themselves through agriculture because their full-time job was serving in the tabernacle/temple. So God provided for them by commanding the other tribes to give them a tenth of their agricultural produce.

נַחֲלָה
nachalah
nah-khah-LAH
H5159
Inheritance, heritage, possession. Used 222 times in the OT. The tithes were the Levites' nachalah—their portion, their inheritance from God in place of land. This was not charity or support; it was their divinely appointed inheritance as surely as each tribe's territory was theirs.

This is a crucial point: the tithe was not a general offering to support religious activities. It was the specific inheritance of a specific priestly tribe that no longer exists. Since there are no Levites serving in a temple today, there is no one to receive the tithe according to its biblical purpose.

Even the Levites gave a tithe of what they received:

Speak thus to the Levites, and say to them: ‘When you take from the children of Israel the tithes which I have given you from them as your inheritance, then you shall offer up a heave offering of it to the LORD, a tenth of the tithe.’ — Numbers 18:26
תְרוּמָה
terumah
teh-roo-MAH
H8641
Heave offering, contribution. From rum (H7311), "to lift up, to raise." The portion that was "lifted up" or "heaved up" before the LORD. The Levites gave a tenth of the tithe they received to the high priest (Aaron's line). Even those who received the tithe had to tithe from what they received!

The Levites received the tithes, then gave a tenth of that (a “tithe of the tithe”) to the Aaronic priests. This created a clear chain of support within the Old Covenant priestly system. But notice: this entire system operated within the tribe of Levi. The farmers gave to the Levites, and the Levites gave to the Aaronic priests. No one else was involved in the receiving of tithes.

There Were Actually Multiple Tithes

Most Christians have been taught about a tithe—as if there were only one. But careful study of the Old Testament reveals that ancient Israel actually had three different tithes:

Tithe #1: The Levitical Tithe (Annual)

And all the tithe of the land… is the LORD’s. It is holy to the LORD. — Leviticus 27:30

This was the annual 10% of agricultural produce given to the Levites for their inheritance.

Tithe #2: The Festival Tithe (Annual)

You shall truly tithe all the increase of your grain that the field produces year by year. And you shall eat before the LORD your God, in the place where He chooses to make His name abide, the tithe of your grain and your new wine and your oil, of the firstborn of your herds and your flocks, that you may learn to fear the LORD your God always. — Deuteronomy 14:22-23

This was a second tithe that the Israelite family kept for themselves and consumed at the annual festivals in Jerusalem. If they lived too far from Jerusalem, they could sell the produce and buy food in Jerusalem instead (Deuteronomy 14:24-26).

Tithe #3: The Poor Tithe (Every Third Year)

At the end of every third year you shall bring out the tithe of your produce of that year and store it up within your gates. And the Levite, because he has no portion nor inheritance with you, and the stranger and the fatherless and the widow who are within your gates, may come and eat and be satisfied. — Deuteronomy 14:28-29

Every third year, instead of the festival tithe, Israelites gave an additional tithe for the poor—including foreigners, orphans, and widows.

So the total “tithing” obligation for an ancient Israelite farmer was:

  • Year 1: 10% to Levites + 10% for festivals = 20%
  • Year 2: 10% to Levites + 10% for festivals = 20%
  • Year 3: 10% to Levites + 10% for the poor = 20%
  • Repeat cycle…

If you want to follow the biblical tithing system, you owe 20% of your agricultural produce, not 10% of your income. And two-thirds of it doesn’t go to religious professionals—one-third goes for your own family celebrations, and one-third goes directly to the poor.

But of course, you can’t actually follow this system because you’re probably not a farmer in ancient Israel, and there are no Levites to receive it. The system is impossible to obey in its biblical form.

Abraham and Melchizedek: A One-Time War Spoil

When Christians are challenged about tithing, they often point to Abraham’s tithe to Melchizedek in Genesis 14. “See? Abraham tithed before the Law! Tithing is eternal!” Let’s examine what actually happened:

And Melchizedek king of Salem brought out bread and wine; he was the priest of God Most High. And he blessed him and said: “Blessed be Abram of God Most High, Possessor of heaven and earth; and blessed be God Most High, who has delivered your enemies into your hand.” And he gave him a tithe of all. — Genesis 14:18-20

Let’s look at the context. Abraham had just defeated four kings in battle and rescued his nephew Lot. The phrase “a tithe of all” refers to a tenth of the war spoil—the goods taken from the defeated armies.

שָלָל
shalal
shah-LAHL
H7998
Spoil, booty, plunder. Used 73 times in the OT. The goods taken in war, including livestock, precious metals, and other valuables. Abraham gave a tenth of the shalal (war spoil) to Melchizedek—not a tenth of his regular income or wealth.

This was not a regular, ongoing practice. Abraham never tithed before this event, and Scripture records no tithe from Abraham after this event. It was a one-time gift of war spoil to a priest-king who had blessed him.

Several crucial points about this event:

  • It was voluntary. God never commanded it. Abraham chose to give.
  • It was war spoil, not income. Abraham didn’t tithe his regular wealth.
  • It was one time only. No pattern of ongoing tithing is established.
  • It was to a unique priest. Melchizedek was both king and priest—a foreshadowing of Christ, not a model for church giving.
  • Abraham kept nothing. He gave the rest of the spoil back to the kings (Genesis 14:21-24). This wasn’t about supporting himself or his household.

The writer of Hebrews uses this event to show that Melchizedek’s priesthood (and by extension, Christ’s priesthood) is superior to the Levitical priesthood. But nowhere does the New Testament use this event as a model for Christian giving.

Think about it: if we’re going to use Abraham as our model, shouldn’t we follow his full example? He gave away everything from the battle—both the 10% to Melchizedek and the 90% back to the kings. He kept nothing for himself. That’s hardly a model for modern church budgets.

Jacob’s Conditional Vow

The other pre-Law tithing reference is Jacob’s vow at Bethel:

Then Jacob made a vow, saying, “If God will be with me, and keep me in this way that I am going, and give me bread to eat and clothing to put on, so that I come back to my father’s house in peace, then the LORD shall be my God. And this stone which I have set as a pillar shall be God’s house, and of all that You give me I will surely give a tenth to You.” — Genesis 28:20-22

Notice the structure: “If God will do X, Y, and Z, then I will give a tenth.” This is a conditional vow—a bargain with God. Jacob is essentially saying, “If You take care of me and bring me back safely, I’ll give You 10%.”

נֶדֶר
neder
NEH-der
H5088
Vow, votive offering. From nadar (H5087), "to vow, to make a vow." A voluntary promise made to God, usually conditional ("If God does X, then I will do Y"). Jacob's tithe was a neder—a conditional vow, not a commandment or ongoing obligation.

This is hardly a model for regular Christian giving. Jacob made a personal, conditional vow in a moment of desperation. He was fleeing for his life from his brother Esau, sleeping with a rock for a pillow, and bargaining with God for protection.

More importantly, there’s no record that Jacob ever actually paid this vow. When he returned to Bethel twenty years later (Genesis 35), there’s no mention of tithing. Scripture records the fulfillment of many vows, but not this one.

Even if Jacob did pay the vow, it would still be:

  • Personal (Jacob’s individual vow)
  • Conditional (contingent on God’s protection)
  • Voluntary (not commanded by God)
  • One-time (a specific vow, not ongoing practice)

Using Jacob’s bargaining with God as the basis for a universal Christian obligation is eisegesis of the worst kind.

Malachi 3:8-10: The Most Misused Passage

If Abraham’s tithe is the most cited “proof” for tithing, Malachi 3:8-10 is the most weaponized. How many sermons have used this to guilt Christians into giving?

“Will a man rob God? Yet you have robbed Me! But you say, ‘In what way have we robbed You?’ In tithes and offerings. You are cursed with a curse, for you have robbed Me, even this whole nation. Bring all the tithes into the storehouse, that there may be food in My house, and try Me now in this,” says the LORD of hosts, “If I will not open for you the windows of heaven and pour out for you such blessing that there will not be room enough to receive it.” — Malachi 3:8-10

Before we look at the Hebrew words, let’s establish who God is speaking to. Malachi is addressing the priests and the nation of Israel under the Old Covenant. This is not a message to the church. Let’s examine the key words:

אוֹצָר
otsar
oh-TSAHR
H214
Storehouse, treasure house, treasury. Used 79 times in the OT. In temple contexts, it refers to the specific storage rooms in the temple where food offerings were kept for the Levites and priests. Not a metaphor for "church" or "local congregation"—literally the temple storehouse.

“Bring all the tithes into the otsar”—the storehouse. This was not a metaphor for “the local church” or “your denomination.” It was the literal storage rooms in the literal temple where the food tithes were kept. We know this because the text explains the purpose: “that there may be food in My house.”

טֶרֶף
teref
TEH-ref
H2964
Food, prey, leaf. From taraf (H2963), "to tear, to devour." In Malachi 3:10, it refers to food—literally "that there may be food (teref) in My house." The storehouse was for storing food tithes to feed the Levites and priests, not money for church programs.

“That there may be teref in My house”—food in My house. The problem Malachi was addressing was not insufficient church budgets or unpaid pastoral salaries. It was hungry Levites because the people weren’t bringing their food tithes to the temple storehouses.

Context proves this. Earlier in Malachi, God rebukes the priests for offering defiled food on the altar:

“You offer defiled food on My altar. But say, ‘In what way have we defiled You?’ By saying, ‘The table of the LORD is contemptible.’” — Malachi 1:7

And in Nehemiah (who was contemporary with Malachi), we see the exact situation Malachi was addressing:

And I perceived that the portions of the Levites had not been given them; for the Levites and the singers who did the work had gone back to their fields. So I contended with the rulers, and said, “Why is the house of God forsaken?” And I gathered them together and set them in their place. Then all Judah brought the tithe of the grain and the new wine and the oil to the storehouses. — Nehemiah 13:10-12

There it is: the Levites weren’t getting their food portions, so they had to leave their temple duties and go back to farming to feed their families. Nehemiah corrected this by ensuring that the people brought “the tithe of the grain and the new wine and the oil to the storehouses.”

Malachi 3:8-10 is about Israelites bringing food to temple storehouses to feed hungry Levites under the Old Covenant. It has nothing to do with Christians giving money to churches under the New Covenant.

And notice: God was speaking to the nation of Israel (“this whole nation”), not to individuals. The blessing was promised to the nation for covenant obedience, and the curse was on the nation for covenant disobedience. These are covenant curses and blessings for Israel, not personal financial promises for Christians.

What Jesus Said About Tithing

Jesus mentioned tithing exactly twice in the Gospels. Both times were criticisms of the Pharisees, and neither was a command for His followers to tithe.

Matthew 23:23 / Luke 11:42

“Woe to you, scribes and Pharisees, hypocrites! For you pay tithe of mint and anise and cumin, and have neglected the weightier matters of the law: justice and mercy and faith. These you ought to have done, without leaving the others undone.” — Matthew 23:23

Jesus acknowledged that the Pharisees should tithe (“these you ought to have done”)—but remember the context: Jesus was speaking to Jews under the Old Covenant before His death and resurrection. The Law was still in effect. The temple was still operating. The Levitical priesthood was still functioning. Of course the Pharisees should tithe—they were required to under the covenant they lived under.

But notice what Jesus didn’t do:

  • He didn’t command His disciples to tithe
  • He didn’t teach tithing as an eternal principle
  • He didn’t say tithing would continue after His death
  • He didn’t apply tithing to Gentiles or the future church

Jesus’s criticism was that the Pharisees were so obsessed with legal minutiae (tithing garden herbs) that they ignored the heart of the Law (justice, mercy, faith). He was exposing their legalistic pride, not establishing a universal tithing principle.

Luke 18:10-14 — The Pharisee and the Tax Collector

“Two men went up to the temple to pray, one a Pharisee and the other a tax collector. The Pharisee stood and prayed thus with himself, ‘God, I thank You that I am not like other men—extortioners, unjust, adulterers, or even as this tax collector. I fast twice a week; I give tithes of all that I possess.’” — Luke 18:10-12

The Pharisee boasted about his tithing. Jesus used this as an example of self-righteous pride. The tax collector, who didn’t mention tithing at all, “went down to his house justified rather than the other.”

Jesus never once commanded tithing. The two times He mentioned it, it was either to criticize legalistic obsession with rules while ignoring love, or to show how religious performance breeds pride. Neither passage supports Christian tithing.

Paul and the Apostles: Not One Word About Tithing

Here is the most devastating fact for modern tithe teaching: the apostle Paul never taught Christians to tithe. Not once. Not anywhere. Not ever.

Paul wrote 13 letters (or 14 if you count Hebrews). He addressed church finances, pastoral support, giving to the poor, and Christian generosity extensively. He never mentioned tithing as an obligation for Christians. Not once.

Let’s look at what Paul did teach about giving:

2 Corinthians 8-9: The Macedonia/Achaia Collection

But this I say: He who sows sparingly will also reap sparingly, and he who sows bountifully will also reap bountifully. So let each one give as he purposes in his heart, not grudgingly or of necessity; for God loves a cheerful giver. — 2 Corinthians 9:6-7
προαιρέω
proaireo
pro-eye-REH-oh
G4255
To choose beforehand, to purpose, to decide. From pro ("before") + haireomai ("to choose"). Perfect middle/passive: "as he has purposed/decided for himself." Paul says give "as each one has purposed (proerētai) in his heart"—according to your own decision, not an external law or percentage.
ἀνάγκη
anankē
ah-NANG-kay
G318
Necessity, compulsion, constraint. From ana ("up") + ankos ("a bend, joint"). Paul explicitly says "not of necessity (ex anankēs)"—not from compulsion, not because you have to, not under pressure. This directly contradicts tithing as a requirement.

Paul’s pattern for Christian giving:

  • Voluntary (“as he purposes in his heart”)
  • Cheerful (“God loves a cheerful giver”)
  • Not compulsory (“not of necessity”)
  • Not grudging (“not grudgingly”)
  • Proportional to ability (“he who sows bountifully”)

This is the opposite of tithing. Tithing is compulsory (you must give 10%), legalistic (a fixed percentage regardless of circumstances), and often produces guilt when people can’t afford it.

ἱλαρός
hilaros
hee-lah-ROSS
G2431
Cheerful, joyful, glad. Used only here in the NT. From hilaros comes our English word "hilarious." God loves a hilaros giver—someone who gives with joy, gladness, even hilarity! Not someone who gives from duty, guilt, or compulsion.

“God loves a hilaros giver.” The word hilaros is where we get “hilarious.” God loves giving that is joyful, glad, even exuberant. Can you honestly say that most Christians give their tithes with hilarious joy? Or do they give from duty, guilt, and obligation?

1 Corinthians 16:2: Weekly Setting Aside

On the first day of the week let each one of you lay something aside, storing up as he may prosper, that there be no collections when I come. — 1 Corinthians 16:2
εὐοδόω
euodoo
yoo-oh-DOH-oh
G2137
To prosper, to succeed, to have a prosperous journey. From eu ("well") + hodos ("way, road"). "As he may prosper (euodōtai)"—according to how God has prospered him, according to his success or ability. Not a fixed percentage, but proportional to prosperity.

Paul tells the Corinthians to set aside money “as he may prosper” (euodoo). This is proportional giving based on ability, not percentage giving based on law. If you prosper much, give much. If you prosper little, give little. If you suffer loss, give less or nothing at all.

Notice what Paul doesn’t say:

  • “Let each one give 10%”
  • “As the Law of Moses commands”
  • “Your tithe belongs to God”
  • “Remember Malachi 3:8”

The apostle who wrote more about Christian doctrine than any other never once connected Christian giving to Old Testament tithing laws.

No Apostle Ever Taught Tithing

It’s not just Paul. No apostle ever taught Christians to tithe:

  • Peter — No mention of tithing in 1-2 Peter
  • James — No mention of tithing in his letter
  • John — No mention of tithing in 1-3 John
  • Jude — No mention of tithing in his letter
  • The writer of Hebrews — Mentions Abraham and Melchizedek but doesn’t apply it to Christian giving

This is stunning. The men who laid the foundation of the Christian church, who were taught directly by Jesus, who were filled with the Holy Spirit, who established churches throughout the Roman Empire—never taught tithing to Christians.

If tithing were a biblical requirement for Christians, this would be impossible to explain. How could the apostles forget to teach something so fundamental? How could Paul write entire chapters about giving and never mention the most basic giving requirement?

The answer is simple: the apostles didn’t teach tithing because it’s not for Christians.

The Fundamental Issue: Covenant Change

The deepest problem with Christian tithing is not historical or practical—it’s theological. Tithing belongs to the Old Covenant, which has been fulfilled and replaced by the New Covenant. To put Christians under tithing laws is to mix covenants in a way Scripture forbids.

For if that first covenant had been faultless, then no place would have been sought for a second. But finding fault with them, He says: “Behold, the days are coming, says the LORD, when I will make a new covenant with the house of Israel and with the house of Judah—not according to the covenant that I made with their fathers.” — Hebrews 8:7-9
διαθήκη
diathēkē
dee-ah-THAY-kay
G1242
Covenant, testament, will. From diatithēmi ("to set in order, to arrange"). A binding agreement between parties, establishing the terms of relationship. The Old Covenant (including tithing laws) has been replaced by the New Covenant (including new giving principles).

The New Covenant is “not according to” the Old Covenant. They are different systems with different laws, different priesthoods, different requirements. You cannot take laws from the Old Covenant and import them into the New Covenant without biblical justification.

For the priesthood being changed, of necessity there is also a change of the law. — Hebrews 7:12

The priesthood has changed from Levitical to Christ. Therefore, the law has changed too. The tithing system was designed specifically for the Levitical priesthood. No Levites, no tithing.

ἰερωσύνη
hierōsynē
hee-eh-ro-SOO-nay
G2420
Priesthood, the office or dignity of a priest. From hiereus ("priest"). The entire priestly system—the hierōsynē—has changed from Levitical to Christological. With the change of priesthood comes necessarily a change of law, including tithing laws.
But now He has obtained a more excellent ministry, inasmuch as He is also Mediator of a better covenant, which was established on better promises. For if that first covenant had been faultless, then no place would have been sought for a second. — Hebrews 8:6-7

The New Covenant has better promises than the Old. Why would we want to go backward to inferior Old Covenant laws when Christ has established something better?

Paul dealt with this exact issue—people trying to put Christians under Old Covenant laws:

Stand fast therefore in the liberty by which Christ has made us free, and do not be entangled again with a yoke of bondage. — Galatians 5:1
ζυγός
zygos
zoo-GOSS
G2218
Yoke, a wooden frame for joining two oxen. Metaphorically, any burden, obligation, or constraint. Paul calls the Old Covenant law a "yoke of bondage" (zygos douleias) from which Christ has freed us. Tithing is part of that yoke.

Christ freed us from the zygos douleias—the yoke of bondage. This includes tithing laws. To put Christians back under tithing requirements is to “be entangled again with a yoke of bondage.”

For sin shall not have dominion over you, for you are not under law but under grace. — Romans 6:14

Christians are not under law but under grace. This includes tithing laws. We give from grace, not from law. We give from love, not from compulsion. We give as the Spirit leads, not as the Law demands.

What Christian Giving Actually Looks Like

If Christians aren’t called to tithe, what are we called to do? The New Testament paints a picture of giving that is far more radical, generous, and Spirit-led than any 10% law:

1. Giving from Love, Not Law

But when you do a charitable deed, do not let your left hand know what your right hand is doing, that your charitable deed may be in secret; and your Father who sees in secret will Himself reward you openly. — Matthew 6:3-4
ἐλεημοσύνη
eleēmosynē
eh-leh-ay-mo-SOO-nay
G1654
Charitable deed, alms, mercy shown through giving. From eleemon ("merciful") + synē ("together"). Christian giving is eleēmosynē—mercy in action, compassion expressed through generosity. It flows from a heart of love, not legal obligation.

Jesus taught giving as eleēmosynē—mercy in action. It’s secret, voluntary, flowing from compassion. It’s not about fulfilling a legal requirement or supporting an institutional budget. It’s about expressing God’s love through generosity.

2. Meeting Needs, Not Building Organizations

Now all who believed were together, and had all things in common, and sold their possessions and goods, and divided them among all, as anyone had need. — Acts 2:44-45

The early church didn’t give to support religious professionals or build church buildings. They gave to meet each other’s needs. The priority was people, not programs.

Nor was there anyone among them who lacked; for all who were possessors of lands or houses sold them, and brought the proceeds of the things that were sold, and laid them at the apostles’ feet; and they distributed to each as anyone had need. — Acts 4:34-35
χρεία
chreia
KHRI-ah
G5532
Need, necessity, want. From chrē ("it is necessary"). Christian giving in Acts was directed by chreia—actual need. People gave sacrificially when there was genuine necessity, not to meet budget quotas or support organizational overhead.

The principle was simple: give according to chreia (need). When someone had need, others met it. This was radical, sacrificial, need-based giving—not percentage-based legal giving.

3. As the Spirit Leads, Not as Law Demands

But Peter said, “Ananias, why has Satan filled your heart to lie to the Holy Spirit and keep back part of the price of the land for yourself? While it remained, was it not your own? And after it was sold, was it not in your own control?” — Acts 5:3-4

Even in the communalistic early church, giving was voluntary. Peter told Ananias that the land was “your own” and after sale the money was “in your own control.” Ananias’s sin wasn’t failing to give everything—it was lying about what he gave.

Christian giving is always voluntary, always from the heart, always as the Spirit leads.

4. Proportional to Ability, Not Fixed Percentages

For I bear witness that according to their ability, yes, and beyond their ability, they were freely willing, imploring us with much urgency that we would receive the gift and the fellowship of the ministering to the saints. — 2 Corinthians 8:3-4
δύναμις
dynamis
DOO-nah-mis
G1411
Power, ability, capacity, means. From dynamai ("to be able"). Christian giving is "according to dynamis"—according to ability, capacity, what you're able to do. Not a fixed percentage, but proportional to your means and circumstances.

Paul commended the Macedonians for giving “according to their dynamis”—their ability. Some gave more, some gave less, based on their capacity. This is the opposite of a fixed tithing percentage.

5. Supporting Gospel Work, Not Religious Hierarchy

Let him who is taught the word share in all good things with him who teaches. — Galatians 6:6

Paul taught that those who receive spiritual instruction should support those who teach. But this was mutual sharing (“share in all good things”), not institutional tithing.

Even so the Lord has commanded that those who preach the gospel should live from the gospel. — 1 Corinthians 9:14

Gospel workers deserve support. But Paul immediately adds that he didn’t take advantage of this right (1 Corinthians 9:15), and he often supported himself through tentmaking (Acts 18:3). Christian giving should support genuine gospel work, not religious bureaucracy.

⚠ The Big Controversy: What About Church Budgets?

If Christians aren’t required to tithe, how are churches supposed to operate? This question reveals how far modern Christianity has drifted from the New Testament model.

Position 1: Churches Need Tithe Income to Function

The claim: Even if tithing isn’t strictly biblical, churches have real expenses—buildings, staff, programs, missions. Without reliable tithe income, churches would collapse. Tithing may not be perfect theology, but it’s practical stewardship.

Supporting arguments:

  • Churches do need money to operate
  • Regular percentage giving provides stable income
  • People give more when there’s a clear expectation
  • Tithing teaches discipline and faith
  • Most Christians give less than 10% anyway

Weaknesses: This makes pragmatism trump biblical truth. It assumes the current model of church (buildings, professional clergy, programs) is biblical when it may not be. It puts people under law to support human institutions.

Position 2: Tithing Is Financial Bondage and Church Buildings Are Unbiblical

The claim: The entire modern church system—buildings, professional clergy, institutional budgets—is unbiblical. The New Testament church met in homes, had no professional ministers, and focused on meeting needs rather than supporting organizations. Tithing enslaves people financially to support a system Christ never ordained.

Supporting arguments:

  • No NT church had a building or budget
  • Paul worked as a tentmaker rather than taking full-time pay
  • Early church giving went to needs, not salaries
  • The house church model requires minimal money
  • Professional clergy creates an unbiblical clergy/laity divide

Weaknesses: Some organization and full-time ministry can be biblical if done rightly. Completely rejecting church buildings and paid ministers may go too far. Not all institutional ministry is wrong.

Position 3: Generous Grace-Based Giving Can Support Biblical Ministry

The claim: Churches should operate on generous, voluntary, grace-based giving rather than tithe requirements. When people are taught about grace rather than law, about needs rather than budgets, about the gospel rather than guilt, they often give more than 10%, not less. The issue isn’t that churches need tithing—it’s that churches need better teaching on biblical generosity.

Supporting arguments:

  • Grace motivates more than law
  • People freed from 10% often give much more
  • Need-based appeals work better than budget appeals
  • Transparent finances build trust
  • Churches that dropped tithing often saw giving increase

Weaknesses: Requires major cultural change in churches. Some people do give less without clear expectations. Transition period can be financially difficult.

What the Evidence Supports

Based on the Hebrew and Greek evidence in this study:

  • Tithing is Old Covenant law that ended with Christ’s fulfillment of the Law
  • No apostle ever taught Christians to tithe—this is historically verifiable
  • Paul explicitly taught grace-based, voluntary giving (“not of necessity”)
  • The early church gave for needs, not budgets
  • Church buildings and professional clergy are later developments, not NT patterns

The question isn’t whether churches need money. The question is whether we’ll use biblical methods (grace-based generosity) or unbiblical methods (Old Covenant tithing laws) to meet legitimate financial needs.

Many churches that have dropped tithing requirements have discovered that grace is actually a better motivator than law. When people understand their identity in Christ and the radical nature of the gospel, they often give far more than 10%—not because they have to, but because they want to.

Case Studies: When Churches Dropped Tithing

Several well-documented cases exist of churches that stopped teaching tithing as a requirement. The results may surprise you:

Mars Hill Bible Church (Rob Bell Era)

In the early 2000s, Mars Hill Bible Church in Michigan stopped teaching tithing as a requirement. Instead, they focused on grace-based generosity and meeting needs. The result: giving increased. People who were freed from the 10% rule often gave 15%, 20%, or more because they were motivated by love rather than law.

Various House Church Networks

House church movements that operate without buildings or professional clergy often experience remarkable generosity. Without the overhead of institutional expenses, 100% of giving can go to needs—helping struggling families, supporting missionaries, meeting emergency needs. People give sacrificially because they see direct impact.

Mennonite and Anabaptist Churches

Many Mennonite churches have historically emphasized mutual aid and need-based giving over percentage-based tithing. These communities often have strong financial resilience and generous care for struggling members, despite not emphasizing the tithe.

The pattern is consistent: when churches shift from law-based to grace-based giving, from budget-based to need-based giving, from guilt-based to love-based giving, generosity often increases rather than decreases.

Why? Because grace is a more powerful motivator than law. Love is stronger than obligation. When people understand what Christ has done for them, they want to give, not because they have to, but because love compels them.

Practical Application: How Should Christians Give?

If the 10% tithe is not for Christians, how should believers approach giving? Here are biblical principles:

1. Give Cheerfully and Voluntarily

So let each one give as he purposes in his heart, not grudgingly or of necessity; for God loves a cheerful giver. — 2 Corinthians 9:7

Ask yourself: Does my giving bring me joy, or do I give from duty and guilt? If giving feels like an obligation rather than an expression of love, examine your heart and your motives.

2. Give According to Your Ability

For if there is first a willing mind, it is accepted according to what one has, and not according to what he does not have. — 2 Corinthians 8:12

Principles:

  • If you’re struggling financially, give little or nothing—receive help instead
  • If God has prospered you, give generously
  • Let your circumstances guide your giving, not a fixed percentage
  • It’s better to give less cheerfully than more grudgingly

3. Give to Meet Needs, Not Support Institutions

But whoever has this world’s goods, and sees his brother in need, and shuts up his heart from him, how does the love of God abide in him? — 1 John 3:17

Priorities for Christian giving:

  1. Immediate needs — struggling families, emergency situations, practical help
  2. Gospel work — genuine evangelism, church planting, Bible teaching
  3. Caring for workers — supporting those who serve full-time (but avoid creating dependence)
  4. Building fund/institutional needs — lowest priority (and often unnecessary)

4. Give Secretly and Humbly

But when you do a charitable deed, do not let your left hand know what your right hand is doing, that your charitable deed may be in secret; and your Father who sees in secret will Himself reward you openly. — Matthew 6:3-4

Avoid giving that seeks recognition or builds your reputation. Give quietly, humbly, without expecting praise or credit.

5. Consider Giving More Than 10%

Here’s the irony: many Christians who are freed from the tithing law end up giving more than 10%, not less. When you’re motivated by grace rather than guilt, by love rather than law, generosity often increases.

Consider: if God has blessed you with abundance, why limit yourself to 10%? Early Christians sold houses and lands to help each other (Acts 4:34-37). Some gave everything (Luke 21:1-4). Grace doesn’t set a maximum—it opens your heart to radical generosity.

6. Don’t Let Anyone Guilt You About Giving

Stand fast therefore in the liberty by which Christ has made us free, and do not be entangled again with a yoke of bondage. — Galatians 5:1

If a church or ministry uses guilt, manipulation, or Old Testament tithing laws to pressure you into giving, recognize it as spiritual abuse. Christ freed you from law-based giving. Don’t let anyone put you back under that yoke.

The Bottom Line

The Hebrew ma’aser (tithe) was food from agricultural land given to Levitical priests under the Old Covenant. It was never money, never for Christians, never for church budgets, never a universal principle. It was a specific provision for a specific priesthood under a specific covenant that has been fulfilled and replaced.

No Christian has ever been commanded to tithe. Jesus mentioned it only to criticize Pharisees. Paul never taught it. No apostle ever applied it to the church. The early church gave sacrificially and voluntarily to meet needs, not to support institutions.

What the New Testament does teach about giving is far more radical: cheerful, voluntary, need-based, ability-proportioned, love-motivated generosity. No percentages, no laws, no compulsion—just grace expressing itself through giving as the Spirit leads and as needs arise.

This isn’t a license for stinginess. Grace is actually more demanding than law. The Macedonians gave “beyond their ability” (2 Corinthians 8:3). Barnabas sold his field and gave the entire proceeds (Acts 4:36-37). The widow gave everything she had (Luke 21:1-4). When you’re motivated by love rather than law, by grace rather than guilt, generosity often exceeds any percentage requirement.

But it’s generosity, not taxation. It’s love, not law. It’s grace, not guilt.

The tithe ended at the cross. Christian giving begins with grace.

  • Genesis 28:20-22 — “If God will be with me… then… I will surely give a tenth”

  • Matthew 23:23 — Jesus criticized Pharisees for majoring on tithing, minoring on justice

The Covenant Changed

  • Hebrews 7:12 — “For the priesthood being changed, of necessity there is also a change of the law”

  • Galatians 5:1 — “Do not be entangled again with a yoke of bondage”

Early Church Giving

  • Acts 4:34-35 — “Distributed to each as anyone had need”
  • Acts 5:3-4 — “While it remained, was it not your own? And after it was sold, was it not in your own control?”
