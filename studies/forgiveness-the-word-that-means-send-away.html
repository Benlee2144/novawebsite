Forgiveness

The Word That Means “Send Away” — How a Physical Act of Removal Became a Feeling You’re Supposed to Have

~75 min read • 14 Greek & Hebrew words explored • 50+ cross-references
As far as the east is from the west, so far has He removed our transgressions from us. — Psalm 103:12

“I forgive you.” In modern English, these three words describe an emotional achievement. You were angry; now you are not. You were hurt; now you have released the resentment. Forgiveness, as the modern world understands it, is a psychological process—something that happens inside your feelings. Therapists counsel it. Self-help books prescribe it. Preachers demand it. And the measuring stick is always the same: do you still feel angry? If yes, you have not truly forgiven. If no, congratulations—you have performed the moral act of forgiveness.

There is just one problem: that is not what the word means.

Not in Greek. Not in Hebrew. Not in any language the Bible was written in. The biblical concept of forgiveness has almost nothing to do with emotions and almost everything to do with physical removal. The primary Greek word translated “forgive” in the New Testament—αφιημι (aphiēmi)—literally means to send away, to release, to let go, to dismiss. The primary Hebrew concept behind it is a goat being driven into the wilderness carrying the sins of an entire nation on its head—physically removed from the camp, never to return.

Forgiveness in the Bible is not an emotion you achieve. It is a transaction—a sending away, a release from debt, a physical removal of the thing that stood between two parties. It is closer to a legal pardon than a psychological process. And when you understand this, every passage about forgiveness in the Bible snaps into focus with a clarity that the English word completely obscures.

This study is going to take you into the Greek. Into the Hebrew. Into the Day of Atonement. Into the courtroom language of the ancient world. By the end, “forgive us our debts” will never sound like a religious cliché again. It will sound like what it is: a desperate plea for God to physically remove what we owe—to send it away into a wilderness from which it can never return.

The Greek Verb: αφιημι (Aphiēmi)

αφιημι
Aphiēmi
ah-FEE-ay-mee
G863
To send away, to let go, to release, to dismiss, to leave, to abandon, to permit. A compound verb: απο´ (away from) + ιημι (to send, to hurl, to put in motion). Occurs 146 times in the New Testament.

Breaking the Word Apart

Like most significant Greek words, αφιημι is a compound. And like most compounds, its meaning is plainly visible in its components:

απο´
Apo
ah-POH
G575
A preposition meaning: away from, separation, departure. One of the most common Greek prepositions, always carrying the sense of movement away from a point of origin. It is the root of English words like apostle (one sent away), apostasy (standing away from), and apology (a word sent away in defense).
ιημι
Hiēmi
HEE-ay-mee
To send, to put in motion, to hurl, to let fly. An ancient verb describing the act of sending something on its way—releasing it, launching it, letting it go from your hand. Used of arrows being loosed, ships being launched, and prisoners being released. The fundamental idea is something leaving—being set into motion away from where it was.

Put them together: απο´ (away from) + ιημι (to send) = αφιημι — to send away. To release something so it moves away from you. To let it go.

This is not abstract or metaphorical in Greek. It is physical. When a first-century Greek speaker heard αφιημι, they heard the same word used for releasing a prisoner from chains, dismissing a crowd from an assembly, letting an arrow fly from a bowstring, or abandoning a location. The word carries motion—something was here, and now it is being sent somewhere else. Away. Gone.

How αφιημι Is Actually Used in the New Testament

Here is what modern readers miss entirely: αφιημι is used 146 times in the New Testament, and the majority of those uses have nothing to do with forgiveness. The translators chose “forgive” for some occurrences and completely different English words for others—obscuring the fact that it is the same Greek word throughout. Watch:

Translation Greek Word Example
“Leave” αφιημι “They immediately left their nets” (Matthew 4:20)
“Left” αφιημι “He left them and departed” (Matthew 16:4)
“Let” / “Permit” αφιημι “Let the children come to Me” (Mark 10:14)
“Forsake” αφιημι “My God, why have You forsaken Me?” (Matthew 27:46)
“Yield up” αφιημι “He yielded up His spirit” (Matthew 27:50)
“Forgive” αφιημι “Forgive us our debts” (Matthew 6:12)

Every single one of these is αφιημι. The same word. When Peter left his nets, he sent them away from himself. When Jesus left the Pharisees, He released Himself from their presence. When Jesus said “let the children come,” He said “send them toward Me—release them.” When He cried “why have You forsaken Me,” He used the word for sending away—why have You sent Me away? And when He yielded up His spirit, He sent it away from His body.

Now read “forgive us our debts” again. Same word. Same meaning. Send away our debts. Release them. Let them go. Put them in motion away from us so they are no longer here.

Forgiveness is not a feeling. It is a sending. Something was binding you—a debt, an offense, a transgression—and forgiveness is the act of sending it away so it no longer exists between you and the other party. It is gone. Released. In motion away from you, never to return.

The Greek Noun: ἀφεσις (Aphesis)

ἀφεσις
Aphesis
AH-feh-sis
G859
Release, dismissal, sending away, remission, pardon, deliverance. The noun form of αφιημι. Occurs 17 times in the New Testament.

This is the word used in the phrase “remission of sins” throughout the New Testament. And it is the same word the Septuagint uses for the Year of Jubilee—the great release when all debts were cancelled, all slaves were freed, and all land was returned to its original owners (Leviticus 25). When Luke 4:18 records Jesus reading from Isaiah in the synagogue—“He has sent Me to proclaim release to the captives”—the word is ἀφεσις. Sending away. Liberty. The chains are removed. The prisoner walks.

“Remission of sins” is therefore not a vague theological concept. It is the release of sins—the sending away of them, as a prisoner is released from a jail, as a debt is released from a ledger, as a slave is released from a master. The sin is not excused. It is not overlooked. It is not minimized. It is sent away. It no longer has a claim on you. It has been dismissed from the courtroom.

Aphesis in the Septuagint: The Year of Jubilee

The Septuagint—the Greek translation of the Hebrew Bible that Jesus and the apostles used—employs ἀφεσις for a concept so radical that modern economics cannot fathom it:

And you shall consecrate the fiftieth year, and proclaim liberty (ἀφεσις in the LXX) throughout all the land to all its inhabitants. It shall be a Jubilee for you; and each of you shall return to his possession, and each of you shall return to his family. — Leviticus 25:10

Every fifty years, the entire economy reset. Every debt was sent away. Every slave was released. Every foreclosed property was returned. The Hebrew word is דְּרוֹר (d’ror, H1865)—“liberty, free-flowing, release.” The Septuagint translated it as ἀφεσις—the same word used for “forgiveness of sins.”

This means that when a first-century Jewish person heard “ἀφεσις of sins,” they heard Jubilee for the soul. Total economic reset, but applied to your spiritual ledger. Every debt you owed God—sent away. Every obligation that held you in bondage—released. Every claim that sin had on your life—dismissed. You go home. You start over. You are free.

The Other Greek Word: χαριζομαι (Charizomai)

χαριζομαι
Charizomai
kah-RID-zoh-my
G5483
To grant as a favor, to give freely, to bestow graciously, to forgive out of grace. From the root χαρις (charis, G5485)—grace, unmerited favor, gift. Occurs 23 times in the New Testament.

The New Testament has a second word translated “forgive,” and the English reader has no idea it exists. χαριζομαι comes from χαρις (charis)—the word for grace. Where αφιημι describes the mechanics of forgiveness (sending away), χαριζομαι describes the motivation: it is done as an act of pure, unearned generosity.

Paul is the primary user of χαριζομαι, and his usage reveals something extraordinary about how he understood forgiveness:

And be kind to one another, tenderhearted, forgiving (χαριζομενοι) one another, even as God in Christ forgave (εχαρισατο) you. — Ephesians 4:32

Paul does not use αφιημι here. He uses χαριζομαι—“grace one another, just as God graced you.” The forgiveness Paul describes between believers is not merely releasing a debt. It is giving a gift. The offender deserves payment; you give them a gift instead. They owe you; you hand them an inheritance. The debt is not just erased—it is replaced with something beautiful. That is χαριζομαι.

...bearing with one another, and forgiving (χαριζομενοι) one another, if anyone has a complaint against another; even as Christ forgave (εχαρισατο) you, so you also must do. — Colossians 3:13

Again, χαριζομαι. Again, the model is Christ. And the standard is not “stop feeling angry.” The standard is: do what Christ did—absorb the debt and respond with a gift. The forgiveness Christ demonstrated was not an emotional achievement. It was a costly act of grace. He did not merely stop being angry at humanity. He took the debt onto Himself and paid it, then gave us righteousness in exchange. That is what χαριζομαι describes.

The Two Words Together

Word Meaning Focus Used By
αφιημι Send away, release The mechanics—what happens to the debt Jesus, the Gospels, Acts
χαριζομαι Grace freely, give as a gift The motivation—why the debt is released Paul, the Epistles

Jesus, speaking to a primarily Jewish audience steeped in the imagery of the scapegoat and the Day of Atonement, used αφιημι—the word that connects to the physical sending away of sin. Paul, writing to Gentile churches who needed to understand the nature of this God who forgives, used χαριζομαι—the word that reveals forgiveness as an act of extravagant, unearned grace. Both words are translated “forgive” in English. Both words are doing different work. And the reader who only has the English sees none of it.

The Old Testament: The Scapegoat — Where Forgiveness Becomes Visible

If you want to understand what forgiveness looks like, you do not read a theology textbook. You go to Leviticus 16 and watch a goat walk into the desert carrying the sins of an entire nation on its head. Because that is what God designed as the visual definition of forgiveness—and every Greek word in the New Testament points back to this scene.

Aaron shall lay both his hands on the head of the live goat, confess over it all the iniquities of the children of Israel, and all their transgressions, concerning all their sins, putting them on the head of the goat, and shall send it away (šālaĥ) into the wilderness by the hand of a suitable man. The goat shall bear on itself all their iniquities to an uninhabited land; and he shall release (šālaĥ) the goat in the wilderness. — Leviticus 16:21–22

The Day of Atonement (Yom Kippur) involved two goats. One was slaughtered as a sin offering. The other—the live goat, the scapegoat—had a different role. The high priest placed both hands on its head, confessed all the sins of Israel over it, and then sent it away into the wilderness. Not just outside the camp. Into uninhabited land—a place of no return. The Hebrew is אֶרֶץ גְּזֵרָה (eretz g’zerah)—a “cut-off land,” a place of separation, a barren wasteland where nothing lives and no one goes.

This is forgiveness in physical form. The sins are not overlooked. They are not excused. They are not minimized. They are placed on a substitute, and the substitute carries them away—physically, visibly, irreversibly—into a place of total separation. They are sent away. They are gone.

The Hebrew Words in the Scapegoat Scene

שָׁלַח
Šālaĥ
shah-LAKH
H7971
To send, to send away, to let go, to release, to dispatch. The Hebrew equivalent of the Greek αφιημι. Used for sending messengers, sending away a wife in divorce, releasing prisoners, and—critically—sending the scapegoat into the wilderness. Occurs over 800 times in the Hebrew Bible.

The word used for sending the scapegoat into the wilderness is שָׁלַח (šālaĥ)—“to send away.” This is the Hebrew verb the Septuagint translators rendered as αφιημι and αποστελλω throughout the Old Testament. When the New Testament uses αφιημι for “forgive,” the Hebrew ear hears the scapegoat being sent into the desert. The sin is being sent away. It is the same verb, the same action, the same directional movement: away.

נָשָׂא
Nāśā’
nah-SAH
H5375
To lift, to carry, to bear, to take away, to forgive. One of the most important and versatile Hebrew verbs. When used of sin: to carry it away, to bear it on behalf of another. The goat “shall bear (נָשָׂא) upon itself all their iniquities” (Leviticus 16:22). Occurs over 650 times in the Hebrew Bible.

Nāśā’ is the Hebrew word that fuses “forgiveness” with “carrying.” When used of sin, it means to lift it off someone and carry it away. The scapegoat nāśā’—lifts, bears, carries—the iniquities of the people. The sin is transferred from the people to the goat, and the goat carries it away into oblivion.

This is why David writes in Psalm 32:1:

Blessed is he whose transgression is forgiven (נְשׂוּי, n’śúy—carried away), whose sin is covered. — Psalm 32:1

The word translated “forgiven” is nāśā’—“carried away, lifted off.” David is saying: blessed is the person whose transgression has been physically removed from them—picked up, carried off, taken away. Not excused. Not overlooked. Removed.

סָלַח
Sālaĥ
sah-LAKH
H5545
To forgive, to pardon. Used exclusively of God. No human is ever the subject of sālaĥ in the Hebrew Bible. This word describes a kind of forgiveness that only God can perform. Occurs 46 times in the Hebrew Bible.

This is the remarkable word. Sālaĥ is reserved exclusively for God. In all 46 occurrences, God is always the subject. No human ever sālaĥ’s another human. There is a kind of forgiveness that only God can do—and the Hebrew language encodes this distinction directly into the vocabulary.

When Solomon prays at the dedication of the temple, he uses sālaĥ seven times: “then hear in heaven and forgive (סָלַחְתָּ) the sin of Your people” (1 Kings 8:34, 36, 39, 50; 2 Chronicles 6:25, 27, 30, 39). Every time, the forgiveness is something only God can perform. It is divine prerogative. When Jesus says “your sins are forgiven” in the Gospels, the Pharisees react with fury precisely because they understand: He is claiming to do what only God can do.

כָּפַר
Kāphar
kah-FAHR
H3722
To cover, to make atonement, to appease, to make reconciliation. The root of Yom Kippur (“Day of Covering/Atonement”). The idea is not that sin disappears, but that it is covered—hidden from view, rendered invisible before God by the blood of the sacrifice. Occurs 102 times in the Hebrew Bible.

Kāphar adds a third dimension to the Hebrew understanding of forgiveness. While nāśā’ carries sin away and sālaĥ pardons it from above, kāphar covers it. Together, these three words paint a complete picture: sin is covered by blood, carried away by a substitute, and pardoned by God. Three actions, three Hebrew words, one forgiveness. And none of them describe a feeling.

Psalm 103 — The Anatomy of Divine Forgiveness

Psalm 103 is the single most comprehensive passage on forgiveness in the Hebrew Bible. David uses four distinct images to describe what God does with sin—and every one of them is physical, spatial, and directional. Not one of them describes a feeling.

Bless the LORD, O my soul, and forget not all His benefits: who forgives (סָלַח) all your iniquities, who heals all your diseases, who redeems your life from destruction, who crowns you with lovingkindness and tender mercies. — Psalm 103:2–4

First image: sālaĥ—divine pardon. God does what only God can do.

He has not dealt with us according to our sins, nor punished us according to our iniquities. — Psalm 103:10

Second image: the ledger is not balanced. What you owed is not collected. The debt is released.

For as the heavens are high above the earth, so great is His mercy toward those who fear Him. — Psalm 103:11

Third image: vertical distance. The mercy is measured in height—the immeasurable gap between heaven and earth. God’s forgiveness is not proportional to the offense. It is infinitely disproportionate.

As far as the east is from the west, so far has He removed (הִרְחִיק, hirchiq) our transgressions from us. — Psalm 103:12

Fourth image—and the most devastating: horizontal distance. The Hebrew verb is רָחַק (rāĥaq, H7368)—“to be far, to make distant, to remove to a great distance.” God has removed our transgressions from us. Not just covered them. Not just pardoned them. Physically relocated them to the maximum possible distance—“as far as the east is from the west.”

Notice: David does not say “as far as the north is from the south.” North and south have endpoints—the North Pole and the South Pole. If you walk north long enough, you eventually start going south. But east and west have no endpoints. If you walk east, you never start going west. You walk east forever. East from west is an infinite distance. David is saying: God has sent your sins to a place you can never reach by traveling in any direction. They are infinitely removed.

That is what forgiveness is. Not “I’m choosing not to be angry anymore.” Not “I’ve decided to let it go emotionally.” Forgiveness is infinite removal. The sin is sent away to a distance that cannot be measured, to a place from which it cannot return. It is the scapegoat walking into the desert, never to be seen again.

The Lord’s Prayer — What You’re Actually Asking

And forgive (ἀφες, aphes) us our debts (οφειληματα, opheilēmata), as we forgive (αφηκαμεν, aphēkamen) our debtors (οφειλεταις, opheiletais). — Matthew 6:12

Read this in Greek and the Lord’s Prayer transforms:

ἀφες (aphes) is the aorist imperative of αφιημι—“send away!” It is a command in the urgent, one-time-action tense. Not “keep forgiving” (that would be present tense). It is “right now, send them away.”

οφειληματα (opheilēmata, G3783) is not “sins” in the moral sense. It is debts—financial obligations, things owed. Matthew uses the economic metaphor. Luke’s version (Luke 11:4) uses ἁμαρτιας (hamartias)—“sins, failures, misses of the mark.” But Matthew’s word choice reveals the underlying logic: sin is a debt. You owe something. And you are asking God to send that debt away—to release it, dismiss it, let it go.

Then the conditional: “as we have sent away what our debtors owe us.” The word αφηκαμεν (aphēkamen) is the aorist indicative—“as we have sent away.” Past tense. Already done. You are not promising to forgive in the future. You are declaring that you have already released what others owe you. And on that basis, you are asking God to do the same for you.

This is not an emotional transaction. It is a legal and economic one. You are asking the Judge to dismiss the case. You are asking the Banker to cancel the debt. You are asking the King to release the prisoner. And your standing to ask is based on the fact that you have already done the same for those who owed you.

The Terrifying Addendum

For if you forgive (αφη̂τε) men their trespasses, your heavenly Father will also forgive (αφησει) you. But if you do not forgive (αφη̂τε) men their trespasses, neither will your Father forgive (αφησει) your trespasses. — Matthew 6:14–15

Every word here is αφιημι. If you send away what others owe you, God will send away what you owe Him. If you refuse to release them, God will refuse to release you.

This is far more serious than “try to stop being angry.” It is a release or be held proposition. The person who refuses to forgive is like a debtor who has been pardoned by the king but then demands payment from a fellow servant—which is exactly the parable Jesus tells in Matthew 18:21–35.

The Parable of the Unforgiving Servant — Forgiveness as Economics

In Matthew 18:21–35, Jesus tells the most detailed forgiveness parable in the Bible. And every word confirms that forgiveness is a financial transaction, not an emotional state.

A servant owes his king ten thousand talents (μυριων ταλαντων, myriōn talantōn). In modern currency, this is approximately $6–12 billion. The number is intentionally absurd. Jesus is describing an unpayable debt—a sum so staggering that no individual in the ancient world could conceive of repaying it. He would have had to work for over 200,000 years to earn it.

The king, “moved with compassion” (σπλαγχνισθεις, splanchnistheis—a gut-level, visceral compassion), released (απελυσεν, apelysen) him and forgave (αφηκεν, aphēken) him the debt. Two different words, two different actions: the king released the man (let him go free) and sent away the debt (dismissed the obligation). The debt did not shrink. It was not restructured. It was sent away. The entire $12 billion. Gone.

That same servant then finds a fellow servant who owes him one hundred denarii (εκατον δηναρια)—approximately $10,000. A real debt. Not insignificant. But compared to $12 billion? It is one six-hundred-thousandth of what was forgiven. And the forgiven servant grabs his fellow by the throat and demands: “Pay me what you owe!”

The king’s response is rage: “You wicked servant! I forgave (αφηκα, aphēka) you all that debt because you begged me. Should you not also have had compassion on your fellow servant, just as I had pity on you?” (v.32–33). He hands the servant to the torturers until the unpayable debt is paid—which means forever.

The lesson is devastating: you cannot receive a sent-away debt and then refuse to send away the debts owed to you. Forgiveness is not optional for those who have been forgiven. And it is not a feeling. It is an action—the deliberate release of what someone owes you, knowing full well what it costs you to let it go.

“Father, Forgive Them” — What Jesus Actually Said From the Cross

Then Jesus said, “Father, forgive (ἀφες, aphes) them, for they do not know what they do.” — Luke 23:34

ἀφες—the same aorist imperative from the Lord’s Prayer. “Send it away, Father. Right now. Dismiss what they owe. Release them from this debt they are incurring by crucifying the Son of God.”

Jesus is not saying “Father, I’m choosing not to be angry at them.” He is making a legal petition to the Judge of all the earth. He is asking the Father to send away the debt that these men are creating in real time as nails are driven through His hands. He is the High Priest entering the Holy of Holies, placing His hands on the scapegoat, and asking the Father to send the sin into the wilderness of non-existence.

And notice: He gives the reason for the petition: “for they do not know what they do.” This is not ignorance as an excuse. It is ignorance as a mitigating factor in a legal proceeding. Jesus is presenting His case to the Judge: “Father, these men are incurring an infinite debt. They do not understand the magnitude of what they are doing. On the basis of their ignorance, and on the basis of the blood I am shedding right now, send this away.”

This is not emotional. This is covenantal, sacrificial, legal, and priestly. It is the Lamb of God doing what the scapegoat could only foreshadow: bearing the sin of the world and sending it away by His own death.

What Forgiveness Is Not

Modern Christianity has collapsed forgiveness into an emotion, and in doing so has created enormous confusion. Here is what the Bible does not teach about forgiveness:

Forgiveness Is Not Reconciliation

αφιημι (sending away the debt) and καταλλαγη (katallagē, G2643—reconciliation) are different Greek words describing different things. You can forgive someone—release what they owe you—without restoring the relationship. A king can pardon a criminal without inviting him to dinner. A creditor can cancel a debt without entering a business partnership. Forgiveness removes the legal barrier between two parties. Reconciliation rebuilds the relational bridge. The first is a prerequisite for the second, but they are not the same thing.

This matters enormously for victims of abuse, betrayal, and violence. The Bible commands forgiveness—the release of the debt. It does not command you to trust an untrustworthy person, to return to an abusive situation, or to pretend the offense never happened. You can send the debt away without sending yourself back into danger.

Forgiveness Is Not Forgetting

The phrase “forgive and forget” is nowhere in Scripture. When God says “I will remember their sin no more” (Jeremiah 31:34), the Hebrew is לֹא אֶזְכָּר—עוֹד (lo’ ezkār ōd). Zākar (H2142) in Hebrew does not mean “to recall a memory.” It means to act on—to bring something up for action, to call to account. When God says “I will not remember,” He is saying “I will not bring this up for action again. I will not call you to account for it. The case is closed.” It is a judicial statement, not a neurological one. God does not get divine amnesia. He makes a covenant decision not to act on the debt.

Forgiveness Is Not Excusing

To forgive is not to say “it was not that bad” or “you didn’t mean it” or “it doesn’t matter.” The entire sacrificial system exists precisely because sin matters. If sin could be excused, no blood would be necessary. If offenses could simply be shrugged off, there would be no need for a cross. Forgiveness acknowledges the full weight of the debt and then sends it away anyway. It is not minimization. It is costly release.

Forgiveness Is Not a Feeling

Nowhere in Scripture is forgiveness described as an emotional state. It is described as an action: sending away, releasing, carrying off, pardoning, gracing. You may still feel pain after you forgive someone. You may still grieve the loss. You may still carry scars. The feeling is not the measure. The release is the measure. Have you let go of the debt? Have you stopped demanding payment? Have you sent it away? Then you have forgiven—regardless of how you feel.

The Bottom Line

Biblical forgiveness is not a psychological process. It is not an emotional achievement. It is not something that happens in your feelings. It is a deliberate act of release—the sending away of a debt, an offense, a transgression, so that it no longer stands between the offender and the offended.

In the Hebrew world, it looked like a goat walking into the desert carrying the sins of a nation on its head—never to return. In the Greek world, it sounded like a prisoner’s chains being struck off, a slave being set free, a debt being struck from the ledger. In the economy of God, it cost the blood of His Son—the ultimate scapegoat, who bore the sins of the world on His body and carried them away into the grave, from which He rose but the sins did not.

When you pray “forgive us our debts,” you are not asking God to feel better about you. You are asking Him to send away what you owe—to release it into the infinite distance between east and west, where it can never be found again. And when He commands you to forgive others, He is not asking you to manufacture an emotion. He is asking you to do what He did—take the debt, absorb the cost, and send it away.

Forgiveness is not a feeling. It is a sending.

Something bound you. A debt. An offense. A wound.
Forgiveness picks it up, walks it to the edge of the wilderness,
and lets it go.

It does not ask if you feel ready.
It asks if you are willing to release.
