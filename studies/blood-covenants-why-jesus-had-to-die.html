Blood Covenants

Why Jesus HAD To Die — And Why “Just Forgive Us” Was Never an Option

~60 min read • 12 Hebrew & Greek words explored • 40+ cross-references
And He took bread, gave thanks and broke it, and gave it to them, saying, “This is My body which is given for you; do this in remembrance of Me.” Likewise He also took the cup after supper, saying, “This cup is the new covenant in My blood, which is shed for you.” — Luke 22:19-20

Every Christian knows “Jesus shed His blood for us.” It is the centerpiece of every Gospel presentation, every communion service, every hymn about the cross. But almost nobody understands why blood. Why not just a declaration from heaven? Why not simply divine forgiveness spoken into existence by the God who spoke galaxies into being? Why did someone have to bleed? Why did someone have to die?

The standard answer—“because God is holy and sin requires punishment”—is not wrong, but it is incomplete. It treats the cross like a courtroom transaction: crime, penalty, payment. And while that legal framework is part of the picture, it misses the deeper framework that the entire Bible is actually built on. A framework so old that it predates Moses, predates Abraham, predates the written Word itself. A framework that was the bedrock of every ancient Near Eastern culture for thousands of years.

That framework is covenant.

Not contract. Not agreement. Not deal. Covenant—the most binding, most serious, most unbreakable commitment that two parties could ever make. More binding than marriage. More solemn than a king’s oath. More permanent than death. And once you understand what a blood covenant actually was—what it meant, how it worked, what it cost—the cross stops being a confusing act of divine violence and becomes the most logical, most inevitable, most beautiful event in human history.

The entire Bible—from Genesis to Revelation—is a covenant document. The word “testament” in “Old Testament” and “New Testament” is the Latin word testamentum, which the Vulgate used to translate the Greek diatheke (διαθήκη), which itself translates the Hebrew berith (בְּרִית). The book you hold in your hands is not primarily a rule book, a history book, or a theology textbook. It is a covenant document—and every page makes more sense when you read it as one.

The Hebrew Word for Covenant: בְּרִית (Berith)

בְּרִית
Berith
beh-REET
H1285
Covenant, pact, treaty, alliance. A solemn, binding agreement sealed with blood. Occurs 284 times in the Hebrew Bible.

The Root and Its Meaning

The etymology of berith is debated among scholars, but the two strongest proposals both point to the same reality:

Option 1: From the root ברה (barah)—meaning “to eat, to consume food.” This connects covenant to the covenant meal that sealed every agreement. When two parties shared bread and wine together after cutting covenant, they were literally consuming each other’s life. The shared meal was not optional—it was the final seal. This is directly what Jesus is doing at the Last Supper.

Option 2: From an Akkadian root birîtu—meaning “to bind, to fetter.” This connects covenant to the idea of a bond so strong it functions like chains. Once you entered a berith, you were bound—permanently, irreversibly. The Akkadian cognate appears in ancient Mesopotamian treaties from as far back as the third millennium BC.

Both etymologies reveal the same truth: a berith is a binding bond sealed through shared life. It is not a contract you can opt out of. It is not a promise you can take back. It is a permanent fusion of identity, resources, and destiny between two parties—and the penalty for breaking it is death, because the covenant was made in blood, which is life.

How the Bible “Makes” a Covenant

Here is a detail that most English readers miss entirely. In English, we say you “make” a covenant. But the Hebrew does not use the verb asah (עָשָׂה, to make). It uses a completely different verb:

כָּרַת
Karat
kah-RAHT
H3772
To cut, to cut off, to cut down. The standard Hebrew idiom for making a covenant is karat berith (כָּרַת בְּרִית)—literally, “to cut a covenant.”

You don’t “make” a covenant in Hebrew. You cut one. Because every covenant required the cutting of flesh—either animal or human. Blood had to flow. Something had to die. The phrase karat berith appears over 80 times in the Old Testament, and every single time it carries the implication of blood, death, and an irreversible commitment.

This is not metaphor. This is the literal mechanics of how covenants worked in the ancient Near East, documented in Hittite suzerainty treaties, Akkadian vassal agreements, and Egyptian diplomatic records spanning over two thousand years. The Bible did not invent the blood covenant. God entered into a practice that the entire ancient world already understood—and then He elevated it to its ultimate expression on the cross.

Karat Berith (כָּרַת בְּרִית) in Scripture

  • Genesis 15:18 — “On that day the LORD cut a covenant with Abram.” (The Hebrew literally says karat.)
  • Exodus 24:8 — “Moses took the blood, sprinkled it on the people, and said, ‘This is the blood of the covenant which the LORD has cut with you.’”
  • Jeremiah 34:18 — “The men who have transgressed My covenant, who have not kept the words of the covenant which they cut before Me—I will make them like the calf which they cut in two and passed between its parts.” God explicitly references the covenant-cutting ceremony and threatens that those who break it will suffer the fate of the animal.
  • Psalm 50:5 — “Gather My saints together to Me, those who have cut a covenant with Me by sacrifice.”

What a Blood Covenant Actually Was

In the ancient Near East, a blood covenant was not a metaphor, not a handshake, and not an agreement you could dissolve with a lawyer. It was the most binding commitment two parties could make—more serious than marriage, more permanent than death. When two people (or a person and God) entered into a blood covenant, they were saying: “My life is now yours. Your life is now mine. Everything I have belongs to you. Everything you face, I face. And the only way out is death.”

The historian H. Clay Trumbull, in his landmark 1885 study The Blood Covenant: A Primitive Rite and Its Bearings on Scripture, documented that blood covenants existed across virtually every ancient culture—Semitic, African, Asian, Pacific Islander, and Native American. This was not a local custom. It was a universal human understanding of the most sacred bond possible. Trumbull wrote: “The blood covenant is, in its very nature, a covenant of life... the sharing of blood is the sharing of life, and of all that life includes.”

The ceremony had specific, documented steps. Every step has a direct parallel in Scripture:

Step 1: The Cutting of the Animal

Two parties would select an animal—typically a heifer, a ram, or a goat—and cut it in half, laying the two halves opposite each other with a path of blood between them. The Hebrew for this act uses the same verb: karat. The animal represented the covenant makers themselves. Its death was a visual prophecy: this is what happens to the one who breaks this covenant.

The shedding of the animal’s blood was not symbolic. It was substitutionary. The animal died in place of the humans, absorbing the death-penalty that the covenant demanded as its guarantee. Every Israelite who later brought a lamb to the Temple for sacrifice was participating in this same system: an innocent life given so that the covenant-breaker could live.

Step 2: The Walk of Death

Both parties would then walk through the pieces, passing between the severed halves of the animal in a figure-eight pattern (the symbol of infinity in many ancient cultures). This walk was the most solemn part of the entire ceremony. By walking through the blood, each party was declaring:

“May what happened to this animal happen to me if I break this covenant.” — The covenant oath, documented in Hittite, Akkadian, and Hebrew sources

You were pledging your own life. Not your money. Not your property. Not a fine. Your life. The walk through the blood meant: “I am already dead. The only reason I’m still breathing is because this animal died in my place. And if I betray this covenant, my blood will flow like this animal’s blood.”

Jeremiah 34:18-20 explicitly references this practice. When the leaders of Judah broke their covenant to free Hebrew slaves, God said: “I will make them like the calf which they cut in two and passed between its parts—the officials of Judah, the officials of Jerusalem, the eunuchs, the priests, and all the people of the land who passed between the parts of the calf—I will give them into the hand of their enemies.” God is invoking the covenant curse. They walked through. They swore on their lives. They broke it. Now the curse activates.

Step 3: The Exchange of Robes

After the walk of death, the two parties would exchange their outer robes. In the ancient world, your robe was your identity. It bore your family’s colors, your tribal markings, your status. To give someone your robe was to say: “My identity is now yours. When people see you, they see me. You carry my name, my reputation, my standing.”

The Exchange of Robes in Scripture

  • 1 Samuel 18:3-4 — “Jonathan and David made [karat, cut] a covenant... And Jonathan stripped himself of the robe that was on him and gave it to David, with his armor.” Jonathan, the prince, gave David his royal robe and weapons. This was not friendship. It was blood covenant. Jonathan was giving David his identity, his right to the throne, and his military strength.
  • Isaiah 61:10 — “He has clothed me with the garments of salvation, He has covered me with the robe of righteousness.” This is covenant language. God gave you His robe. His righteousness, His identity, His standing before the universe—it covers you. Not because you earned it. Because He exchanged it with you in covenant.
  • Galatians 3:27 — “For as many of you as were baptized into Christ have put on Christ.” The Greek enduo (ἐνδύω) means “to clothe oneself, to put on a garment.” You have put on Christ—His robe, His identity, His name.
  • 2 Corinthians 5:21 — “He made Him who knew no sin to be sin for us, that we might become the righteousness of God in Him.” The ultimate robe exchange. He took your filthy garments (sin). You received His spotless robe (righteousness). Covenant exchange of identity.

Step 4: The Exchange of Weapons

Each party would hand their weapons to the other—their sword, their shield, their belt. This meant: “Your enemies are now my enemies. My strength is now your strength. If anyone attacks you, they attack me.” It was a mutual defense pact sealed in blood.

The Exchange of Weapons in Scripture

  • 1 Samuel 18:4 — “Jonathan... gave it [his robe] to David, with his armor, including his sword and his bow and his belt.” Robe AND weapons. Full covenant exchange.
  • Ephesians 6:10-17 — “Put on the whole armor of God.” Belt of truth, breastplate of righteousness, shield of faith, helmet of salvation, sword of the Spirit. This is not YOUR armor. It is God’s armor given to you. Covenant exchange of weapons. His strength becomes yours. His defensive power covers you. His sword is in your hand.
  • Psalm 18:34-35 — “He teaches my hands to make war... You have also given me the shield of Your salvation.” David understood covenant. God’s shield. God’s training. Given to David through the covenant bond.

Step 5: The Exchange of Names

In a blood covenant, each party took part of the other’s name. Your name in the ancient world was not a label—it was your identity, your authority, your lineage. To take someone’s name meant you carried their authority and they acknowledged you as their own.

The Exchange of Names in Scripture

  • Genesis 17:5 — “No longer shall your name be called Abram, but your name shall be Abraham.” God inserted the letter ה (He) from His own name יהוה (YHWH) into Abram’s name. Abram (אַבְרָם, “exalted father”) became Abraham (אַבְרָהָם, “father of many”). God literally put a piece of His name into Abraham’s. Covenant exchange.
  • Genesis 17:15 — “As for Sarai your wife... her name shall be Sarah.” Same letter (ה, He) added. Sarai became Sarah. The covenant name exchange extended to the entire household.
  • God takes humanity’s name: Jesus called Himself “Son of Man” (υἱὸς τοῦ ἀνθρώπου) over 80 times in the Gospels. God took YOUR title. He called Himself by the human name.
  • Humanity takes God’s name: You are called “Christian”—Christ-one. “Christ” (Χριστός) is the Greek translation of Hebrew Mashiach (מָשִׁיחַ, Messiah, the Anointed One). You carry His name. That is covenant.

Step 6: The Cutting of Hands and Mingling of Blood

Each party would cut their palm or wrist and clasp hands, mingling their blood together. This was the origin of the “blood brother” concept found across virtually every ancient culture. The mingled blood meant: our lives are now one. My blood runs in you; yours runs in me. We are no longer two separate people—we are one covenant unit.

The Covenant Scars in Scripture

  • John 20:27 — “Then He said to Thomas, ‘Reach your finger here, and look at My hands; and reach your hand here, and put it into My side.’” The resurrected Jesus kept His scars. A glorified, resurrection body—capable of walking through walls, appearing and disappearing—and yet the scars remain. Why? Because they are covenant marks. They are the permanent, eternal proof that the covenant was cut and is still in effect. They will be visible forever (Revelation 5:6—the Lamb still appears “as though it had been slain”).
  • Isaiah 49:16 — “See, I have inscribed you on the palms of My hands.” God has your name engraved (the Hebrew chaqaq, חָקַק, means to cut in, to carve, to engrave) on His palms. Your name is cut into God’s hands. That is a covenant scar bearing the name of the covenant partner.
  • Galatians 6:17 — “I bear in my body the marks of the Lord Jesus.” The Greek stigmata (στίγματα) originally referred to marks of ownership burned or cut into the flesh of servants or soldiers. Paul understood covenant marks.

Step 7: The Covenant Meal

Finally, the two parties would share a meal together—bread and wine. The bread represented the body (the physical life), and the wine represented the blood (the life force). By eating the bread and drinking the wine, each party was symbolically taking the other’s life into themselves. “Your body becomes part of my body. Your blood becomes part of my blood. We are one.”

Now read the Last Supper again:

And He took bread, gave thanks and broke it, and gave it to them, saying, “This is My body which is given for you; do this in remembrance of Me.” Likewise He also took the cup after supper, saying, “This cup is the new covenant in My blood, which is shed for you.” — Luke 22:19-20

Jesus is not inventing a religious ritual. He is performing the final step of a blood covenant ceremony. The bread is His body. The wine is His blood. He is inviting His disciples—and through them, all who believe—to internalize His life. To take His body into their body. His blood into their blood. This is the covenant meal, and every time communion is taken, it is not a memorial service. It is a covenant renewal ceremony—a declaration that the bond is still active, still sealed, still in force.

Paul understood this, which is why he warned the Corinthians so severely about taking communion unworthily (1 Corinthians 11:27-30). It was not about personal piety. It was about covenant integrity. To take the covenant meal dishonestly—without recognizing what it was—was to treat the blood of the covenant as common. And in the ancient world, disrespecting a covenant’s blood was the gravest offense possible.

Genesis 15: The Covenant That Changed Everything

Genesis 15 is one of the most important chapters in the entire Bible. It is the moment God bound Himself to humanity through blood. And it contains a detail so shocking, so theologically explosive, that it is astounding how rarely it gets preached. Let us walk through it carefully.

The Setup (Genesis 15:1-6)

God comes to Abraham in a vision and says, “Do not be afraid, Abram. I am your shield, your exceedingly great reward” (v.1). Abraham is old. He has no children. He asks God how any promises can be fulfilled when he has no heir. God takes him outside, shows him the stars, and says, “So shall your descendants be” (v.5).

Then verse 6: “And he believed in the LORD, and He accounted it to him for righteousness.” This is the first time the word “believed” (aman, אָמַן, H539—to trust, to be firm, to be faithful) and the word “righteousness” (tsedaqah, צְדָקָה, H6666) appear together in Scripture. Abraham’s faith—not his works, not his obedience, not his sacrifices—was counted as righteousness. Paul will build the entire theology of justification by faith on this verse (Romans 4:3, Galatians 3:6).

The Ceremony (Genesis 15:9-11)

God tells Abraham to bring a three-year-old heifer, a three-year-old female goat, a three-year-old ram, a turtledove, and a young pigeon. Abraham cuts the animals in half and lays the pieces opposite each other (v.10). He knew exactly what was happening. This was standard covenant procedure. Abraham had likely participated in blood covenants before—they were ubiquitous in Mesopotamian culture. He was preparing for God to cut covenant with him.

Verse 11: “And when the vultures came down on the carcasses, Abram drove them away.” The vultures represent the forces that try to steal, corrupt, and consume the covenant. Abraham guarded it. This detail is not incidental—the enemy always attacks the covenant.

The Shock (Genesis 15:12-17)

Now here is the moment that should stop every reader:

Now when the sun was going down, a deep sleep fell upon Abram; and behold, horror and great darkness fell upon him. — Genesis 15:12

The Hebrew word for this deep sleep is תַּרְדֵּמָה (tardemah, H8639). This is the same word used in Genesis 2:21 when God put Adam into a deep sleep to create Eve. It is not ordinary sleep. It is a God-induced, supernatural unconsciousness. Abraham did not choose to fall asleep. God put him out.

In any normal blood covenant, both parties walk through the pieces together. Both are saying, “Kill me if I break this.” Both are binding their own lives to the agreement. Abraham knew this. He had prepared the animals. He was ready to walk. And then God knocked him unconscious.

And it came to pass, when the sun went down and it was dark, that behold, there appeared a smoking oven and a burning torch that passed between those pieces. — Genesis 15:17

The “smoking oven” (tannur ashan, תַּנּוּר עָשָׁן) and the “burning torch” (lappid esh, לַפִּיד אֵשׁ)—these are theophanies, visible manifestations of God. The smoking oven parallels the pillar of cloud; the burning torch parallels the pillar of fire that will later lead Israel through the wilderness (Exodus 13:21). This is God Himself walking through the pieces.

But notice: the text says the smoking oven and burning torch “passed between those pieces.” Not “one passed one way and one the other.” Many scholars, including the ancient rabbis and the church father Origen, understood that God walked through both directions—once for Himself and once for Abraham. Both directions of the covenant walk. Both sides of the death pledge.

God was declaring:

“If you break this covenant, Abraham—I will pay the price.
And if I break this covenant—I will pay the price.
Either way, the death falls on Me.”

God signed His own death warrant. Thousands of years before Calvary, in a field in Canaan, with the blood of animals on the ground and Abraham unconscious beside them, God voluntarily bound Himself to die for a covenant He knew humanity would break.

Abraham was asleep. He contributed nothing. He walked nowhere. He pledged nothing. God did it alone. Both sides. That is the gospel—told in Genesis 15, millennia before a manger in Bethlehem or a cross on Golgotha.

The Genesis 15 Pattern Fulfilled

  • Hebrews 6:13-18 — “For when God made a promise to Abraham, because He could swear by no one greater, He swore by Himself... that by two immutable things, in which it is impossible for God to lie, we might have strong consolation.” The two immutable things are God’s word and God’s oath. He swore by Himself because there is no higher authority. His own nature is the guarantee.
  • Hebrews 9:15-17 — “For where there is a testament [diatheke, covenant], there must also of necessity be the death of the testator. For a testament is in force after men are dead.” The covenant required the death of the one who made it. God made it. God died. Covenant activated.

Why Blood Specifically: דָּם (Dam)

דָּם
Dam
dahm
H1818
Blood, lifeblood. Occurs 360 times in the Hebrew Bible. The vehicle of life itself.

The theological key to everything is in Leviticus 17:11:

For the life of the flesh is in the blood, and I have given it to you upon the altar to make atonement for your souls; for it is the blood that makes atonement for the soul. — Leviticus 17:11

Three profound declarations in one verse:

1. “The life of the flesh is in the blood.” The Hebrew word for “life” here is נֶפֶשׁ (nephesh, H5315)—which means soul, life force, the animating essence of a being. The blood does not merely represent life. It carries life. It is the vehicle of the nephesh. Modern science confirms what the ancients understood intuitively: when the blood stops flowing, the life ends. Blood carries oxygen to every cell, removes waste, fights disease, heals wounds. It is, in the most literal sense, the river of life in the body.

2. “I have given it to you upon the altar.” God gave the blood. It is a gift. The sacrificial system was not humanity’s idea—it was God’s provision. He didn’t demand blood because He is bloodthirsty. He provided blood because He knew the covenant would be broken, and blood—life—was the only currency that could cover the debt.

3. “It is the blood that makes atonement for the soul.” The word “atonement” is כָּפַר (kaphar, H3722)—and it does not mean “to pay a penalty.” It means “to cover.” Its root meaning is to smear, to coat, to cover over. It is the same root used for the pitch that covered Noah’s ark (Genesis 6:14—kaphar the ark with kopher). The blood covers the covenant-breaker. It hides them from the death that the broken covenant demands. It doesn’t pretend the breach didn’t happen—it stands between the breach and the consequences.

כָּפַר
Kaphar
kah-FAHR
H3722
To cover, to make atonement, to smear, to coat, to appease. Root of Yom Kippur (יוֹם כִּפּוּר)—the Day of Covering.

This is why Hebrews 9:22 says: “Without the shedding of blood there is no remission.” The Greek word for “remission” is ἄφεσις (aphesis, G859)—meaning release, forgiveness, letting go. Without blood (life given), there is no release from the covenant curse. Not because God is cruel, but because the covenant He Himself established operates on blood. He set the terms. And He honored them.

Why “Just Forgive Us” Was Never an Option

This is the question that trips up more people than almost any other: “If God is all-powerful, why couldn’t He just forgive sin without all the blood and violence? Why the cross? Why not just say ‘I forgive you’ and be done with it?”

The answer is not that God couldn’t in terms of raw power. The answer is that He wouldn’t—because doing so would make Him something He is not: a covenant-breaker.

God voluntarily bound Himself to a blood covenant in Genesis 15. He didn’t have to. No one forced Him. He chose to. And once a blood covenant is cut, it cannot be dissolved by declaration. Not even by God. Not because He lacks the power, but because His nature is the guarantee. He swore by Himself (Hebrews 6:13). To break His own covenant would be to violate His own nature—and God cannot deny Himself (2 Timothy 2:13).

Consider it this way: If a judge’s own child commits a crime, the judge can forgive the child personally as a father. But he cannot, as a judge, simply dismiss the case and pretend it didn’t happen—not without undermining the entire legal system he swore to uphold. His personal love and his official integrity are both real, and they must both be satisfied. The cross is where God’s love and God’s integrity meet. He didn’t choose between forgiving us and keeping His word. He found a way to do both—by keeping the covenant terms Himself.

Malachi 3:6 — “For I am the LORD, I do not change.” The Hebrew is lo shaniti (לֹא שָׁנִיתִי)—“I have not changed.” His character, His promises, His covenant terms—they do not shift. He locked Himself in. And He honored every letter of what He locked Himself into.

That is not cruelty. That is integrity at a level human beings can barely comprehend. The cross is not the act of an angry God punishing an innocent victim. It is the act of a covenant-keeping God fulfilling the death pledge He made to Himself on behalf of the covenant partner (humanity) who broke it.

The Two Great Covenants

The Old Covenant (Sinai) — Conditional

At Sinai, God made a covenant with the nation of Israel. Unlike Genesis 15, this one was bilateral—both parties had obligations:

Now therefore, if you will indeed obey My voice and keep My covenant, then you shall be a special treasure to Me above all people. — Exodus 19:5

“If... then.” Conditional. Both sides walked through, so to speak. God promised blessing, protection, and presence. Israel promised obedience. And it was sealed with blood:

And Moses took the blood, sprinkled it on the people, and said, “This is the blood of the covenant which the LORD has made with you according to all these words.” — Exodus 24:8

Israel broke it. Constantly. The golden calf incident happened while Moses was still on the mountain receiving the covenant terms. The entire history of Israel is the story of a covenant partner who cannot keep their end of the deal. The sacrificial system—the lambs, the goats, the bulls, the Day of Atonement (Yom Kippur)—was a temporary covering. The blood of animals could kaphar (cover) sin, but it could not remove it (Hebrews 10:4). It was a placeholder—a preview of the real sacrifice to come.

The New Covenant (The Cross) — Unconditional

The New Covenant is not a modification of the Old. It is a return to the Genesis 15 pattern. God makes it alone. Both sides. Humanity is asleep.

Behold, the days are coming, says the LORD, when I will make a new covenant with the house of Israel and with the house of Judah—not according to the covenant that I made with their fathers in the day that I took them by the hand to lead them out of the land of Egypt, My covenant which they broke. — Jeremiah 31:31-32

Notice: God says, “My covenant which they broke.” He doesn’t say He broke it. They did. And the consequence of the broken covenant—the death pledge—should fall on them. But Genesis 15 already determined who would pay: God Himself.

Jesus (God in flesh) walked through the covenant alone. He fulfilled both sides. He kept the law perfectly (humanity’s obligation) and He paid the death price (the covenant curse for breaking it). Your performance is not part of this equation. That is why it is called grace. You didn’t cut this covenant. He did. Both sides. It is between the Father and the Son, and you are the beneficiary.

The New Covenant Promises

  • Jeremiah 31:33 — “I will put My law in their minds, and write it on their hearts; and I will be their God, and they shall be My people.”
  • Jeremiah 31:34 — “For I will forgive their iniquity, and their sin I will remember no more.”
  • Hebrews 8:6 — “But now He has obtained a more excellent ministry, inasmuch as He is also Mediator of a better covenant, which was established on better promises.”
  • Hebrews 10:14 — “For by one offering He has perfected forever those who are being sanctified.” One offering. Forever. Perfected. Done.
  • Hebrews 10:17-18 — “Their sins and their lawless deeds I will remember no more. Now where there is remission of these, there is no longer an offering for sin.” If God remembers no more, and no more offering is needed, then the covenant is fully, finally, permanently satisfied.

What This Changes About How You Live

If your salvation depends on a covenant between the Father and the Son—a covenant you didn’t make and you can’t break—then your security is not based on your performance. It is based on one question: Will God break His covenant with Himself?

The answer is no. He cannot. It is not in His nature. “If we are faithless, He remains faithful; He cannot deny Himself” (2 Timothy 2:13). Your faithfulness is not what holds the covenant together. His faithfulness is.

Every moment of guilt. Every “am I really saved?” Every fear that you’ve gone too far, sinned too many times, fallen too hard. Those are all based on one lie: that the covenant depends on you. It doesn’t. You are not a party to it. You are the inheritance. You are what the covenant was about, not what the covenant depends on.

Abraham was asleep. He contributed nothing. God did it alone. That is the gospel, and it was told in Genesis 15 before a single word of the New Testament was ever written.

For when God made a promise to Abraham, because He could swear by no one greater, He swore by Himself, saying, “Surely blessing I will bless you, and multiplying I will multiply you.” — Hebrews 6:13-14

He swore by Himself. There is no higher court of appeals. No one above Him to hold Him accountable. His own nature—unchanging, unbreakable, incapable of lying—is the guarantee. And that nature does not change (Malachi 3:6, Hebrews 13:8, James 1:17).

That is why the cross was not optional. Not because God is bloodthirsty. Not because He enjoys suffering. But because He is a covenant keeper—the only one who has ever existed—and He keeps His word at the cost of His own life.
