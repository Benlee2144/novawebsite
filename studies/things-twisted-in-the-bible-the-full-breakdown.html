Things Twisted In The Bible — The Full Breakdown

13 Doctrines Traced Back to the Original Text — Before Translation and Tradition Changed Everything

~120 min read • 25 Hebrew & Greek words explored • 90+ cross-references
Making the word of God of no effect through your tradition which you have handed down. And many such things you do. — Mark 7:13

The Bible has not changed. The Hebrew manuscripts in the Dead Sea Scrolls match the Masoretic text we use today with stunning accuracy across a thousand years of copying. The Greek New Testament manuscripts—over 5,800 of them—give us a text that textual scholars consider remarkably stable. The problem was never the text. The problem is what happened between the text and your English Bible.

Translation is interpretation. Every time a translator chooses one English word for a Hebrew or Greek term, they are making a theological decision. And when the translators work for a king who gives them explicit rules about which words to use—as happened with the King James Version in 1611—those decisions become political. When the resulting translation goes unchallenged for four centuries, those political choices start to feel like Scripture itself.

This study traces thirteen doctrines back to the original languages. Not to attack anyone’s faith—but to hand it back to them with the packaging removed. Every single claim in this study can be verified with a Strong’s Concordance, an interlinear Bible, and a basic understanding of how words work. We are not introducing new ideas. We are stripping away the additions to reveal what was always there.

The pattern is always the same: the original text says one thing, a translation choice or theological tradition changes the meaning, centuries of repetition make the new meaning feel like it was always there, and anyone who questions it gets called a heretic.

Let’s break the cycle. Here are thirteen things that got twisted.

1. “Hell” — Three Different Words, One Bad Translation

Open any English Bible and search for “hell.” You will find it dozens of times. You will assume it means one place—a fiery underground torture chamber where the wicked go forever. But the English word “hell” is doing the work of three completely different words in the original languages, and none of them mean what you were taught.

The King James translators took three distinct Hebrew and Greek concepts—each with their own meaning, context, and theological weight—and collapsed them all into a single English word. That is like translating “house,” “apartment,” and “tent” all as “prison.”

❧ The First Word: Sheol

שְׁאוֹל
sh’owl
sheh-OLE
H7585
Sheol — The grave, the unseen realm, the underworld. The abode of all the dead—righteous and wicked alike. Not a place of punishment. Not a place of reward. Simply the realm of the dead.

Sheol appears 65 times in the Hebrew Old Testament. It is where everyone went when they died. Jacob said he would go down to Sheol in mourning for Joseph (Genesis 37:35). Job asked God to hide him in Sheol as a place of refuge (Job 14:13). David said God would not abandon his soul to Sheol (Psalm 16:10). The righteous went there. The wicked went there. It was not punishment—it was simply death.

The root of the word is debated but likely connected to sha’al (H7592), “to ask, to inquire”—possibly a reference to necromancy, the practice of consulting the dead. The place that “demands” or “asks for” all living things. It is depicted as below, as dark, as silent. But never as fire. Never as torture.

❧ The Second Word: Hadēs

ἅδης
hadēs
HAH-days
G86
Hadēs — From a (negative particle) + eidō (“to see”). Literally: “the unseen place.” The Greek equivalent of Sheol—the realm of the departed, not a place of fiery torment.

When the Hebrew Bible was translated into Greek (the Septuagint, ~250 BC), the translators chose hadēs for sheol almost every time. The words are functional equivalents. Hadēs in Greek culture was the unseen realm where all the dead resided—it was the name of both the place and the god who ruled it. But the New Testament writers were using the Jewish meaning (equivalent to Sheol), not the full Greek mythology.

When Jesus said “the gates of Hadēs will not prevail against my church” (Matthew 16:18), He was not describing a fiery torture chamber. He was saying death itself would not stop what He was building. The “gates” of Hadēs are the gates of death—and they cannot hold back the ekklēsia.

❧ The Third Word: Geenna

γέεννα
geenna
GHEH-en-nah
G1067
Geenna — From Hebrew gē-hinnom (H1516 + H2011), “Valley of Hinnom.” A literal valley south of Jerusalem. The city dump where trash burned constantly, and historically the site of pagan child sacrifice (2 Kings 23:10). Jesus used it as vivid imagery for destruction and waste.

This is the word Jesus actually used when warning people about judgment. And it was a real, physical place His audience could see from the city walls. The Valley of Hinnom (ge-Hinnom, Gehenna) ran along the south side of Jerusalem. In Israel’s darkest periods, it was where kings Ahaz and Manasseh burned children as offerings to Molech (2 Chronicles 28:3; 2 Chronicles 33:6). King Josiah desecrated it to stop the practice (2 Kings 23:10). By Jesus’s day, it had become the city’s garbage dump—a place where refuse smoldered continually.

When Jesus said “it is better for you to enter life maimed than with two hands to go into Gehenna” (Mark 9:43), His listeners knew exactly what He meant. He was using the most vivid image available—the smoking garbage dump outside town where worthless things were thrown away. It was a metaphor for destruction and waste, not a description of an eternal torture dimension.

The KJV translators wrote “hell” for all three of these words. Sheol (the grave), Hadēs (the unseen realm), and Gehenna (Jerusalem’s trash dump) all became the same English word. Three completely different concepts—collapsed into one terrifying image that none of them originally conveyed.

And the image of eternal conscious torment in fire? That owes more to Dante Alighieri’s Inferno (1320 AD) and medieval Catholic art than to anything in the Hebrew or Greek text. The early church held three views of the afterlife for the wicked—eternal torment was only one of them, and for the first five centuries, it was not even the dominant one. Origen of Alexandria (185–254 AD), Gregory of Nyssa (335–395 AD), and Clement of Alexandria (150–215 AD) all taught apokatastasis—the eventual restoration of all things. They read the Greek. They spoke it natively. And they did not see “eternal hell” in the text.

❧ Key References: The Three “Hells”

  • Genesis 37:35 — Jacob expects to go to Sheol—the righteous patriarch, not a sinner
  • Job 14:13 — Job asks God to hide him in Sheol—as refuge, not punishment
  • Psalm 16:10 — God will not abandon David’s soul to Sheol
  • Matthew 16:18 — “The gates of Hadēs”—death’s power, not a torture chamber
  • Mark 9:43 — Jesus uses Gehenna—the burning garbage dump outside Jerusalem
  • 2 Kings 23:10 — Josiah desecrates the Valley of Hinnom to stop child sacrifice
  • 2 Chronicles 28:3 — King Ahaz burns his children in ge-Hinnom

2. “Eternal” — The Word That Doesn’t Mean What You Think

The entire doctrine of eternal conscious torment hangs on one Greek word being translated as “forever.” If that word doesn’t mean “forever,” the doctrine collapses. So let’s look at the word.

αἰώνιος
aiōnios
ah-ee-OH-nee-os
G166
Aiōnios — Adjective derived from aiōn (G165), meaning “of the age, age-lasting, pertaining to an age.” Translated “eternal” and “everlasting” in most English Bibles, but the root word means “an age”—a period of time with a beginning and an end.
αἰών
aiōn
ah-ee-OWN
G165
Aiōn — An age, a period of time, an era. From a root meaning “continued duration.” Used of past ages, the present age, and ages to come. The word itself does not inherently mean “infinite”—it means “an era with a particular character or quality.”

Aiōnios is the adjective form of aiōn. An aiōn is an age—a span of time. Aiōnios means “pertaining to an age” or “age-lasting.” The HELPS Word-studies entry for G166 is remarkably honest: it defines aiōnios as “age-like, i.e. an age-characteristic”—describing the quality of an age, not necessarily infinite duration.

In Matthew 25:46, Jesus says: “These will go away into aiōnios punishment, but the righteous into aiōnios life.” The standard translation reads “eternal punishment” and “eternal life.” But the Greek says kolasin aiōnion—“pruning/correction of the age”—and zōēn aiōnion—“life of the age.”

Notice: the same word aiōnios modifies both the punishment and the life. If aiōnios means “infinite” when applied to punishment, it must mean “infinite” when applied to life—and vice versa. But here is the key: aiōnios life is defined by Jesus Himself not as “living forever” but as “knowing God” (John 17:3). It describes a quality of life, not merely a duration.

The same word appears in Romans 16:25—“the mystery kept secret since aiōnios times”—where it clearly means “for a long time” or “through the ages,” not “literally infinite.” The mystery was revealed, so the “aiōnios” period ended.

The word for truly endless or indestructible in Greek is aidios (G126), from aei (“always”). Paul uses aidios in Romans 1:20 for God’s “eternal power.” If the New Testament authors wanted to say “truly never-ending,” they had a word for it. For punishment, they chose aiōnios—the age-word—instead.

The early church father Origen of Alexandria (185–254 AD) taught universal reconciliation (apokatastasis) based precisely on this reading of aiōnios. He was one of the greatest scholars of the early church, a native Greek speaker who compiled the Hexapla—a six-column parallel of the Old Testament in Hebrew, Greek transliteration, the Septuagint, and three other Greek versions. He was not considered a heretic for this view during his lifetime. The condemnation came centuries later, at the Fifth Ecumenical Council (553 AD), and even that condemnation is historically disputed.

❧ Key References: Aiōnios

  • Matthew 25:46 — Aiōnios punishment and aiōnios life—same word for both
  • John 17:3 — Jesus defines aiōnios life as knowing God—quality, not just duration
  • Romans 16:25 — Aiōnios used for a period that clearly ended
  • Romans 1:20 — Paul uses aidios (G126) when he means truly eternal
  • Jude 1:7 — Sodom suffers “aiōnios fire”—but that fire is not still burning today

3. “Church” — The Word Jesus Never Said

Jesus never said “church.” Not once. The word He used was ekklēsia—and it had nothing to do with a building, a denomination, a Sunday morning service, or a 501(c)(3) tax-exempt organization. It was a political word borrowed from Greek civic life, and it meant something that would have terrified the religious establishment.

ἐκκλησία
ekklēsia
ek-klay-SEE-ah
G1577
Ekklēsia — From ek (G1537, “out from”) + kaleō (G2564, “to call”). A “called-out assembly.” In Greek civic life: citizens summoned out of their homes into the public square to govern, deliberate, and make binding decisions. Not a religious term. A political one.

In Athens, the ekklēsia was the governing assembly of citizens. When the herald blew the trumpet, citizens left their homes and gathered to debate laws, declare war, elect leaders. This was not a worship service. It was a legislative body. When Jesus said “I will build my ekklēsia” (Matthew 16:18), His listeners heard: “I am assembling a governing body. A called-out people with legislative authority.”

So how did ekklēsia become “church”? The English word “church” comes from the Greek kyriakos (κυριακός), meaning “belonging to the Lord”—a completely different Greek word that appears only twice in the New Testament (1 Corinthians 11:20; Revelation 1:10), referring to “the Lord’s supper” and “the Lord’s day.” It is never used for the assembly of believers.

The substitution was deliberate. When King James I commissioned his translation in 1611, he gave his translators fifteen specific rules. Rule #3 stated: “The old ecclesiastical words to be kept; viz. the word church not to be translated congregation.” The king literally ordered them not to translate ekklēsia accurately. Why? Because “assembly” or “congregation” implies people gathering freely. “Church” implies an institution—with a building, a hierarchy, and a power structure that a king can control.

William Tyndale had correctly translated ekklēsia as “congregation” in 1526—and the institutional church hated him for it. Among the charges at his trial was his refusal to use “church” instead of “congregation.” He was strangled and burned at the stake in 1536.

For the first three centuries, there were no church buildings. Christians met in homes (Romans 16:5; 1 Corinthians 16:19; Colossians 4:15; Philemon 1:2). The building-based model came from Emperor Constantine in the 4th century, when Christianity became Rome’s state religion and the empire’s existing basilica architecture was repurposed for Christian gatherings. The building replaced the people as the primary meaning of the word.

❧ Key References: Ekklēsia

  • Matthew 16:18 — “I will build my ekklēsia”—a called-out governing assembly, not a building
  • Acts 19:32, 39, 41 — Ekklēsia used for a secular civic assembly in Ephesus—proving the word is not inherently religious
  • Romans 16:5 — “The ekklēsia in their house”—people gathering in homes
  • 1 Corinthians 16:19 — Aquila and Priscilla’s house assembly
  • Colossians 4:15 — Nympha’s house assembly

4. “Pastor” — One Mention, Not a Job Title

The word “pastor” appears in the New Testament exactly one time—Ephesians 4:11. One time. The Greek word is poimēn, and it means “shepherd.” It is listed alongside apostles, prophets, evangelists, and teachers as a gift given to the body—not a title, not an office, not a CEO position.

ποιμήν
poimēn
poy-MANE
G4166
Poimēn — A shepherd, one who tends a flock. Used literally of shepherds in Luke 2:8 and metaphorically of Christ in John 10:11. Appears as a spiritual gift in Ephesians 4:11. The KJV translates it “pastor” exactly once; every other occurrence is “shepherd.”

The word poimēn appears 18 times in the New Testament. Seventeen of those times, the KJV translates it “shepherd.” One time—Ephesians 4:11—they translated it “pastor.” Why the inconsistency? Because in Ephesians 4:11 it is describing a role in the community, and translating it “shepherd” would not support the institutional office that already existed in 1611.

The modern “senior pastor” model—one man at the top of a hierarchy, running the organization, delivering sermons from a stage—does not exist anywhere in the New Testament. The early assemblies were led by groups of elders (presbyteroi, G4245), always plural: Acts 14:23 — “they appointed elders in every assembly”; Titus 1:5 — “appoint elders in every city.” No single leader. No hierarchy. No spotlight.

The one-man pastor system mirrors the Roman institutional model that gave us church buildings. It came from the same 4th-century merger of Christianity with imperial politics. The episcopos (overseer/bishop) slowly accumulated singular authority over multiple assemblies, and by the medieval period, the hierarchical clergy structure was fully entrenched. But it is tradition, not Scripture.

❧ Key References: Poimēn and Plural Leadership

  • Ephesians 4:11 — The only NT verse translated “pastor”—listed as a gift, not a title
  • Acts 14:23 — Elders (plural) appointed in every assembly
  • Acts 20:28 — Paul addresses multiple overseers: “shepherd (poimainō) the assembly of God”
  • Titus 1:5 — Appoint elders (plural) in every city
  • 1 Peter 5:1–3 — Peter tells elders (plural) to shepherd willingly, “not lording it over those entrusted to you”

5. “Tithe” — The Old Testament Tax That Got Repackaged

Every Sunday, millions of Christians hear that God requires 10% of their income. They are told that Malachi 3:8 says they are “robbing God” if they do not tithe. They are told this is a timeless, universal command. None of that is accurate. The tithe in the Old Testament was something completely different from what modern churches describe.

מַעֲשֵׂר
ma‘aser
mah-ah-SAYR
H4643
Ma‘aser — A tenth part, a tithe. From the root ‘eser (H6235), “ten.” In the Torah: an agricultural tax on the produce of the land and the increase of livestock. It was never money. Always crops and animals.

Israel’s tithe system was the taxation structure of a theocratic nation. There were actually three tithes:

The Levitical Tithe (Leviticus 27:30; Numbers 18:21): 10% of agricultural produce given to the Levites, who had no land inheritance and served as the religious workforce.

The Festival Tithe (Deuteronomy 14:22–27): A second 10% that the family was to use for celebrating at the appointed feasts. God literally commanded them to eat it and rejoice—“spend the money for whatever your heart desires—for oxen or sheep, for wine or strong drink.”

The Poor Tithe (Deuteronomy 14:28–29): Every third year, an additional 10% stored locally for the Levite, the foreigner, the orphan, and the widow.

Total: approximately 23%, not 10%—and it was an agricultural tax, not a spiritual obligation on all income. Fishermen, carpenters, tentmakers—people who did not farm—never tithed. Jesus was a carpenter (Mark 6:3). He never tithed. Paul was a tentmaker (Acts 18:3). He never tithed.

And that Malachi 3:8–10 passage about “robbing God”? Read the context. Malachi is writing to the Levitical priests, not the general population. Malachi 1:6 opens with: “A son honors his father, and a servant his master—if then I am a Father, where is my honor? says the LORD of hosts to you, O priests.” The entire book is an indictment of the priesthood. When God says “you are robbing me,” He is talking to the priests who were skimming from the temple storehouse—not to regular people in 2026.

The New Testament never commands Christians to tithe. Paul’s instructions on giving are explicit: “Each one must give as he has decided in his heart, not reluctantly or under compulsion, for God loves a cheerful giver” (2 Corinthians 9:7). “Not under compulsion” is the opposite of “you owe God 10%.”

❧ Key References: The Tithe

  • Leviticus 27:30 — The tithe is the “seed of the land” and “fruit of the tree”—agricultural, not monetary
  • Deuteronomy 14:22–27 — The festival tithe: eat it, drink it, rejoice before the Lord
  • Deuteronomy 14:28–29 — The poor tithe: every third year for widows, orphans, and foreigners
  • Malachi 1:6 — Malachi is addressing the priests, not the people
  • Malachi 3:8–10 — “Robbing God”—spoken to priests about the temple storehouse
  • 2 Corinthians 9:7 — “Not reluctantly or under compulsion”—the NT standard for giving

6. “Submit to Your Authorities” — The Obedience Verse

Romans 13:1 has been used to justify blind obedience to every government, every pastor, every authority figure who claims divine backing. “Let every soul be subject to the governing authorities.” End of discussion, right? Not quite. The Greek tells a more nuanced story, and so does the life of the man who wrote it.

ὑποτάσσω
hypotassō
hoo-pot-AS-so
G5293
Hypotassō — From hypo (G5259, “under”) + tassō (G5021, “to arrange, appoint”). A military term meaning “to arrange under, to place in order under.” In the middle/passive voice (as used in Romans 13:1): a voluntary positioning of oneself within an ordered structure. Not blind obedience. Voluntary alignment with order.

Hypotassō in the middle voice is reflexive—it describes a voluntary action the subject takes upon themselves. It is not the same as hypakouō (G5219), which means “to obey, to listen and comply.” Paul chose the word for voluntary arrangement, not the word for compulsory obedience. The distinction matters enormously.

Paul wrote Romans 13 while part of a movement that was illegal in Rome. He was a citizen of an empire that had crucified his Lord, was actively persecuting his communities, and would eventually execute him. Peter—who would have agreed with Paul on the value of order—also told the Sanhedrin: “We must obey God rather than men” (Acts 5:29).

Paul was not saying “obey every government no matter what.” He was saying God works through order, and anarchy is not the Kingdom’s way. Voluntarily arrange yourself within God-ordained structures—but when those structures contradict God, the higher authority takes precedence. The same Paul who wrote “be subject to governing authorities” was beaten, imprisoned, shipwrecked, and beheaded by those very authorities. He was not a man who practiced blind submission.

The abuse of this verse is staggering. It was used to justify the divine right of kings, to silence abolitionists who opposed slavery, to tell German Christians to submit to the Nazi regime, to tell colonized peoples that resisting their oppressors was sinning against God. Every authoritarian power structure in Christian history has weaponized Romans 13:1. And every time, they ignored Acts 5:29, the Hebrew midwives defying Pharaoh (Exodus 1:17), Daniel defying Nebuchadnezzar (Daniel 3:18), and the entire prophetic tradition of standing against unjust power.

❧ Key References: Authority and Obedience

  • Romans 13:1 — Hypotassō (voluntary arrangement), not hypakouō (compulsory obedience)
  • Acts 5:29 — “We must obey God rather than men”—the apostolic qualifier
  • Exodus 1:17 — Hebrew midwives disobey Pharaoh’s order to kill babies—and God blesses them for it
  • Daniel 3:18 — Shadrach, Meshach, Abednego refuse the king’s command
  • Daniel 6:10 — Daniel defies the law and keeps praying

7. “Women Be Silent” — The Verse Ripped From Context

1 Corinthians 14:34—“Women should remain silent in the assemblies.” This verse has been used for centuries to exclude women from teaching, preaching, and leading in the church. But three chapters earlier in the same letter, Paul gives instructions for women praying and prophesying in the assembly (1 Corinthians 11:5). He cannot be issuing a universal silencing order.

σιγάω
sigaō
see-GAH-oh
G4601
Sigaō — To be silent, to hold one’s peace, to keep quiet. Used in 1 Corinthians 14 three times: for tongues-speakers (v. 28), for prophets (v. 30), and for women (v. 34). In each case, it addresses a specific disorder in the Corinthian assembly—not a universal prohibition.

The Corinthian assembly was notoriously chaotic. Paul spends all of chapter 14 trying to bring order. He tells tongues-speakers to be silent if there is no interpreter (v. 28). He tells prophets to be silent when another prophet begins speaking (v. 30). And he tells certain women to be silent—likely those who were disrupting the gathering by shouting questions across the room. In the ancient world, men and women often sat on separate sides of the assembly space. Women without formal education were calling out questions to their husbands during the teaching.

Paul even says in the passage itself: “If they want to learn anything, let them ask their own husbands at home” (1 Corinthians 14:35). This is a specific instruction about a specific problem—disruptive questioning during the assembly—not a universal ban on women speaking.

Paul’s own ministry contradicts the “women can’t lead” reading. He calls Junia “outstanding among the apostles” (Romans 16:7). He calls Phoebe a diakonos (deacon/minister) of the assembly at Cenchreae and a prostatis (patron, leader, protector) (Romans 16:1–2). Priscilla is named before her husband Aquila in four of six mentions and is described as having taught Apollos (Acts 18:26). The “women cannot lead or teach” interpretation requires ignoring a substantial portion of the New Testament.

Some scholars have noted that 1 Corinthians 14:34–35 may be a quotation that Paul is responding to, not his own instruction. The word “What!” (ἦ) that begins verse 36—“What! Did the word of God come from you? Or was it to you only that it came?”—uses the Greek particle of strong disagreement or indignation. Paul may be quoting a Corinthian faction and then rebuking them. This reading has gained traction among textual scholars, particularly since a few Western manuscripts place these verses after verse 40, suggesting scribal uncertainty about their placement.

❧ Key References: Women in the Assembly

  • 1 Corinthians 11:5 — Paul gives rules for women praying and prophesying—they’re clearly speaking
  • 1 Corinthians 14:34 — Sigaō—the same “be silent” used for tongues-speakers and prophets in the same chapter
  • Romans 16:1–2 — Phoebe: diakonos (deacon) and prostatis (patron/leader)
  • Romans 16:7 — Junia: “outstanding among the apostles”
  • Acts 18:26 — Priscilla teaches Apollos the way of God more accurately
  • Galatians 3:28 — “There is neither male nor female, for you are all one in Christ Jesus”

8. “Born Again” — It Doesn’t Mean What the Altar Call Says

“You must be born again.” It is the most quoted Jesus phrase in American evangelicalism. It has been turned into a prayer formula, a membership requirement, a bumper sticker. But the Greek word Jesus used does not primarily mean “again.” It means something much more radical.

ἄνωθεν
anōthen
AH-no-then
G509
Anōthen — From anō (“above”) + the suffix -then (“from”). Primary meaning: “from above, from a higher place.” Secondary meaning: “from the beginning, again.” Used in John 3:3, 7; John 19:11; James 1:17; James 3:15, 17. In most other NT uses, it clearly means “from above.”

In John 3:3, Jesus tells Nicodemus: “Unless one is born anōthen, he cannot see the kingdom of God.” Nicodemus takes it as “again”—he asks “Can a man enter his mother’s womb a second time?” Jesus corrects him: “Unless one is born of water and the Spirit” (John 3:5). He was not talking about repetition. He was talking about origin. Born from above—from a higher dimension of reality.

The same word appears in John 19:11—“You would have no authority unless it had been given you anōthen”—clearly “from above.” In James 1:17—“Every good gift and every perfect gift is anōthen”—clearly “from above.” In James 3:17—“the wisdom that is anōthen”—clearly “from above.” The primary meaning of the word across the New Testament is “from above,” not “again.”

Jesus was describing a spiritual event—your spirit coming alive to God’s realm. The Kingdom becomes visible to you (“cannot see the kingdom”—perception, not geography). This is not a prayer you repeat. It is a birth you undergo. And the “repeat after me” sinner’s prayer? It does not exist anywhere in Scripture. Not once. It was invented during the American revivalist movement of the 1800s, popularized by figures like Charles Finney and D.L. Moody.

(For the complete deep dive, see: How We’re Truly Saved — The Narrow Road.)

❧ Key References: Born From Above

  • John 3:3 — “Born anōthen”—from above, not merely “again”
  • John 3:5–6 — “Born of water and the Spirit”—two births from two realms
  • John 19:11 — Anōthen = “from above” unambiguously
  • James 1:17 — Every good gift is “from above” (anōthen)
  • James 3:17 — “The wisdom from above” (anōthen)

9. “Faith” — Not Belief, Not Mental Agreement

The English word “faith” has been flattened into intellectual agreement. “Do you have faith?” now means “Do you believe the right facts?” But the Greek word behind it describes something dramatically different—something the demons have no trouble with.

πίστις
pistis
PIS-tis
G4102
Pistis — From peithō (G3982, “to persuade, be persuaded”). Trust, reliance, loyalty, faithfulness, firm persuasion. Not mere intellectual agreement. The settled confidence that leads to full reliance—throwing your full weight onto someone or something.

James 2:19 demolishes the “faith = correct belief” equation: “Even the demons believe—and tremble.” The demons have flawless theology. They know Jesus is the Son of God—they said it to His face (Mark 1:24). They believe every doctrinal proposition. But they do not have pistis—trust, surrender, reliance, the posture of the heart that throws its full weight onto God.

“Saved by faith” (Ephesians 2:8) does not mean saved by mentally agreeing to a list of facts. It means saved by full reliance on God—by the settled persuasion that leads to trust, and trust that leads to obedience, and obedience that flows from love. That is a relationship, not a theology quiz.

This is why Jesus said people who never heard His name would enter the Kingdom (Matthew 25:37–40)—“Lord, when did we see you hungry?”—while people with perfect doctrine got turned away: “Lord, Lord, did we not prophesy in your name?” “I never knew you” (Matthew 7:22–23). The word “knew” there is ginōskō (G1097)—intimate, experiential knowing. It was never about correct information. It was about the posture of the heart.

❧ Key References: Pistis

  • James 2:19 — Demons “believe”—belief alone is not pistis
  • Ephesians 2:8 — “By grace through pistis”—through trust, reliance, not just mental acceptance
  • Hebrews 11:1 — Pistis is the “substance” (hypostasis) of things hoped for—it has weight
  • Matthew 25:37–40 — People who never heard His name enter the Kingdom—heart over doctrine
  • Matthew 7:22–23 — Perfect doctrine, no relationship: “I never ginōskō you”

10. “The Rapture” — A 200-Year-Old Invention

The word “rapture” does not appear in the Bible. Not in any translation. Not in any manuscript. The pre-tribulation rapture doctrine—the idea that believers will be secretly snatched away from the earth before a period of tribulation—was invented by John Nelson Darby in the 1830s. Before Darby, no Christian in 1,800 years of church history taught it.

ἁρπάζω
harpazō
har-PAD-zo
G726
Harpazō — To seize, snatch up, take away by force. From a root meaning “to seize bounty.” Used in 1 Thessalonians 4:17 for believers being “caught up.” The Latin Vulgate translated this as rapiemur (from rapio, “to seize”)—which gave us the English word “rapture.” But the word itself just means “to be seized and brought to a meeting point.”
ἀπάντησις
apantēsis
ah-PAN-tay-sis
G529
Apantēsis — A meeting, an encounter. A Roman civic technical term: citizens go out of the city to meet an approaching dignitary and then escort him back in. Used only three times in the NT: Matthew 25:6 (virgins meet the bridegroom), Acts 28:15 (believers meet Paul on the road), 1 Thessalonians 4:17 (we meet the Lord in the air).

1 Thessalonians 4:17 says: “Then we who are alive and remain shall be caught up (harpazō) together with them in the clouds to meet (eis apantēsin) the Lord in the air.”

The key is apantēsis. In the Greco-Roman world, when a dignitary or emperor was approaching a city, the citizens would go out to meet him on the road—and then escort him back into the city. It was a welcoming procession, not an evacuation. The dignitary was coming to them. They went out to receive him and bring him in.

This is confirmed by the only other NT uses of apantēsis. In Matthew 25:6, the virgins go out to meet the bridegroom—and accompany him back to the wedding. In Acts 28:15, the Roman Christians go out to meet Paul—and walk back with him into Rome. The pattern is always the same: go out to greet, then escort back.

Paul’s original audience understood: Jesus is coming here. We go out to meet Him and escort Him back to earth. Not: we escape while earth burns. The Lord’s Prayer itself says it: “Your kingdom come, your will be done, on earth as it is in heaven” (Matthew 6:10). Revelation 21 shows the New Jerusalem coming down out of heaven to earth (Revelation 21:2). The direction is always toward earth, not away from it.

The pre-tribulation rapture was systematized by Darby, popularized by the Scofield Reference Bible (1909), and cemented in popular culture by Hal Lindsey’s The Late Great Planet Earth (1970) and Tim LaHaye’s Left Behind series (1995). The Left Behind franchise sold over 80 million books. But the doctrine behind it is younger than the United States of America.

  • Matthew 25:6 — Virgins go out for apantēsis and return with the bridegroom
  • Acts 28:15 — Believers go out for apantēsis and escort Paul back to Rome
  • Matthew 6:10 — “On earth as it is in heaven”—the Kingdom comes here
  • Revelation 21:2 — New Jerusalem comes down to earth—not us going up

11. “Lucifer” — A Name That Doesn’t Exist in the Hebrew

Ask any Christian who Lucifer is and they will tell you: the fallen angel, formerly heaven’s worship leader, kicked out for pride. They will probably reference Isaiah 14. But open Isaiah 14 in Hebrew and the name “Lucifer” is nowhere to be found. It is not a Hebrew word. It is not a name. And the passage is not about a fallen angel.

הֵילֵל
hēylel
hay-LALE
H1966
Hēylel — “Shining one, morning star.” From the root halal (H1984), “to shine, to praise.” Used exactly once in the entire Old Testament: Isaiah 14:12. A title (“shining one, son of the dawn”), not a proper name. Refers to the planet Venus as the morning star.

Isaiah 14:12 (KJV): “How art thou fallen from heaven, O Lucifer, son of the morning!” But the Hebrew reads: “How you have fallen from heaven, hēylel ben-shachar”—“shining one, son of the dawn.” It is an epithet. A poetic description. Not a name.

Now read the context. Isaiah 14:4 explicitly identifies the subject: “You will take up this taunt against the king of Babylon.” The entire passage is political poetry mocking a human king who exalted himself and was brought low. The “falling from heaven” language is the same kind of hyperbolic imagery used throughout the prophets for the downfall of arrogant rulers.

So how did a Hebrew title become the proper name of Satan? Through the Latin Vulgate. When Jerome translated the Hebrew Old Testament into Latin (around 400 AD), he rendered hēylel as lucifer—a Latin word meaning “light-bearer” or “morning star” (referring to Venus). In Latin, lucifer was not a proper name—it was a common noun. Early church father Tertullian (160–220 AD) was among the first to connect Isaiah 14 to Satan, and the identification gradually solidified through medieval theology.

The full “Lucifer mythology”—a fallen angel named Lucifer who was heaven’s worship leader, led a rebellion, took a third of the angels with him, and was cast out for pride—is a patchwork stitched from four unrelated passages: Isaiah 14 (a taunt against the king of Babylon), Ezekiel 28 (a lament over the king of Tyre), Luke 10:18 (“I saw Satan fall like lightning”), and Revelation 12 (the dragon cast out of heaven). None of these passages were originally written about each other. They were harmonized into a single narrative over centuries of theological tradition.

(For the complete deep dive, see: What The Bible Actually Says About Satan.)

❧ Key References: The “Lucifer” Mythology

  • Isaiah 14:4 — “Take up this taunt against the king of Babylon”—the passage names its subject
  • Isaiah 14:12 — Hēylel ben-shachar—“shining one, son of the dawn”—a title, not a name
  • Ezekiel 28:12 — “Take up a lamentation over the king of Tyre”—different passage, different king
  • Luke 10:18 — Jesus says “I saw Satan fall like lightning”—a statement, not a backstory
  • Revelation 12:9 — The dragon cast out—apocalyptic imagery, not a historical biography

12. “Pray the Prayer, Go to Heaven When You Die” — The Biggest Twist

The gospel Jesus preached: “The Kingdom of God is at hand.” Present tense. Here. Now. Available. On earth. The gospel most modern churches preach: “Accept Jesus so you can go to heaven when you die.” Future tense. Later. Somewhere else. After you’re dead. These are two fundamentally different messages.

βασιλεία
basileia
bah-see-LAY-ah
G932
Basileia — Kingdom, royal rule, reign. Not primarily a place but a reign—the active exercise of sovereign authority. When Jesus says “the Kingdom of God is at hand,” He means God’s reign is breaking in. It is happening. Not someday—now.

Jesus barely talked about “going to heaven.” He talked about the Kingdom coming to earth. The Lord’s Prayer: “Your kingdom come, your will be done, on earth as it is in heaven” (Matthew 6:10). Revelation 21: the New Jerusalem comes down out of heaven to earth (Revelation 21:2–3)—“Behold, the dwelling of God is with men.” The direction is always the same: heaven comes here.

The “fly away to heaven” gospel is closer to Greek Platonism—the idea that the physical world is a prison and the soul must escape to a higher spiritual realm—than to anything Jesus or the Hebrew prophets taught. The Hebrew worldview was always about God redeeming this creation, not evacuating souls from it. The prophets dreamed of a renewed earth where justice dwells (Isaiah 65:17; Isaiah 11:6–9), not of disembodied spirits floating in clouds.

The shift happened in the 3rd and 4th centuries, when Christianity merged with Greek philosophy and Roman politics. Clement of Alexandria and Origen brought Platonic categories into Christian theology. When Constantine made Christianity the state religion (380 AD under Theodosius), the focus shifted to afterlife management. A gospel about the Kingdom arriving on earth empowers people now. A gospel about escaping after death keeps people passive and controllable. The institutional church had every incentive to emphasize the second version.

The “repeat after me” sinner’s prayer as the entry point to salvation is a product of 19th-century American revivalism. The concept of “accepting Jesus into your heart” appears nowhere in the New Testament. The earliest Christians understood salvation as entering into the basileia—the reign of God—which began in this life through death to self, transformation by the Spirit, and participation in the new creation. It was not a transaction. It was a total life change.

❧ Key References: Kingdom on Earth

  • Mark 1:15 — “The Kingdom of God is at hand”—the actual gospel Jesus preached
  • Matthew 6:10 — “On earth as it is in heaven”—direction is always earthward
  • Luke 17:21 — “The Kingdom of God is within you” / “in your midst”—present tense
  • Revelation 21:2–3 — New Jerusalem comes down—God’s dwelling is with men on earth
  • Isaiah 65:17 — “New heavens and a new earth”—renewed creation, not abandonment
  • Romans 8:21 — Creation itself will be “set free from its bondage to corruption”

13. “Original Sin” — Augustine’s Addition

The idea that every human being is born guilty of Adam’s sin—not merely affected by it, but personally culpable for it from birth—was developed by Augustine of Hippo (354–430 AD). It became foundational to Western Christianity. But the Eastern Orthodox Church never accepted it. And the verse Augustine used to build it says something different in Greek than it did in the Latin translation he was reading.

Romans 5:12: “Therefore, just as through one man sin entered the world, and death through sin, and so death spread to all men, because all sinned.”

The critical phrase is the last one. The Greek reads: eph’ hō pantes hēmarton (ἐφ᾿ ᾧ πάντες ἤμαρτον)—“because all sinned.” Each person sinned individually. Death spread because every human, in their own lives, sinned.

But Augustine read the Latin Vulgate, not the Greek. The Latin rendered eph’ hō as in quo—“in whom.” In the Latin, the verse reads: “death spread to all men, in whom all sinned”—meaning everyone sinned in Adam. The entire human race participated in Adam’s sin and inherited his guilt. That is a fundamentally different claim, and it rests on a Latin mistranslation of a Greek prepositional phrase.

The Eastern church fathers—who read Romans in Greek, the language it was written in—understood the passage differently. John Chrysostom (347–407 AD), the greatest Greek preacher of the early church, read eph’ hō as “because” and taught that humans inherit the consequences of Adam’s sin (mortality, a weakened nature) but not the guilt. The Eastern Orthodox tradition has maintained this reading for 1,600 years. Inherited corruption, yes. Inherited guilt, no.

Original sin as Augustine defined it became the foundation for several Western doctrines: infant baptism (the baby must be baptized to wash away inherited guilt), total depravity (humans are so fundamentally evil they cannot even respond to God without irresistible grace), and the Calvinist framework of unconditional election. All of these depend on Augustine’s reading of a Latin text that departs from the Greek.

The starting point changes everything. If humans are born guilty and fundamentally evil, the gospel is a rescue from an irredeemably wicked nature. If humans are born into a broken world with a corrupted but not irredeemable nature, the gospel is a restoration—God making right what went wrong. Same Scripture, different starting assumptions, dramatically different theology.

❧ Key References: Inherited Sin

  • Romans 5:12 — Greek eph’ hō = “because”; Latin in quo = “in whom.” The difference built a doctrine.
  • Ezekiel 18:20 — “The son shall not bear the guilt of the father”—individual accountability in the OT
  • Deuteronomy 24:16 — “Each shall be put to death for his own sin”—not another’s
  • Romans 5:18 — Paul balances Adam’s transgression with Christ’s righteousness for “all men”
  • Romans 3:23 — “All have sinned”—active verb, personal action, not inherited status

The Pattern — How It Always Works

Thirteen different doctrines. Thirteen different topics. But the mechanism is always the same. It operates like clockwork:

Step 1: The original text says something specific in Hebrew or Greek.
Step 2: A translation choice or theological tradition changes the meaning—sometimes deliberately, sometimes through honest misunderstanding.
Step 3: Centuries of repetition make the new meaning feel like it was always there.
Step 4: Anyone who questions it gets called a heretic—or worse.

~250 BC
Septuagint — Hebrew Bible translated into Greek. Mostly faithful, but translation choices (like hadēs for sheol) begin introducing Greek philosophical connotations.
~200 AD
Tertullian begins connecting Isaiah 14 to Satan. The “Lucifer” mythology starts taking shape.
~400 AD
Jerome’s Latin Vulgate — Translates hēylel as lucifer, ekklēsia through the lens of established institutions. Augustine reads eph’ hō as in quo and builds original sin.
313–380 AD
Constantine and Theodosius — Christianity becomes the state religion. Buildings replace house gatherings. Hierarchical clergy replaces plural eldership. The institutional “church” is born.
553 AD
Fifth Ecumenical Council — Condemns Origen’s apokatastasis (universal restoration). The age-lasting (aiōnios) punishment view begins to narrow toward “eternal torment only.”
1320 AD
Dante’s Inferno — Vivid literary depiction of hell cements the “eternal torture” image in popular imagination more than any Bible passage ever did.
1536 AD
William Tyndale executed. Among his “crimes”: translating ekklēsia as “congregation” instead of “church.”
1611 AD
King James Version — Rule #3: “The old ecclesiastical words to be kept.” Ekklēsia locked into “church.” Sheol, Hadēs, and Gehenna all become “hell.”
1830s
John Nelson Darby invents the pre-tribulation rapture doctrine. Popularized by the Scofield Reference Bible (1909).
1800s
American Revivalism — The “sinner’s prayer” and “altar call” are invented. Charles Finney, D.L. Moody, and Billy Sunday popularize a “decision-based” salvation model unknown to the early church.

The Bible did not change. The manuscripts are remarkably stable. What changed is the lens through which we read it—filtered through Latin translations, Greek philosophy, Roman politics, medieval art, Reformation debates, and American revivalism. Every layer added distance between you and the original text.

The good news? You can strip those layers away. The Hebrew and Greek are still there. The tools to read them are free. The early church fathers are available online. No gatekeeper can stand between you and the text. That is what this entire website exists to demonstrate.
