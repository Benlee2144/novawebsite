Hell

The Three Words They Mashed Into One — How Sheol, Hades, Gehenna, and Tartarus Became a Single Doctrine That the Early Church Didn’t Believe

~120 min read • 22 Hebrew & Greek words explored • 85+ cross-references
“...which I did not command, nor did it come into My heart.” — Jeremiah 7:31 (God speaking about burning people in the Valley of Hinnom)

Open any English Bible. Search for the word “hell.” Depending on your translation, you will find it between 13 and 54 times. In the King James Version, it appears 54 times. In the ESV, 14. In the NASB, 13. In Young’s Literal Translation—a translation that prioritizes accuracy over readability—it appears zero times.

Zero. The most literal English translation of the Bible does not contain the word “hell.” Not once. Because the word “hell” is not a translation. It is an interpretation—a decision by translators to collapse four completely different Hebrew and Greek words, referring to four completely different concepts, into a single English word that carries connotations none of the original words possess.

Those four words are:

  • שְׁאוֹל (Sheol) — Hebrew. The grave. The realm of the dead. Where everyone goes.
  • ᾅδης (Hadēs) — Greek. The unseen realm. The New Testament equivalent of Sheol.
  • γέεννα (Geenna) — Greek, from Hebrew ge-hinnom. A literal valley south of Jerusalem. A garbage dump.
  • ταρταρόω (Tartaroō) — Greek. An angelic prison. Used exactly once. Not for humans.

Each of these words has a specific meaning, a specific history, and a specific context. They refer to different places, different concepts, and different destinies. The English word “hell”—which conjures images of an underground fire dimension ruled by a red devil with a pitchfork, where sinners are tortured for eternity—does not accurately represent any of them.

This study will take you through each word in its original language. We will examine every verse where each word appears. We will trace the historical development of the “hell” doctrine from the early church through Augustine, through Dante, through the Reformers, to the modern altar call. And we will let the text speak for itself—in the languages it was actually written in.

✝

Word 1: שְׁאוֹל (Sheol) — The Grave

שְׁאוֹל
Sheol
sheh-OHL
H7585
The grave, the pit, the realm of the dead, the underworld. The common destination of all the dead—righteous and wicked alike. Occurs 65 times in the Hebrew Bible. The KJV translates it as “hell” 31 times, “grave” 31 times, and “pit” 3 times.

Etymology and Meaning

The etymology of Sheol is debated among Hebrew scholars, but the leading proposals all point to the same concept:

Option 1: From the root שׁאל (sha’al, H7592)—meaning “to ask, to inquire, to demand.” Sheol is the place that “asks for” everyone—it demands all the living. “Sheol and Abaddon are never satisfied” (Proverbs 27:20). It is the insatiable grave that swallows every generation.

Option 2: From a root meaning “hollow, empty place.” This connects Sheol to the concept of an empty underworld—a vast, silent, hollow space beneath the earth where the dead exist in a state of diminished consciousness.

In either case, Sheol is not a place of punishment. It is not a place of fire. It is not selective—it does not sort the righteous from the wicked. It is simply the place of the dead. Every human being, regardless of moral character, went to Sheol. And the Old Testament is explicit about this.

The Righteous Go to Sheol

If Sheol is “hell”—a place of punishment for the wicked—then the following verses make no sense:

Righteous People Who Expected to Go to Sheol

  • Genesis 37:35 — Jacob, the patriarch of Israel, after hearing Joseph is dead: “I shall go down into Sheol (שְׁאֹלָה) to my son in mourning.” Jacob—whom God renamed Israel, with whom God made a covenant, who saw the ladder reaching to heaven—expected to go to Sheol. If Sheol is hell, Jacob expected to burn.
  • Genesis 42:38 — Jacob again: “You shall bring down my gray hairs with sorrow to Sheol.” Jacob uses Sheol as the natural destination of death, not as punishment.
  • Genesis 44:29,31 — Jacob twice more: “You shall bring down my gray hairs with sorrow to Sheol.” Four times Jacob references going to Sheol. Not once does he express fear of punishment. It is simply where the dead go.
  • Job 14:13 — Job—whom God Himself called “blameless and upright” (Job 1:8)—says: “Oh, that You would hide me in Sheol, that You would conceal me until Your wrath is past.” Job asks God to put him in Sheol as a place of refuge. He wants to be hidden there until God’s anger passes. If Sheol is a place of torment, this is the most absurd request in Scripture—asking to escape suffering by going to the place of suffering.
  • Job 17:13-16 — Job: “If I wait, Sheol is my house; I have made my bed in the darkness... We shall go down together to the bars of Sheol, and rest together in the dust.” Sheol is described as a house. A bed. A place of rest. Not a furnace.
  • Psalm 88:3 — Heman the Ezrahite, a Levite musician, one of the wisest men in Israel (1 Kings 4:31): “My life draws near to Sheol.” He is dying and expects Sheol as the natural destination.

What Sheol Is Like

The Old Testament describes Sheol in consistent, specific terms. None of them match the popular concept of “hell”:

The Nature of Sheol in the Hebrew Bible

  • Psalm 6:5 — “For in death there is no remembrance of You; in Sheol who will give You thanks?” No consciousness of God in Sheol. No praise. No worship. No awareness. This is silence, not screaming.
  • Psalm 115:17 — “The dead do not praise the LORD, nor any who go down into silence.” Sheol is silence. The Hebrew is דּוּמָה (dumah)—stillness, quiet, the silence of death.
  • Psalm 139:8 — “If I ascend into heaven, You are there; if I make my bed in Sheol, behold, You are there.” God is present in Sheol. If Sheol is a place of separation from God (the most common theological definition of “hell”), how is God there? David says God’s presence extends even into the realm of the dead.
  • Ecclesiastes 9:5-6 — “For the living know that they will die; but the dead know nothing, and they have no more reward, for the memory of them is forgotten. Also their love, their hatred, and their envy have now perished.” The dead know nothing. No consciousness, no emotion, no memory. This is not torment. It is cessation.
  • Ecclesiastes 9:10 — “Whatever your hand finds to do, do it with your might; for there is no work or device or knowledge or wisdom in Sheol where you are going.” Solomon addresses everyone—“where you are going.” Sheol is the universal destination. No activity, no knowledge, no wisdom. The opposite of conscious torment.
  • Isaiah 38:18 — King Hezekiah: “For Sheol cannot thank You, death cannot praise You; those who go down to the pit cannot hope for Your truth.” Sheol is a place without hope, without praise, without thanksgiving. It is characterized by absence—not by suffering.

God’s Sovereignty Over Sheol

Sheol Is Under God’s Authority

  • Deuteronomy 32:22 — “For a fire is kindled in My anger, and shall burn to the lowest Sheol.” God’s judgment can reach Sheol, but this describes the extent of God’s wrath (it reaches everywhere), not the nature of Sheol itself.
  • 1 Samuel 2:6 — Hannah’s prayer: “The LORD kills and makes alive; He brings down to Sheol and brings up.” God has the power to bring people up from Sheol. Sheol is not permanent and not inescapable for God.
  • Psalm 16:10 — “For You will not leave my soul in Sheol, nor will You allow Your Holy One to see corruption.” A messianic psalm. Peter quotes this in Acts 2:27 as prophecy fulfilled in the resurrection of Jesus. If Sheol is “hell,” David is prophesying that the Messiah would go to hell. The text says the Messiah went to the place of the dead and God brought Him back. That is resurrection, not a rescue from eternal punishment.
  • Psalm 49:15 — “But God will redeem my soul from the power of Sheol, for He shall receive me.” פָדָה (padah, H6299)—“to ransom, to redeem.” God redeems souls out of Sheol. The psalmist has hope that death is not the final word.
  • Hosea 13:14 — “I will ransom them from the power of Sheol; I will redeem them from death. O Death, I will be your plagues! O Sheol, I will be your destruction!” God declares war on Sheol itself. He will destroy it. Paul quotes this in 1 Corinthians 15:55 as fulfilled in the resurrection of Christ: “O Death, where is your sting? O Hades, where is your victory?”
  • Amos 9:2 — “Though they dig into Sheol, from there My hand shall take them.” No one can hide from God—not even in Sheol. God’s reach extends everywhere.

The Translation Problem

The KJV translated the same Hebrew word שְׁאוֹל three different ways:

English Translation Times Used (KJV) Implication
“Hell” 31 Place of punishment
“Grave” 31 Neutral burial place
“Pit” 3 Physical hole in the ground

Same word. Three different translations. The translators did not make their choice based on the Hebrew—they made it based on context and theological assumption. When the righteous person was heading to Sheol, they translated it “grave.” When the wicked person was heading there, they translated it “hell.” Same word. Same destination. Two completely different English renderings based on the translator’s preexisting theology.

That is not translation. That is doctrinal injection. The Hebrew text makes no distinction between the Sheol of the righteous and the Sheol of the wicked. The translators created that distinction by choosing different English words for the same Hebrew word.

Word 2: ᾅδης (Hadēs) — The Unseen Realm

ᾅδης
Hadēs
HAH-days
G86
The unseen world, the realm of the dead. The Greek equivalent of Hebrew Sheol. A compound word: α- (alpha privative, meaning “not, without”) + ἰδεῖν (idein, “to see”) = the unseen place. Occurs 10 times in the New Testament.

Etymology

The word ᾅδης is formed from the alpha privative (α-, meaning “not” or “un-”) and the root ἰδεῖν (idein, from ὅραω, horaō, “to see”). It literally means “the unseen place” or “the invisible realm.” It is not defined by fire, punishment, or torment. It is defined by invisibility—it is the realm beyond human sight.

When the Septuagint translators (3rd–2nd century BC) translated the Hebrew Old Testament into Greek, they consistently translated שְׁאוֹל (Sheol) as ᾅδης (Hades). This was a deliberate equivalence: Sheol = Hades. Same concept, different language. The grave. The place of the dead. The unseen world.

Every Use of Hades in the New Testament

All 10 Occurrences of ᾅδης

  • Matthew 11:23 — “And you, Capernaum, who are exalted to heaven, will be brought down to Hades.” Jesus pronounces judgment on Capernaum. The language is humiliation—being brought low from a position of pride. Parallel to Isaiah 14:13-15 about the king of Babylon: “You said in your heart, ‘I will ascend to heaven’... yet you shall be brought down to Sheol.” The imagery is descent, degradation, death of a city’s status—not individual people burning in fire.
  • Matthew 16:18 — “On this rock I will build My church, and the gates of Hades (πύλαι ᾅδου) shall not prevail against it.” “The gates of Hades” is a Hebrew idiom for death. Jesus is saying that death itself will not overcome His church. This is a statement about the power of resurrection over death—not about a building withstanding an attack from a fire dimension. The “gates” (πύλαι, pulai) are defensive structures. Hades is on the defensive, trying to hold people in. The church breaches those gates through the resurrection.
  • Luke 10:15 — “And you, Capernaum... will be thrust down to Hades.” Parallel to Matthew 11:23. Same pronouncement, same meaning.
  • Luke 16:23 — “And being in torments in Hades, he lifted up his eyes and saw Abraham afar off, and Lazarus in his bosom.” The Rich Man and Lazarus. We will examine this passage in detail below. For now, note: the rich man is in Hades—not Gehenna, not the Lake of Fire. Hades is temporary. It gets emptied and destroyed (Revelation 20:13-14). Whatever is happening to the rich man here, it is not the final state.
  • Acts 2:27 — Peter quoting Psalm 16:10: “For You will not leave my soul in Hades, nor will You allow Your Holy One to see corruption.” Jesus went to Hades. Jesus—the sinless Son of God—went to Hades after the crucifixion. If Hades is “hell” (a place of punishment for the wicked), then Jesus was punished as wicked. That is theologically impossible. Jesus went to the place of the dead—and God brought Him back. That is resurrection.
  • Acts 2:31 — Peter continues: “His soul was not left in Hades, nor did His flesh see corruption.” Peter is explaining that David’s psalm was a prophecy of Christ’s resurrection. The entire argument depends on Hades being the place of the dead (not a place of punishment), and resurrection being the escape from it.
  • Revelation 1:18 — The risen Christ: “I am He who lives, and was dead, and behold, I am alive forevermore. Amen. And I have the keys of Hades and of Death.” Jesus holds the keys—He has authority over Hades and Death. These are paired as a unit: Death and Hades. They are the enemy to be conquered, not the punishment to be feared. And Jesus has already conquered them.
  • Revelation 6:8 — “And the name of him who sat on it was Death, and Hades followed with him.” The fourth horseman. Death and Hades personified as companions—they ride together. Where death goes, Hades follows. Hades is the receptacle of the dead, not a punitive dimension.
  • Revelation 20:13 — “The sea gave up the dead who were in it, and Death and Hades delivered up the dead who were in them.” Hades gives up its dead. It is a temporary holding place, not a final destination. Everyone in Hades is released for judgment. If Hades were the final punishment, there would be no reason to empty it.
  • Revelation 20:14 — “Then Death and Hades were cast into the lake of fire. This is the second death.” Hades itself is destroyed. It is thrown into the lake of fire. If Hades is hell, then hell goes to hell. That is incoherent. Unless Hades and the Lake of Fire are two completely different things. Which they are. Hades is temporary. The Lake of Fire is its destruction.

The pattern is unmistakable. Hades is:

  • Temporary — it gets emptied and destroyed (Rev 20:13-14)
  • The realm of all the dead — including Jesus (Acts 2:27,31)
  • Under Christ’s authority — He holds its keys (Rev 1:18)
  • Paired with Death — as companions, not as punishments (Rev 6:8, 20:13-14)
  • Not the final state — it is distinct from the Lake of Fire (Rev 20:14)

Translating Hades as “hell” in English Bibles collapses all of this nuance into a single misleading word that suggests finality, punishment, and eternal duration—none of which the Greek word carries.

The Rich Man and Lazarus (Luke 16:19–31)

This passage is the single most cited text in defense of the traditional “hell” doctrine. It is also the most misunderstood. Let us read it carefully and note what the text actually says—and what it does not say.

Context: A Series of Parables

Luke 16 is part of an extended section of Jesus’s teaching that begins in Luke 15:1: “Then all the tax collectors and the sinners drew near to Him to hear Him. And the Pharisees and scribes complained, saying, ‘This Man receives sinners and eats with them.’” Everything that follows is Jesus’s response to the Pharisees:

  • Luke 15:3-7 — Parable of the Lost Sheep
  • Luke 15:8-10 — Parable of the Lost Coin
  • Luke 15:11-32 — Parable of the Prodigal Son
  • Luke 16:1-13 — Parable of the Unjust Steward
  • Luke 16:19-31 — The Rich Man and Lazarus

Every narrative in this section is a parable. They are all addressed to the Pharisees. They are all teaching through illustration. There is no literary marker in Luke’s text indicating that Jesus suddenly switches from parabolic teaching to literal eschatological revelation in 16:19. The structure, audience, and rhetorical style are continuous.

What the Text Actually Says

1. The rich man is in Hades—not Gehenna, not the Lake of Fire. The Greek word in Luke 16:23 is ᾅδης. Even if taken literally, this is the temporary realm of the dead that gets emptied and destroyed in Revelation 20. It is not the final state. It is not “hell” as popularly understood.

2. The rich man can see Abraham and Lazarus. He has a conversation with Abraham across a chasm. He recognizes Lazarus. He pleads for mercy. He shows concern for his five brothers. None of this matches the popular image of “hell”—an isolated chamber of unrelenting, inescapable torment.

3. The rich man’s “torment” is βασανος (basanos). This Greek word (G931) originally meant a touchstone—a stone used to test the purity of gold. By extension, it came to mean testing, examination, and then (later) torment or torture. The LXX uses βασανιζω in Ezekiel 3:20 for “testing.” In this parabolic context, the rich man is being tested or examined—confronted with the truth of his life—not necessarily subjected to literal physical torture.

4. The point of the story is about the Pharisees, not about the afterlife. The climax is in verses 29-31: Abraham says, “They have Moses and the Prophets; let them hear them.” The rich man says, “If one goes to them from the dead, they will repent.” Abraham replies: “If they do not hear Moses and the Prophets, neither will they be persuaded though one rise from the dead.” This is a devastating indictment of the Pharisees, who had Moses and the Prophets and still refused to believe—and who would shortly witness someone rise from the dead (Jesus) and still not be persuaded.

5. The story parallels an Egyptian folk tale. The tale of Si-Osiris (also called “Setme Khamwas”), preserved in Demotic Egyptian papyri, tells the story of a rich man and a poor man who die and have their afterlife positions reversed. The rich man suffers while the poor man is honored. This tale was widely known in the ancient Near East. Jesus appears to be adapting a familiar story format that His audience would recognize—using their own cultural framework to make a spiritual point. This is exactly how parables work.

To build a comprehensive doctrine of eternal conscious torment on this passage requires: (a) ignoring that it follows four consecutive parables, (b) ignoring that the audience is the Pharisees, (c) ignoring that the word is Hades (temporary) and not Gehenna or the Lake of Fire, (d) ignoring the main point of the story (about hearing Moses and the Prophets), and (e) treating parabolic imagery as literal geography of the afterlife. That is a lot of ignoring.

Word 3: γέεννα (Geenna) — The Valley of Hinnom

γέεννα
Geenna
GHEH-en-nah
G1067
From Hebrew גֵּיא־הִנֹּם (Gei-Hinnom)—“Valley of Hinnom.” A literal geographic location: a valley on the south and southwest side of Jerusalem. Occurs 12 times in the New Testament: 11 from Jesus, 1 from James. You can visit it today. It is now a park.

The History of the Valley

To understand what Jesus meant when He said “Gehenna,” you must understand what the Valley of Hinnom was. It has a long, dark, and specific history in the Hebrew Bible:

The Valley of Hinnom in the Old Testament

  • Joshua 15:8 — First mention. A geographic boundary marker for the tribal territories of Judah and Benjamin. “The border went up by the Valley of the Son of Hinnom (גֵּיא בֶן־הִנֹּם) to the southern slope of the Jebusite city (which is Jerusalem).” Just a valley. A border.
  • Joshua 18:16 — Same geographic reference for Benjamin’s territory. Still just a valley.
  • 2 Chronicles 28:3 — King Ahaz (c. 735–715 BC): “He burned incense in the Valley of the Son of Hinnom, and burned his children in the fire, according to the abominations of the nations whom the LORD had cast out before the children of Israel.” The valley becomes associated with the worst possible evil: child sacrifice to the Ammonite god Molech (also spelled Moloch). Metal statues of Molech were heated with fire, and living children were placed in the arms of the idol to burn alive, while drums were beaten to drown out the screaming.
  • 2 Chronicles 33:6 — King Manasseh (c. 697–643 BC): “He caused his sons to pass through the fire in the Valley of the Son of Hinnom.” Manasseh was the most wicked king in Judah’s history (2 Kings 21:9—“Manasseh seduced them to do more evil than the nations whom the LORD had destroyed”).
  • 2 Kings 23:10 — King Josiah (c. 640–609 BC): “And he defiled Topheth, which is in the Valley of the Son of Hinnom, that no man might make his son or his daughter pass through the fire to Molech.” תֹּפֶת (Topheth, H8612) was the specific site within the valley where the child sacrifices occurred. The word may derive from toph (תֹּף, drum)—the drums that drowned the screams. Josiah physically desecrated the site to make it unusable for sacrifice. He turned it into a place of refuse and shame.

God’s Response to the Valley of Hinnom

This is critical. God did not create a burning place for sinners modeled on the Valley of Hinnom. God’s response to what happened in that valley was horror and condemnation:

And they have built the high places of Topheth, which is in the Valley of the Son of Hinnom, to burn their sons and their daughters in the fire, which I did not command, nor did it come into My heart. — Jeremiah 7:31

Read that again. God says burning people in fire “did not come into My heart.” The Hebrew is וְלֹא עָלְתָה עַל־לִבִּי (v’lo aletah al-libbi)—literally, “and it did not go up upon My heart.” The concept of burning people alive was so repulsive to God that He says it never even entered His mind as a possibility. And yet the doctrine of eternal conscious torment says God designed a version of the Valley of Hinnom—except bigger, hotter, and lasting forever—as His plan for billions of people. The contradiction is staggering.

Therefore behold, the days are coming, says the LORD, when it will no more be called Topheth, or the Valley of the Son of Hinnom, but the Valley of Slaughter; for they will bury in Topheth until there is no room. — Jeremiah 7:32
They have built also the high places of Baal, to burn their sons with fire for burnt offerings unto Baal, which I commanded not, nor spake it, neither came it into My mind. — Jeremiah 19:5

God repeats Himself. Twice in Jeremiah, He denies any connection between Himself and the concept of burning people. If God says the idea of burning humans “never entered His mind,” can we then say He built an eternal version of that exact practice as the destiny of most of humanity? The theology contradicts the God who speaks it.

By Jesus’s Time: The Garbage Dump

After Josiah defiled the Valley of Hinnom in 621 BC, it was never used for sacrifice again. By the Second Temple period (516 BC–70 AD), the valley had become Jerusalem’s garbage dump. According to rabbinic tradition and archaeological evidence:

  • Trash, refuse, and dead animals were thrown into the valley.
  • Fires burned continuously to consume the waste.
  • Maggots and worms fed on the organic material that the fire didn’t reach.
  • It was a place of destruction and disposal—things thrown there were consumed and ceased to exist.
  • The smell, the smoke, the perpetual burning made it the most vivid possible image of waste, destruction, and irreversible loss.

When Jesus said “Gehenna,” His audience did not picture an invisible underground fire dimension. They pictured the dump. The valley they could see from the city walls. The place where worthless things were thrown away and destroyed—not preserved forever in torment.

Every Use of Gehenna by Jesus

All 12 Occurrences of γέεννα in the New Testament

  • Matthew 5:22 — “Whoever says ‘You fool!’ shall be in danger of Gehenna fire.” Part of the Sermon on the Mount. Jesus is escalating: anger → insult → contempt, with escalating consequences: judgment → council → Gehenna. He is using Gehenna as the ultimate image of waste and destruction. The point is about the seriousness of contempt, not a literal geography lesson about the afterlife.
  • Matthew 5:29-30 — “If your right eye causes you to sin, pluck it out and cast it from you; for it is more profitable for you that one of your members perish, than for your whole body to be cast into Gehenna.” Jesus is using hyperbole—a recognized rhetorical device He uses throughout the Sermon on the Mount. He is not literally commanding self-mutilation. He is saying: it is better to lose something valuable than to let your whole life be thrown onto the garbage heap. The image is destruction and waste, not eternal torture.
  • Matthew 10:28 — “Do not fear those who kill the body but cannot kill the soul. But rather fear Him who is able to destroy (απολέσαι, apolesai) both soul and body in Gehenna.” The verb is απόλλυμι (apollumi, G622)—“to destroy utterly, to bring to ruin, to cause to perish.” God is able to destroy. Not “able to preserve alive in torment.” The word is destruction. Things thrown into Gehenna were destroyed. They did not exist forever in a burning state.
  • Matthew 18:9 — “It is better for you to enter into life with one eye, rather than having two eyes, to be cast into Gehenna fire.” Parallel to Matthew 5:29. Same hyperbolic rhetorical structure. Same imagery of waste and destruction.
  • Matthew 23:15 — “Woe to you, scribes and Pharisees, hypocrites! For you travel land and sea to win one proselyte, and when he is won, you make him twice as much a son of Gehenna as yourselves.” A “son of Gehenna”—a Hebraism meaning “one destined for destruction, one characterized by worthlessness.” Jesus is calling the Pharisees and their converts garbage-dump people. The insult is about their spiritual worthlessness, not a prediction about their eternal address.
  • Matthew 23:33 — “Serpents, brood of vipers! How can you escape the condemnation of Gehenna?” Again, aimed at the Pharisees. “Condemnation of Gehenna”—the Greek is κρίσεως τῆς γεέννης (kriseōs tēs geennēs). Krisis (G2920) means judgment, decision, verdict—not necessarily “condemnation.” The judgment of Gehenna: the verdict that something is worthless and belongs on the garbage heap.
  • Mark 9:43,45,47 — Three-fold repetition of the hand/foot/eye hyperbole: “Better to enter life maimed than to be cast into Gehenna.” Mark 9:43 adds: “into the fire that shall never be quenched (τὸ πῦρ τὸ ἀσβεστον).” ἀσβεστον (asbestos, G762) means “unquenchable”—a fire that cannot be put out. This does not mean it burns forever. It means it burns until it has consumed everything. You cannot extinguish it before it finishes its work. Jeremiah 17:27 uses the same concept: “I will kindle a fire in the gates [of Jerusalem] and it shall not be quenched.” That fire is long out. Jerusalem’s gates are not still burning. Unquenchable means unstoppable, not eternal.
  • Mark 9:48 — “Where ‘their worm does not die, and the fire is not quenched.’” Jesus is directly quoting Isaiah 66:24. The full verse: “And they shall go forth and look upon the corpses (פִּגְרֵי, pigrei) of the men who have transgressed against Me. For their worm shall not die, and their fire shall not be quenched.” The Hebrew word is פֶּגֶר (peger, H6297)—“corpse, carcass, dead body.” These are dead bodies, not living people. Worms eat dead things. Fire consumes dead things. Isaiah is describing the aftermath of destruction—corpses being consumed by worms and fire. Not living beings consciously experiencing eternal pain.
  • Luke 12:5 — “Fear Him who, after He has killed, has power to cast into Gehenna.” The sequence: first killed, then cast into Gehenna. Not cast alive into Gehenna. Killed first. Then the body is disposed of in the dump. Destruction, then disposal. Not preservation in torment.
  • James 3:6 — “The tongue is a fire, a world of iniquity... and it is set on fire by Gehenna.” The only non-Jesus use of Gehenna. James uses it metaphorically—the tongue is “set on fire by Gehenna.” This is not about the afterlife. It is about the destructive, consuming power of careless speech. Gehenna is the image of destruction, not a literal location James is warning people they will go to.

In every use of Gehenna, the consistent image is destruction, waste, consumption, and loss—not preservation in conscious torment. Things thrown into the Valley of Hinnom were destroyed. They were consumed by fire and worms until nothing remained. Jesus uses this image to warn of the utter waste of a life lived apart from God—a life thrown away, destroyed, consumed. The garbage dump does not preserve its contents forever. It destroys them.

Word 4: ταρταρόω (Tartaroō) — The Angelic Prison

ταρταρόω
Tartaroō
tar-tah-ROH-oh
G5020
To cast into Tartarus, to consign to the deepest abyss. Borrowed from Greek mythology: Tartarus was the deepest region of the underworld, a prison for the Titans and those who offended the gods. Occurs exactly 1 time in the entire New Testament. Used exclusively for fallen angels—never for humans.
For if God did not spare the angels who sinned, but cast them down to Tartarus (ταρταρώσας, tartarosas) and delivered them into chains of darkness, to be reserved for judgment… — 2 Peter 2:4

Several critical observations:

1. This is a verb, not a noun. Peter doesn’t name a place called “Tartarus.” He uses the verb form: ταρταρώσας—“having cast into Tartarus.” It is an action (imprisonment), not a geographic location.

2. It is exclusively for angels, not humans. The subject is “the angels who sinned.” This is widely understood as a reference to the events of Genesis 6:1-4, where “the sons of God” (בְנֵי־הָאֱלֹהִים, b’nei-ha’elohim) took human wives and produced the Nephilim. This event is expanded in detail in 1 Enoch (the Book of Enoch), which was widely read and respected in Second Temple Judaism. Jude 6 parallels this: “And the angels who did not keep their proper domain, but left their own abode, He has reserved in everlasting chains under darkness for the judgment of the great day.”

3. It is a holding place, not a final destination. The angels are “reserved for judgment”—they are awaiting their final sentence. Tartarus is a prison, not a courthouse. The verdict has not been delivered. They are held in chains until it is.

4. Peter borrows the term from Greek mythology deliberately. His audience included Gentile converts who were familiar with Greek cosmology. In Greek mythology, Tartarus was the prison where Zeus confined the Titans after defeating them. Peter uses a term his readers would understand—the deepest possible prison—to communicate the severity of these angels’ confinement. He is not endorsing Greek mythology. He is using its vocabulary.

This word has nothing to do with human beings, human destiny, or the afterlife. It is mentioned once, in reference to imprisoned angels, and it was translated as “hell” in the KJV—further muddying the waters of an already confused English translation.

The Lake of Fire (λίμνη τοῦ πυρός) — The Second Death

λίμνη τοῦ πυρός
Limnē tou Puros
LIM-nay too poo-ROSS
“Lake of fire.” Appears only in Revelation (19:20; 20:10,14,15; 21:8). The text itself defines it: “This is the second death” (Revelation 20:14, 21:8). It is distinct from Hades, which is thrown into it.

The Lake of Fire is not Sheol. It is not Hades. It is not Gehenna. It is not Tartarus. It is a fifth concept—found exclusively in the book of Revelation, a book written in apocalyptic symbolic language.

Every Reference to the Lake of Fire

  • Revelation 19:20 — “The beast was captured, and with him the false prophet... These two were cast alive into the lake of fire burning with brimstone.” The first occupants of the lake of fire are the beast and the false prophet—symbolic figures, not individual humans.
  • Revelation 20:10 — “The devil, who deceived them, was cast into the lake of fire and brimstone where the beast and the false prophet are. And they will be tormented day and night forever and ever (εἰς τους αἰῶνας τῶν αἰωνων).” The devil joins them. The phrase “forever and ever” is εἰς τους αἰῶνας τῶν αἰωνων (eis tous aiōnas tōn aiōnōn)—literally, “into the ages of the ages.” We will examine aiōn below.
  • Revelation 20:14 — “Then Death and Hades were cast into the lake of fire. This is the second death.” The text defines its own term. The lake of fire is not eternal conscious torment. It is the second death. Death. Not eternal life in pain. Death.
  • Revelation 20:15 — “And anyone not found written in the Book of Life was cast into the lake of fire.”
  • Revelation 21:8 — “But the cowardly, unbelieving, abominable... shall have their part in the lake which burns with fire and brimstone, which is the second death.” Again: the text defines the lake of fire as the second death.

Death, Not Eternal Living Torment

If the wicked are tormented forever in the Lake of Fire, they have eternal life—just a painful version. But the Bible consistently contrasts eternal life with death and destruction, not with eternal life in torment:

The Biblical Contrast: Life vs. Death

  • John 3:16 — “For God so loved the world that He gave His only begotten Son, that whoever believes in Him should not perish (απόληται, apolētai) but have everlasting life.” Two options: perish or everlasting life. Not “everlasting torment” or “everlasting life.” The verb απόλλυμι (apollumi, G622) means to destroy, to bring to utter ruin, to cause to cease to exist. The opposite of eternal life is not eternal torture. It is destruction.
  • Romans 6:23 — “For the wages of sin is death (θάνατος, thanatos), but the gift of God is eternal life in Christ Jesus our Lord.” The wage is death. Not eternal conscious torment. Death. Cessation. The end of existence. Contrasted with the gift of eternal life.
  • Matthew 7:13-14 — “Wide is the gate and broad is the way that leads to destruction (απώλειαν, apōleian), and there are many who go in by it. Narrow is the gate and difficult is the way which leads to life.” Two paths: destruction or life. Not torment or life.
  • Philippians 3:19 — “Whose end is destruction (απώλεια).” Their end. Their finish. Destruction.
  • 2 Thessalonians 1:9 — “These shall be punished with everlasting destruction (ὀλεθρον αἰωνιον, olethron aiōnion) from the presence of the Lord.” ὄλεθρος (olethros, G3639) means ruin, destruction, death. “Everlasting destruction”—destruction that is complete and permanent. Not destruction that somehow continues forever as a conscious experience.
  • James 4:12 — “There is one Lawgiver, who is able to save and to destroy (απολέσαι).” Two abilities: save or destroy. Not save or torment eternally.
  • 2 Peter 3:7 — “Reserved for fire until the day of judgment and perdition (απωλείας) of ungodly men.” Perdition: utter destruction, complete loss.

The biblical vocabulary is relentlessly consistent: απόλλυμι (destroy), απώλεια (destruction), ὀλεθρος (ruin), θάνατος (death). These words all mean the same thing: cessation, destruction, the end of existence. Not one of them means “kept alive in conscious pain forever.”

The αἰών (Aiōn) Problem: “Eternal” Doesn’t Mean What You Think

αἰών
Aiōn
eye-OHN
G165
An age, an era, a period of time. A long but finite duration. NOT inherently “eternal” or “infinite.” The English word “eon” derives from this Greek word. Occurs 122 times in the New Testament.
αἰώνιος
Aiōnios
eye-OH-nee-oss
G166
Age-lasting, pertaining to an age, enduring for an age. The adjective form of αἰών. Translated “eternal,” “everlasting,” or “forever” in most English Bibles. Occurs 71 times in the New Testament.

The doctrine of eternal conscious torment stands or falls on whether αἰώνιος means “literally infinite, without end, lasting forever” or “pertaining to an age, age-lasting, enduring for a long period.” The answer is found in how the New Testament itself uses the word:

Uses of αἰώνιος That Cannot Mean “Infinite”

  • Romans 16:25 — “The mystery kept secret since the world began (χρόνοις αἰωνίοις, chronois aiōniois).” Literally: “in times age-lasting.” The mystery was kept secret for a long time, but it has now been revealed. It was not kept secret for infinite time, because it had a beginning (creation) and an end (the revelation in Christ). Aiōnios here clearly means “for a long age”—not “literally forever.”
  • 2 Timothy 1:9 — “Grace which was given to us in Christ Jesus before time began (προ χρόνων αἰωνίων).” Before aiōnios times. If aiōnios means “infinite,” then this says “before infinite time”—which is logically impossible. It means “before the ages began.”
  • Titus 1:2 — “God, who cannot lie, promised before time began (προ χρόνων αἰωνίων).” Same construction. “Before the ages.”
  • Jude 7 — “Sodom and Gomorrah... are set forth as an example, suffering the vengeance of eternal fire (πυρος αἰωνίου).” Sodom and Gomorrah suffered “eternal fire.” But Sodom and Gomorrah are not still burning. The fire completed its work. The cities were destroyed. The fire was “eternal” in its effect (permanent destruction), not in its duration (burning forever).
  • Philemon 15 — “Perhaps he departed for a while for this purpose, that you might receive him forever (αἰώνιον).” Paul writes about Onesimus the slave. He says Philemon might receive him back “forever”—meaning “permanently, for the rest of his life.” Not literally for infinite time. Both Paul and Philemon were mortal.

Matthew 25:46 — The Key Verse

And these will go away into everlasting (αἰώνιον) punishment (κόλασιν), but the righteous into eternal (αἰώνιον) life. — Matthew 25:46

This verse is the strongest argument for eternal conscious torment. The logic: if “eternal life” is truly eternal, then “eternal punishment” must also be truly eternal. Same adjective, same duration.

But notice the word for “punishment”: κόλασιν (kolasin, G2851). This word means pruning, correction, remedial punishment. It comes from κολάζω (kolazō)—“to prune, to curtail, to check, to correct.” Aristotle (Rhetoric 1.10.17) distinguished kolasis from timoria (τιμωρία): kolasis is punishment for the benefit of the one being punished (corrective); timoria is punishment for the satisfaction of the punisher (retributive). The New Testament chose kolasis—the corrective word.

Furthermore, even if aiōnios means “lasting for an age,” the contrast still works: the righteous receive life that lasts for the age to come (which, because God sustains it, does not end), while the wicked receive age-lasting punishment (which, because it is corrective and finite, accomplishes its purpose and concludes). The adjective describes the quality of belonging to the age to come, not necessarily infinite duration in every context.

What the Early Church Actually Believed

The doctrine of eternal conscious torment was not the majority position of the early church. This is a historical fact, documented in the writings of the church fathers themselves. Of the six major theological schools in the first five centuries of Christianity:

School Location Language Position on Final Punishment
Alexandria Egypt Greek Universal reconciliation (apokatastasis). All will eventually be restored. Taught by Clement of Alexandria and Origen.
Caesarea Palestine Greek Universal reconciliation. Founded by Origen’s students. Continued his teaching.
Antioch Syria Greek/Syriac Universal reconciliation. Theodore of Mopsuestia, Diodore of Tarsus.
Edessa/Nisibis Mesopotamia Syriac Universal reconciliation. Closest to the original Aramaic-speaking church.
Ephesus/Asia Minor Modern Turkey Greek Annihilationism (conditional immortality). The wicked cease to exist.
Carthage/Rome North Africa / Italy Latin Eternal torment. Taught by Tertullian, codified by Augustine.

Four out of six schools taught universal reconciliation—that God would eventually restore all things and all people. One taught annihilationism—that the wicked would simply cease to exist. Only one—the Latin-speaking school in the Western Roman Empire—taught eternal conscious torment. And this is the one that won, not because of better exegesis, but because of political power.

Key Early Church Fathers

Origen of Alexandria (c. 185–253 AD): The most prolific and influential theologian of the first three centuries. A native Greek speaker who read the New Testament in his mother tongue. Taught αποκατάστασις (apokatastasis, G605)—“the restoration of all things” (cf. Acts 3:21). Argued that aiōnios punishment was corrective and temporary, leading ultimately to the reconciliation of all creation to God. His views were widespread and mainstream for centuries.

Clement of Alexandria (c. 150–215 AD): Origen’s teacher. Wrote in Stromata that God’s punishments are “saving and disciplinary” (σωτηρίους καὶ παιδευτικάς), not vindictive or eternal.

Gregory of Nyssa (c. 335–395 AD): One of the Cappadocian Fathers. Canonized as a saint by both the Catholic and Orthodox churches. Explicitly taught universal reconciliation. In On the Soul and the Resurrection, he wrote that just as gold is purified by fire (the dross is burned away, not the gold), so God’s fire purifies souls—destroying evil, not the person.

Gregory of Nazianzus (c. 329–390 AD): Another Cappadocian Father. Called “The Theologian” by the Eastern church (only three people bear this title). Spoke of God’s fire as purifying, not punitive. Expressed hope for universal restoration.

Isaac of Nineveh (c. 613–700 AD): Syrian mystic and bishop. Wrote: “Those who are punished in Gehenna are scourged by the scourge of love... It is not right to say that sinners in Gehenna are deprived of the love of God... But the power of love works in two ways: it torments those who have played the fool, even as happens here when a friend suffers from a friend; but it becomes a source of joy for those who have observed its duties.” For Isaac, the “fire” of judgment is God’s love—experienced as purifying pain by those who resisted it, and as joy by those who embraced it.

How Eternal Torment Won: Augustine

Augustine of Hippo (354–430 AD) was the most influential theologian in Western Christian history. He codified the doctrine of eternal conscious torment in City of God (Book 21). He also:

  • Did not read Greek fluently. He worked primarily from the Latin Vulgate and Latin translations. The nuances of aiōn, aiōnios, kolasis, and apollumi were not available to him in their original force.
  • Argued against Origen by name. In City of God 21.17-23, Augustine specifically attacks the “merciful ones” (misericordes) who believed in universal reconciliation. He acknowledges that this was a widespread position and argues against it—proving it was common, not fringe.
  • Used political power. Augustine’s theology aligned with the interests of the Western Roman church, which needed doctrinal uniformity and institutional authority. Terrified people are obedient people. A God who will torture you forever if you step out of line is a powerful motivator for institutional compliance.

After Augustine, the Western church largely adopted eternal conscious torment as dogma. The Eastern church was slower to follow. The Second Council of Constantinople (553 AD) condemned certain propositions attributed to Origen (though scholars debate whether Origen actually held all the views condemned), but even this council did not formally define eternal conscious torment as official doctrine. The condemnation was political as much as theological.

The Cultural Additions

4th–5th Century
Augustine codifies eternal torment in Latin, working from the Vulgate. The Greek-speaking East is unconvinced but politically outmaneuvered.
553 AD
Second Council of Constantinople condemns certain Origenist propositions. The political context: Emperor Justinian pushes the condemnation to resolve theological disputes disrupting imperial unity.
1320 AD
Dante Alighieri publishes the Inferno—the first book of the Divine Comedy. A poem. Fiction. Not Scripture. Not theology. A medieval Italian political satire in which Dante places his personal enemies in various circles of a hell he invented. The nine circles, the frozen lake of Satan, the specific tortures mapped to specific sins—all of this is Dante, not the Bible. And yet Dante’s imagery has shaped the popular concept of “hell” more than any other single source, including Scripture.
14th–16th Century
Medieval art floods Europe with depictions of hell: demons with pitchforks, cauldrons of boiling sinners, grotesque torture chambers. Artists like Hieronymus Bosch (The Garden of Earthly Delights, c. 1500) created vivid, terrifying images that became embedded in Western cultural consciousness. None of this is in the Bible.
1741
Jonathan Edwards preaches “Sinners in the Hands of an Angry God.” The most famous sermon in American history. Edwards describes God dangling sinners over the pit of hell “like a spider, or some loathsome insect over the fire.” The sermon caused people to faint, scream, and clutch the pews in terror. It became the template for revivalist preaching and cemented eternal conscious torment in American evangelical culture.

The doctrine of eternal conscious torment is built on: (1) a Latin theologian who didn’t read Greek, (2) a political church council, (3) an Italian poem, (4) medieval horror art, and (5) a Colonial-era sermon designed to terrify. None of these are Scripture. The actual Greek and Hebrew words of the Bible describe death, destruction, and the grave—not infinite conscious suffering.

The Character of God

Ultimately, the question of “hell” is not a linguistic question or a historical question. It is a question about the character of God. Is the God revealed in Scripture—the God who is love, whose mercy endures forever, who takes no pleasure in the death of the wicked, who desires all to be saved—is this God going to torture billions of conscious beings for infinite time as punishment for finite sins committed during a seventy-year life they did not ask for?

What God Says About His Own Character

  • 1 John 4:8 — “God is love (ὁ θεος αγάπη εστίν).” Not “God has love” or “God shows love.” God is love. It is His nature, His essence, His identity.
  • 1 Timothy 2:4 — “God our Savior, who desires all men to be saved and to come to the knowledge of the truth.” Not some men. Not the elect. All (πάντας, pantas) men.
  • 2 Peter 3:9 — “The Lord is... not willing that any should perish but that all should come to repentance.” God’s will: that none perish. If God is omnipotent and His will is that none perish, and yet the majority perish in eternal torment, then God’s will is frustrated by the very creatures He made.
  • Ezekiel 33:11 — “As I live, says the Lord GOD, I have no pleasure in the death of the wicked, but that the wicked turn from his way and live. Turn, turn from your evil ways! For why should you die, O house of Israel?” God swears by His own life: He takes no pleasure in the death of the wicked. Eternal conscious torment is not death—it is eternal dying without the relief of death. Would God take pleasure in that?
  • Psalm 136 — “His mercy ( חֶסֶד, chesed) endures forever.” Repeated 26 times in one psalm. Chesed—covenant loyalty, steadfast love, mercy that does not quit. It endures forever. Does it endure forever for the saved but expire for the lost? The psalmist makes no such distinction.
  • Lamentations 3:31-33 — “For the Lord will not cast off forever. Though He causes grief, yet He will show compassion according to the multitude of His mercies. For He does not afflict willingly (מִלִּבּוֹ, milibo—literally “from His heart”), nor grieve the children of men.” God does not afflict from His heart. Affliction is not His nature. It is not what He wants.

The doctrine of eternal conscious torment requires a God who:

  • Is love—but tortures people forever
  • Desires all to be saved—but fails to save most
  • Takes no pleasure in death—but inflicts something worse than death for eternity
  • Says burning people “never entered His mind”—but designed an eternal version of exactly that
  • Has mercy that endures forever—except for those in “hell”
  • Does not afflict willingly—but created a system that requires the majority of His creatures to suffer infinitely

Either these verses about God’s character are true, or the doctrine of eternal conscious torment is true. They cannot both be true. The text forces a choice. And the text was written in Greek and Hebrew words that consistently describe death, destruction, and the grave—not infinite conscious suffering.

The Bottom Line

The English word “hell” does not appear in the original text of the Bible. What does appear is: Sheol (the grave, 65 times), Hades (the unseen realm, 10 times), Gehenna (Jerusalem’s garbage dump, 12 times), and Tartarus (an angelic prison, 1 time). English translators collapsed all four into one word and built a doctrine that the Greek-speaking early church did not believe.

The biblical vocabulary of final punishment is destruction (απώλεια), death (θάνατος), perishing (απόλλυμι), and ruin (ὀλεθρος). The Revelation text defines the Lake of Fire as “the second death”—not the second life in torment. The word aiōnios means “age-lasting,” not “literally infinite.” The word kolasis means “corrective punishment,” not “retributive torture.” God Himself says burning people “never entered His mind.” And four of the six earliest theological schools taught universal reconciliation.

The doctrine of eternal conscious torment was codified by a Latin theologian who did not read Greek, popularized by a medieval Italian poem, visualized by horror artists, and weaponized by revivalist preachers. It is tradition dressed as Scripture.

The actual text, in the actual languages it was written in, tells a different story: a story of a God whose mercy endures forever, who desires all to be saved, who takes no pleasure in the death of the wicked, and who will one day destroy Death and Hades themselves—not preserve them for eternity.

“The last enemy that will be destroyed is death.” — 1 Corinthians 15:26
