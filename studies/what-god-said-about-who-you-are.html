What God Said About Who You Are

Every Name He Gave You in the Original Languages — and Why the English Barely Scratches the Surface

~75 min read • 25 Hebrew & Greek words explored • 65+ cross-references
See what kind of love the Father has given to us, that we should be called children of God — and so we are. — 1 John 3:1

Introduction: The Names You Were Given

Most people who have spent any time in a church have heard some version of “you are a child of God.” It sounds nice. It shows up on bookmarks and coffee mugs. But like a lot of things that get repeated until they become wallpaper, the phrase has lost its weight. It has become religious background noise—a warm sentiment with no teeth.

The original languages tell a different story. When the writers of the New Testament described who you are in relation to God, they did not reach for vague, sentimental words. They used precise, loaded, culturally explosive terms that would have stopped a first-century reader in their tracks. They used legal language from Roman adoption law. They used intimate family language that no Jew would have dared apply to their relationship with Yahweh. They used words that described a change so fundamental it was not improvement but a new species of existence.

This study is not about what is wrong with a translation. It is about what is waiting inside the original words—depth that English can gesture toward but never fully carry. Every term explored here is verifiable in any Greek lexicon. None of this is hidden. It is just overlooked.

Here is what God said about who you are.

Agapētos: “Beloved” — More Than a Word

The most common identity word God uses for His people in the New Testament is one most readers skip right over:

ἀγαπητός
agapētos
ah-gah-pay-TOSS
G27
Beloved, dearly loved, worthy of love. Adjective from agapaō (“to love”). Used 62 times in the NT. This is the word the Father uses for Jesus at His baptism: “This is my beloved (agapētos) Son.” And it is the same word Paul, Peter, and John use for believers. You are addressed with the same word God uses for His own Son.

Pause on that. When God spoke from heaven over Jesus at the Jordan River, He said:

This is My beloved Son, in whom I am well pleased. — Matthew 3:17

The word is agapētos. And when Paul writes to the believers in Rome, Corinth, Ephesus, Philippi, Colossae, and Thessalonica, what does he call them? Agapētoi—the plural of the same word. “Beloved ones.”

To all who are in Rome, beloved of God, called saints. — Romans 1:7
Therefore, as the elect of God, holy and beloved, put on tender mercies, kindness, humility, meekness, longsuffering. — Colossians 3:12

This is not a greeting card. The word the Father reserves for His Son is the same word applied to you. In the original Greek, there is no downgrade. No lesser version. No “beloved, but not as beloved as Jesus.” The same agapētos. You are beloved with the same quality of love.

ἀγαπάω
agapaō
ah-gah-PAH-oh
G25
To love (with deliberate, chosen, self-giving love). Used 143 times in the NT. This is not phileō (affectionate love between friends) or eros (romantic love, not used in the NT). Agapaō is volitional—a love that chooses, commits, and gives regardless of the worthiness of the recipient. It is the word used in John 3:16 (“God so loved”) and 1 John 4:8 (“God is love”).

The root verb agapaō describes love that chooses its object regardless of merit. It is not a feeling. It is a commitment. When the NT says you are agapētos (beloved), it means you are the chosen object of God’s deliberate, self-giving, nothing-you-did-to-earn-it love. This is not “God loves you” as a bumper sticker. This is a statement about reality—about who you are.

John goes further:

See what kind of love the Father has given to us, that we should be called children of God—and so we are. — 1 John 3:1
ποταπήν
potapēn
po-tah-PAYN
G4217
What sort of, what kind of, from what country. Originally potapos meant “from what land?”—implying something so foreign and extraordinary that you have to ask where it comes from. John is saying: “Look at this love—what KIND is this? Where does love like this even come from?” It is an expression of astonishment.

Potapēn agapēn—“what kind of love.” John is not calmly informing you that God loves you. He is marveling. He is saying: look at this. What kind of love is this? Where does love like this even exist? And then the kicker: “that we should be called children of God—and so we are.” Not metaphorically. Not sentimentally. And so we are.

Tekna Theou: Children of God — Not Servants, Not Subjects

The New Testament uses two different Greek words for “children” in relation to God. The distinction matters enormously:

τέκνον
teknon
TEK-non
G5043
Child (born of a parent). From tiktō (“to give birth, to bring forth”). Emphasizes ORIGIN and NATURE—a child who shares the parent’s nature because they came from the parent. Used for believers in John 1:12, 1 John 3:1-2, Romans 8:16-17, Philippians 2:15. You are a teknon of God—you share His nature because you came from Him.
υἱός
huios
hwee-OSS
G5207
Son. Emphasizes POSITION, RIGHTS, and MATURITY—a son who has the legal standing and inheritance of the father. Used in Galatians 3:26, 4:6-7, Romans 8:14,19. A huios is not just born of the father; he bears the father’s authority and name.

Teknon emphasizes nature—you share God’s nature because you were born from Him. Huios emphasizes position—you have the legal standing, rights, and inheritance of a son. The NT applies both to believers. You have the nature (teknon) AND the standing (huios).

But as many as received Him, to them He gave the right to become children (tekna) of God, to those who believe in His name. — John 1:12
ἐξουσία
exousia
ex-oo-SEE-ah
G1849
Authority, right, power, permission. From exesti (“it is permitted, it is lawful”). NOT dynamis (raw power). Exousia is AUTHORIZED power—the legitimate right to act. God gave you the EXOUSIA—the authorized right—to become His tekna. This is a legal grant of status.

God gave the exousia—the authorized right—to become His children. This is legal language. It is not “God lets you feel like His child.” It is “God grants you the legal authority and standing of a child in His household.” And the next verse specifies the origin:

Who were born, not of blood, nor of the will of the flesh, nor of the will of man, but of God. — John 1:13

Born of God. You share His nature. Not metaphorically. John insists on this:

Beloved, now we are children (tekna) of God; and it has not yet been revealed what we shall be, but we know that when He is revealed, we shall be like Him, for we shall see Him as He is. — 1 John 3:2
ὅμοιοι
homoioi
HO-moy-oy
G3664
Like, similar, resembling. From homos (“same”). “We shall be like (homoioi) Him.” Not merely near Him. Not merely with Him. LIKE Him—sharing His likeness, resembling Him. The trajectory of the child is to grow into the likeness of the Father.

“We shall be homoioi Him”—like Him. The child grows into the likeness of the Father. This is not servitude. This is not “unworthy worm who owes God everything.” This is a child becoming what the Father is. The trajectory of the gospel is not groveling sinners kept at arm’s length from a holy God. It is children growing into the full likeness of their Father.

Huiothesia: Placed as Sons — Roman Adoption and Your Legal Standing

Paul uses a term that would have electrified every Roman citizen who heard it:

υἱοθεσία
huiothesia
hwee-oh-theh-SEE-ah
G5206
Adoption as sons, placement as a son. From huios (“son”) + thesis (“a placing, a setting”). A Roman legal term. In Roman law, adoption (adoptio) was irrevocable. The adopted son received full legal rights: the father’s name, full inheritance rights, all previous debts canceled, and a completely new legal identity. The old family had no more claim. Used 5 times in the NT (Rom 8:15, 8:23, 9:4; Gal 4:5; Eph 1:5).

In the Roman world, huiothesia (adoption) was one of the most powerful legal acts possible. Here is what Roman adoption legally meant:

  • All previous debts were canceled. The adopted son’s old obligations, legal liabilities, and debts ceased to exist. He was legally a new person.
  • He received the father’s name. He was no longer identified by his previous family. He carried his new father’s name and status.
  • He became a full heir. Not a partial heir. Not a second-class heir. He had the same inheritance rights as a naturally-born son—and in some cases, greater rights, because adoption was a deliberate choice.
  • The adoption was irrevocable. A natural son could be disowned under Roman law. An adopted son could not. The adoption could never be undone. This is staggering: the adopted son had more legal security than a biological son.
  • His old family lost all claim. His previous father, previous family name, previous identity—all legally severed. He was, in the eyes of the law, a completely new person with a completely new history.

Now read Paul:

For you did not receive the spirit of bondage again to fear, but you received the Spirit of adoption (huiothesia) by whom we cry out, “Abba, Father.” — Romans 8:15
ἀββα
abba
AB-bah
G5
Aramaic: Father, papa, daddy. An intimate family term. In first-century Judaism, addressing God as “Abba” was virtually unheard of—it was considered too intimate, too familiar. Jesus used it (Mark 14:36). Paul says the Spirit within you uses it. You call the Creator of the universe “Abba”—not because you are being casual, but because you are family.

The Spirit of huiothesia—the Spirit of being-placed-as-a-son. Not the spirit of slavery. Not the spirit of fear. Not the spirit of “unworthy servant groveling before an angry God.” The spirit of a child who has been legally, permanently, irrevocably placed in the family, who looks up and calls the Creator Abba.

And because you are sons, God has sent forth the Spirit of His Son into your hearts, crying out, “Abba, Father!” Therefore you are no longer a slave but a son, and if a son, then an heir of God through Christ. — Galatians 4:6-7
κληρονόμος
klēronomos
klay-ro-NO-moss
G2818
Heir, one who inherits. From klēros (“a lot, a portion, an inheritance”) + nomos (“law”). A legal term: one who inherits by law. Paul says you are a klēronomos of God—a legal heir of the Creator. In Romans 8:17, he specifies: “heirs of God and CO-heirs (synklēronomoi) with Christ.” You inherit alongside Christ, not beneath Him.

No longer a slave but a son. And if a son, an heir. This is not theological abstraction. Every Roman citizen who heard this letter read aloud in Galatia knew exactly what Paul was saying. You have been adopted into the household of God with the same legal force as Roman adoptio. Your old debts are canceled. Your old identity is gone. You carry a new name. You are a full heir—and the adoption cannot be revoked.

The Spirit Himself bears witness with our spirit that we are children of God, and if children, then heirs—heirs of God and joint heirs (synklēronomoi) with Christ. — Romans 8:16-17
συγκληρονόμοι
synklēronomoi
soong-klay-ro-NO-moy
G4789
Joint heirs, co-heirs, fellow heirs. From syn (“together with”) + klēronomos (“heir”). Not heirs of a lesser portion. Not servants who get the scraps after the Son receives the inheritance. CO-heirs. What Christ inherits, you inherit with Him. Together.

Synklēronomoi Christou—co-heirs with Christ. Not servants who receive a portion. Not employees who earn a wage. Co-heirs. What the Son inherits, you inherit with Him, alongside Him. This is the legal standing of a fully adopted Roman son—applied to your relationship with the living God.

Klētos and Hagios: Called Ones and Set-Apart Ones

Two identity words that appear constantly in Paul’s letters, usually in the opening greeting:

κλητός
klētos
klay-TOSS
G2822
Called, invited, appointed. From kaleō (“to call”). A klētos is someone who has been personally called—like an official invitation, a summons, an appointment. Used in Rom 1:1,6-7; 1 Cor 1:2,24; Jude 1:1; Rev 17:14. You are not a volunteer. You are not someone who showed up. You were called—summoned by name.
To all who are in Rome, beloved of God, called (klētois) saints. — Romans 1:7

Klētois hagiois—“called holy ones.” You were summoned. Invited by name. Not because you applied. Not because you qualified. Because God called you. The word klētos carries the weight of a royal summons—an invitation you did not earn and cannot boast about.

ὅγιος
hagios
HAH-gee-oss
G40
Holy, set apart, sacred, consecrated. Used 233 times in the NT. Translated “saints” when applied to believers (but “saints” in English has acquired connotations of exceptional holiness that the Greek does not carry). Hagios means SET APART for a purpose—dedicated, reserved, belonging to God. Every believer is a hagios. Not because of moral perfection. Because God set you apart.

Here is what hagios does NOT mean: it does not mean “morally perfect person who never sins.” The English word “saint” has been corrupted to mean someone with a halo—a dead person the Catholic church officially canonized after verifying miracles. That is not what the Greek says. Paul addresses the Corinthians as hagioi (1 Corinthians 1:2)—the same Corinthians who were getting drunk at communion, suing each other, and tolerating sexual immorality. They were a mess. And Paul calls them hagioi—set apart ones.

Because hagios describes your status, not your performance. A temple vessel was hagios—not because the cup was morally superior to other cups, but because it was set apart for God’s use. You are hagios because God set you apart. Your identity is “set apart for God”—not “person who has achieved sufficient moral purity.”

The Hebrew equivalent deepens this further:

קָדוֹשׁ
qadosh
kah-DOHSH
H6918
Holy, sacred, set apart. The primary Hebrew word for “holy.” Used for God (“Holy, holy, holy”—Isa 6:3), for Israel (“a holy nation”—Ex 19:6), and for objects dedicated to God. The root qdsh means to consecrate, to set apart, to dedicate. Holiness is fundamentally about belonging—being set apart FOR someone.

In Hebrew, qadosh is first and foremost about belonging. The Sabbath was qadosh—it belonged to God. The temple vessels were qadosh—they belonged to God. Israel was qadosh—they belonged to God. Holiness is not primarily about purity rules (though it includes that). It is about who you belong to. You are holy because you are His.

Kainē Ktisis: New Creation — Not a Better Version of the Old

Paul makes one of the most staggering identity statements in the entire New Testament:

Therefore, if anyone is in Christ, he is a new creation; old things have passed away; behold, all things have become new. — 2 Corinthians 5:17
καινὴ κτίσις
kainē ktisis
kie-NAY KTEE-sis
G2537 + G2937
New creation. Kainē = new in KIND, new in quality, unprecedented (not neos = new in time, recent, young). Ktisis = creation, the act of creating (from ktizō, “to create”). A kainē ktisis is not a renovation. Not an upgrade. Not a patched version. It is something of a DIFFERENT KIND that did not exist before—brought into being by an act of creation.

Two Greek words for “new” exist, and the distinction is critical:

νέος
neos
NEH-oss
G3501
New in TIME, recent, young. A neos coat is a recently made coat—same kind of coat, just newer. Neos wine is young wine—same substance, just freshly made.

Paul does not use neos. He uses kainē. You are not a newer version of your old self. You are not You 2.0. You are a new kind of thing—something that did not exist before, brought into being by the creative power of God. The word ktisis (creation) is the same word used for God’s original creation of the world. What God did in Genesis 1, He does in you: He creates.

For we are His workmanship, created in Christ Jesus for good works, which God prepared beforehand that we should walk in them. — Ephesians 2:10
ποίημα
poiēma
POY-ay-mah
G4161
Workmanship, handiwork, something made. From poieō (“to make, to create”). The English word “poem” comes from this Greek word. You are God’s poiēma—His crafted work, His masterpiece, His poem. This is an artisan word. You are not a mass-produced product. You are handmade.

Poiēma. God’s workmanship. His handiwork. The word English gets “poem” from. You are God’s poem—His crafted, intentional, artistic creation. Not an accident. Not a sinner barely tolerated. A masterwork.

Basileion Hierateuma: A Royal Priesthood — What You Have Access To

Peter takes the language God used for Israel at Sinai and applies it directly to all believers:

But you are a chosen generation, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, His own special people, that you may proclaim the praises of Him who called you out of darkness into His marvelous light. — 1 Peter 2:9

Four identity statements, each loaded:

γένος ἐκλεκτόν
genos eklekton
GEH-noss ek-lek-TOHN
G1085 + G1588
Chosen race/kind. Genos = race, family, kind, offspring (from ginomai, “to become”). Eklekton = chosen, selected, picked out (from eklegomai, “to choose out”). You are not just chosen individually—you belong to a chosen GENOS, a new kind of people, a new race that exists because God chose it into being.
βασίλειον ἱεράτευμα
basileion hierateuma
bah-SIL-ay-on hee-eh-RAH-tew-mah
G934 + G2406
Royal priesthood. Basileion = royal, kingly (from basileus, “king”). Hierateuma = a body of priests, a priesthood. Not individual priests but a collective priesthood. Every one of you has priestly access to God—no mediator required, no special class needed, direct access to the presence of the Creator.
ἔθνος ὅγιον
ethnos hagion
ETH-noss HAH-gee-on
G1484 + G40
Holy nation. Ethnos = nation, people group (source of “ethnic”). Hagion = holy, set apart. A nation set apart—a people who exist across every human ethnicity, drawn from every nation on earth, yet forming one holy ethnos under God.
λαὸς εἰς περιποίησιν
laos eis peripoiēsin
lah-OSS ice peh-ree-POY-ay-sin
G2992 + G4047
A people for [God’s own] possession. Laos = people. Peripoiēsis = acquisition, possession, preservation (from peripoieō, “to acquire for oneself, to preserve”). You are a people God ACQUIRED for Himself—His own possession, His treasured property. Not servants He tolerates. Treasure He purchased.

Read those four titles together: A chosen race. A royal priesthood. A holy nation. God’s own treasured possession. This is not the language of a God who barely tolerates sinners. This is the language of a Father who is proud of His children. Who chose them. Who made them royalty. Who set them apart as His own.

Peter is quoting God’s words to Israel at Sinai (Exodus 19:5-6), using the Hebrew terms:

סְגֻלָּה
səgullah
seh-gool-LAH
H5459
Treasured possession, special treasure, personal property. Used 8 times in the OT. A king’s private treasure—not his national treasury, but his PERSONAL collection. The most valued items in the kingdom. God calls His people His səgullah—His private, personal, most valued treasure.

Səgullah—a king’s private treasure. Not the national treasury that belongs to the state. The king’s personal jewelry box. The items he values most in the world. That is what God calls you.

Tselem and Eikōn: Made in the Image — What It Means to Carry the Likeness

Before any covenant, any law, any religious system—before Abraham, before Moses, before the church—God said something about every human being:

Then God said, “Let Us make man in Our image, according to Our likeness.” — Genesis 1:26
צֶלֶם
tselem
TSEH-lem
H6754
Image, likeness, resemblance, representative figure. Used 17 times in the OT. In the ancient Near East, a king would set up a tselem (statue/image) of himself in distant provinces to represent his authority and presence. To be made in God’s tselem means you are His representative on earth—carrying His authority, reflecting His nature, standing in for Him in creation.
דְּמוּת
dəmuth
deh-MOOTH
H1823
Likeness, resemblance, model, pattern. From damah (“to be like, to resemble”). While tselem is the image (the statue, the representation), dəmuth is the likeness (the resemblance, the family similarity). Together: you are God’s representative (tselem) who resembles Him (dəmuth).

In the ancient world, when a king conquered a territory, he would set up a tselem—an image of himself—in the new province. The image said: “This territory belongs to the king. His authority is here. He is represented here.” To deface the king’s image was treason.

When Genesis says you are made in God’s tselem, it is saying: you are God’s representative in creation. You carry His authority. You reflect His nature. You stand in His place in the physical world. Every human being—regardless of race, gender, status, or religion—is a walking image of the Creator. To harm a person is to deface the image of God.

The Greek New Testament takes this further with Paul:

For whom He foreknew, He also predestined to be conformed to the image (eikōn) of His Son, that He might be the firstborn among many brethren. — Romans 8:29
εἰκών
eikōn
eye-KOHN
G1504
Image, likeness, representation. The Greek equivalent of Hebrew tselem. Source of the English word “icon.” An eikōn is not a faint copy—it is a true representation. Christ is the eikōn of the invisible God (Col 1:15). You are being conformed to the eikōn of Christ. The image is being restored, completed, brought to full expression.

The trajectory: created in the tselem of God (Genesis 1), being conformed to the eikōn of Christ (Romans 8:29), until we are homoioi—like Him (1 John 3:2). The image of God in you is not static. It is being brought to completion. You are not a failed image trying to earn restoration. You are an image being unveiled—the glory that was always there, being progressively revealed.

But we all, with unveiled face, beholding as in a mirror the glory of the Lord, are being transformed into the same image (eikōn) from glory to glory, just as by the Spirit of the Lord. — 2 Corinthians 3:18
μεταμορφούμεθα
metamorphoumetha
meh-tah-mor-FOO-meh-thah
G3339
We are being transformed. From meta (“change”) + morphē (“form, essential nature”). Present passive: something is being done TO you, continuously, right now. The English word “metamorphosis” comes directly from this. Like a caterpillar becoming a butterfly—not a costume change, but a fundamental transformation of what you ARE. And it is happening from glory to glory.

Metamorphoumetha—we are being metamorphosed. Present tense. Passive voice (it is being done to you by the Spirit). From glory to glory. Not from shame to barely-acceptable. From glory to glory. The transformation is already underway, and its direction is toward more glory, not less.

Known Before the Foundation of the World

Perhaps the most staggering identity statement of all:

He chose us in Him before the foundation of the world, that we should be holy and without blame before Him in love, having predestined us to adoption as sons by Jesus Christ to Himself, according to the good pleasure of His will. — Ephesians 1:4-5
πρὸ καταβολῆς κόσμου
pro katabolēs kosmou
pro kah-tah-bo-LAYS KOS-moo
G4253 + G2602 + G2889
Before the foundation of the world. Pro = before. Katabolē = foundation, casting down (from kataballō, “to throw down, to lay a foundation”). Kosmos = world, ordered universe. Before the universe was laid down—before time, space, matter, energy—God already knew you and chose you.

Before the foundation of the world. Before there was a world. Before there was time. Before there was anything at all, God knew you and chose you. You are not an afterthought. You are not a random accident in a meaningless universe. You were in the mind of God before the cosmos existed.

Your eyes saw my substance, being yet unformed. And in Your book they all were written, the days fashioned for me, when as yet there were none of them. — Psalm 139:16
גָּלְמִי
golmi
gol-MEE
H1564
Unformed substance, embryo. Used only once in the entire OT (Psalm 139:16). From galam (“to wrap up, to fold”). Before you had form, before you had shape, before you were anything recognizable—God’s eyes saw you. And every day of your life was written in His book before any of them existed.

Golmi—a word so rare it appears only once in the entire Hebrew Bible. Your unformed substance. Before you had a face, before you had a name, before your mother knew you existed, God’s eyes were on you. And every day of your life was already written.

This is not fate or determinism. It is being known. The God who exists outside of time saw every moment of your life before any of them happened, and He chose you anyway. Not reluctantly. Not with conditions. Paul says it was “according to the eudokian of His will”:

εὐδοκία
eudokia
yoo-do-KEE-ah
G2107
Good pleasure, delight, satisfaction, desire. From eu (“good, well”) + dokeō (“to think, to seem good”). God’s adoption of you was not reluctant duty. It was His EUDOKIA—His delight, His good pleasure, something that made Him glad. Choosing you pleased God.

Choosing you was God’s eudokia—His delight. Not His obligation. Not His duty. His pleasure. God is glad about you.

What Nothing Can Change

Paul, in what may be the most powerful passage he ever wrote, asks a question and answers it with absolute finality:

Who shall separate us from the love of Christ? Shall tribulation, or distress, or persecution, or famine, or nakedness, or peril, or sword? … For I am persuaded that neither death nor life, nor angels nor principalities nor powers, nor things present nor things to come, nor height nor depth, nor any other created thing, shall be able to separate us from the love of God which is in Christ Jesus our Lord. — Romans 8:35,38-39
χωρίσαι
chōrisai
kho-REE-sigh
G5563
To separate, to divide, to put apart, to divorce. From chōris (“apart from, without”). Paul uses divorce language: NOTHING can divorce you from God’s love. Not death. Not life. Not angels. Not rulers. Not anything in the present or the future. Not the heights of heaven or the depths of the abyss. Not any created thing in the entire universe.

Look at the scope of Paul’s list:

  • Death — the ultimate human fear. Cannot separate you.
  • Life — everything life throws at you. Cannot separate you.
  • Angels — supernatural beings. Cannot separate you.
  • Principalities (archai, G746) — rulers, authorities, governing powers. Cannot separate you.
  • Powers (dynameis, G1411) — supernatural or natural forces. Cannot separate you.
  • Things present — everything happening right now. Cannot separate you.
  • Things to come — everything that will ever happen. Cannot separate you.
  • Height (hypsōma, G5313) — the highest point in the cosmos. Cannot separate you.
  • Depth (bathos, G899) — the deepest abyss. Cannot separate you.
  • Nor any other created thing — anything else in existence. Cannot separate you.

Paul has covered every category of reality—temporal, spatial, spiritual, physical, present, future—and declared that none of it can sever you from God’s love. This is not wishful thinking. This is an apostle who had been beaten, shipwrecked, imprisoned, stoned, and left for dead, writing under the inspiration of the Holy Spirit, saying: I have tested this in every extremity of human suffering, and nothing can break it.

πέπεισμαι
pepeismai
peh-PICE-my
G3982
I am persuaded, I have become convinced, I stand convinced. Perfect passive of peithō (“to persuade, to convince”). The perfect tense means: I was convinced at some point in the past and I REMAIN convinced now. The passive means: I was persuaded by evidence, not by wishful thinking. Paul’s conviction is settled, tested, and unshakable.

Pepeismai—“I stand persuaded.” Perfect tense: a past conviction that remains in full force. This is not hope. It is settled certainty. Paul has been convinced by evidence—by a life of suffering, by the faithfulness of God through every trial—and his conviction has not moved.

This is your identity: beloved, chosen, adopted with irrevocable legal standing, co-heir with Christ, new creation, royal priest, holy one, God’s treasured possession, His poem, created in His image, being transformed from glory to glory, known before the foundation of the world, chosen with delight—and held by a love that nothing in all creation can break.

The Bottom Line

The original languages do not present God as a distant, disappointed deity who barely tolerates sinners. They present a Father who calls His children agapētos (beloved)—the same word He uses for His own Son. A God who chose you before the cosmos existed, not out of duty but out of eudokia (delight). A Father who adopted you with the irrevocable force of Roman huiothesia, canceled your old debts, gave you His name, and made you a synklēronomos (co-heir) with Christ.

You are not a sinner who got lucky. You are kainē ktisis—a new creation, a new kind of being that didn’t exist before. You are God’s poiēma—His poem, His masterwork. You are basileion hierateuma—royal priesthood, with direct access to the Creator. You carry His tselem—His image, His representation in the physical world. And you are being metamorphoumetha—metamorphosed, continuously, from glory to glory, into the full eikōn of Christ.

None of this depends on your performance. It depends on who God is and what He said. And what He said is in the Greek and Hebrew, available to anyone who looks. The original languages don’t make you smaller. They make you staggeringly, almost unbearably significant—not because of anything you did, but because of who made you and what He calls you.

Beloved. Child. Heir. Co-heir. New creation. Poem. Royal priest. Image-bearer. Chosen before the world began. Held by a love nothing can break.

That is what God said about who you are.

Key Verses

Beloved (Agapētos)

  • Matthew 3:17 — “This is My beloved (agapētos) Son”—the same word applied to you
  • Romans 1:7 — “Beloved (agapētois) of God, called saints”
  • Colossians 3:12 — “As the elect of God, holy and beloved (agapētoi)”
  • 1 John 3:1 — “See what kind (potapēn) of love… that we should be called children of God”

Children and Heirs

  • John 1:12-13 — “He gave the right (exousia) to become children (tekna) of God… born of God”
  • Romans 8:14-17 — “Sons (huioi) of God… Spirit of adoption (huiothesia)… Abba, Father… co-heirs (synklēronomoi) with Christ”
  • Galatians 4:4-7 — “No longer a slave but a son, and if a son, then an heir (klēronomos)”
  • 1 John 3:2 — “We shall be like (homoioi) Him”

New Creation and God’s Masterwork

  • 2 Corinthians 5:17 — “New (kainē) creation (ktisis)”—new in kind, not just in time
  • Ephesians 2:10 — “His workmanship (poiēma)”—His poem, His crafted masterwork
  • Galatians 6:15 — “Neither circumcision nor uncircumcision, but a new creation”

Royal Priesthood and God’s Treasure

  • 1 Peter 2:9 — “Chosen race, royal priesthood, holy nation, His own possession”
  • Exodus 19:5-6 — “You shall be My special treasure (səgullah)”
  • Deuteronomy 7:6 — “The LORD has chosen you to be a people for Himself, a special treasure (səgullah)”

Image-Bearers Being Transformed

  • Genesis 1:26-27 — “Let Us make man in Our image (tselem), according to Our likeness (dəmuth)”
  • Romans 8:29 — “Conformed to the image (eikōn) of His Son”
  • 2 Corinthians 3:18 — “Being transformed (metamorphoumetha) into the same image, from glory to glory”
  • Colossians 1:15 — “He is the image (eikōn) of the invisible God”

Known Before Time, Held by Unbreakable Love

  • Ephesians 1:4-5 — “Chose us before the foundation of the world… according to the good pleasure (eudokia) of His will”
  • Psalm 139:13-16 — “Your eyes saw my unformed substance (golmi)… all my days were written”
  • Romans 8:35-39 — “Nothing shall be able to separate (chōrisai) us from the love of God”
  • Jeremiah 1:5 — “Before I formed you in the womb I knew you”
