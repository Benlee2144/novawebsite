Church — The Word They Refused to Translate

How a Deliberate Mistranslation Built the Entire Institutional Church — and Got William Tyndale Burned at the Stake for Translating Correctly

~90 min read • 22 Hebrew & Greek words explored • 60+ cross-references
For where two or three are gathered together in My name, I am there in the midst of them. — Matthew 18:20

Introduction: The Billion-Dollar Word

Every Sunday, hundreds of millions of people “go to church.” They drive to a building. They sit in pews. They listen to a professional clergyman deliver a sermon from a raised platform. They put money in a collection plate. They call this building “the church.” They call the organization that owns the building “the church.” They call the denomination that oversees the organization “the church.” The word “church” is so embedded in Christian vocabulary that questioning it feels like questioning Christianity itself.

Here is the problem: the word “church” does not appear in the Greek New Testament. Not once. The Greek word that gets translated “church” is ekklēsia—and it does not mean “church.” It never meant “church.” It has no etymological connection to the word “church.” The two words come from entirely different Greek roots, carry entirely different meanings, and describe entirely different things.

Ekklēsia means “assembly”—a gathering of citizens called out to conduct public business. It was a civic term from Athenian democracy. When Jesus said “I will build my ekklēsia,” He was not describing a building, a denomination, a hierarchy with bishops and budgets. He was describing a gathering of people—called out, assembled together, with Him in the middle.

And the word “church”? It comes from a completely different Greek word: kyriakon—“belonging to the Lord.” It entered English through the German Kirche and the Scottish kirk. It originally referred to the building—the Lord’s house. Not the people. The building.

This is not a minor translation issue. It is a deliberate substitution that was enforced by royal decree, maintained by ecclesiastical power, and paid for in blood. William Tyndale, the first man to translate the New Testament into English from Greek, translated ekklēsia correctly as “congregation.” They burned him alive for it.

Ekklēsia: What the Word Actually Means

Let’s start with the word itself:

ἐκκλησία
ekklēsia
ek-klay-SEE-ah
G1577
An assembly, a gathering of citizens called out from their homes into a public meeting. From ek (“out of”) + kaleō (“to call”). A compound word meaning literally “the called-out ones” or “the called-out assembly.” Used 114 times in the NT. In classical Greek: the citizen assembly of a Greek city-state, where free citizens gathered to vote on laws and policy. NOT a religious word. NOT a building. NOT an institution.

Two roots:

ἐκ
ek
ek
G1537
Out of, from within. A preposition indicating origin, source, or motion outward. “Out from the midst of.”
καλέω
kaleō
kah-LEH-oh
G2564
To call, to summon, to invite, to name. Used 148 times in the NT. The root of ekklēsia, klētos (“called one”), and klēsis (“calling”).

Ek + kaleō = called out. An ekklēsia is an assembly of people who have been called out—called out of their homes, called out of their normal routines, called together for a specific purpose.

Ekklēsia Before the New Testament

This word existed for centuries before Jesus used it. In classical Athens (5th century BC), the ekklēsia was the principal assembly of Athenian democracy. A herald (kēryx) would go through the streets announcing the assembly. Citizens would leave their homes and gather on the Pnyx hill. There they debated and voted on laws, foreign policy, military campaigns, and civic matters. Every free male citizen had the right to speak and vote.

Key features of the Athenian ekklēsia:

  • It was the people, not the place. The assembly met on the Pnyx, but the Pnyx was not “the ekklēsia.” The people were. If they gathered in the agora instead, that gathering was still the ekklēsia.
  • Every citizen participated. There was no professional class of speakers while everyone else watched. Citizens spoke, debated, and voted.
  • It had authority. The ekklēsia made real decisions. It was not a spectator event. It was the governing body.
  • It was temporary. The assembly gathered, conducted business, and dispersed. It was an event, not an institution.

This is the word Jesus chose. Not hieron (temple). Not synagōgē (synagogue). Not naos (sanctuary). He chose the Greek word for a citizen assembly where every member participates and has a voice.

Ekklēsia in the Septuagint (Greek Old Testament)

The Septuagint (LXX)—the Greek translation of the Hebrew Bible, completed around 250–100 BC—uses ekklēsia approximately 77 times, almost always translating the Hebrew word:

קָהָל
qahal
kah-HAHL
H6951
Assembly, congregation, gathering. From qahal (verb: to assemble, to gather). The assembled people of Israel gathered together, especially for religious or judicial purposes. Used 123 times in the OT. The “congregation of Israel” = the assembled people, not a building or institution.

When Moses gathered all Israel at Sinai, that was the qahal—the assembly. When Solomon dedicated the temple before all Israel, that was the qahal. When Ezra read the Torah to the returned exiles, that was the qahal. Always the people gathered. Never the building. Never the institution.

Stephen makes this connection explicit in his speech before the Sanhedrin:

This is he who was in the assembly (ekklēsia) in the wilderness with the Angel who spoke to him on Mount Sinai, and with our fathers, the one who received the living oracles to give to us. — Acts 7:38

Stephen calls Israel at Sinai an ekklēsia. No building. No clergy. No denomination. Just the assembled people of God in the wilderness. If your English Bible translates this as “church in the wilderness,” it is importing a concept that did not exist and Stephen did not intend.

What Jesus Actually Said

Jesus uses the word ekklēsia exactly three times in the Gospels—all in Matthew. That’s it. Three times. And each usage demolishes the modern concept of “church”:

Usage 1: Matthew 16:18

And I also say to you that you are Peter, and on this rock I will build My ekklēsia, and the gates of Hadēs shall not prevail against it. — Matthew 16:18

Oikodomēsō mou tēn ekklēsian—“I will build my assembly.” What did a first-century Jewish audience hear? Not “I will establish a hierarchical religious institution with bishops, cathedrals, and a head office in Rome.” They heard: “I will build my gathering—my called-out assembly of people.”

οἰκοδομέω
oikodomeō
oy-ko-do-MEH-oh
G3618
To build, to construct, to edify. From oikos (“house”) + domeō (“to build”). Used metaphorically throughout the NT for building UP people (edification), not constructing buildings. Paul uses it repeatedly for strengthening the community: “build one another up” (1 Thess 5:11).

And notice: “the gates of Hadēs shall not prevail against it.” Gates are defensive structures. Gates don’t attack. This means the ekklēsia is on the offensive—advancing, pushing forward—and the gates of death cannot hold against it. This is not a passive audience sitting in pews. This is an advancing assembly.

πύλαι ⊥δου
pylai Hadou
POO-lie HAH-doo
G4439 + G86
Gates of Hadēs. Pylai = gates (defensive barriers). Hadēs = the unseen realm, the place of the dead. In Hebrew thought: the gates of Sheol = the power of death. The assembly is storming forward and death’s defenses cannot hold.

Usage 2 & 3: Matthew 18:17

And if he refuses to hear them, tell it to the ekklēsia. But if he refuses even to hear the ekklēsia, let him be to you like a heathen and a tax collector. — Matthew 18:17

Jesus is describing conflict resolution. Step 1: go to the person privately. Step 2: bring one or two witnesses. Step 3: “tell it to the ekklēsia.” This is not “report it to the pastor” or “file a complaint with the church office.” It is “bring it before the gathered assembly.” The whole community hears. The whole community decides. There is no mention of a clergy figure handling it on everyone’s behalf.

Three verses later, Jesus says:

For where two or three are gathered together in My name, I am there in the midst of them. — Matthew 18:20
συνηγμένοι
synēgmenoi
soo-nayg-MEH-noy
G4863
Having been gathered together. From syn (“together with”) + agō (“to lead, to bring”). Perfect passive participle: “having been led together.” Related to synagōgē (the noun form). Two or three people gathered in His name = the minimum viable ekklēsia. No building. No budget. No 501(c)(3).

Two or three people. That’s it. That’s the ekklēsia in its simplest form. Not a cathedral. Not a megachurch with a coffee bar and a worship band. Two or three people gathered in His name, with Christ in the middle. That is the assembly Jesus described.

Proof That Ekklēsia Is Not a Religious Word

If you still think ekklēsia inherently means something religious or Christian, consider its uses in Acts 19. A silversmith named Demetrius stirs up a riot in Ephesus because Paul’s preaching is bad for the idol-making business. A mob forms. They rush into the theater. Luke describes the scene:

Some therefore cried one thing and some another, for the ekklēsia was confused, and most of them did not know why they had come together. — Acts 19:32
But if you have any other inquiry to make, it shall be determined in the lawful ekklēsia. — Acts 19:39
And when he had said these things, he dismissed the ekklēsia. — Acts 19:41

This is a pagan riot mob in a Roman theater. There is nothing Christian about this gathering. Yet Luke uses the word ekklēsia three times to describe it. Why? Because ekklēsia simply means “assembly.” It has no inherent religious content. It is a civic word. A group of people gathered together is an ekklēsia—whether they’re voting on Athenian tax policy, rioting over silversmith profits, or gathering in Jesus’s name.

Most English Bibles translate ekklēsia as “assembly” in these three verses. But the same word in the same book gets translated “church” everywhere else. This is not translation. It is selective interpretation. The translators knew perfectly well what the word meant. They just refused to use the same English word consistently because it would undermine the institutional concept of “church.”

Where “Church” Actually Comes From

If ekklēsia means “assembly,” then where did the word “church” come from? It comes from a completely different Greek word:

κυριακόν
kyriakon
koo-ree-ah-KOHN
Not in Strong’s (adjective form of G2962 kyrios)
Belonging to the Lord. Adjective from kyrios (“lord, master”). The phrase “kyriakon dōma” meant “the Lord’s house”—i.e., the building. This word referred to the PLACE of worship, not the people. It entered Germanic languages as kirche/kirk and eventually became the English “church.”
κύριος
kyrios
KOO-ree-oss
G2962
Lord, master, sir. Used 717 times in the NT. Kyrios is used of God, of Jesus, and of human masters. The adjective kyriakon/kyriakē means “belonging to the Lord” — used only twice in the NT: “the Lord’s (kyriakon) supper” (1 Cor 11:20) and “the Lord’s (kyriakē) day” (Rev 1:10). Neither refers to a building or institution.

Here is the etymology:

Greek
Kyriakon (κυριακόν) = “belonging to the Lord.” Applied to the building where Christians met: “the Lord’s house.”
Proto-Germanic
*kirika — borrowed from Greek kyriakon/kyrikē through contact with Greek-speaking Christians.
Old English
cirice (pronounced “CHEE-ri-cheh”) — still meant the building, the Lord’s house.
Middle English
chirche / churche — gradually acquiring broader institutional meaning.
Modern English
Church — now applied to buildings, organizations, denominations, and the universal body of believers.
German
Kirche (same root). Scottish: Kirk.

So: ekklēsia (ek + kaleō = “called out assembly”) became “church” (from kyriakon = “the Lord’s building”). The people became a building. The assembly became an institution. The gathered community became a place you drive to on Sunday morning. And this transformation was not accidental—it was engineered.

William Tyndale: Burned for a Word

In 1525, William Tyndale completed the first-ever English New Testament translated directly from Greek. He was a brilliant scholar who spoke seven languages. And he made a decision that would cost him his life: he translated ekklēsia as “congregation.”

He also translated several other words accurately:

Every one of Tyndale’s translations was more accurate than the institutional alternative. And every one of them threatened church power:

  • If ekklēsia is a “congregation,” you don’t need a church building or hierarchy.
  • If presbyteros is an “elder,” you don’t need ordained priests.
  • If metanoeō is “repent,” you don’t need the sacrament of penance (and the priestly power that comes with it).
  • If agapē is “love,” you don’t need to funnel charitable giving through the church.

Sir Thomas More—the Lord Chancellor of England—wrote that Tyndale’s translations were deliberate heresies designed to destroy the church. He was particularly enraged by “congregation” instead of “church” and “elder” instead of “priest.”

In 1536, Tyndale was betrayed, arrested, tried for heresy, strangled, and burned at the stake in Vilvoorde, Belgium. His last words: “Lord, open the King of England’s eyes.”

Two years later, King Henry VIII authorized the Great Bible—which was roughly 90% Tyndale’s translation. But with one critical change: they put “church” back.

The King James Rules: “Church” by Royal Decree

In 1604, King James I of England commissioned a new Bible translation. He issued fifteen rules that the translators had to follow. Rule number three:

The old ecclesiastical words to be kept, viz.: the word “church” not to be translated “congregation,” &c. — Rule 3 of the Rules to Be Observed in the Translation of the Bible (1604)

Read that again. The king of England explicitly ordered the translators to use “church” instead of “congregation.” This was not a scholarly decision. It was a political command. James I was the head of the Church of England. If ekklēsia was translated as “congregation,” it would undermine the institutional authority of his church.

The KJV translators were some of the finest Greek scholars in England. They knew exactly what ekklēsia meant. They translated it as “assembly” in Acts 19 (the pagan riot). But they were ordered to write “church” everywhere else. And the KJV became the dominant English Bible for 400 years, cementing “church” as the standard translation.

Every major English translation since has followed suit—not because the Greek demands it, but because tradition demands it. The NIV, ESV, NASB, NKJV—they all translate ekklēsia as “church.” Not because the word means “church.” Because changing it would be too controversial.

Notable exceptions:

  • Tyndale (1525): “congregation” (the translator was executed)
  • Young’s Literal Translation (1862): “assembly”
  • Darby Translation (1890): “assembly”
  • Rotherham’s Emphasized Bible (1902): “assembly”

Every translator who was free from political or ecclesiastical pressure translated the word correctly. The ones who wrote “church” were either ordered to or simply following tradition.

What the Ekklēsia Actually Looked Like

If ekklēsia means “assembly” and not “church,” what did the assembly actually look like in the first century? The New Testament gives us a detailed picture:

They Met in Houses

Greet Priscilla and Aquila, my fellow workers in Christ Jesus… Likewise greet the ekklēsia that is in their house. — Romans 16:3,5
The ekklēsiai of Asia greet you. Aquila and Priscilla greet you heartily in the Lord, with the ekklēsia that is in their house. — 1 Corinthians 16:19
Greet the brethren who are in Laodicea, and Nymphas and the ekklēsia that is in his house. — Colossians 4:15
To Philemon our beloved friend and fellow laborer… and to the ekklēsia in your house. — Philemon 1:1-2

Four explicit references to assemblies meeting in homes. No church buildings existed until the late 3rd century. For nearly 300 years, Christians met in living rooms, dining rooms, and courtyards. The assembly was the people in the room.

Everyone Participated

How is it then, brethren? Whenever you come together, each of you has a psalm, has a teaching, has a tongue, has a revelation, has an interpretation. Let all things be done for edification. — 1 Corinthians 14:26

Each one, every one. Without exception. When Paul says “each of you” (hekastos hymōn) has a psalm, a teaching, a revelation—he means EACH person, not just the professional clergy. The assembly was participatory, not spectatorial.

“Each of you has a psalm, has a teaching.” Not “the pastor has a 45-minute sermon and the rest of you listen.” Paul describes a participatory gathering where every member contributes. This is the Athenian ekklēsia model applied to the people of God: every citizen has a voice.

No Clergy/Laity Division

Peter calls ALL believers “a chosen generation, a royal priesthood, a holy nation, His own special people (laos)” (1 Peter 2:9).

ALL believers are a royal priesthood. Not some. Not the ordained. All. If every member is a priest, there is no separate priestly class. If every member has a psalm, a teaching, a revelation (1 Corinthians 14:26), there is no one-man pulpit ministry. The ekklēsia was a community of equals where Christ alone was the head.

How the Assembly Became the Institution

If the first-century ekklēsia was a participatory house gathering with no clergy/laity divide, how did it become the hierarchical institutional church? The transformation was gradual and well-documented:

30–70 AD
The apostolic era. Assemblies meet in homes. Leadership is plural (elders, not a single pastor). Every member participates. No church buildings exist. The ekklēsia IS the gathered people.
~96 AD
1 Clement. Clement of Rome writes to Corinth, beginning to distinguish between “those who make the offerings” (clergy) and “the layman” (laikos). This is the first known use of a clergy/laity distinction in Christian writing. The word laikos does not appear in the NT.
~110 AD
Ignatius of Antioch pushes for a single bishop (episkopos) to preside over each city’s assembly. “Do nothing without the bishop.” This is a clear departure from the NT pattern of plural elders.
~200 AD
Tertullian and Cyprian develop sacerdotal theology—the idea that Christian leaders are “priests” (sacerdotes) who offer sacrifices. The elder (presbyteros) is becoming a priest (hiereus)—a category the NT NEVER applies to Christian leaders.
~250 AD
Purpose-built church buildings begin appearing. The assembly shifts from homes to dedicated structures. The building starts being called “the church.” The people begin to be called “churchgoers.”
313 AD
Constantine’s Edict of Milan. Christianity becomes legal. Constantine begins funding massive basilicas (church buildings). The assembly model is replaced by the basilica model: one speaker at the front, a passive audience in rows, architecture designed for hierarchy.
380 AD
Edict of Thessalonica. Christianity becomes the mandatory state religion. “Church” now means the state-sponsored institution. The participatory assembly of Acts is unrecognizable.
500–1500 AD
The medieval period. The clergy/laity divide is total. Laity cannot read Scripture (it’s in Latin). Priests mediate all access to God. The “church” is the institution, the hierarchy, the building. The word ekklēsia—the called-out assembly of participating citizens—is a forgotten memory.

The pattern is clear: the people became spectators, the leaders became clergy, the gathering became an institution, and the assembly became a building. Every step moved further from what ekklēsia meant. And the word “church” (from kyriakon = “the Lord’s building”) perfectly captured this transformation—which is exactly why it was preferred over “assembly” or “congregation.”

⚠ The Big Controversy: Does Structure Equal Apostasy?

This is where it gets heated. If the institutional church is a departure from the NT ekklēsia, does that mean all church structure is wrong? Three positions:

Position 1: The Institutional Church Is Valid (Catholic/Orthodox/Mainline Protestant)

The claim: The church evolved under the guidance of the Holy Spirit. The development of bishops, creeds, councils, and institutions was organic and divinely directed. “Church” is a legitimate translation because the ekklēsia naturally developed into the church. Apostolic succession guarantees continuity.

Key arguments:

  • Bishops appear in the NT (episkopos — 1 Timothy 3:1-7)
  • Some structure is clearly present (elders, deacons)
  • The church has thrived for 2,000 years under this model
  • Complete decentralization leads to chaos (see Corinth)

Weakness: The NT episkopos is a local elder/overseer, not a regional bishop with hierarchical authority. The structures that developed by 300 AD have no precedent in the apostolic writings.

Position 2: The Institutional Church Is a Corruption (Restoration/House Church Movement)

The claim: The post-apostolic church abandoned the NT pattern. Buildings, clergy, hierarchy, passive congregations—none of this matches Acts or Paul’s letters. The church needs to return to the participatory house-assembly model.

Key arguments:

  • The NT clearly describes house gatherings, plural leadership, and universal participation
  • The clergy/laity divide has no basis in the Greek text
  • Every member is a priest (1 Peter 2:9)
  • Hierarchy concentrates power and enables abuse

Weakness: Even in the NT, some structure exists (elders, apostles, teachers). Paul exercises authority over assemblies. Complete structurelessness can also be dangerous.

Position 3: Flexible Forms, Fixed Principles

The claim: The NT prescribes principles (every member participates, Christ alone is head, leadership is servant-oriented, the assembly is the people not the building), but not rigid forms. A house church and a cathedral can both be legitimate if they embody the principles. The problem isn’t buildings or organization per se—it’s when structures replace the principles.

Key arguments:

  • The NT shows diversity (house churches, city-wide gatherings, itinerant apostles)
  • Principles like mutual participation, servant leadership, and Christ’s headship can be applied in multiple forms
  • Cultural context changes; first-century Roman house layouts aren’t normative for all time

Weakness: Can be used to justify any structure (“We have mutual participation!”) even when the practice is clearly spectatorial.

What the Original Languages Support

Based on the Greek evidence:

  • Ekklēsia means “assembly”—the gathered people, not a building or institution. This is not debatable.
  • Hekastos (each one) in 1 Corinthians 14:26 indicates universal participation. This is clear.

  • Presbyteros (elder) and episkopos (overseer) are used interchangeably in the NT (see Acts 20:17,28 and Titus 1:5-7), indicating they are the same role. Leadership is always plural (elders, never elder).

What This Changes

If ekklēsia means “assembly” and not “church,” several things shift:

“Going to church” is a category error. You cannot “go to” an assembly. You are the assembly. The ekklēsia is not a place you attend. It is a gathering you participate in. Wherever two or three gather in His name, that is the ekklēsia (Matthew 18:20). It happens in your living room as validly as in a cathedral.

The “senior pastor” model has no NT precedent. The idea of one person leading an assembly—preaching every week, making decisions, being the “head” under Christ—is nowhere in the Greek text. Leadership was always plural (elders), always servant-oriented (diakonos), and always alongside an assembly where “each one” contributed. The one-man show is a post-apostolic development.

The building is not sacred. The NT never calls a building holy, sacred, or set apart. The people are the temple (1 Corinthians 3:16—“you [plural] are the temple [naos] of God”). Pouring millions into buildings while the assembled people sit passively is a complete inversion of NT priorities.

ναός
naos
nah-OSS
G3485
Temple, sanctuary, the inner dwelling place of God. Distinguished from hieron (the entire temple complex with its courts). Paul says the PEOPLE are the naos—God’s inner dwelling place. Not the building. The building is just a building.

“Church membership” is an invented concept. The NT knows nothing of signing a membership card, joining a church roll, or transferring your membership. You are part of the ekklēsia by being a believer who gathers with other believers. That’s it. There is no bureaucratic process.

Tyndale was right. He translated the word accurately and was killed for it. The word “church” was forced into English Bibles by royal decree to protect institutional power. Every time you read “church” in your English Bible, you are reading a political decision, not a translation.

The Bottom Line

The word ekklēsia means “assembly”—a gathering of people called out together. It was a civic term from Athenian democracy where every citizen had a voice. It was used in the Greek Old Testament for the gathered people of Israel. Jesus chose this word deliberately. He did not say “I will build my temple,” or “my synagogue,” or “my institution.” He said “I will build my ekklēsia”—my assembly, my gathering, my called-out people.

The word “church” comes from a completely different Greek word (kyriakon) that originally meant “the Lord’s building.” It was imposed on English Bibles by royal decree. William Tyndale was executed for translating correctly. King James I explicitly ordered his translators to write “church” instead of “congregation.” The entire institutional concept of “church”—the building, the hierarchy, the clergy/laity divide, the passive audience, the single pastor—is a post-apostolic development with no basis in the original Greek.

What does the Greek describe? People gathered in homes. Every member contributing. Plural servant-leaders. Christ alone as head. Two or three gathered in His name with Him in the midst. No building required. No professional clergy required. No budget required. Just the called-out ones, assembled together.

The ekklēsia is not where you go on Sunday. It is who you are when you gather.

Ekklēsia = The Gathered People (Not a Building)

  • Matthew 16:18 — “I will build my ekklēsia”
  • Matthew 18:17 — “Tell it to the ekklēsia” (the assembled community decides)
  • Matthew 18:20 — “Where two or three are gathered… I am there”
  • Acts 7:38 — Israel at Sinai called an ekklēsia (no building, no institution)
  • Acts 19:32,39,41 — A pagan riot mob called an ekklēsia (proves the word is not inherently religious)

The Assembly Met in Houses

  • Romans 16:5 — “The ekklēsia that is in their house” (Priscilla & Aquila)
  • 1 Corinthians 16:19 — “The ekklēsia that is in their house”
  • Colossians 4:15 — “The ekklēsia that is in his house” (Nymphas)
  • Philemon 1:2 — “The ekklēsia in your house”

Every Member Participates — No Clergy/Laity Divide

  • 1 Corinthians 14:26 — “Each of you (hekastos) has a psalm, a teaching, a revelation”
  • 1 Peter 2:9 — “You are a royal priesthood” (ALL believers are priests)
  • 1 Corinthians 3:16 — “YOU (plural) are the temple of God”
  • Ephesians 4:11-12 — Leaders exist to equip the saints for THEIR work of ministry
  • Hebrews 10:24-25 — “Stir up one another”—mutual encouragement, not spectatorship

Leadership Is Plural, Servant-Oriented, Non-Hierarchical

  • Acts 20:17,28 — Elders (presbyterous) = overseers (episkopous)—same people, same role
  • Titus 1:5-7 — Elder and overseer used interchangeably
  • Mark 10:42-45 — “Whoever desires to be great among you shall be your servant (diakonos)”
  • Matthew 23:8-10 — “Do not be called Rabbi/Father/Teacher—you are all brethren”
  • 1 Peter 5:1-3 — Elders: “Not as lords over those entrusted to you, but as examples”

Christ Alone Is Head

  • Ephesians 1:22-23 — “He put all things under His feet, and gave Him to be head (kephalē) over all things to the ekklēsia”
  • Ephesians 5:23 — “Christ is head of the ekklēsia”
  • Colossians 1:18 — “He is the head of the body, the ekklēsia”
