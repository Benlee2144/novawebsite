The Sermon on the Mount

What Jesus Actually Said in the Original Greek — And Why Almost None of It Means What You Were Told

~200 min read • 60+ Greek & Hebrew words explored • 150+ cross-references

And when he was set down, his disciples came unto him: and he opened his mouth, and taught them.

— Matthew 5:1–2

Introduction: The Most Misquoted Speech in History

Everyone thinks they know the Sermon on the Mount. “Turn the other cheek.” “Don’t judge.” “Love your enemies.” Nice sentiments. Hallmark card theology. The kind of thing you embroider on a pillow and hang in the guest bathroom. Except none of those phrases mean what you think they mean. Not even close. When you go back to the Greek—the actual words that Matthew recorded—the Sermon on the Mount isn’t a collection of pleasant moral suggestions. It’s an act of war. Jesus wasn’t offering lifestyle tips. He was systematically dismantling every religious, social, and political assumption his audience held, and replacing it with something so radical that 2,000 years later we still haven’t caught up.

Let’s start with what’s happening structurally. Matthew places this sermon at the beginning of Jesus’ public ministry for a reason. Moses went up a mountain and brought down the Torah. Now Jesus goes up a mountain and delivers a new teaching. Matthew is telling his Jewish audience: this is the new Moses, and this is the new Torah. But here’s what makes it explosive—Jesus doesn’t quote Moses and add commentary like a normal rabbi would. He says, “You have heard it was said… but I say to you” (Matthew 5:21-22). No rabbi in history had ever done that. Rabbis cited other rabbis. Jesus cited himself as the authority. Either this man was delusional, or he was claiming to be the one who gave the Torah in the first place.

The Greek word for “taught” in verse 2 sets the stage:

ἐδίδασκεν
edidasken
eh-DEE-das-ken
Imperfect active indicative of didáskō (G1321). The imperfect tense means continuous, ongoing action in past time—“he was teaching them,” not “he taught them (once).” This wasn’t a single speech delivered on one afternoon. Matthew is presenting the ongoing content of Jesus’ teaching, compiled into one literary unit. This is the curriculum of the Kingdom.

Part One: The Beatitudes — Eight Declarations That Inverted the World (Matthew 5:3–12)

The Beatitudes are not commands. They are not “things to aspire to.” They are declarations—announcements of a new reality. Jesus is not saying “try to be meek.” He is saying the meek already are blessed, right now, whether anyone recognizes it or not. This is a king announcing the values of his kingdom. And those values are the exact opposite of every empire that has ever existed.

The word “blessed” in English is the first problem:

μακάριος
makarios
mah-KAH-ree-os
(G3107) In classical Greek, makarios described the state of the gods—the bliss of divine beings who are beyond suffering. It was also used for the wealthy elite who lived carefree lives. Homer uses it for the gods on Olympus. The Greek islands called the “Blessed Isles” (Makariōn Nēsoi) were the paradise where heroes went after death. When Jesus uses this word for the poor, the mourning, and the persecuted, he is doing something linguistically violent. He’s taking the word for divine bliss and attaching it to the people his society considered cursed. A better translation: “Congratulations to…” or “To be envied are…”

Blessed Are the Poor in Spirit (5:3)

Blessed are the poor in spirit, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven.

— Matthew 5:3
πτωχός
ptōchos
ptoh-KHOS
(G4434) This is not the word for “working poor” (πένης penēs). Ptōchos means destitute—begging poor, crouching poor. It comes from πτώσσω (ptōssō, to cower, to crouch). These are people who have absolutely nothing and know it. “Poor in spirit” (πτωχοὶ τῷ πνεύματι) means spiritually bankrupt—people who have zero pretense of self-sufficiency before God. They have nothing to bring, nothing to brag about, no spiritual resume. Compare Luke’s version (Luke 6:20): just “blessed are the poor”—no “in spirit.” Luke may preserve the rawer version. Jesus may have meant both: the literally destitute AND those who know they are spiritually empty.

The kingdom of heaven belongs to the people with nothing. Not to the powerful, not to the religiously accomplished, not to the theologically educated. To the empty. This is the foundation of everything that follows. If you don’t start here, you don’t get the rest of the sermon.

Blessed Are Those Who Mourn (5:4)

Blessed are those who mourn, for they shall be comforted.

— Matthew 5:4
πενθέω
pentheō
pen-THEH-oh
(G3996) The strongest Greek word for grief. This is not sadness or disappointment. Pentheō is the grief of mourning the dead—wailing, tearing garments, the raw grief that cannot be contained. In the LXX, it translates Hebrew אָבַל (aval), the formal mourning period. Jesus is declaring divine bliss on people who are in the deepest possible grief. The word “comforted” (παρακληθήσονται, from παρακαλέω parakaleō, G3870) means “called alongside”—someone comes and stands with you. Not “cheered up.” Accompanied in the pain.

Blessed Are the Meek (5:5)

Blessed are the meek, for they shall inherit the earth.

— Matthew 5:5
πραΰς
praus
prah-OOS
(G4239) “Meek” in English sounds like “weak.” The Greek word is the opposite. Praus was used for a war horse that had been trained—an animal of enormous power that was under control. It’s strength under discipline, not weakness. Aristotle defined prautēs (the noun form) as the mean between excessive anger and the inability to be angry at all. A praus person CAN destroy you—they choose not to. Jesus is quoting Psalm 37:11 almost verbatim (LXX: οἱ πραεῖς κληρονομήσουσιν γῆν). The meek don’t seize the earth by force. They inherit it—it’s given to them.

⚔️ Our Analysis Differs

The popular reading of “meek” as “gentle” or “humble” has been used for centuries to teach passive submission to authority—particularly to oppressed groups. “Be meek, don’t make trouble.” But the Greek word means controlled power, not absence of power. Moses is called the most praus man on earth (Numbers 12:3, LXX)—the man who confronted Pharaoh, shattered the golden calf, and interceded with terrifying intensity before God. Meekness is not doormat theology. It’s a lion on a leash.

Blessed Are Those Who Hunger and Thirst for Righteousness (5:6)

Blessed are those who hunger and thirst for righteousness, for they shall be satisfied.

— Matthew 5:6
δικαιοσύνη
dikaiosynē
dee-kai-oh-SOO-nay
(G1343) This is the Greek word behind “righteousness”—but in the Hebrew mind (and Matthew writes for Jews), this word carries the full weight of צְדָקָה (tsedaqah, H6666). Tsedaqah is not private moral perfection. It is relational rightness—things set right between God and people, between people and people, between humanity and creation. It includes justice, equity, covenant faithfulness, and active restoration. When the prophets thunder about “righteousness,” they mean: the widow gets fed, the orphan is protected, the oppressed go free, the scales balance (Isaiah 61:1–3, Micah 6:8, Amos 5:24).

Hungering and thirsting for dikaiosynē is not hungering to be personally holy (though it includes that). It’s aching for a world where everything is set right. Where the corrupt judge is removed. Where the slave is freed. Where the cheated are repaid. It’s a hunger for cosmic restoration. And the promise: they SHALL be satisfied (χορτασθήσονται chortasthēsontai, from G5526—to be filled to the point of no more hunger, used for animals gorging at pasture). The satisfaction will be total.

Blessed Are the Merciful (5:7)

Blessed are the merciful, for they shall receive mercy.

— Matthew 5:7
ἐλεήμων
eleēmōn
el-eh-AY-mohn
(G1655) From ἔλεος (eleos, G1656)—mercy, compassion, pity that moves to action. In the LXX, eleos often translates חֶסֶד (hesed, H2617)—covenant faithfulness, loyal love. But it also translates רַחֲמִים (rachamim, H7356)—womb-compassion, the visceral tenderness a mother feels for her child. The word is rooted in the body, not the mind. Mercy in the Bible is not a legal pardon from a judge who decides to be nice. It’s a gut-wrenching response to suffering that compels you to act.

Blessed Are the Pure in Heart (5:8)

Blessed are the pure in heart, for they shall see God.

— Matthew 5:8
καθαρός
katharos
kah-thah-ROS
(G2513) Clean, pure, unmixed, without alloy. Used for metals that have been refined (gold without dross), for grain that has been winnowed (chaff removed), for an army purged of cowards. The “heart” (καρδία kardia, G2588) in Hebrew thought is not emotions—it is the center of the will, the seat of thought and decision. Katharos in kardia = undivided intention, unmixed motives, no hidden agenda. You’re not performing for an audience. Your inner reality matches your outer presentation. This echoes Psalm 24:3–4: “Who shall ascend the hill of YHWH?… He who has clean hands and a pure heart (לְבַב בַּר levav bar).”

Blessed Are the Peacemakers (5:9)

Blessed are the peacemakers, for they shall be called sons of God.

— Matthew 5:9
εἰρηνοποιός
eirēnopoios
ay-ray-noh-poi-OS
(G1518) A compound word: εἰρήνη (eirēnē, peace, G1515) + ποιέω (poieō, to make/do, G4160). Peace-makers, not peace-keepers or peace-lovers. The difference is everything. A peacekeeper avoids conflict. A peacemaker wades into conflict and creates shalom where there was none. Eirēnē translates שָׁלוֹם (shalom, H7965)—not merely the absence of war, but the presence of wholeness, completeness, everything functioning as designed. Making shalom is active, costly, dangerous work. It means confronting injustice, reconciling enemies, and absorbing hostility without retaliating. The title “sons of God” (υἱοὶ θεοῦ) echoes Roman propaganda—Caesar was called “son of god” (divi filius) and claimed to bring pax Romana through military force. Jesus says the REAL sons of God bring peace through self-sacrifice, not conquest.

Blessed Are the Persecuted (5:10–12)

Blessed are those who are persecuted for righteousness’ sake, for theirs is the kingdom of heaven. Blessed are you when others revile you and persecute you and utter all kinds of evil against you falsely on my account. Rejoice and be glad, for your reward is great in heaven, for so they persecuted the prophets who were before you.

— Matthew 5:10–12
δεδιωγμένοι
dediōgmenoi
deh-dee-ohg-MEH-noi
Perfect passive participle of διώκω (diōkō, G1377)—to pursue, to chase, to hunt down. The same word used for a hunter pursuing prey. The perfect tense indicates a completed action with ongoing results: these are people who HAVE BEEN hunted and continue to bear the marks of it. Notice: Jesus bookends the Beatitudes with the same promise. The poor in spirit (v.3) and the persecuted (v.10) both receive “the kingdom of heaven.” This literary structure (called an inclusio) means everything between those two bookends describes life in the kingdom. The kingdom belongs to the empty and the hunted. If your theology produces comfort for the powerful and silence from the oppressed, you have the wrong theology.

And then Jesus shifts from third person (“blessed are those”) to second person (“blessed are you”). It becomes personal. He’s looking his disciples in the eyes. “When they persecute you.” Not if. When.

Part Two: Salt, Light, and the Most Misunderstood Verse About the Law (Matthew 5:13–20)

You Are the Salt of the Earth (5:13)

You are the salt of the earth, but if salt has lost its taste, how shall its saltiness be restored? It is no longer good for anything except to be thrown out and trampled under people’s feet.

— Matthew 5:13
μωρανθῇ
mōranthē
moh-ran-THAY
Aorist passive subjunctive of μωραίνω (mōrainō, G3471)—to make foolish, to become tasteless, to become insipid. The root is μωρός (mōros)—a fool, a moron. English gets “moron” from this word. Salt that has “lost its taste” has literally “become foolish.” This isn’t a chemistry lesson about sodium chloride degrading. Salt in the ancient world (particularly from the Dead Sea region) was impure—mixed with gypsum and other minerals. The salt could leach out, leaving a white powder that looked like salt but was useless. Jesus is saying: you can look like a disciple on the outside while having nothing of substance left inside. And once that happens, there’s no re-salting.

You Are the Light of the World (5:14–16)

You are the light of the world. A city set on a hill cannot be hidden. Nor do people light a lamp and put it under a basket, but on a stand, and it gives light to all in the house. In the same way, let your light shine before others, so that they may see your good works and give glory to your Father who is in heaven.

— Matthew 5:14–16
φῶς
phōs
FOHS
(G5457) Light. In the OT, light is YHWH’s domain. “YHWH is my light” (Psalm 27:1). Israel was called to be “a light to the nations” (אוֹר גּוֹיִם or goyim, Isaiah 42:6, 49:6). Jesus takes the vocation given to Israel and transfers it to his disciples. “YOU are the light of the world.” Not “you should try to be.” You ARE. The “city on a hill” likely evokes Jerusalem, built on Mount Zion, visible for miles. Jesus’ community is the new visible city—the place where God’s reign is on display for the nations to see.

Not One Stroke of the Law (5:17–20)

Do not think that I have come to abolish the Law or the Prophets; I have not come to abolish them but to fulfill them. For truly, I say to you, until heaven and earth pass away, not an iota, not a dot, will pass from the Law until all is accomplished.

— Matthew 5:17–18
πληρόω
plēroō
play-ROH-oh
(G4137) To fill up, to make full, to bring to completion, to fill to the brim. This is NOT the word for “to do” or “to obey” (that would be ποιέω poieō or τηρέω tēreō). And it’s not the word for “to end” or “to finish” (that would be τελέω teleō). Plērōō means to fill something up to its fullest meaning. Picture an empty cup: the Torah was the cup, Jesus is the wine that fills it. He’s not erasing it. He’s not just obeying it. He’s showing what it was always pointing to—its fullest, deepest, intended meaning.

⚔️ Our Analysis Differs

Two popular but opposite readings both miss the mark. Reading 1: “Jesus fulfilled the Law so we don’t have to keep it anymore”—this contradicts what Jesus literally says in the next verse (“not one iota will pass”). Reading 2: “Christians must keep all 613 commandments of Torah”—this ignores what “fulfill” means and what Jesus does for the rest of the sermon (deepening, transforming, and sometimes radically reinterpreting the commandments). The text says something more interesting than either camp admits: Jesus fills the Torah to its intended meaning. Murder was always about anger. Adultery was always about lust. Oaths were always about truthfulness. Jesus goes to the root. The Torah isn’t abolished—it’s unpacked.

Verse 20 is the bomb that detonates the whole section:

For I tell you, unless your righteousness exceeds that of the scribes and Pharisees, you will never enter the kingdom of heaven.

— Matthew 5:20

The Pharisees were the most righteous people in Israel. They tithed herbs from their garden. They fasted twice a week. They memorized Torah. If their righteousness isn’t enough, whose is? That’s exactly the point. Jesus isn’t raising the bar higher so you try harder. He’s demonstrating that the entire approach—external rule-keeping as the measure of righteousness—is the wrong framework. The rest of the sermon shows what the RIGHT framework looks like: transformation of the heart, not performance of the law.

Part Three: “You Have Heard… But I Say” — Six Radical Reversals (Matthew 5:21–48)

These six teachings are called the “antitheses”—not because Jesus opposes the Torah, but because he opposes the surface-level reading of it. He goes behind the behavior to the heart condition that produces it.

First Antithesis: Murder and Anger (5:21–26)

You have heard that it was said to those of old, “You shall not murder; and whoever murders will be liable to judgment.” But I say to you that everyone who is angry with his brother will be liable to judgment; whoever insults his brother will be liable to the council; and whoever says, “You fool!” will be liable to the hell of fire.

— Matthew 5:21–22
ὀργίζω
orgizō
or-GHEE-zoh
(G3710) To be angry, to be provoked to wrath. From ὀργή (orgē, G3709)—settled, deliberate anger (as opposed to θυμός thymos, G2372, which is explosive rage). This is the slow-burn, the contempt that dehumanizes another person over time. Jesus escalates through three levels: anger → insult (ῥακά rhaka, G4469—Aramaic for “empty-head,” a term of contempt) → “you fool” (μωρέ mōre, from μωρός mōros—the same root as the “foolish” salt). Each escalation matches a higher court: judgment → the Sanhedrin → Gehenna. The point: dehumanizing another person in your heart is the root from which murder grows. Deal with the root.

Then Jesus says something staggering (vv.23–24): if you’re at the altar—literally in the act of worship—and remember that someone has something against you, leave. Leave the sacrifice. Go reconcile first. God doesn’t want your worship if you’re harboring broken relationships. Reconciliation outranks ritual. Every time.

Second Antithesis: Adultery and Lust (5:27–30)

You have heard that it was said, “You shall not commit adultery.” But I say to you that everyone who looks at a woman with lustful intent has already committed adultery with her in his heart.

— Matthew 5:27–28
ἐπιθυμέω
epithymeō
ep-ee-thoo-MEH-oh
(G1937) To set the desire upon, to crave, to covet. This is NOT the same as noticing someone is attractive. Epithymeō is intentional, directional desire—setting your will toward possessing. The phrase πρὸς τὸ ἐπιθυμῆσαι αὐτήν (pros to epithymēsai autēn) literally means “toward the desiring of her”—a purposeful gaze aimed at consuming. The LXX uses this same word in the 10th commandment: “You shall not covet (ἐπιθυμήσεις epithymēseis) your neighbor’s wife” (Exodus 20:17). Jesus is connecting commandment 7 (adultery) back to commandment 10 (covetousness). The act starts as a gaze that treats another person as an object to possess.

The hyperbolic language about gouging out eyes and cutting off hands (vv.29–30) is Jewish rhetorical exaggeration (hyperbole was a standard rabbinic teaching technique). Jesus is not commanding self-mutilation. He’s saying: take the source of temptation seriously enough to take radical action. Whatever feeds the destructive desire—eliminate it, no matter the cost.

Third Antithesis: Divorce (5:31–32)

It was also said, “Whoever divorces his wife, let him give her a certificate of divorce.” But I say to you that everyone who divorces his wife, except on the ground of sexual immorality, makes her commit adultery.

— Matthew 5:31–32
πορνεία
porneia
por-NAY-ah
(G4202) Sexual immorality in the broadest sense—any sexual unfaithfulness. The root is πόρνη (pornē, a prostitute, G4204). This is Matthew’s “exception clause” (παρεκτὸς λόγου πορνείας—“except on the matter of porneia”). Scholars debate what porneia covers: adultery specifically? All sexual sin? Incestuous marriages prohibited in Leviticus 18? The word itself has a wide range. What’s clear: Jesus is protecting women. In 1st-century Judaism, only men could initiate divorce. A divorced woman with no family support faced destitution or prostitution. The certificate (ἀποστάσιον apostasion, G647, from Deuteronomy 24:1) was itself a protection—proof she was free to remarry. Jesus goes further: don’t divorce her at all (except for porneia), because you’re throwing her into danger.

Fourth Antithesis: Oaths (5:33–37)

Again you have heard that it was said to those of old, “You shall not swear falsely, but shall perform to the Lord what you have sworn.” But I say to you, Do not take an oath at all… Let what you say be simply “Yes” or “No”; anything more than this comes from evil.

— Matthew 5:33–37
ὀμνύω
omnyō
om-NOO-oh
(G3660) To swear an oath, to take a vow. In the ancient world, oaths invoked God (or heaven, earth, Jerusalem, your own head—Jesus lists all of these) as a witness guaranteeing your truthfulness. The problem: the oath system created two tiers of speech—things you said under oath (binding) and things you said otherwise (negotiable). It enabled people to be selectively honest. Jesus eliminates the two-tier system. If your character is trustworthy, you don’t need oaths. Your yes is yes and your no is no. Anything beyond that (τὸ περισσὸν to perisson—the excess, what goes beyond) is ἐκ τοῦ πονηροῦ (ek tou ponērou—from the evil one, or from evil). The need to guarantee your words proves your words can’t be trusted.

Fifth Antithesis: Retaliation — The Most Misread Passage (5:38–42)

You have heard that it was said, “An eye for an eye and a tooth for a tooth.” But I say to you, Do not resist the one who is evil. But if anyone slaps you on the right cheek, turn to him the other also.

— Matthew 5:38–39
ἀντιστῆναι
antistēnai
an-tee-STAY-nai
Aorist active infinitive of ἀνθίστημι (anthistēmi, G436)—to set against, to stand against, to resist in kind, to retaliate violently. This is a MILITARY word. It’s the word for armed resistance, meeting force with force. Jesus is not saying “don’t resist.” He’s saying “don’t resist violently, in kind.” He then gives three examples of creative, nonviolent resistance—each one brilliant and subversive.

⚔️ Our Analysis Differs

This passage has been used to teach passive submission to abuse. “Turn the other cheek” = be a doormat. That reading is dead wrong, and here’s why.

The right cheek: In a right-handed culture, to hit someone’s right cheek you must use the back of your hand. A backhanded slap was not a fight—it was an insult of superiority, the way a master struck a slave, a Roman struck a Jew, a man struck a woman. It said: “You are beneath me.” Turning the other cheek forces the aggressor to either hit you with their fist (treating you as an equal) or stop. It refuses the shame without retaliating. It’s a challenge, not submission.

The cloak (v.40): If sued for your tunic (χιτών chitōn, G5509—inner garment), give your cloak (ἱμάτιον himation, G2440—outer garment) too. This would leave you naked. In Jewish law (Leviticus 20, Genesis 9:20–27), the SHAME of nakedness falls on the one who caused it, not the naked person. By stripping down, you expose the injustice of the system that would take your last garment. You shame the oppressor.

The second mile (v.41): Roman soldiers could legally compel a civilian to carry their gear for ONE mile (a law called angareia, from ἀγγαρεύω angareuō, G29). Going beyond one mile put the SOLDIER at legal risk—he could be disciplined for making a civilian carry too far. By going two miles, you take back power. You turn forced labor into a choice. You make the oppressor uncomfortable.

All three examples are acts of creative, dignified, nonviolent resistance. Not passivity. Not doormat theology. Walter Wink called this “Jesus’ Third Way”—neither fight nor flight, but a third option that exposes injustice while refusing to mirror it.

Sixth Antithesis: Love Your Enemies (5:43–48)

You have heard that it was said, “You shall love your neighbor and hate your enemy.” But I say to you, Love your enemies and pray for those who persecute you, so that you may be sons of your Father who is in heaven.

— Matthew 5:43–45
ἀγαπάω
agapaō
ah-gah-PAH-oh
(G25) The verb form of ἀγάπη (agapē, G26). This is NOT an emotion. Agapaō is a choice—a decision to act for the good of another regardless of how you feel about them. You cannot command an emotion. You CAN command a choice. That’s why Jesus can command love for enemies—because he’s commanding action, not feeling. “Hate your enemy” is NOT in the Torah (Leviticus 19:18 says “love your neighbor” but never says “hate your enemy”). This was a popular addition, possibly from the Qumran community (the Dead Sea Scrolls’ “Community Rule” 1QS 1:10 instructs members to “hate all the sons of darkness”). Jesus strips away the human addition and extends the command beyond its original boundary: your “neighbor” now includes your enemy.

The climax of the antitheses:

You therefore must be perfect, as your heavenly Father is perfect.

— Matthew 5:48
τέλειος
teleios
TEH-lay-os
(G5046) From τέλος (telos—end, goal, completion). Teleios does NOT mean “morally flawless.” It means complete, mature, having reached the intended goal, fully developed. A teleios fruit is ripe. A teleios person is mature. Luke 6:36 has the parallel: “Be merciful (οἰκτίρμονες oiktirmones, G3629) as your Father is merciful.” Matthew’s “perfect” is Luke’s “merciful.” The “perfection” of God that Jesus points to is specifically God’s indiscriminate mercy—he sends rain on the just and unjust alike (v.45). Be COMPLETE in your love the way God is complete in his. Love without boundaries, without calculations, without keeping score.

Part Four: The Secret Life — Giving, Prayer, and Fasting Without an Audience (Matthew 6:1–18)

Chapter 6 shifts from ethics (how you treat others) to devotion (how you relate to God). And the entire section is structured around one devastating word:

ὑποκριτής
hypokritēs
hoo-poh-kree-TACE
(G5273) An actor, a stage performer. In Greek theater, actors wore masks—they played a role that wasn’t who they really were. The word literally means “one who answers from behind a mask.” Jesus uses it for religious people who perform their piety for an audience. They’re acting. Their generosity, prayer, and fasting are performances designed to produce applause, not genuine encounters with God. And they get exactly what they wanted: human admiration. That’s their full payment (ἀπέχουσιν apechousin—a business term meaning “paid in full,” G568). The receipt is stamped. Transaction complete. They got the applause. God owes them nothing.

Three parallel teachings follow the same structure: DON’T do X like the hypocrites (for human approval), DO it in secret, and your Father who sees in secret will reward you.

Giving (6:2–4): Don’t trumpet your generosity. Let your left hand not know what your right hand does. Give with zero audience.

Prayer (6:5–15): Don’t pray to be seen. Go to your inner room (ταμεῖον tameion, G5009—a storage room, the most private room in a house). Pray where nobody can admire your spirituality.

Fasting (6:16–18): Don’t look gloomy to advertise your fasting. Wash your face, look normal. Only God needs to know.

The Lord’s Prayer — The Model, Not the Recitation (6:9–13)

Pray then like this: Our Father in heaven, hallowed be your name. Your kingdom come, your will be done, on earth as it is in heaven. Give us this day our daily bread, and forgive us our debts, as we also have forgiven our debtors. And lead us not into temptation, but deliver us from evil.

— Matthew 6:9–13

“Pray like this” (οὕτως oun houtōs—“in this manner”). Not “pray these exact words.” This is a template, not a formula. Let’s take it apart:

Πάτερ ἡμῶν
Pater hēmōn
PAH-tehr hay-MOHN
“Our Father”—πατήρ (patēr, G3962). In Aramaic, Jesus likely said אַבָּא (Abba)—the intimate family word for father. Not “Daddy” (that overstates it), but not the formal “O Father” either. It’s the normal, warm, direct way a child addressed their father. Addressing God this way was unusual in Judaism—not unheard of, but intimate in a way most prayers were not. Notice: “OUR Father,” not “MY Father.” The prayer is communal from the first word. You cannot pray this prayer alone without invoking the community.
ἁγιασθήτω τὸ ὄνομά σου
hagiasthētō to onoma sou
hah-gee-as-THAY-toh toh OH-noh-mah soo
“Let your name be hallowed/sanctified.” ἁγιάζω (hagiazō, G37) means to set apart, to treat as holy, to honor as sacred. The “name” (ὄνομα onoma, G3686) is the person’s character and reputation. In Hebrew thought, the name IS the person. “Hallowed be your name” = “May your character be recognized as sacred.” This isn’t asking God to make himself holy (he already is). It’s asking that the world would recognize who God truly is.
ἐλθέτω ἡ βασιλεία σου
elthetō hē basileia sou
el-THEH-toh hay bah-see-LAY-ah soo
“Let your kingdom come.” βασιλεία (basileia, G932) = reign, rule, kingship, kingdom. Not primarily a PLACE but a REIGN. “Your kingdom come” = “May your rule break into THIS world.” The next phrase proves it: “on earth as it is in heaven” (ὡς ἐν οὐρανῷ καὶ ἐπὶ γῆς). The goal is not to escape earth for heaven. The goal is for heaven’s reality to invade earth. God’s reign manifesting HERE. This is the prayer that Christianity has been praying for 2,000 years—and it’s not a prayer to go up. It’s a prayer for God to come down.
ἐπιούσιος
epiousios
eh-pee-OO-see-os
(G1967) This word appears NOWHERE else in all of Greek literature. It exists only here and in Luke 11:3. Scholars have debated its meaning for centuries. Options: (1) ἐπί + οὐσία = “for existence,” bread necessary for survival. (2) ἐπί + ἰοῦσα (from εἶμι, “to come”) = bread “for the coming day,” tomorrow’s bread given today. (3) “Super-substantial” bread (Jerome’s Latin: supersubstantialis). Origen (3rd century) noted the word seemed to be coined by the Gospel writers themselves. The most likely meaning: “Give us today the bread we need for today.” Don’t stockpile. Don’t hoard. Trust God for today. Tomorrow will worry about itself (Jesus says this explicitly in 6:34). This echoes the manna in the wilderness (Exodus 16)—gather only what you need for today. Those who hoarded found it rotted.
ὀφείλημα
opheilēma
oh-FAY-lay-mah
(G3783) A debt, something owed. NOT the typical word for “sin” (ἁμαρτία hamartia) or “transgression” (παράβασις parabasis) or “trespass” (παράπτωμα paraptōma). Jesus uses a financial/economic word. Sin is a DEBT—something owed that hasn’t been paid. And the condition is brutal: “as we also have forgiven (ἀφήκαμεν aphēkamen—perfect tense, already done) our debtors.” We’re asking God to forgive us in the same manner we have already forgiven others. Jesus doubles down on this immediately after the prayer (vv.14–15): if you don’t forgive others, your Father won’t forgive you. This isn’t earning forgiveness. It’s demonstrating that you understand what forgiveness IS. If you’ve truly received it, you give it.

Part Five: Where Your Treasure Is — Money, Anxiety, and Divided Loyalty (Matthew 6:19–34)

You Cannot Serve God and Money (6:24)

No one can serve two masters, for either he will hate the one and love the other, or he will be devoted to the one and despise the other. You cannot serve God and money.

— Matthew 6:24
μαμωνᾶς
mamōnas
mah-moh-NAS
(G3126) An Aramaic word (מָמוֹן mamon) meaning wealth, possessions, property. Jesus doesn’t use the normal Greek word for money (ἀργύριον argyrion). He uses this Aramaic loanword, almost personifying it—Mammon, like a rival deity demanding worship. The word “serve” (δουλεύω douleuō, G1398) is the verb form of δοῦλος (doulos—slave). You cannot be a slave to two masters. Not “it’s hard to.” You CANNOT. οὐ δύνασθε (ou dynasthe)—you do not have the ability. It’s structurally impossible. Your money serves God or you serve your money. There is no middle option.

Do Not Be Anxious (6:25–34)

Therefore I tell you, do not be anxious about your life, what you will eat or what you will drink, nor about your body, what you will put on. Is not life more than food, and the body more than clothing?

— Matthew 6:25
μεριμνάω
merimnaō
meh-rim-NAH-oh
(G3309) To be anxious, to be divided in mind, to be pulled apart. Related to μερίζω (merizō, to divide). Anxiety literally divides you. Your mind is split between the present and the feared future. Jesus is not saying “don’t plan” or “don’t be responsible.” He’s saying don’t let fear of tomorrow tear you apart today. The argument he makes is from lesser to greater: God feeds birds who don’t farm. God clothes wildflowers that exist for a day. You are worth more than they are. If God handles the small things, he handles you. This isn’t wishful thinking—it’s an argument from the observable character of God in creation.

The section closes with one of the most practical sentences Jesus ever spoke: “Sufficient for the day is its own trouble” (6:34). κακία (kakia, G2549)—trouble, hardship, evil. Each day has enough of its own. Don’t borrow tomorrow’s problems. Deal with today.

Part Six: The Most Misquoted Verse in the Bible (Matthew 7:1–6)

Judge not, that you be not judged. For with the judgment you pronounce you will be judged, and with the measure you use it will be measured to you.

— Matthew 7:1–2
κρίνω
krinō
KREE-noh
(G2919) To judge, to decide, to evaluate, to determine, to condemn. The word has a wide range—from everyday discernment to legal sentencing. The command μὴ κρίνετε (mē krinete) is present imperative with the negative—“stop judging” or “don’t make it a habit to judge.” But WHICH kind of judging? Context decides.

⚔️ Our Analysis Differs

“Don’t judge” has become the one Bible verse that non-religious people quote to shut down all moral evaluation. But that reading contradicts Jesus in the same passage. Five verses later (7:6), Jesus says “do not give dogs what is holy or throw your pearls before pigs.” To identify “dogs” and “pigs” requires judgment. In verse 15 he says “beware of false prophets”—identifying false prophets requires judgment. In verse 20, “by their fruits you will recognize them”—fruit inspection IS judgment.

What Jesus forbids is hypocritical condemnation—the plank-in-your-own-eye variety (vv.3–5). He’s saying: don’t set yourself up as the moral authority over others while ignoring your own failures. Deal with your own mess FIRST (“first take the log out of your own eye”)—THEN you will see clearly to help your brother with his speck. The goal isn’t to stop evaluating. It’s to stop being a hypocrite about it. Judge yourself first, with the same standard you apply to others.

Part Seven: Ask, Seek, Knock — The Tense That Changes Everything (Matthew 7:7–12)

Ask, and it will be given to you; seek, and you will find; knock, and it will be opened to you.

— Matthew 7:7
αἰτεῖτε... ζητεῖτε... κρούετε
aiteite... zēteite... krouete
ai-TEH-teh... zay-TEH-teh... KROO-eh-teh
All three verbs are present imperative—continuous, ongoing action. Not “ask once.” “Keep asking. Keep seeking. Keep knocking.” The present tense commands persistence, not a one-time request. αἰτέω (aiteō, G154) = to ask, to request. ζητέω (zēteō, G2212) = to seek, to search actively. κρούω (krouō, G2925) = to knock, to strike a door. There’s an escalation: asking is verbal, seeking involves moving, knocking involves standing at a closed door and refusing to leave. Each step requires more effort and more commitment. God rewards persistence, not perfection.

Then Jesus gives the Golden Rule as the summary of everything (7:12):

So whatever you wish that others would do to you, do also to them, for this is the Law and the Prophets.

— Matthew 7:12

This is the same claim he made in 5:17—he came to fulfill the Law and the Prophets. Now he says the entire Torah and Prophets are summarized in this principle. Rabbi Hillel (a generation before Jesus) said something similar in the negative: “What is hateful to you, do not do to another” (Babylonian Talmud, Shabbat 31a). Jesus flips it to the positive: don’t just avoid harm—actively DO good. The negative form sets a floor. The positive form removes the ceiling.

Part Eight: The Narrow Gate, False Prophets, and the Two Builders (Matthew 7:13–29)

The Narrow Gate (7:13–14)

Enter by the narrow gate. For the gate is wide and the way is easy that leads to destruction, and those who enter by it are many. For the gate is narrow and the way is hard that leads to life, and those who find it are few.

— Matthew 7:13–14
ἀπώλεια
apōleia
ah-POH-lay-ah
(G684) Destruction, waste, loss, ruin. From ἀπόλλυμι (apollymi, G622)—to destroy, to lose, to perish. This is NOT a synonym for “hell” or “eternal torment.” The word means destruction—the loss of something. A wasted life. A ruined existence. It’s the word used for the “waste” of expensive perfume poured on Jesus (Mark 14:4). The wide road leads to the disintegration of everything that matters. The narrow road leads to ζωή (zōē, G2222)—life, vitality, the quality of existence that God has and gives.

Not Everyone Who Says “Lord, Lord” (7:21–23)

Not everyone who says to me, “Lord, Lord,” will enter the kingdom of heaven, but the one who does the will of my Father who is in heaven. On that day many will say to me, “Lord, Lord, did we not prophesy in your name, and cast out demons in your name, and do many mighty works in your name?” And then will I declare to them, “I never knew you; depart from me, you workers of lawlessness.”

— Matthew 7:21–23
ἀνομία
anomia
ah-noh-MEE-ah
(G458) Lawlessness. ἀ (alpha privative = without) + νόμος (nomos = law). Without Torah. Without instruction. These people prophesied, cast out demons, and did miracles—in Jesus’ name. Their ministry was real. But Jesus says “I never knew you” (οὐδέποτε ἔγνων ὑμᾶς—never, at any point, did I know you). γινώσκω (ginōskō, G1097) in Hebrew thought carries the meaning of intimate, covenantal relationship. God “knew” Israel (Amos 3:2). Adam “knew” Eve (Gen 4:1). These miracle-workers had power but no relationship. They operated in God’s name without knowing God’s person. This is the most terrifying passage in the sermon—maybe in the entire Bible. You can do supernatural things in Jesus’ name and still be a stranger to him.

The Two Builders (7:24–27)

Everyone then who hears these words of mine and does them will be like a wise man who built his house on the rock. And the rain fell, and the floods came, and the winds blew and beat on that house, but it did not fall, because it had been founded on the rock. And everyone who hears these words of mine and does not do them will be like a foolish man who built his house on the sand.

— Matthew 7:24–26
πέτρα
petra
PEH-trah
(G4073) Bedrock, cliff, massive rock formation. Not a loose stone (that would be πέτρος petros or λίθος lithos). Petra is immovable, foundational. The same word used in Matthew 16:18 (“on this petra I will build my assembly”). The rock isn’t a nice metaphor for stability. In Israel’s geography, wadis (dry riverbeds) flood suddenly in rainy season. Building on sand in a wadi looks fine until the flash flood hits. Both houses looked identical in good weather. The storm revealed the difference. The wise builder doesn’t just hear Jesus’ words but does them (ποιεῖ poiei—present tense, continuous action). The foolish builder hears the same words and does NOT do them. Same teaching. Same exposure. Opposite foundations. The difference is doing, not hearing.

Matthew closes the sermon with this observation:

And when Jesus finished these sayings, the crowds were astonished at his teaching, for he was teaching them as one who had authority, and not as their scribes.

— Matthew 7:28–29
ἐξουσία
exousia
ex-oo-SEE-ah
(G1849) Authority, right, jurisdiction, liberty of action. From ἐκ (out of) + εἰμί (to be)—literally “out of being,” authority that flows from who you ARE, not from who authorized you. The scribes taught by quoting authorities: “Rabbi Hillel said… Rabbi Shammai said…” Jesus never quoted anyone. He spoke on his own authority. “BUT I SAY TO YOU.” The crowds weren’t just impressed by his teaching. They were ἐξεπλήσσοντο (exeplēssonto, from ἐκπλήσσω ekplēssō, G1605)—struck with astonishment, blown away, knocked out of their normal state. This wasn’t polite admiration. It was shock.

What the Sermon on the Mount Actually Demands

Here’s what three chapters of Greek taught us.

The Sermon on the Mount is not a moral checklist. It is not a list of rules for nice people. It is not achievable by human effort—and that’s the point. When Jesus says the poor, the mourning, and the persecuted are makarios, he’s announcing a kingdom where everything is inverted. When he says anger is murder and lust is adultery, he’s showing that external behavior was never the standard—the heart was. When he says love your enemies, he’s describing a way of being that requires something more than willpower.

The sermon is designed to make you realize you cannot do it. Not on your own. It strips away every religious performance, every moral achievement, every spiritual resume, and leaves you with one thing: dependence on the Father. The poor in spirit. The empty. The ones who have nothing to bring.

That’s where the kingdom starts. That’s where the sermon starts. And if you missed it at the beginning, the ending hits you again: “I never knew you”—even miraculous power without genuine relationship is worthless.

The original Greek reveals a Jesus far more radical, far more dangerous, and far more beautiful than the domesticated version most of us were given. He wasn’t offering suggestions. He was describing what happens when God’s reign invades a human life—from the inside out, with no area left untouched.

Build on the rock. Not the sand. Not the impressive-looking sand that weathers the first storm. The rock underneath everything: hear these words, and do them.
