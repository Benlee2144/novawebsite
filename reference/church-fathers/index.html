Church Fathers

The Earliest Christian Writers After the Apostles — Their Lives, Quotes & Works

Apostolic Fathers (c. 70–150 AD)
Clement of Rome
c. 35–99 AD • Bishop of Rome
Clement was the third or fourth bishop of Rome. Tradition identifies him with the Clement mentioned by Paul in Philippians 4:3. His letter to the Corinthians (c. 96 AD) is one of the oldest surviving Christian documents outside the New Testament, written to address division in the Corinthian church — remarkably similar to the problems Paul addressed 40 years earlier.
“The apostles received the gospel for us from the Lord Jesus Christ; Jesus the Christ was sent from God. Christ, therefore, is from God, and the apostles are from Christ.”— 1 Clement 42:1–2

Key Works

📜 1 Clement (c. 96 AD) — Letter to the Corinthians on church order and unity
📜 2 Clement (attributed, likely mid-2nd century) — Earliest surviving Christian sermon
Read 1 Clement →
Ignatius of Antioch
c. 35–108 AD • Bishop of Antioch, Martyr
Ignatius was the second or third bishop of Antioch (where believers were first called “Christians,” Acts 11:26). Condemned to death by wild beasts, he wrote seven letters to churches while being escorted to Rome for execution. His letters are passionate defenses of Christ’s real humanity and divinity, church unity under the bishop, and the reality of the Eucharist.
“Where the bishop is, there let the people be, as where Jesus Christ is, there is the catholic church.”— Ignatius, Letter to the Smyrnaeans 8:2 (first use of “catholic church”)

Key Works (7 Letters)

📜 To the Ephesians, Magnesians, Trallians, Romans, Philadelphians, Smyrnaeans, and to Polycarp
Read Ignatius’s Letters →
Polycarp of Smyrna
c. 69–155 AD • Bishop of Smyrna, Martyr
Polycarp was a direct disciple of the apostle John. Irenaeus recalls sitting at Polycarp’s feet as a boy, listening to him recount conversations with John and others “who had seen the Lord.” At age 86, Polycarp was burned at the stake for refusing to deny Christ. His martyrdom account is the earliest detailed Christian martyrdom narrative outside the New Testament.
“Eighty-six years I have served Him, and He has done me no wrong. How can I blaspheme my King who saved me?”— Polycarp, at his martyrdom (Martyrdom of Polycarp 9:3)

Key Works

📜 Letter to the Philippians — Rich in NT quotations; shows which books were considered authoritative
📜 Martyrdom of Polycarp — Account of his trial and execution (c. 155 AD)
Read Polycarp’s Letter →
The Didache
c. 50–120 AD • Anonymous
“The Teaching of the Twelve Apostles” is possibly the oldest surviving Christian document outside the New Testament. It’s a practical manual covering ethics (the “Two Ways”), baptism, fasting, prayer (includes the Lord’s Prayer), the Eucharist, and how to identify true vs. false prophets.
“But concerning baptism, baptize this way: Having first said all these things, baptize into the name of the Father, and of the Son, and of the Holy Spirit, in living [running] water.”— Didache 7:1

Apologists & Early Theologians (c. 100–250 AD)
Justin Martyr
c. 100–165 AD • Philosopher, Apologist, Martyr
Justin was a pagan philosopher who converted to Christianity after encountering an old man who pointed him to the Hebrew prophets. He became the most important Christian apologist of the 2nd century. His First Apology includes the earliest detailed description of Christian Sunday worship: Scripture reading, a homily, communal prayers, and the Eucharist.
“We have been taught that Christ is the first-born of God, and we have shown that He is the Word of whom every race of men were partakers; and those who lived reasonably are Christians, even though they have been thought atheists.”— Justin Martyr, First Apology 46

Key Works

📜 First Apology (c. 155 AD) — Defense of Christianity to Emperor Antoninus Pius
📜 Dialogue with Trypho — Debate with a Jewish philosopher on Christ in the OT
Read First Apology →
Irenaeus of Lyon
c. 130–202 AD • Bishop of Lyon
Irenaeus was a student of Polycarp (who was a student of John), making him only two generations removed from the apostles. His massive work Against Heresies is the most important early refutation of Gnosticism. He is the earliest writer to argue for the authority of all four Gospels (and only four).
“The glory of God is a human being fully alive, and the life of a human being is the vision of God.”— Irenaeus, Against Heresies IV.20.7

Key Works

📜 Against Heresies (c. 180 AD) — 5-volume refutation of Gnosticism
📜 Demonstration of the Apostolic Preaching — Summary of Christian faith from the OT
Read Against Heresies →
Tertullian
c. 155–220 AD • Theologian, Carthage
Tertullian was the first major Christian writer in Latin and coined many theological terms still used today, including “Trinity” (trinitas), “three persons, one substance,” and “Old/New Testament.” A brilliant and combative writer, he defended Christianity against Roman persecution and heresy.
“The blood of the martyrs is the seed of the Church.”— Tertullian, Apologeticum 50:13

Key Works

📜 Apologeticum (c. 197 AD) — Defense of Christianity to Roman governors
📜 Against Marcion — Defense of the OT and its God against Marcion’s dualism
📜 Against Praxeas — Earliest detailed Latin exposition of the Trinity
Read Apologeticum →
Origen of Alexandria
c. 185–253 AD • Scholar, Theologian
Origen was arguably the most brilliant and prolific scholar of the early church. He produced the Hexapla, a massive side-by-side comparison of six versions of the Old Testament. His commentaries covered nearly every book of the Bible.
“The Scriptures were written by the Spirit of God, and have a meaning, not such only as is apparent at first sight, but also another, which escapes the notice of most.”— Origen, De Principiis IV.1.9

Key Works

📜 Hexapla (c. 240 AD) — Six-column OT comparison (mostly lost; fragments survive)
📜 De Principiis (On First Principles) — First systematic theology
📜 Contra Celsum — Defense of Christianity against the philosopher Celsus
