How to Read the Interlinear

A Plain-English Guide to Understanding the Original Hebrew and Greek — No Seminary Required

Study to show yourself approved to God, a workman who does not need to be ashamed, rightly dividing the word of truth. — 2 Timothy 2:15

What Is an Interlinear Bible?

An interlinear Bible shows you the original Hebrew or Greek text with an English translation directly beneath each word. Instead of reading a translator’s interpretation, you see the actual words the biblical authors wrote—and you can check for yourself whether the English matches.

Our interlinear pages show you: the original word (in Hebrew or Greek characters), the transliteration (how to pronounce it in English letters), the Strong’s number (a reference code linking to the lexicon entry), and the English gloss (a basic translation).

Greek Grammar Basics (New Testament)

You don’t need to learn Greek. But understanding five things will let you catch 90% of what translators get wrong:

1. The Article (“the”)

Greek has a definite article (ho, hē, to = “the”) but no indefinite article (“a/an”). When a Greek noun has the article, it points to a specific, definite thing. When it lacks the article, it can be indefinite (“a god”) or qualitative (describing nature/character). This is why John 1:1 is so debated: theos ēn ho logos—theos has NO article, making it qualitative (“the Word was divine”) rather than definite (“the Word was THE God”).

2. Verb Tenses

Greek verb tenses express kind of action, not just time:

  • Aorist — Simple, one-time action. “He saved.” A snapshot.
  • Present — Ongoing, continuous action. “He is saving” / “He keeps saving.”
  • Perfect — Completed action with ongoing results. “He has saved (and the effects continue).” When Jesus said tetelestai (“It is finished”), the perfect tense means: it is accomplished, and it stays accomplished forever.
  • Imperfect — Continuous action in the past. “He was saving” / “He used to save.”

This matters enormously. “Whoever believes” in John 3:16 uses the present participle—ho pisteuōn—meaning “the one who is continuously believing/trusting.” Not a one-time mental agreement. An ongoing active trust.

3. Noun Cases

Greek nouns change form depending on their role in the sentence:

  • Nominative — The subject (who/what does the action)
  • Genitive — Possession, source, or description (“of”)
  • Dative — Indirect object, location, instrument (“to/for/by/in”)
  • Accusative — Direct object (receives the action)

Why this matters: prepositions change meaning depending on the case. En + dative = “in.” Eis + accusative = “into.” “Believe in God” vs. “believe into God” is a massive difference that English obscures.

4. Voice

  • Active — The subject does the action: “God saves.”
  • Passive — The subject receives the action: “We are saved (by God).”
  • Middle — The subject acts on/for itself, or participates in the action: “He saved himself” or “He got himself saved.” English doesn’t have this voice, so translators often obscure it.

5. Gender

Greek nouns have grammatical gender (masculine, feminine, neuter) that doesn’t always match biological gender. Pneuma (spirit) is neuter. Pronouns must match their noun’s gender. So when the Holy Spirit is called pneuma, the grammatically correct pronoun is “it,” not “he.” When Jesus calls the Spirit the ParaklÄ“tos (a masculine noun), the pronoun shifts to “he”—because of grammar, not theology.

Hebrew Grammar Basics (Old Testament)

1. Hebrew Reads Right to Left

The first thing that trips people up. Hebrew text flows from right to left. Our interlinear shows the Hebrew in its natural order with English glosses beneath each word.

2. The Verb System

Hebrew verbs are built on three-letter roots. The root carries the core meaning, and different patterns (called binyanim or “stems”) modify it:

  • Qal — Simple active: “he broke”
  • Niphal — Simple passive or reflexive: “it was broken”
  • Piel — Intensive active: “he shattered”
  • Hiphil — Causative: “he caused to break”

This matters: shub in Qal means “to turn/return.” In Hiphil, it means “to cause to return / to restore.” Same root, very different meaning depending on the stem. Translators sometimes miss this.

3. Construct Chains

Hebrew shows possession by chaining nouns together: dvar-YHWH = “word-of-YHWH” (the word of the LORD). The first noun is in the “construct state” (shortened form), the second in the “absolute state.” Understanding this helps you see relationships English translations flatten.

4. The Plural of Majesty

Elohim is grammatically plural but takes singular verbs when referring to YHWH. This is the plural of majesty—a Hebrew way of expressing greatness, not numerical plurality. (See our Trinity study for the full breakdown.)

5. Perfect vs. Imperfect

  • Perfect — Completed action (past or certain future): “He created”
  • Imperfect — Incomplete/ongoing action (present, future, habitual): “He creates / He will create”

The “prophetic perfect” is when a prophet uses the perfect tense for a future event—it’s so certain, it’s spoken of as already done. Isaiah 53 does this extensively.

How to Use Strong’s Numbers

Every Hebrew and Greek word in the Bible has been assigned a number by James Strong (1890). These numbers are your bridge between English and the original languages:

  • H + number = Hebrew word (Old Testament). Example: H7585 = Sheol
  • G + number = Greek word (New Testament). Example: G3341 = metanoia

Reading Tips

  • When translations disagree, check the Greek/Hebrew. If the KJV, NIV, and ESV all translate a word differently, the interlinear shows you what the original actually says.
  • Look at verb tenses. “Believes” (present continuous) is very different from “believed” (past one-time). English often flattens this.
  • Check the article. “God” with the article and “God” without the article are making different claims in Greek.
  • Follow the Strong’s number. Click through to the lexicon, then to the word usage page. See how the same word is used in other contexts. Context determines meaning.
  • Compare with the parallel translations. Our Parallel Bible shows KJV, YLT, ASV, and WEB side by side.
