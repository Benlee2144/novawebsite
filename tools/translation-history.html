The History of Bible Translation

From the Septuagint to Modern English — Who Translated What, What They Changed, and Why

Every English Bible you’ve ever read is the product of choices. Translators decided which Hebrew or Greek word to use, which meaning to select from multiple options, and which theological tradition to preserve. Understanding who made those choices and why is essential to reading the Bible honestly.

~250 BC
The Septuagint (LXX) — Jewish scholars in Alexandria translate the Hebrew OT into Greek. This becomes the Bible of the early church. When NT authors quote the OT, they usually quote the Septuagint, not the Hebrew directly. Read the LXX on this site →
~382–405 AD
Jerome’s Latin Vulgate — Jerome translates the Bible into Latin. This becomes the standard Bible of Western Christianity for 1,000+ years. Key decision: he translates the Greek metanoeō as Latin paenitentiam agite (“do penance”)—creating the entire penitential system of the Catholic Church from a mistranslation. He also transliterates baptizō instead of translating it.
1382
Wycliffe’s Bible — John Wycliffe produces the first complete English Bible, translated from the Latin Vulgate (not from Hebrew/Greek). The Catholic Church condemned it. Wycliffe’s followers (Lollards) were persecuted and killed.
1525–1536
Tyndale’s New Testament — William Tyndale translates directly from the Greek for the first time in English. He translates ekklēsia as “congregation” (not “church”), presbuteros as “elder” (not “priest”), and metanoeō as “repent” (not “do penance”). For this, he was strangled and burned at the stake. His last words: “Lord, open the King of England’s eyes.”
1560
The Geneva Bible — Translated by Protestant refugees in Geneva. The first English Bible with verse numbers. Includes extensive marginal notes challenging royal authority (which is why King James hated it). The Bible of Shakespeare, the Pilgrims, and early America.
1611
King James Version (KJV) — Commissioned by King James I specifically to replace the Geneva Bible (whose notes he found seditious). Key political rules given to translators: (1) Keep “church” instead of “congregation” (protecting institutional authority). (2) Keep “baptize” instead of “immerse” (protecting the Church of England’s practice of sprinkling). (3) Keep ecclesiastical words that supported the existing church hierarchy. Beautiful English. Politically compromised translation.
1885
Revised Version (RV) — First major revision of the KJV. Used better Greek manuscripts (Codex Sinaiticus, Vaticanus) discovered since 1611. Removed the Comma Johanneum (1 John 5:7) from the main text.
1898
Young’s Literal Translation (YLT) — Robert Young attempts the most literal English translation possible, preserving Greek verb tenses and Hebrew constructions. Awkward to read but invaluable for study. Read it in our Parallel Bible →
1901
American Standard Version (ASV) — American revision of the RV. Consistently translates YHWH as “Jehovah” (debatable but at least consistent). Very literal.
1952
Revised Standard Version (RSV) — Major modern revision. Controversial for translating Isaiah 7:14 as “young woman” instead of “virgin” (the Hebrew almah means “young woman”—the word for “virgin” is betulah). Some pastors literally burned copies.
1978
New International Version (NIV) — “Dynamic equivalence” translation (translates ideas, not words). Extremely popular but takes significant liberties. Adds “sinful nature” where Paul wrote “flesh” (sarx). The 2011 revision changed some of these.
1995–2020
NASB, ESV, CSB, NRSV — Modern literal translations with varying degrees of transparency to the original. The ESV and NASB are closest to the Greek/Hebrew structure. All still carry KJV-era word choices for “church,” “baptize,” and “hell.”

The Pattern

Notice what happens throughout this history: every time someone tried to translate the Bible accurately—letting the original languages speak for themselves—the institutional church fought back. Tyndale was killed. Wycliffe was condemned. The Geneva Bible was suppressed. King James imposed political rules on translation. The pattern is consistent: accurate translation threatens institutional power.

That’s why tools like this site exist. When you can read the interlinear, check the lexicon, and compare translations yourself, no institution can control what you believe the Bible says. The text is open. The tools are free. The only question is whether you’ll look.
