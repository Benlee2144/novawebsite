Five Words That Change Everything You Think You Know About the Bible

You shall know the truth, and the truth shall make you free. — John 8:32

Welcome to The Unveiled Word

This site exists for one reason: to show you what the Bible actually says in the original Hebrew and Greek—before translators, traditions, and centuries of institutional religion changed the message. You don’t need a seminary degree. You don’t need to know Greek or Hebrew. You just need to be willing to look.

Start with these five words. Each one was mistranslated or reframed by English Bibles, and each one changes something major about what you’ve been taught.

Word 1: Metanoia (μετάνοια) — “Repentance”

μετάνοια
metanoia
meh-TAH-noy-ah
G3341
A change of mind. From meta (“after, change”) + nous (“mind”). NOT “feel sorry for your sins.” A complete shift in thinking and perception.

What you were taught: “Repent of your sins” — feel guilty, confess, promise to do better.

What the Greek says: Change your mind. See differently. The phrase “repent of your sins” appears zero times in the Greek New Testament.

Word 2: Aiōnios (αἰώνιος) — “Eternal”

αἰώνιος
aiōnios
eye-OH-nee-oss
G166
Of the age, age-lasting, pertaining to an age (aiōn). NOT necessarily “eternal” or “everlasting.” Used of things that clearly ended (the Aaronic priesthood, Jonah in the fish).

What you were taught: “Eternal punishment” means never-ending conscious torment.

What the Greek says: “Age-lasting correction.” The word describes duration relative to an age, not infinity. This changes the entire hell debate.

Word 3: Ekklēsia (ἐκκλησία) — “Church”

ἐκκλησία
ekklēsia
ek-klay-SEE-ah
G1577
A called-out assembly, congregation, gathering. From ek (“out of”) + kaleō (“to call”). Used of any assembly—political, civic, or religious. NOT a building, institution, or denomination.

What you were taught: “Church” = a building where you go on Sunday, an institution with clergy and hierarchy.

What the Greek says: A gathering of called-out people. No building. No clergy/laity division. No denominational structure. King James himself ordered translators to keep “church” instead of translating it as “assembly”—because “assembly” would have undermined the institutional church.

Word 4: Sheol (שְׁאוֹל) — “Hell”

שְׁאוֹל
Sheol
sheh-OLE
H7585
The grave, the realm of the dead. Where ALL dead go—righteous and wicked. Not a place of punishment. Jacob expected to go there (Gen 37:35). Job asked God to hide him there (Job 14:13).

What you were taught: “Hell” = a place of eternal fire where bad people go.

What the Hebrew says: The grave. The place of the dead. Everyone goes there. The Old Testament has no concept of “hell” as a place of eternal punishment. Translators mashed Sheol, Hades, Gehenna, and Tartarus into one English word to create a doctrine that doesn’t exist in the original text.

Word 5: Baptizō (βαπτίζω) — “Baptize”

βαπτίζω
baptizō
bap-TID-zoh
G907
To immerse, dip, submerge. From baptō (“to dip”). Used in Greek literature for sinking ships, dying cloth (fully submerging), and drowning. NEVER means to sprinkle or pour.

What you were taught: “Baptism” can mean sprinkling, pouring, or immersion—the method doesn’t matter.

What the Greek says: Immersion. Always. The English word “baptize” is not a translation—it is a transliteration. They carried the Greek word directly into English instead of translating it, because translating it as “immerse” would have challenged the practice of sprinkling.
