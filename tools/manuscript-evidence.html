Manuscript Evidence

What Texts Do We Actually Have, How Old Are They, and Where Do They Differ?

We don’t have the original manuscripts of any biblical book. What we have are copies of copies. But we have an extraordinary number of them—far more than any other ancient text. Understanding what manuscripts exist, how old they are, and where they differ is essential for serious Bible study.

Passages That May Not Be Original

These passages are found in later manuscripts but absent from the earliest and best witnesses:

Verses Missing from Early Manuscripts

  • Mark 16:9-20 — The “long ending” of Mark. Absent from Sinaiticus and Vaticanus. Most scholars believe Mark originally ended at 16:8. The added ending contains snake handling and drinking poison.
  • John 7:53–8:11 — The woman caught in adultery. Beautiful story, but absent from all early Greek manuscripts. Writing style differs from John. Some manuscripts place it in Luke. Likely a true oral tradition added later.
  • 1 John 5:7b-8a — The Comma Johanneum (“the Father, the Word, and the Holy Spirit; and these three are one”). Absent from ALL Greek manuscripts before the 14th century. A later addition to support Trinitarian theology. See our Trinity study.
  • Acts 8:37 — The Ethiopian eunuch’s confession of faith. Absent from early manuscripts. Added later, possibly from a baptismal liturgy.

These textual issues don’t undermine the Bible. If anything, the transparent manuscript evidence strengthens our confidence: we can identify what was added and recover what was original. The original text is not lost—it’s buried under layers that scholars have painstakingly peeled away.
