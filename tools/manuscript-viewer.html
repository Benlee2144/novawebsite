Codex Sinaiticus (א)

c. 330–360 AD

The oldest complete New Testament manuscript. Discovered at St. Catherine's Monastery, Mount Sinai by Constantine Tischendorf in 1844. Now split between the British Library, Leipzig University, Saint Catherine's Monastery, and the National Library of Russia.

Codex Vaticanus (B)

c. 325–350 AD

Considered by many scholars as the most important Greek manuscript. Has been in the Vatican Library since at least 1475. Contains most of the Old and New Testaments in Greek. Missing the Pastoral Epistles, Philemon, and Revelation.

Codex Alexandrinus (A)

c. 400–440 AD

A fifth-century manuscript containing most of the Bible in Greek. Given as a gift to King Charles I of England in 1627. Now housed in the British Library. One of the earliest and most complete manuscripts of the Greek Bible.

Codex Bezae (D)

c. 400 AD

A bilingual Greek-Latin manuscript of the Gospels and Acts. Known for its many unique readings not found in other manuscripts. Named after Theodore Beza, who donated it to Cambridge University in 1581. The most controversial major manuscript.

Textus Receptus

1516 AD (Erasmus)

The "Received Text" compiled by Erasmus of Rotterdam. Based on a handful of late medieval manuscripts. The basis for the King James Version (1611) and most Reformation-era translations. Now superseded by critical texts based on earlier manuscripts.

Majority Text

Various (5th–15th century)

The reading found in the majority of surviving Greek manuscripts (~5,800 total). Also called the Byzantine Text. Later than the Alexandrian manuscripts but numerically dominant. The basis for the Greek Orthodox lectionary tradition.
